In Rokugan the gods are always reaching out. A dream-omen is not a message you request and receive like a letter; it is guidance you open yourself to. You do not put a question to the Fortune and get an answer back. You lay yourself down in the right place, with the right heart, and you listen for the one small part of yourself that speaks the god's will - and most of the time you cannot hear it clearly. That difficulty does not merely reflect humanity's limited understanding of the world, it reveals a fundamental truth about the nature of reality itself.

## Why dreams are hard to read

Three hundred years ago Kitsu Okura set down what he called the six doctrines of why the Fortunes speak to us as they do. The heavens are never silent - a thousand Fortunes are reaching toward you at once, each pulling a different way. A single god, to reach a mortal, must muddy its own voice so as not to crush the small vessel it is speaking to, and it will not spend the great price of speaking plainly when a faithful servant already has guidance enough to discern its will. And the struggle to understand is itself part of what the god wants of you.

So a night spent seeking an omen carries a real chance of **no dream at all**, and a real chance of **a dream that means nothing** - a scrap of the sleeping mind, or another Fortune's crosstalk, wearing the shape of a sending. Noise and silence are expected. They are the price of listening for one voice in a sky full of them.

## What shapes what you receive

Because the heavens are always transmitting, the thing that varies is not the god's mood but the **listener**. The circumstances in which you lie down set your odds:

- **Where you sleep** - a temple of the Fortune you seek, a fitting shrine, holy ground of the right aspect.
- **How you prepare** - guided prayer by a qualified holy person, fasting, purification, the handling of a relevant relic.
- **What is in your heart** - a sincere, open, undivided sleeper who truly seeks the god's will, not one hunting for permission to do what they already meant to do.
- **What you have lately done** - the deeds you carry into sleep can tune you toward or away from a given Fortune's ear.

Even under near-ideal conditions the chance of a blank or an empty dream never falls to nothing. A pilgrim who handled holy relics, prayed under a qualified monk, and slept in the god's own temple with an open heart still might have only a slightly better-than-not chance of being graced with an auspicious dream hinting at the will of the Fortunes. Worse circumstances fare worse.

## When something is listening back

The Fortunes are not the only powers that can reach a sleeper. A mischievous or malevolent spirit that has taken an interest in someone can send a dream of its own - not mere noise, but an active lie, meant to mislead or to afflict. This is one reason such beseeching is done within temples: their holy walls turn away most ill influences, and a sleeper beneath them is far likelier to hear the Fortune alone. Most of the time.

But those walls are not proof against everything. A person already afflicted or pursued by some ill force may find it has followed them even into their dreams beneath a temple's roof. A grave personal sin, or a dark thing the whole party has done together, can weigh the dreams down - dimming the chance of true guidance, and opening the way for a malevolent power to mislead or torment one of the sleepers. And when the very matter the group seeks guidance upon is itself entangled with powerful spirits, that entanglement follows them into sleep as surely as anything they carry in their own hearts.

## Reading the omen

Each person who sleeps seeking guidance receives one dream, resolved by a single die. What they wake with falls into one of three kinds:

- **No dream** - or nothing they can hold onto. The sending, if there was one, slid off them.
- **A meaningless dream** - vivid, often, and easy to over-read, but bearing on nothing.
- **A true sending** - a fragment of the god's actual leaning, strange and open to interpretation, never a plain instruction.

On any single dream you are not told which kind you got - telling the true sending from the noise from the nothing is the whole art. But **across three, four, five sleepers**, a real message is present and can be recovered, if the group is willing to compare what they saw, weigh it, argue over it, and set the noise aside. The gods reward the shrewd who can read them.

## When the dream is only a feeling

A sending need not be a picture at all. Sometimes a sleeper wakes remembering nothing they saw, and still cannot shake a strong feeling fixed on someone or something: waking filled with tenderness toward a particular person, or with dread about the road ahead, or with an unaccountable certainty circling one face of the matter at hand. When such a sending is true, that feeling is the god's leaning carried as pure affect instead of an image - and it is still to be weighed rather than obeyed, for it names no reason. When it is false, this is among the easiest shapes for a lie to wear: the feeling fixes on the wrong thing entirely - a fellow traveler, the foul weather, the aches of the journey, a fear the dreamer already carried into sleep - and it feels every bit as much like true intuition. A feeling with no picture behind it is the hardest omen to interrogate, and the easiest to trust too far.

## The significant dream

Now and then a sleeper is granted a dream of unmistakable weight - the surest sending the night can give. Such a dream does not merely inform; it opens into a **lucid dream**, where the dreamer is given choices: to look deeper into what they themselves were shown, or to sense the shape of what the others received. The more powerful the sending, the more they may learn. And a determined dreamer with the right gifts or resources can sometimes reach for such a dream on purpose, chasing it across a restless night - though a dream pursued is never a dream commanded.

## The mechanics

Each sleeper resolves their night with a single roll (one ten-sided die). Under settled conditions - beseeching a Fortune in its own temple, with no ill force at work and nothing spiritually perilous in the question - the results fall out as:

- **20%** no dream, or none they can keep
- **20%** a random, meaningless dream
- **50%** a true, significant dream
- **10%** a significant dream that opens into lucid dreaming

The die faces are **scrambled** for each night's sitting, so a low roll is as likely to carry a true sending as a high one - no one can read their result off the number. The single fixed point is the **10**, which is always the lucid dream, and which every player is told on sight. A lucid dream is always a gift of further opportunity; but where a party courts spiritual peril, the deeper sight it opens can carry deeper danger with it.

When some ill force has taken an interest, a sin or spiritual weight lies on the party, or the matter itself stirs powerful spirits, these odds shift: the chance of nothing, and of an actively **misleading** dream, rises, and what guidance comes grows harder to trust.

### Rerolls

A dream may be rerolled, and a reroll **replaces** the dream - you dream again, and keep the new dream, whatever it proves to be. A character may earn several rerolls over a single night, drawing on more than one of the means below at once - spending void points for one reroll, then a Player Character Point for another, then Lucky for a third. The ways to reach for another dream:

- spending a **Player Character Point** - which may be done again and again, one reroll for each point spent;
- the advantage **Lucky**, once per adventure;
- a **Togashi Ise Zumi**'s fourth-dan gift, for which dream rolls count as contested - though the monk must keep the rerolled dream even if it is worse;
- spending **void points scaled by your Honor**: **(6 minus your Honor)** points, declared after you see your roll and before you wake, and payable with the void a night's rest restores - so the honorable reroll cheaply, and the dishonorable dearly. Honor runs in half-steps, so a character at 3.5 Honor owes a cost that falls between 2 and 3 points, settled by a 50/50 roll that rounds it up or down. Spending void points may only produce one additional dream roll, regardless of whether a character has enough void points to continue rerolling.

The Merchant fifth-dan reroll does not reach dream rolls. And a reroll is never blind: should it land on no dream or a meaningless one, you are told as much - so a reroll can never quietly confound a true sending with a convincing lie.
