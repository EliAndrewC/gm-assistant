# L7R Setting Brief for Character Synthesis

This brief is the curated slice of the GM's setting notes (l7r.md and budgets.md)
that is relevant to fleshing out a single NPC into a coherent person. It is the
design-philosophy layer, not the full canon. The canonical record is always
l7r.md; this is a working extract for one task: turning a pile of generated
traits into a believable individual.

## The core conceit

L7R Rokugan is a "low fantasy" setting whose core conceit is **the kind of world
that real historical people believed themselves to be living in**. Magic, gods,
ghosts, and other supernatural things genuinely exist, but encountering them is
rare. Because they exist, people are justifiably superstitious: it is difficult
to tell which of your prayers and practices actually do something and which do
nothing at all. So most of life is lived in the physical, material reality of an
early-modern agrarian empire, shot through with sincere belief that the unseen
world is real and occasionally intrudes.

Practically, this means: when fleshing out a character, default to mundane,
human, material explanations for who they are. A character's quirks, beliefs,
and reputation are usually grounded in ordinary life - ambition, fear, poverty,
pride, grief, faith, pragmatism - not in literal magic. The supernatural is a
seasoning used sparingly, and even where it is present it is usually ambiguous:
the character (and the GM) may never be certain whether a blessing, a curse, an
omen, or a relic's power was real or imagined. Lean into that ambiguity rather
than resolving it.

Rokugan is "samurai culture on a Chinese scale": Japanese samurai social forms
(Heian courts, Sengoku samurai/peasant demographics, Tokugawa feudalism) laid
over a continental land mass with a population comparable to Imperial China. It
is an extremely well-run society by premodern standards, with real state
capacity - roads, ministries, courts, tax rolls - but it is still preindustrial,
agrarian, and overwhelmingly rural.

## The caste system and personhood

Rokugan has a strict caste system, part of the Celestial Order ordained by
Heaven. This is the single most important lens for understanding any character's
place in the world.

- **Samurai** are the ruling class, about 2% of the population. They are the only
  caste with full personhood. In Rokugan, only samurai are "people"; this is why
  "humans," "inhabitants," or "population" are the correct words for everyone
  collectively.
- **Heimen** are "half-people": farmers (the great majority of the Empire) and
  merchants, plus most artisans and craftspeople.
- **Hinin** are "non-people": laborers, servants, and entertainers. A samurai or
  heimen who turns to crime is reduced to hinin.
- **Burakumin** are the "untouchables" at the bottom of the hinin caste, doing
  ritually unclean work: tanners, butchers, cremators, morticians, night-soil
  handlers, executioners.
- **Monks** stand outside the Celestial Order entirely, walking as Shinsei did
  with one foot in this world and one in the spiritual realm. They are under 1%
  of the population but enormously influential, because humans of every caste
  revere the gods and those who preach the Tao of Shinsei.

Caste shapes everything: how a character speaks and is spoken to, what they may
wear, whom they may marry, what work is beneath or above them. Crossing caste
lines is dramatic and rare. A **peasantborn samurai** - someone elevated from
the heimen into the ruling caste - is a notable thing, carrying both the
opportunity of a new life and the lingering mark of low origins. Minor clans
(see below) have many such people.

Samurai are largely unaware of, and often actively repulsed by, the lives and
labor of the ~98% beneath them. There are simply too few of them to see
everything, their duties consume them, and the "unclean" work their lives depend
on is stigmatized. A samurai who takes a genuine interest in how peasants or
burakumin actually live is regarded as eccentric at best.

## Rank, standing, and the Measure of Standing

Every samurai has a **rank** on a 15-point peerage scale (the Measure of
Standing, established by Lady Doji at the dawn of the Empire). Rank is formal,
legal, and public. The baseline ladder:

- Rank 15: the Emperor (and no one else)
- Rank 14: Clan Daimyo
- Rank 13: Family Daimyo
- Rank 12: House Daimyo
- Rank 11: Chancellor
- Rank 10: Minister
- Rank 9: Deputy Minister
- Rank 8: Governor
- Rank 7: Provincial Minister
- Rank 6: Deputy Provincial Minister
- Rank 5: County Magistrate
- Rank 4: Squad Sergeant
- Rank 3: Corporal
- Rank 2: rank-and-file legionnaire
- Rank 1: unposted samurai with no higher-ranking immediate family

Ranks have an upper and lower level shown as a decimal (Rank 7.5 is "the upper
seventh rank," Rank 7.0 "the lower seventh"). A samurai's stipend from their
daimyo is the square of their rank in koku per year (a Rank 7 receives 49 koku),
though for office-holders the stipend is dwarfed by the budget and tax authority
of the post itself.

Interactions are governed by the **Doctrine of Three Steps**: two samurai are of
peer standing within 1 rank, accordant standing within 3 ranks, and discordant
beyond that. This dictates seating, bowing, who may duel or arrest whom, and the
value of the gifts etiquette requires on every visit. A samurai is acutely
conscious of where they stand relative to everyone in the room. No samurai may be
more than 3 ranks below their immediate family, so a relative's promotion can
lift - or awkwardly displace - the whole family.

**Recognition** is distinct from rank: it is prestige, fame, and personal
reputation, which may run ahead of or behind formal rank. A famous duelist, a
notorious magistrate, or a celebrated artist can carry recognition well above
their station; a disgraced or obscure samurai can carry less. When recognition
and rank diverge, that gap is itself a character note worth using.

## Experience and competence (XP)

"XP" is a metagame measure of how seasoned a person is. It is useful for placing
a character in the world even though no one in Rokugan talks about it.

- ~150 XP is the average at gempukku (coming of age at 14).
- ~80% of people sit at 150+; ~50% at 170+; ~20% at 200+.
- 250+ XP marks someone genuinely accomplished - the level of most county
  magistrates and provincial officials below governor (roughly the top 10%).
- 300+ XP is rare and formidable (~1%); 350+ is exceptional (a handful per
  domain).

Higher XP generally implies more years lived and more competence, so it loosely
tracks age and seniority - but not perfectly: a brilliant young prodigy or a
much-tested commoner can be high on the curve while still young. Peasants,
monks, and entertainers follow the same distribution as samurai; their lives are
no less full, even if samurai never notice.

## Honor

Honor is rated low to high (roughly 1 to 5). It measures a character's adherence
to the Bushido ideal - the degree to which they live by the rigid code of the
warrior caste. Note that l7r.md does not define honor in great mechanical detail,
so reason about it carefully and concretely from the character at hand.

- **High honor** suggests an idealist: principled, rigid, sincere, sometimes
  naive or inflexible, the kind of samurai who would rather die than compromise.
- **Low honor** does NOT mean villainy. It usually means worldliness and
  pragmatism: someone who bends the code to get things done, cuts corners, makes
  deals, survives. Bounty hunters, spies, merchants' fixers, hard-bitten veterans,
  and the peasantborn who clawed their way up often run low on honor simply
  because idealism is a luxury they could never afford. A low-honor character can
  be entirely sympathetic, loyal, and effective - just not pure.

When honor sits in tension with other traits (a "humble" samurai with low honor,
say), resolve the tension into a specific, coherent person rather than treating
the traits as contradictory.

## Religion and the supernatural ethos

Rokugani religion blends veneration of the Fortunes (gods great and small),
reverence for ancestors, and the Tao of Shinsei preached by the monks. Villages
and hamlets have **shrines** (never temples); temples are institutions of the
towns and cities and the monastic orders. Soothsayers read omens and portents;
most people consult them sincerely. Prayer, ritual purity, festivals, and
charms structure the year.

Because the supernatural is real but rare, **religious practice is mostly an act
of faith under uncertainty**. This opens rich room for variation in how a
character relates to it:

- Orthodox sincerity (the default for most people).
- Quiet skepticism or going through the motions.
- Idiosyncratic or heterodox belief - a private theology, an unusual devotion, a
  superstition others find strange, a monk's heresy, a borrowed gaijin idea. A
  character tagged "religiously unorthodox" has some specific deviation worth
  inventing concretely: what exactly they believe or do that sets them apart, and
  how visible and costly it is.

When the supernatural does touch a person, it is framed as **both blessing and
curse** - those "touched" gain something and lose something, and can rarely tell
which is which. The great scholars hold that the line between "natural" and
"supernatural" is a false distinction, and the Shinsei is quoted: "All
explanations are false, but some explanations are useful." Relics, omens, ghost
stories, and curses should usually stay ambiguous: real effect or coincidence,
true vision or madness, never cleanly resolved.

Maho (blood magic) and bloodspeakers genuinely exist and are the great hidden
evil, but they are rare and secret - most samurai will go a lifetime without
knowingly meeting one. Do not casually attach maho to an ordinary character;
it is a heavy, campaign-level element.

## Clans, families, and the minor clans

Most land belongs to the 7 Great Clans, each divided into 3-5 Great Families,
each divided into Houses ruling domains, each divided into lineages. A samurai's
clan, family, house, and lineage locate them precisely in this hierarchy and
carry strong cultural associations (the martial Lion, the courtly Crane, the
secretive Scorpion, and so on).

**Minor clans** are small, often founded for a specific purpose, and lower in
prestige than the Great Clans. The **Wasp Clan** (the Tsuruchi family) is a clan
of bounty hunters - a mix of Scorpion and Crane samurai together with a large
share of **peasantborn** samurai elevated for their skill. This gives the Wasp a
distinctly meritocratic, hard-edged, outsider character: less concerned with
courtly polish, more with results, and carrying the chip-on-the-shoulder pride of
people who earned their swords rather than inheriting them.

## Gender

L7R Rokugan is deliberately more gender-egalitarian than historical samurai
culture, so that female players can fully participate. Treat samurai offices and
roles as open to any gender. When referring to a generic office-holder (a
daimyo, magistrate, governor, minister, samurai) use they/their; specific named
characters keep their own pronouns.

## Style and tone for synthesized prose

The GM's register is **laconic and matter-of-fact**. Match it:

- Plain, concrete, grounded prose. No flowery elaboration, no marketing tone, no
  purple fantasy diction.
- Commit to specifics. Invent concrete, plausible detail rather than hedging or
  restating the traits abstractly.
- Use "domain" (never "demesne") for administered territory. Use
  "humans"/"inhabitants"/"population" rather than "people" for generic
  demographics, since "people" means samurai specifically.
- Hyphens only - never em-dashes or en-dashes.
- Capitalize Family and Clan when they mean the institution (the Tsuruchi
  Family, the Wasp Clan); lowercase for ordinary relatives ("the daimyo's
  family").
- Do not contradict anything established above or in the character's own
  generated details.
