# Phase 0 Research: Village Visual Variation Knobs

China-first (Song/Ming rice south, per the skill's core principle), Japan corroborating. This file resolves the design unknowns and records the historical grounding that FR-003 requires before any knob ships. The per-knob "why" recorded here is the source that will be copied into `settlements.md` as each knob lands (project policy: every research-driven rule carries its reasoning where the rule lives).

## D1. How different did real same-region rice villages actually look?

**Decision**: Real wet-rice villages of the *same terrain type and region* rhymed but were not identical; meaningful visual variation lived on four axes the generator currently fixes to one value each - (1) terrain/field archetype, (2) settlement form, (3) focal & incidental features, (4) crop/land-use overlay. Recovering variation on these axes is historically honest, not manufactured variety.

**Rationale**: Settlement geography of the Chinese rice south (Knapp; surveys across Fujian/Jiangxi/Guangdong/Hunan/Anhui) classifies village *morphology* into distinct types (团 nucleated / 带-条 linear-banded / 环 ring / 散 dispersed) and *terrain-driven field forms* (broad-valley paddy, 梯田 contour terraces, 圩田/围田 diked polder, 桑基鱼塘 mulberry-dike fish-pond, delta water-town). Two villages a few miles apart in the same valley shared a *type* but differed in cluster arrangement, focal features (a lineage hall, a fengshui pond, a water-mouth complex), and secondary land use. The current skill models exactly one type (broad-valley nucleated comb-fan) with one value on every focal/incidental axis, which is why Kikuta and Hoshigaoka twin.

**Alternatives considered**: (a) "Rice villages were simply samey, accept it" - rejected: true only *within* one type on the *primary* field form; it does not explain identical cluster/headman/focal placement, which is an authoring artifact. (b) Pure RNG jitter of the existing template - rejected: the GM's complaint is precisely that jitter-within-a-template is insufficient; the variation must be structural.

## D2. Family A - the within-archetype knobs (Phase 1, the MVP)

These vary the layout the seed currently only jitters within, so two nucleated broad-valley villages with the same `down_deg` read as different places. Each entry: value space, historical-typing rule, grounding.

- **cluster-position** - where the village sits relative to the field. Values: high-margin (current), a flank, a mid-margin bulge, a valley-mouth vs valley-head end, on a natural rise. *Grounding*: villages sat on whatever non-arable higher/drier ground existed; the "high windward margin" is one common case, not the only one (背山面水 orients the back to high ground and the face to water, but which corner that is depends on the valley). *Typing*: constrained to stay field-adjacent and off the flood-prone toe; the pond/stream source and the cluster must remain hydrologically sensible (cluster not below the drain line).
- **cluster-shape / aspect / orientation** - Values: round disk (current), elongated-along-the-margin, crescent hugging the field, or split into 2 sub-hamlets. *Grounding*: cluster shape followed the available dry ground - a levee gives a linear string, a knoll a round blob, a field-edge a crescent; two-hamlet splits are common where two patches of high ground flank the arable. *Typing*: the split form implies enough separation to read as two hamlets, not one loose blob; the elongated/crescent forms orient along the field margin.
- **lane-skeleton** - the internal road pattern, with the headman house and primary shrine **derived** from it. Values: single spine (current), T, Y, cross, along-the-water. *Grounding*: a nucleated village's lanes followed its site - a spine along a ridge, a T where a spur met the field track, a cross at a small market node, a waterside lane in a stream village. The headman's compound sat at a *focal* point of that skeleton (the head of the main lane / the best ground), never at a fixed pixel offset. *Typing*: along-the-water requires a stream/canal adjacent to the cluster.
- **water-source-position** - Values (pond): a corner (any of four), mid-margin, or a *chain* of small ponds; (stream): entry edge (any). *Grounding*: a *tameike* sat wherever the catchment delivered water above the command area - not always the NW corner; small villages often had a chain of little ponds rather than one big reservoir; a stream diversion entered from whichever edge the valley fed. *Typing*: the source must sit uphill of the field intake (gravity feed); a chain implies a drier/smaller catchment.
- **plot-texture + grain-drift** - Values: plot size (small irregular hand-shaped / medium / larger regular blocks / long strips) and bund regularity (organic vs rectilinear-grid), plus a per-field paddy-grain *drift* off the current rigid uniform 45 deg. *Grounding*: plot size and regularity varied with land-division history - tiny hand-shaped plots in old organically-grown fields, rectilinear blocks under a survey/allotment system (the 条里 jori grid in Japan; well-field/allotment remnants in China), long strips under strip tenancy; and real valleys let the grain drift rather than holding one angle (settlements.md already flags the uniform 45 deg as a known residual). *Typing*: rectilinear-grid regularity implies a planned/surveyed field (a reclamation or allotment context), not an old organically-grown one.
- **focal-feature set** - a rolled/pinned subset of the historically-attested village foci (see D4). *Grounding*: real villages carried a *variable* kit; the current fixed "shrine + graveyard + wells + windbreak" is the floor, and the differentiators are the *optional* foci.

## D3. The roll model (independent, seeded, historically gated)

**Decision**: Unspecified knobs roll INDEPENDENTLY - one deterministic draw per knob off the seed - each constrained by its historical-typing rule; there are no preset "village type" bundles. Coherence comes from the per-knob typing rules (a value invalid for the stated geography/other knobs is excluded from that knob's roll), not from a bundle.

**Rationale**: Independent knobs give the widest variety (the goal) and make "pin one knob, roll the rest" natural (US3). The typing rules keep combinations coherent (e.g. a mulberry-fishpond overlay is excluded in a dry upland; a rectilinear plot grid is excluded in an organically-grown old field). This matches the GM's Session-2026-07-13 clarification.

**Alternatives considered**: weighted preset bundles (roll a named "village type", jitter within) - rejected by the GM: more coherent out of the box but less variety and a coarser pin-one-knob story. Determinism is preserved either way (seeded draws; no wall-clock/`Math.random` - the skill already forbids nondeterministic entropy).

## D4. Focal-feature catalog (FR-009) - historical grounding

Each optional focus, its real basis (China-first), and its placement typing. All placed by the SAME overlap/placement invariants that already gate hand-authored features (off the paddy/marsh/lane, non-overlapping, set-backs).

- **fengshui crescent pond (半月塘 / 风水塘)** - a crescent/half-moon pond in front of the village, ubiquitous in Huizhou and Hakka single-lineage villages (drainage + fire water + fengshui "gathering qi"). *Typing*: sits in front of (down-slope / field-facing of) the cluster; distinct from the irrigation pond.
- **ancestral hall (祠堂)** - the central/head communal building of a single-lineage 宗族 village; larger than a house, on the best ground at the head of the main lane. *Typing*: only in a single-lineage village; occupies a lane focal point (competes with the headman compound for the head-of-lane spot).
- **water-mouth complex (水口)** - at the village's low exit: a grove + a bridge + a small pavilion/temple "locking in" the water and qi (Huizhou classic). *Typing*: at the low/drain exit where the connector track leaves; combines existing grove + bridge + a small hall.
- **mill / waterwheel** - a water-powered hulling/grinding mill on the drain or a diversion; a visible device. *Typing*: on a watercourse with enough fall (the drain outfall or a stream), not on still pond water.
- **market stand / ground** - a periodic-market clearing (草市) at a road/water node; a small open space with a few stalls. *Typing*: at a lane/track junction or the water-mouth, where traffic converges.
- **secondary shrine** - a second small shrine (an earth-god 土地庙 at the field edge, or a subsidiary kami) beyond the primary. *Typing*: away from the primary shrine, on dry ground; the existing shrine-placement invariants apply.

## D5. Family B - archetype knobs (later phases), grounding + incremental plan

Each is a substantial build (new field-geometry generator + archetype-specific validator rules + grounding). Delivered one at a time (FR-010).

- **Field/terrain archetype**: broad-valley comb-fan (have it) / **contour terraces (梯田)** - curved contour bunds following the slope, village perched at the terrace head or foot (Longji, Yuanyang); / **polder grid (圩田/围田)** - rectilinear diked cells, water on multiple sides, village on the dike or a rise (Taihu, Dongting, Poyang lake regions); / **ribbon valley (谷戸-type)** - a narrow valley with a thin ribbon of paddy along a stream; / **mulberry-dike fish-pond (桑基鱼塘)** - a checkerboard of fish ponds with raised mulberry/cane dikes (Pearl River & Taihu deltas). Highest visual payoff: terraces (curved contours) and polder (rectilinear cells) are the furthest from the fan.
- **Settlement form**: nucleated (have it) / **linear** (houses strung along a road, a dike-top, or a canal) / **water-town** (houses fronting canals with stone quays) / **dispersed** (scattered farmsteads, Tonami/Izumo type). Linear is the cheapest big differentiator.
- **Land-use overlay**: rice-mono (have it) / rice + **mulberry-dikes + fish-ponds** / rice + **rape** (golden) / **lotus** ponds / **tea-terrace** fringe - a color-and-texture layer over the field archetype. *Typing*: each overlay is region-typed (mulberry-fishpond = delta; tea = hill fringe; rape = rice-rape rotation belt).

**Sequencing recommendation**: prove the archetype-registry mechanism with ONE Family-B archetype first (linear settlement form OR contour terraces - biggest bang per unit build), before adding the rest.

## D6. The pool-level twin-detector (FR-013)

**Decision**: Add a cross-map check to `check_village.py` that, given a set of village manifests, computes a per-pair structural-difference count over the SC-001 axis set - {cluster centroid region, cluster shape/aspect, headman position relative to the cluster, lane-skeleton type, water-source position, focal-feature set, paddy-grain orientation} - and FLAGS any two same-`down_deg` villages that differ on fewer than a threshold (target: at least 4 of ~7 axes must differ). It runs over the pool (not per-map), so it is a pool-level tool alongside the per-map gate.

**Rationale**: Matches the project's check-driven ethos and mechanically guards the distinctiveness goal against regression to twinning. Same-`down_deg` scoping avoids penalizing villages that already differ trivially by water direction. Cross-map is a new *kind* of check (compares manifests) but reuses the manifest fields the per-map gate already records.

**Alternatives considered**: structural metric as a report + human review only (no gate) - the GM chose the automated detector. Risk to manage: false positives (two genuinely-different villages flagged) - mitigated by tuning the axis set + threshold and by the axes being coarse "region/type" buckets, not pixel positions.

**Open tuning item (for /speckit-tasks + implementation)**: the exact axis discretization (how "cluster centroid region" and "water-source position" bucket into comparable categories) and the threshold (4-of-7 is the target) are set during implementation against the re-varied pool; recorded in `settlements.md` with the reasoning.

## D7. Principle X status (ruff / mypy) for the diagram skill

**Decision (GM-directed 2026-07-13, revised from the initial draft)**: No deferral. The diagram skill is brought to FULL Principle X compliance as this feature's foundation, via a **gate-enforced per-module ratchet**. Concretely: (a) fix the ruff findings and wire `ruff` + `ruff format --check` + `mypy` + `pytest --cov-fail-under=100` into one diagram-skill gate command (a `make done` equivalent) so the checks run together and are maintained going forward; (b) turn on `mypy --strict` in the gate immediately, with **new** feature code strict from day one and the three legacy engine modules (`settlement.py`, `check_village.py`, `waterfields.py`) temporarily relaxed via per-module `mypy` config, then annotated to strict in focused per-module passes within this feature, each pass flipping its module off the relax-list so the gate blocks backsliding.

**Rationale (measured)**: `ruff check` reports ~10 findings (trivial - unused vars, lambda-assignments) - cheap to fix and gate now. `mypy --strict` reports ~2,470 errors (settlement.py 1,266, check_village.py 1,107, waterfields.py 99) because the engine passes an untyped manifest dict - a genuinely large migration. The GM chose the ratchet over (i) a full upfront ~2,470-error block before any knob work, and over (ii) spinning mypy out as a separate feature: the ratchet reaches full compliance inside this feature, keeps a green gate throughout, avoids one unreviewable diff, and - critically - wires the checks into the gate so the gap cannot silently reopen.

**Alternatives considered**: full upfront migration (rejected: large single block that delays the distinctiveness win and lands as one huge diff); mypy as its own separate feature 006 (rejected: moves compliance out of this effort). The ratchet keeps compliance in-effort and gate-enforced. **Implementation note**: mypy's per-module `[[tool.mypy.overrides]]` in `pyproject.toml` is the ratchet mechanism - global strict, with each unmigrated module listed as relaxed until its typing pass removes it.
