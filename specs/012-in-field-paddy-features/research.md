# Phase 0 Research: In-Field & Field-Margin Paddy Features

**This is the Principle XII OPENING BOOKEND.** Grave mounds, feng-shui knolls, rock outcrops, and small
ponds - deliberate non-rice features a paddy tiles around or that sit at its margin. Each element states
the historical reality (China rice-south first, Japan corroborating), whether the proposed design matches,
and what DETERMINES it. Research was independent web research; frequencies are flagged where interpolated.

**Two premises were CORRECTED by the research** (this is the bookend doing its job):
1. The idea of scattering these across the flat paddy is wrong. **Flat, flooded valley-bottom paddy is the
   archetype that hosts them LEAST** - it is the most valuable, most intensively worked, and *wet*: a dry
   grave/knoll/rock sits poorly in standing water. The features are **margin- and slope-biased**.
2. My recollection of a blanket imperial ban on graves-on-arable was **not confirmed** and is dropped. The
   real driver in the rice south is feng-shui + land economy, not statute.

## The organizing finding

> **These features live at the paddy-to-slope MARGIN and on TERRACED/hill paddy, not free-floating in the
> flat wet center.** The one thing that genuinely belongs in the flat wet middle is an open-water POND (a
> low pocket too deep for rice). Everything else biases to the edge or the hill.

## D1. Grave mounds

**Reality.** North China dry plain: graves are islands in the arable (~1/ha, ~55 m2, growing as kin are
interred) - but that is DRY wheat/maize land, the *secondary* reference. **Rice south (primary):** feng-shui
*yin*-siting wants a backing hill and a downslope view over water, so graves go on the **slope / field
margin / a lineage grave-hill**, never in the flooded center. Graves also functioned as enforceable **land
claims** (a marked grave staked lineage territory - in the hills, that meant claiming hill/waste ground).
Japan corroborates a softer version: shared *kyodo bochi* as small clusters at the paddy edge, on the
forest fringe, or in temple grounds - and *sometimes* small clusters among the valley paddy (farmers pass
them daily).

**Design (GM: "both are fine", 2026-07-20 - CALIBRATED LIBERTY, disclosed).** Default: grave mounds as
small clusters at the **field margin** where paddy meets rising slope (rice-south grounding). Additionally
permitted: an **occasional in-field grave island** the flat paddy tiles around - justified by the Japanese
"clusters among the paddy" corroboration and the north-China precedent, taken deliberately for the visual
the GM first pictured. Recorded here as a departure from the strict rice-south default, not an oversight.

> DETERMINES: feng-shui (backing hill + downslope water) + land economy (dead on slope/waste, not prime wet
> paddy) + tenure (grave = lineage claim). Archetypes: terraces / ribbon / valley-margin (+ rare valley
> island). NOT polder, NOT dike-pond. Frequency: **interpolated** for rice-south - a handful per village
> clustered at the slope margin / one lineage grave-hill, NOT per-field.

## D2. Feng-shui knolls

**Reality.** Village fengshui forests are well-documented across southern China - groves preserved on
"dragon-vein" ridges, spurs, and knolls as the "dragon's hair", left unfarmed to store qi (Hongcun's
Leigang Hill "horns"). **Caution:** most coincide with the village **back-grove (chinju no mori)** we
already draw. A free-standing knoll out in the fields does occur, but characteristically at a **spur-toe
descending into the flat**, and it survives partly *because* that higher/rockier ground was a poor paddy
candidate anyway. A true wooded knoll in the flat middle is rare.

> DETERMINES: feng-shui dragon-vein spur/knoll + ground too high/rocky to paddy. Archetypes: valley-margin
> (spur-toe), terraces (wooded spurs), occasional ribbon. **Keep distinct from the back-grove** - a
> standalone in-field knoll is <=1/village and often already the back-grove. Frequency: **interpolated.**

## D3. Rock outcrops

**Reality (archetype expectation confirmed).** Terraces are carved to the mountain's original contour on
real bedrock, so risers/bunds **wrap around unremovable outcrops** - in-field rock is realistic on
`contour_terraces` (Yuanyang/Honghe, Longji). Flat valley paddy is **alluvial** (built on river-valley
flats) - loose stones are cleared into bunds; a bedrock outcrop only appears at the **slope margin**.
Polder is reclaimed silt - **no bedrock, absent**. Ribbon (confined rocky valley floor) - occasional.
Dike-pond (delta silt) - absent.

> DETERMINES: pure geology/topography (bedrock at surface on carved slopes; alluvium has none). Archetypes:
> **terraces (in-field)**, occasional ribbon; valley margin-only & rare; NOT polder/dike-pond. Frequency:
> **interpolated / uncited** - "terraces wrap bedrock" is geomorphological inference, not a counted rate.

## D4. Small ponds

**Reality (real, multi-functional).** (a) **Irrigation reservoirs (tameike):** the hard-data anchor -
>210,000 in Japan, 85% in dry-summer western Japan/Seto, sited at the **head of the irrigation chain**. (b)
**Feng-shui half-moon pond** dug in front of an ancestral hall / homestead (Hakka weilong; Hongcun's
crescent "ox-stomach" pond) - both geomantic and a water store. (c) **Fish/duck/lotus ponds** in low
pockets held as OPEN water (distinct from the reed/lotus *bog* we already model, which is choked). For
`mulberry_dike_fishpond` a pond "obstacle" is meaningless - open water *is* its fabric.

> DETERMINES: low ground (a pocket too deep to paddy, held as open water) / head of the irrigation line (a
> header reservoir near the sluice, distinct from the main village pond) / feng-shui (half-moon by a
> homestead-hall). Archetypes: valley, terraces (header atop the flight), polder (borrow-pits/sumps), ribbon
> (pool). N/A dike-pond. Frequency: tameike density is hard data for dry-summer Japan; half-moon ponds are
> ~1 per major homestead/hall; per-map counts otherwise **interpolated**.

## D5. The white gaps that started this

Kikuta's "white/blank patches" in the paddy are NOT any of the above - they are **tessellation gaps** in
the comb plot geometry showing the parchment background through (no base fill under the field; the plot
polygons do not tile perfectly). Flat wet paddy has no bare islands, so these must be **FILLED** (a
paddy-green base under the plots), NOT dressed up as infertile ground. The deliberate features above are
then placed where the matrix says - they do not "explain" the gaps.
