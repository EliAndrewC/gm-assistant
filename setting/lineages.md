SAMURAI LINEAGES:

Every samurai retainer belongs to a noble lineage.  These are not simply bloodlines, but well-defined political coalitions.  Each lineage whose population makes up at least 10% of the samurai in the domain generally has a representative on the house Chancellery, which sets policy through its advice given to the daimyo.  The "ruling" lineage bears the name of the house, e.g. the Wakashi house is ruled by a daimyo from the Wakashi lineage, and one of the daimyo's close family members (often a spouse or sibling or child) will be the Wakashi chancellor.

As an example, let's consider the Ryusei domain of the Mirumoto family of the Dragon clan.  The Ryusei domain is a roughly-average-sized House with a population of about 4,200 samurai out of a total of 210,000 humans (remember that the median domain has 5,000 samurai out of 250,000 humans, so this is a fairly typical domain).

The Ryusei House Chancellery is composed of representatives from its six largest lineages, which collectively make up almost 90% of the domain. The ruling lineage (i.e. the daimyo's lineage) is the Ryusei lineage, and it and the others are listed below:

Note that these numbers reflect the nature and character of the lineages, as is usually the case for most domains.  Some examples:

-> The Moe lineage is the most commercial, and has the most connection to merchants, as reflected by how they pursue the high-profit stations of Governor and County Magistrate and prioritize seeking positions in the lucrative ministries of Works and Revenue.  Moe samurai are often trained as samurai merchants for this reason.

-> The Sasara lineage is the most pious, and is so religiously observant that they focus on obtaining positions in the Ministry of Rites.  However, they have recently lost many county magistrate positions and currently have no governors, because their religious zeal caused them to align with a new religious movement (the Baitenist movement) to a degree that many officials were stripped of their positions after allowing tenant farmers to leave their lands in mass numbers.

-> The Isa lineage is the most warlike, to a degree which is expected for members of their bloodline but unusual for Dragon clan samurai from Mirumoto houses such as the Ryusei.  Their "bloodthirsty" nature is reflected in their pursuit of positions within the Ministry of War.

Note that in Ryusei lands, all of the lineages are "cosmopolitan lineages" and there are no "provincial lineages" or "dynasty provinces".  To define these terms:

-> Sometimes a province will be staffed almost entirely by members of a specific lineage, whose samurai mostly live there.  This often happens when the geographic area of this province is the home of those samurai from long ago.  For example, the Aki lineage in Daidoji lands is from the province and provincial city of Akishi.

-> Therefore, the Aki are a "provincial lineage".

-> The Aki province of Akishi is a "dynasty province".

-> All of the Ryusei provinces are assigned governors and other officials by the Ryusei chancellery, and therefore these are known as "stewardship provinces".

-> Because none of the Ryusei lineages are tied to any specific province, they are all known as "cosmopolitan lineages".

LINEAGE PATRONAGE AND MUTUAL AID:

Lineages are political and cultural coalitions rather than being primarily fiscal entities.  They do not collect taxes, do not maintain treasuries of their own, and do not directly pay stipends - those flows belong to the Imperial hierarchy of daimyo and provincial governors and county magistrates.  Specifically, samurai stipends are paid by whoever holds the relevant tax-farming cut at the tier where the samurai serves: the daimyo's tax farming covers the capital, each provincial governor's tax farming covers their province, and each county magistrate's tax farming covers their county.  ("Tax farming" is used throughout these documents in its loose modern sense - an office-attached tax allocation closer to the Tokugawa *chigyō* or medieval European prebend than to the strict Roman *publicani* model of auctioned collection rights.  See [`budgets.md` - Ministry Budgets](https://github.com/EliAndrewC/l7r/blob/master/setting/budgets.md#ministry-budgets) for the full terminology note.)  A provincial samurai is paid by their governor regardless of which lineage either of them belongs to.  A samurai's oath of fealty goes to the daimyo, their daily authority comes from their governor, and their political representation comes from their lineage chancellor - and these three relationships are administered by three different people who do not always agree.

That said, each lineage maintains a parallel system of voluntary contributions, administered by the lineage chancellor, which functions as the lineage's informal endowment for supporting its own.  Wealthy lineage members - county magistrates with substantial tax-farming income, provincial ministers in lucrative posts, senior officials with discretionary surplus - are expected to contribute generously to their chancellor for the support of less-fortunate kinsmen and the advancement of promising young samurai.  The contributions are framed as voluntary, and there is no formal mechanism to compel them, but the social pressure is intense: a wealthy member who fails to contribute risks being shamed within their extended family, and the chancellery influences appointment decisions in ways that can suddenly become very inconvenient for an uncharitable lineage member.

The chancellor's role is not just to collect and disburse but to make the patronage visible.  When a young samurai receives a hand-up - a first appointment, a stipend supplement during a lean year, a wedding gift, a sword commissioned on their behalf - the chancellor names the patron explicitly:

"You should be grateful to your uncle Yuan.  His largesse helped me secure this promotion for you, and his generosity will enable you to represent our family with pride until you are able to stand on your own.  Perhaps you will succeed and rise to his level someday and be able to serve as a similar example to the next generation."

This explicit attribution serves two purposes.  First, it confers status on the patron, making their generosity socially legible as a marker of their position within the lineage.  Second, it creates obligation: the recipient knows whose largesse made their advancement possible, and is expected to remember that when they themselves become wealthy.  The cycle is recursive - today's recipient is tomorrow's patron - and lineage cohesion is reinforced across generations.

Many ambitious samurai are drawn to high-paying posts (county magistrate, Minister of Works) not purely for personal enrichment but for the prestige of eventually becoming the lineage's named patron of the next generation.  Many county magistrates describe their motivation in those terms: not "I want to be rich" but "I want to be the uncle whose name my nephews are taught to revere when they receive their first commissions."  The patronage system thus acts as a moderate counterweight to pure self-enrichment by senior officials, channeling some of the wealth that would otherwise be hoarded back into supporting the lineage.
