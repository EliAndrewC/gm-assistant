# Daikoku Dreams - the Masamune sword

Dream-omen scene for Daidoji samurai of the Aki lineage who sleep in a temple of Daikoku in Akishi to seek portents about the errand of Nagamasa's sword. In their prayer each PC attunes to one of the temple's two Daikoku relics and, in doing so, opens themselves to the general guidance of the Fortune of Wealth.

## The question the PCs are contemplating

The PCs are Daidoji of the Aki lineage, set to help pay down their lineage's war debts. Their latest errand is from Governor Kureno of Akishi: to bring Daidoji Nagamasa, a kinsman of the lineage, to part with his family sword - a famous blade of the great smith Daidoji Masamune, second-greatest of all swordsmiths, whose blades are said to hunger for blood - as a "gift exchange" for the lineage, not a sale. Nagamasa will not give it up. Kureno gave no explicit orders, only that the PCs could surely make keeping the sword feel intolerable to him next to parting with it, and that Nagamasa's next of kin have already agreed the sword should pass - which sounds much like leave to press him, to hurt him, and to profit by his death. The roads before them fall into two:

- **Pursue** - try to secure the sword for the lineage, by means each PC judges justified:
  - Win Nagamasa to a true gift-exchange, or pressure him until keeping the blade costs him more than losing it.
  - At the far, unspoken end Kureno hinted (his heirs have already agreed) - make his death serve the lineage.
- **Decline** - do not secure it:
  - Refuse the errand openly, and stand between Nagamasa and whoever the governor sends next.
  - Report a failure to Kureno, true or invented, and turn her off sending anyone at all.

## The divine direction (what Daikoku wants here)

Everything below is **Daikoku's** stance on **this specific matter** - not a universal law, and not what every god would want of this same case. Daikoku is the Fortune of Wealth and Bounty, but of *honest* wealth and the *lasting* prosperity of a house, and so in a matter of gain the weight of the sending falls on how the wealth is come by, not on whether the sword is got. A different Fortune sought over the same case - Bishamon, weighing the lineage's need and the governor's command, or a Fortune of ambition - might care most about the *what* (secure the blade for the house) and little about the *how*. Read these dreams as this god, on this matter.

- **Here, Daikoku cares about honest gain and the house's true prosperity, not about the sword.** The lineage's debt is real, and Daikoku would see it cleared - but by honest gain, patient stewardship, a fair harvest, a true exchange, never by a treasure taken. A gift given under duress is not a gift; a sword taken by force is not wealth but a loss wearing wealth's mask. And Daikoku does not merely withhold favor from the cheat - it *brings ruin* on those who deal by coercion and fraud, so the coerced roads read as omens of ruin, not of mere disapproval.
- **Daikoku's clearest "no" is the false gift, the lie to the governor, and the spilling of a kinsman's blood for an inheritance.** A house that kills its own to grow rich has burned the storehouse to warm one room. On these there is no ambiguity in the sending.
- **On whether the sword is got at all, Daikoku holds no command.** If Nagamasa could be truly, freely won to a fair exchange - a real price, real consent - that would be honest gain and blessed. If he cannot, then to walk away openly, and to spare the house the strife a seizure would bring, is also honest, and also Daikoku's.
- **Declining does not clear the debt, and Daikoku does not pretend it does.** The honest road to prosperity is the longer one - the harvest, the ledger trued, the fair market, the house held whole - and the sending never promises it is the easier one.

The aggregate a group should read from Daikoku on this matter: **come by it honestly or not at all.** Daikoku will not say whether to win the sword or leave it, but it will not bless a coerced gift, a fabricated report, or a convenient death, and it shows plainly that the wealth so gotten curdles in the hand while the house that keeps faith across its own blood endures.

## How to run it

Before rolling, each praying PC declares two things:

1. **Which relic** they took up in guided prayer. The **Balance** attunes them to honest exchange and true gain; the **Tally** attunes them to the house's shared store and its holding-together across the blood.
2. **Which way** they lean. **Pursue** means trying to secure the sword for the lineage, by whatever means the PC judges justified. **Decline** means not securing it, anywhere from an open refusal and protecting Nagamasa to a face-saving report to the governor, true or invented.

### The roll

Each praying PC rolls once. This scene's attunement is near-ideal - a relic handled in guided prayer at the Fortune's own temple - so the odds run about 20% no dream, 20% a meaningless dream, 50% a significant one, and 10% a significant dream that opens into lucid dreaming. The 10 is always the lucid result; the other faces are scrambled fresh for each night's sleep so they cannot be learned in advance.

These are the **baseline** odds: no ill force has taken an interest in the PCs here, the party carries no weight heavy enough to tilt them, and the matter - the honest price of a bargain - stirs no great spirit. Only slightly different circumstances would move them: a PC already hunted by an ill power, guidance sought instead on the blood-hungry blade itself, or a graver sin on the party's hands could raise the chance of nothing, or of an actively misleading dream.

| Roll | Result |
|------|--------|
| 1 | A significant dream |
| 2 | A significant dream |
| 3 | A significant dream |
| 4 | No dream |
| 5 | No dream |
| 6 | A random, meaningless dream |
| 7 | A significant dream |
| 8 | A random, meaningless dream |
| 9 | A significant dream |
| 10 | A significant dream, and lucid dreaming |

### Choosing one relic

The PCs are told of both relics in advance and each chooses which one to approach - the **[Level Beam of Aki Tomobei](/relics/level-beam-of-aki-tomobei)**, a market-steward's beam-balance that will not settle level over a false or a forced bargain, or the **[Twinned Tally of the Aki Granary](/relics/twinned-tally-of-the-aki-granary)**, the two halves of a storehouse tally split between the founder's heirs, which must be brought together to open the house's oldest store. Having chosen, a PC keeps clear of the other: each imbues a different aspect of Daikoku, and to draw on both at once would mix those aspects, which is dangerous. The choice is a deliberate one (and, as ever, a confession of what the PC is really asking).

## Shared band: no dream (poorly remembered)

- You sleep, and there is nothing. Not darkness, not waiting: the cold flagstones, the dark, then gray morning. Whether the god had nothing to say to you, or you brought it nothing, or the sending came and slid off you like water off oiled paper, you cannot tell. You wake with the sense of having set a coin down on a counter to pay for something and turned away, and only later, patting your sleeve, do you wonder what it was you bought, or whether you bought anything at all.
- You dream only the temple hall itself: the cool of the flagstones, the smell of old incense and older wood, a coin shifting in the offering-box as someone drops one in the dark, so faithfully you are not sure you slept. Nothing comes. Your hands are empty and the balance in the corner hangs level and still.

## Shared band: unrelated (noise)

- You are sorting dried beans by color into bowls, and there is one color you have no bowl for, and you keep holding those beans in your palm not knowing where they go, and there are more of them than you thought.
- A merchant you do not know unrolls bolt after bolt of cloth for you, each a color you have never seen and cannot name once it is rolled away again, and he is very proud of them, and you keep nodding, and it goes on a long time.
- It is raining, and you are under the eaves of a shuttered shop watching a dog cross the empty market square with great purpose. It reaches the far side, sits down, looks back the way it came, and waits.
- You are a child counting the beams of a ceiling you are lying beneath, and you lose the count near the end every time and start again, and it is not unpleasant.

## Table A - Balance and Pursue

Attuned to honest exchange, leaning toward securing the sword; asking "is this gain honest?" Daikoku blesses a true exchange and brings ruin on a forced one; a gift given under duress is not a gift.

- You set the sword on one pan of the balance and a purse of gold on the other, and the beam will not come level. The sword-side sinks and sinks, past the peg, past the floor, and the gold rises to the rafters, and you keep pouring more gold onto the pan and it changes nothing.
- You are counting coins into a man's open hand, and they are bright and warm and he is smiling, until you look up and it is a wooden hand, a puppet's, the smile painted on, and behind it someone you cannot see works the strings and holds the true hand closed and empty.
- You and a man each set a thing on the balance - his sword, and something of your own you did not think to give - and the beam settles dead level, and you are both content, and try as you might you cannot make out in the dream what you laid down that weighed as much as a Masamune blade.
- The coin you press into his hand goes cold and gray and heavy the moment he takes it, lead where it was gold, and it goes on cooling until it burns cold in his palm, and he drops it, and it lies between you dull and dead while the sword stays bright on the wall.
- You are weighing rice into bales for the store, honest work, bale after full bale, until you notice the one you are filling is not rice but something dark and wet, and you pack it down all the same, and the scale still reads it good and level and true, and you know the scale is wrong for the first time in its life and that you are the reason.
- You keep no dream you can hold, and carry all morning a low dread fixed not on the sword and not on Nagamasa but on your own hands - on whatever they might reach out and do to close the thing - and it will not lift.

## Table B - Balance and Decline

Attuned to honest exchange, leaning toward walking away; asking "is this gain honest?" A bad bargain honestly refused leaves the balance at rest - but a lie to the governor is a false coin too.

- You lift the sword off the balance and set it back on the wall where it hung, and the empty pan rises and the beam swings level of its own accord, nothing on either side, perfectly even, and it is the most restful thing you have ever seen.
- You are writing a report, and the brush keeps adding a flourish, a line, making a small effort sound like a great one, and the ink beads and will not dry however long you wait, staying wet and shining, and you cannot fold the letter without it smearing.
- You hand a man back the coin he offered and you both bow, and the bow is real, and you walk out of his shop having bought nothing and owing nothing, and the street outside is a market at full bustle, the stalls heavy with goods, and there is money in your sleeve and honest places to spend it.
- A rat sits on the counting-board, on the very coin you did not spend, washing its face, unbothered, and it looks at you the way a creature looks at someone it has decided is not a threat.
- You tell someone the plain truth of a hard thing - you cannot see who - and it costs you, you feel the cost go out of you like a coin leaving your sleeve and not coming back, and the balance in the corner settles level as you speak.
- You keep nothing of the night, only wake lighter than you lay down, an unaccountable ease in your chest pinned to the empty road home and to nothing at all that you are carrying back along it.

## Table C - Tally and Pursue

Attuned to the house's shared store, leaning toward securing the sword; asking "does this enrich the house, or break it?" One treasure won can hollow a house; a house riven across its own blood cannot open its stores.

- You bring the two halves of the granary tally together to open the old store, and they will not meet - one half has warped, or the notches have changed, and turn them how you will they no longer match, and the granary stays shut, and inside you can hear the rats going hungry.
- The sword hangs on the wall of a fine hall, yours now, gleaming, and the hall is very quiet, and you walk through room after room of the Aki house and they are all fine and all empty, swept and cold, no cooking-smell, no children, no one, and the sword the only bright thing in any of them.
- You are paying down the debt, coin by coin, and it is working, the ledger-lines closing one by one, real relief - and each coin you lay down has a drop of dark on it, and the ledger drinks it, and the closed lines are written in it, and the book grows heavier and warmer than a book should be.
- A full storehouse, yours, rice to the rafters, and when you haul the door open the smell is wrong and the rice is all hollow husks, weightless, a whole granary of chaff that fills the hand and weighs nothing, and somewhere down in it a rat lies dead.
- Two of you hold the split tally, you and a kinsman whose face keeps changing - sometimes Nagamasa's, sometimes your own - and one half must be broken for the other to be made whole, and the hall stands waiting for it to be done, and the axe is already in your hand.
- Nothing comes in the night, and yet you wake grieving - a raw, specific grief with no death behind it - fixed on the Aki house itself, on its cold rooms, as though something in them were already lost that no one has yet thought to mourn.

## Table D - Tally and Decline

Attuned to the house's shared store, leaning toward leaving the sword; asking "does this enrich the house, or break it?" Sparing the house the strife is Daikoku's own interest - but the debt stays, and the honest road is the slow one.

- The two halves of the tally come together easy and true and the granary door swings open, and it is not full but it is not empty either - some rice, enough, and room for more - and the rats are few and quiet and fat, and it smells of a working store.
- You are in the fields at harvest, cutting and binding, and it is slow, and your hands hurt, and the debt is not paid and will not be paid this season or the next, and the work goes on, and there is bread that night and there will be bread tomorrow.
- You stand between Nagamasa and someone coming up on him - you cannot see who - and you do not draw, you only stand there, and the someone stops, and turns, and goes, and behind the two of you the sword stays on Nagamasa's wall, and the hall is quiet, and no one has bled.
- The Aki household at a meal, all of them, the whole loud crowded table, poorer than they were and not one seat empty, and someone is telling a story badly and everyone is laughing, and there is not a Masamune blade anywhere in the room.
- You hand the governor's letter back unopened - you cannot see her face, only her hand taking it - and you say a plain true thing, and it costs you, you feel your standing go out of you like coin from a sleeve, and outside the harvest is coming in, cartload after cartload, none of it yours and all of it real.
- You keep nothing of the night but wake full of a plain aching fondness for the whole loud crowded Aki table, the poorer faces and the full seats, and none at all - oddly none - for the bright thing on the wall that you find, waking, you can no longer picture.

## The 10: lucid dreaming in the hall

A 10 gives the PC their meaningful dream (pick from their table) **and** drops them into a **lucid dream** in the temple hall. Reroll 1k1 for **lucid points**: reroll 1-9 is that many points; a rerolled 10 explodes (10 points, reroll and add, and improvise something rare at the deep end). Spend points on the menu below. As long as the PC has at least 1 point they may buy one more option even if it costs more than remains (no penalty for going negative in this scene).

Menu:

*Understand the others' dreams*

- **2 points:** How many of the three categories (no dream, noise, or a meaningful result) the group's dreams touched
- **2 points:** How many sleepers fell into a category you name (no dream, noise, or a meaningful result)
- **1 point:** Which named PCs were in a category you name - available only once you know the category counts (bought above, or the last one deduced by elimination); name every live category but one and the rest fall out for free

*Delve deeper into your own dream*

- **3 points:** See another significant dream from your own table
- **4 points:** See a significant dream from a different table - the relic you did not handle, or the way you did not lean

Deliver every purchase inside the lucid-dream fiction, never as numbers: the PC sees the other sleepers as coins laid out on a counting-board, bright, dull, or missing from their places, and hears the true dreams as coins that ring clean when dropped and the false ones as coins that fall dead. The model feeling to wrap the group-shape purchases in:

> And this you carry up out of the dream like a stone closed in your fist: that of everyone who lay down in the temple hall tonight, Daikoku sent true dreams to some, sent noise to some, and sent nothing at all to others - the sending is not evenly given - and that yours was true, and meant, and meant for you. You do not know the reckoning behind it. You will look at the others' faces in the gray morning and not be able to tell which of them dreamed truly, which dreamed only nonsense, and which did not dream. You know only that you did.

## Design notes (GM only)

- The two poles are **Pursue and Decline**, but Daikoku's blessing does not run along that axis - it runs along **honest gain versus coerced**, and **the house held whole versus riven**. A Pursue PC who could genuinely win a fair exchange (Table A's level-balance vision) is blessed; a Decline PC who lies to the governor (Table B's ink that will not dry) is not. The mentality only chooses which of Daikoku's questions the dream puts to them.
- Daikoku does not merely disapprove of the coerced road - it **brings ruin** on it, per the god's own doctrine, so the Pursue-by-force fragments are omens of ruin: the coin turned to lead, the storehouse of hollow husks, the ledger written in blood, the tally that will not meet. Play them as warnings with teeth, not as tut-tutting.
- **Declining is not free.** The debt is real and remains; Daikoku's honest road to prosperity is the slow one (the harvest, the working store, the modest table). Do not play Decline as a clean escape - Table D shows endurance, not rescue.
- The set never blesses the coerced gift, the fabricated report, or the killing of Nagamasa. The **only** blessed way to *get* the sword in these dreams is Table A's true exchange - a real price and Nagamasa's genuine consent - which the scene leaves pointedly hard to reach, since he is unwilling.
- Which relic a PC reaches for is a confession of what they are really asking. The richest results come from a mismatch: a Pursue PC at the Balance is asked whether the gain is honest; the same PC at the Tally is asked whether it enriches the house or breaks it - and a PC who wants the sword may not like being answered about the house.
- Daikoku is the least martial of the Crane patrons on a matter like this. A PC who argues that the lineage's need, or the governor's command, justifies the seizure has brought that argument to the wrong Fortune; that case belongs to Bishamon or to a lord's will, not to the Fortune of honest gain, and the padded bale weighs light on Daikoku's beam no matter whose granary it is bound for.
