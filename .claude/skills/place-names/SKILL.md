# Place Names

Generate names for provinces, towns, villages, and hamlets in Rokugan. Provides naming rules and emit-time conventions for each scale, plus a tagged pool of pre-generated names that supports variety and webapp-side filtering.

Domain capitals and major castles are not handled by this skill. They are named for the daimyo house (Shiro Ryusei is "Ryusei castle" for the Ryusei house), so they take personal-name material, not place-name material. See `/name` for personal names.

## The four scales

Each scale has its own naming conventions. The same bare-element name often works at more than one scale; suffix-locked names are bound to a single scale.

### Provinces

Two or three syllables, classical or "kuni"-feeling (see the [Historical grounding](#historical-grounding-classical-province-names-kuni) section below for what "kuni" means and why these names read the way they do). No community-type suffix. Sometimes ends in a major-geography element (`-no` 野 plain, `-hara` 原 field, `-yama` 山 mountain, `-kawa` 川 river) when the province is built around that feature.

Cardinal-direction province names (Higashi 東, Kita 北, Nishi 西, Minami 南) are explicitly used by the Fox per canon; other clans may or may not adopt this pattern.

Examples: Owari (尾張, "tail end"), Mikawa (三河, "three rivers"), Hakurei (白嶺, "white ridge"), Asahara (朝原, "morning field").

### Towns

Mid-scale, the county-town level (the lowest unit at which samurai live). Towns either carry a marker suffix or use the bare descriptive element with town-ness inferred from context.

Marker suffixes:

- `-machi` (町, "town-block, district") for an organized town with multiple neighborhoods
- `-ichi` (市, "market town") for towns whose identity centers on the weekly market

Many towns just use the bare descriptive element. Towns often inherit a name from the village they grew out of: a town that was once Yuhimura might now be Yuhi-machi or simply Yuhi, the village suffix shed as the town outgrew its origins.

Note: `Toshi` (都市) as a prefix is reserved for actual cities (Toshi Ranbo), not generic towns.

### Villages

Villages have the richest suffix vocabulary in Rokugan and are generated by combining a descriptive element with a community-type suffix. See the suffix tables, the geographic endings, the descriptive elements, and the emit-time notes below.

### Hamlets

Smaller than villages, no samurai, often a cluster of 30 to 50 people.

**Hamlets do not take community-type suffixes.** A name carrying `-mura`, `-shou`, `-shuku`, `-gou`, etc. is by definition a village; a hamlet is the same name shape *without* the suffix. The Yuhi / Yuhimura pair illustrates: Yuhimura is a village, Yuhi is a hamlet or village district. A hamlet named "Yuhimura" would be confused for a village; the convention is to keep hamlet names bare so the scale is unambiguous from the name alone.

Hamlets typically take:

- A bare descriptive element (Yuhi, Aozawa, Kuromatsu, Natsumori)
- A single geographic-feature ending used as the whole name (Sawa, Tani, Oka, Hama) when the hamlet's identity is bounded by a single feature
- Occasionally a household name (Niwaya, "the Niwa compound") when the hamlet is built around one family

A hamlet name should read as a hamlet at first hearing: short, bare, and unsuffixed.

## Village vs. village district

The "village district" is the administrative unit that bundles a main village with its outlying hamlets. The main village's full name carries the suffix; the village district is referred to by the bare descriptive element with the suffix dropped.

- The **Yuhi** village district is centered on **Yuhimura** (夕日村, "sunset village"). The hamlet of Natsumori is part of the Yuhi district but is not in Yuhimura proper.
- A village named **Kawashuku** sits in the **Kawa** village district.
- A village named **Aomatsu-no-sato** sits in the **Aomatsu** village district.

The village district name and a standalone hamlet name share the same shape (bare descriptive element). In the pool, the `place_types` tag `hamlet` covers both roles: a bare-element entry serves equally as a hamlet name or as a village district name when paired with a suffixed village.

Country monks recording births, deaths, marriages, and travel records typically use the bare district name. Merchant clerks tracking which bale of rice came from where use the full village name with suffix.

## Emit-time rule

**Every place name surfaced to the GM is emitted with a short note explaining its suffix (if any) and any special conventions that suffix carries.** The note is *not* part of the stored name in the pool. A pool entry says only "Yuhimura"; the emit-time note ("`-mura` is the generic word for village; the suffix is dropped for the village district, so Yuhimura sits in the Yuhi district") accompanies the name whenever it appears in generated output, so the GM does not have to look up the meaning each time.

The canonical emit-time notes for each community-type suffix are in the "Emit-time notes per suffix" subsection below. Use those exact notes (lightly adapted to context) rather than improvising fresh ones each time, so the language is consistent across sessions.

Geographic feature endings (`-kawa`, `-sawa`, `-tani`, etc.) and pure descriptive elements (`shira-`, `matsu-`, etc.) generally do **not** require an emit-time note: their meaning is self-evident from the kanji-to-romaji-to-meaning triangle. Provinces, towns, and hamlets generally do not require emit-time suffix notes because they rarely carry suffixes; include the literal kanji meaning when surfaced if it is not self-evident from the romaji.

## Emit-time notes per suffix

The canonical short note to attach to a name whenever the suffix appears in generated output. Keep these to one or two sentences; do not editorialize beyond what is written here.

**`-mura` (村, "village")** - The generic word for village. The `-mura` suffix is dropped when referring to the village district as a whole: Yuhimura is the village proper, but the surrounding district (including its outlying hamlets) is referred to simply as Yuhi. This drop-the-suffix convention applies to most community-type suffixes but is most prominent with `-mura` because `-mura` is by far the most common.

**`-sato` / `-no-sato` (里, "home village, native place")** - A slightly poetic register, used for older long-settled villages with a strong sense of lineage. Often used by country monks in birth records when emphasizing that a peasant was born to the village rather than having moved there.

**`-shou` (庄, "manor estate")** - A village wholly under one samurai or merchant family's rent. The rent collector typically lives in or near the village, and the village headsman answers to the manor steward as much as to the county magistrate.

**`-shuku` (宿, "post-station, inn village")** - A village existing as a lodging stop on a clan road or (where treaty permits) an Imperial road. Includes at least one inn, a smithy, and stabling, with a higher percentage of the population making their living from travelers than from farming. The Imperial Road through Minami province has no `-shuku` villages by treaty; on clan roads such villages are common.

**`-gou` (郷, "rural district")** - A village with an older, semi-administrative identity, old enough to appear by name in the oldest tax rolls of the domain. The suffix is more common in formal documents than in conversation.

**`-ji` / `-dera` (寺, "temple village")** - A village that grew up around a temple, which usually predates the village by generations. The temple's senior monk often holds informal authority alongside the headsman, and the country monk for the village district is generally drawn from this temple.

**`-bashi` (橋, "bridge village")** - A village that exists because the bridge does; without the bridge, the village would not be where it is. Bridge maintenance is a recurring village expense, often subsidized by the county magistrate or by a merchant family with cargo interests.

**`-tsuji` (辻, "crossroads village")** - A small village at a road junction, typically with one inn, one shrine, and a handful of merchant stalls on travel days. Small in population but disproportionately visible to passers-through.

**`-watashi` (渡, "ford or ferry village")** - A village built around a river crossing without a bridge. In flood season the ferry stops running and the village can be cut off from the road; the ferryman is a significant local figure and often a de facto co-headsman.

**`-guchi` (口, "mouth or entrance village")** - A village at the mouth of a valley or entrance to a forest road. Often the last settled place before wilderness or mountain country; may host travelers' shrines and small inns.

**`-ichi` (市, "market village" or "market town")** - A place designated as the weekly market site for a cluster of neighboring village districts. At village scale, the population multiplies several times over on market day; at town scale, the market is a permanent fixture and the local economy is built around it.

**`-tono` (殿, "lord's residence village")** - Rare as a true village-name suffix. The village exists specifically to support a noble's hunting lodge, fishing manor, or seasonal residence. Its economy is bent around the lord's seasonal visits, and the village headsman often doubles as the lodge steward in the off-season.

**`-seki` (関, "barrier or checkpoint village")** - A village built around a samurai checkpoint on a clan road. The checkpoint precedes the village and the village exists to feed and house the bushi posted there. Carries more administrative weight than an ordinary village.

**`-yashiki` (屋敷, "manor estate village")** - Similar to `-tono` but emphasising the buildings rather than the lord. Used for retainers' or merchants' country estates rather than great lords' residences.

**`-machi` (町, "town-block, district")** - The standard suffix for an organized town with multiple neighborhoods. Distinguishes a town from a village in formal records; not used at village or hamlet scale.

## How to choose

1. Decide what scale you are naming (province, town, village, hamlet).
2. For villages, decide what the village is *for*: ordinary farming, a road waystation, a temple support village, a noble's seasonal residence, a market hub, a riverside fishing community. Pick the community-type suffix that signals that function. Resist defaulting to `-mura` unless you mean "this is just a farming village with no other identity."
3. For provinces and hamlets, skip the community-type suffix; pick a bare descriptive element or a geographic feature ending instead. For towns, similar, unless `-machi` is warranted.
4. Pick a descriptive element from terrain, weather, season, color, plant, or animal. The full name should fit on the tongue (two or three syllables of descriptive element plus one or two of suffix is the sweet spot).
5. Confirm the kanji to romaji to meaning triangle reads cleanly (per Constitution Principle XI). If the reading is stylized or non-obvious, plan to explain it in surrounding prose.

## Worked example: variety within a single county

A Crane county of six village districts might contain:

- **Kuromatsu-mura** (黒松村, "black pine village") - the largest and most ordinary
- **Sakuragawa-no-sato** (桜川の里, "cherry river home village") - an old settled village along a small river
- **Ueta-shuku** (上田宿, "upper paddy waystation") - on the clan road north, with an inn and a smithy
- **Hakushou** (白庄, "white estate") - a small village wholly under one merchant family's rent
- **Mori-tono** (森殿, "forest lord's village") - supporting the county magistrate's hunting lodge near a forestry preserve
- **Kawaguchi** (川口, "river mouth") - a fishing and ferry hamlet at the river's mouth

The suffixes do half the worldbuilding work. A reader who hears the six names in sequence has already learned that this is mixed-economy country with a road, a tenanted estate, a noble's seasonal retreat, and water-based commerce.

## Historical grounding: classical province names (kuni)

Research grounding for the classical-name entries in the pool and for the rule that every pool note must explain *why a place would be called that* (audit of 2026-07). "Kuni" (国) means **province**: the roughly 60 provinces of classical Japan, the old top-level map that names like Harima, Iyo, and Mutsu come from.

**Why the literal meanings of kuni-names are so often gibberish** ("scattering-polishing", "however-horse"): most kuni names are older than writing in Japan. They existed as spoken names for centuries before kanji arrived; when scribes finally wrote them down, they picked characters mainly for **sound** (the practice called *ateji*), and an early-8th-century Imperial edict then required every province and district name to be written with **exactly two auspicious characters**. So the literal kanji meaning of a kuni-name is usually an artifact of its spelling, not the reason for the name. The GM-facing takeaway: these names are the Japanese equivalent of "Thames" or "London" - so old that the meaning is worn away, with the written form a later scribe's phonetic dress.

Mechanisms worth knowing (each is used in the relevant pool notes, and each translates directly into Rokugan):

- **Silent padding characters**: Izumi (和泉) was originally just 泉 "spring"; the edict demanded two characters, so a silent 和 was prefixed - written but never read. Kii (紀伊) is old Ki "tree country" plus a silent 伊. The bureaucracy's fingerprints are visible in the spelling.
- **Respelling by decree**: Yamato's original character was deemed undignified and re-spelled 大和 "great harmony"; Yamashiro was 山背 "behind the mountains" until the capital moved there, whereupon it became 山城 "mountain castle". Names get upgraded when their status does.
- **Front/middle/behind and upper/lower = distance from the capital**: when an old region grew too big it was split, and the parts were named by their distance from the Imperial capital. Kibi became Bizen/Bicchu/Bingo (front/middle/back); Koshi became Echizen/Etchu/Echigo; Toyo-no-kuni became Buzen/Bungo; Hi-no-kuni became Hizen/Higo; Fusa became Kazusa/Shimousa (upper/lower); Tanba spun off Tango ("the back of Tan"); Keno split into upper and lower Keno (Shimotsuke). In Rokugan the identical logic works measured from Otosan Uchi.
- **Names that ARE literal**: a minority are genuine descriptions - Mikawa "three rivers", Kawachi "the land within the rivers", Hyuga "sun-facing", Oki "the offing". These need no apology; they are the Springfield class.
- **Poetry fused into spelling**: Asuka (飛鳥 "flying bird") cannot be read from its kanji at all; court poets always prefixed the place with the epithet "of the flying birds" and the epithet became the official spelling. A written name can preserve a fragment of a poem.
- **Relational itineraries**: Awaji is literally "the road to Awa"; Totomi is "the far lake" as opposed to Omi "the near lake", both named from where the capital sits.

**The pool-note rule this grounds**: every entry's `notes` field must leave the reader knowing why a place might carry that name. Transparent descriptive names ("middle village", "stone bridge") need nothing beyond the `meaning` field. But any name whose literal meaning does not explain itself - classical kuni-names, phonetic spellings, borrowed famous names, zodiac-direction districts (Tora-machi is the district on the tiger *bearing*, east-northeast, not a tiger sighting) - must say where the name actually comes from, self-contained, because each webapp detail page is reached by random draw and read in isolation.

## Pool schema

The pre-generated pool is `pool.jsonl`. Each entry has the following fields:

```jsonl
{"name": "<romaji>", "kanji": "<kanji>", "meaning": "<literal English>", "place_types": ["<scale>", ...], "commonality": "<level>", "regional": ["<context>", ...], "suffix": "<suffix or null>", "notes": "<free-form>"}
```

- **`name`** - the romaji name as it would be spoken or written
- **`kanji`** - the kanji writing
- **`meaning`** - literal English (drives the Principle XI kanji to romaji to meaning triangle)
- **`place_types`** - list of scales this entry suits as-written. Values: `province`, `town`, `village`, `hamlet`. (Village district is collapsed into `hamlet` since bare-element names serve both roles.) Bare-element names typically have multiple types; suffix-locked names have one.
- **`commonality`** - single value: `very_common`, `common`, `uncommon`, `rare`, `unique`
- **`regional`** - multi-value tags for geographic context. Fixed vocabulary: `mountain_bordering`, `coastal`, `riverine`, `forested`, `plains`, `valley`, `road_corridor`. Empty list means "works anywhere."
- **`suffix`** - community-type suffix if present (drives which emit-time note is attached), else `null`
- **`notes`** - free-form commentary; used for Springfield-style "this name is like X" content. Every note must leave the reader knowing *why a place might be called that*: transparent descriptive names need nothing beyond `meaning`, but any name whose literal meaning does not explain itself (classical kuni-names, phonetic spellings, borrowed famous names) must carry a self-contained explanation - see the Historical grounding section above

Example entries:

```jsonl
{"name": "Owari", "kanji": "尾張", "meaning": "the tail end, the one at the edge", "place_types": ["province", "town", "village", "hamlet"], "commonality": "very_common", "regional": [], "suffix": null, "notes": "Like Springfield in the US. Multiple Owaris in every domain."}
{"name": "Yuhimura", "kanji": "夕日村", "meaning": "sunset village", "place_types": ["village"], "commonality": "common", "regional": [], "suffix": "-mura", "notes": ""}
{"name": "Yuhi", "kanji": "夕日", "meaning": "sunset, evening sun", "place_types": ["hamlet"], "commonality": "common", "regional": [], "suffix": null, "notes": "Also serves as a village district name when paired with a sunset-themed village like Yuhimura."}
{"name": "Takanezawa", "kanji": "高嶺沢", "meaning": "high peak marsh stream", "place_types": ["village", "hamlet"], "commonality": "uncommon", "regional": ["mountain_bordering"], "suffix": "-sawa", "notes": ""}
```

### How the village_district / hamlet collapse works in the pool

Both roles are served by the same name shape (a bare descriptive element with no community-type suffix). In the pool, this collapses into the single `hamlet` tag. The suffix decision is settled at pool-construction time, not at use time:

- "Yuhi" (no suffix, `place_types: ["hamlet"]`) - can be used directly as a hamlet, or as the village district name paired with any Yuhi-themed village (Yuhimura, Yuhi-shou, etc.)
- "Yuhimura" (`-mura` suffix, `place_types: ["village"]`) - committed to village scale, with the suffix in the stored name

When filtering for villages, you get "Yuhimura" and its kind. When filtering for hamlets, you get "Yuhi" and its kind. To get a village from a bare-element entry, the GM appends a suffix per the rules in the "How to choose" section; this is not the pool-driven flow.
