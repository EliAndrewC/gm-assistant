name: The Treasury Key of Matsu Tameko
japanese_romaji: Kura-no-Kagi
japanese_kanji: 蔵の鍵
fortune: daikoku
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Daikoku, Kyuden Matsu (Matsu family, Lion clan)
named_entity: Matsu Tameko (Matsu daimyo's wife and treasury-keeper of the second century, who defended the family treasury through three lean winters)
relic_type: implement (iron key)
---

A heavy iron key, longer than a man's hand from the heel of the palm to the tip of the middle finger, with a teardrop-shaped bow at the head and a deep-cut ward at the bit. The iron is dark, worn smooth on the shaft where Tameko's grip lay, and pitted along the bow where the cord that once tied it to her sash has long since been removed. From the bow now passes a length of pale yellow silk, knotted in the simple square knot favored by the Matsu of Tameko's century. The key hangs from a small iron hook driven into the cypress beam that runs above the door of the temple's own treasury-chamber — not displayed, but in active use as the ceremonial second-key for the chamber's lock. The temple's working key is kept by the Steward of the Treasury; Tameko's key is brought down only at the audit-days and the petitions days, and is otherwise left on its hook for any monk passing through the corridor to see.

Matsu Tameko was the wife of the Matsu daimyo of the second century. She was a daughter of a minor Akodo lineage, brought into the Matsu family for political reasons, and her husband had little expectation of her beyond the customary. He was wrong in this. When her husband and the senior officers of the house were called away to a long campaign on the northern marches, Tameko took up the keys of the family treasury at her mother-in-law's insistence and held them for nineteen years, through three lean winters in which the harvest failed in the Matsu provinces and the family treasury was the only line of defense between the peasantry and starvation. She fed the peasants. She fed the garrison. She fed the temples — first the Order of Bishamon, as was proper for a Matsu wife, and then the Order of Daikoku, when the Bishamon abbots themselves wrote to the daimyo on campaign begging him to honor the Daikoku monks as well, since it was Daikoku's wisdom that had kept the treasury from emptying. Tameko's husband returned in her twenty-first year of marriage; she returned the keys to him. She died seven years later, of a wasting illness she did not name to her family. The treasury key — *this* key — she had asked, in her last weeks, to be sent to the Temple of Daikoku in Kyuden Matsu, with the request that it never be melted down for its iron.

The temple has used the key as its own treasury-chamber key ever since, with one ceremonial wrinkle: the working key is kept by the Steward of the Treasury, and Tameko's key is brought down to the lock only at the four audits of the year — first month, fourth month, seventh month, tenth month — and at any petition-day on which a treasurer or monk asks to consult the chamber for guidance. On these occasions the Custodian of Sacred Relics lifts Tameko's key down from its hook, fits it to the lock himself, and opens the chamber with it. The petitioner enters the chamber behind the Custodian; the door is closed; the petition is heard within. The key is returned to its hook when the chamber is closed again.

A Steward of the temple, the Order's senior accountants, or the Matsu daimyo's own treasurers may petition the Custodian to be received at the chamber when a budget dispute or a hard reckoning falls before them. The most frequent petitioners are the temple's own Stewards, who must each year reconcile the Order's income against its obligations — the Daikoku temples of the Matsu domain are responsible for a great web of grain-distributions, vassal-house tithes, and almsgiving disbursements, and the reconciliation is not easy work. The Custodian sits with the petitioner in the chamber for the time of a half-glass of incense, and the petitioner sets out his difficulty aloud while the chamber's records and the strongboxes stand witness. The Custodian does not advise. Tameko advises, through the chamber she now holds.

Some petitioners are graced with the lock turning unusually easily under Tameko's key, an auspicious omen that her hand is upon the reckoning and that the petition has been heard. Some find the lock catching once — a small resistance, easily worked through — which is taken as a sign that one item in the reckoning has not yet been correctly set down, and that the petitioner should review his books for the entry Tameko has flagged. Some find the lock catching twice or more, which the Custodian receives with concern; he closes the chamber, returns the key to its hook, and asks the petitioner to come back another day, after he has examined his work with fresh eyes. Most often the lock turns as locks turn, neither smoothly nor stiffly, and the petition is heard without omen — Daikoku, like Tameko, is not given to flourishes when plain work is what the day requires.

The key supports the reckoning; it does not perform it. A treasurer who hopes that the lock will turn smoothly enough to relieve him of finding the gap of three hundred koku in his books will be disappointed; if Tameko's hand is upon his work, it is upon his work, and the work is still his. The temple's records of nine centuries show that the Stewards who consult the chamber most often are also the Stewards whose audits run cleanest — though the records do not say, and the temple does not assert, whether the consultation produces the cleanness or only attends it.

The key cannot be removed from the corridor above the treasury-chamber door. The Matsu daimyo of two generations past, having heard the story of Tameko, asked that the key be brought to the family's annual New Year ceremony at the castle for a single day, that he might honor it in his great-grandmother-by-marriage's name; the Order considered the request for a full season and then declined, with the temple's reasoning recorded in the petition logs: *Tameko gave the key to the temple's treasury, and to the temple's treasury it serves.* The Matsu daimyo accepted the answer without protest, and sent a generous gift of cypress beams the following spring, from which the temple replaced the worn hook above the chamber door.
