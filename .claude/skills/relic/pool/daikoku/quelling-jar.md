A squat stoneware jar, a hand and a half in height, ash-glazed in mottled brown and green, with a low neck and a plain fitted lid. It bears no inscription, no seal, no maker's mark - nothing to lift it above the pickling crock of any farmhouse kitchen. It is a relic only because of what it does, and because of the trouble that it does it.

Set an object inside the jar, cover it with clean water, fit the lid, and leave it a night, and the thing is drawn out cleansed - not of grime and ill influence alone, as any *harae* is meant to cleanse, but of itself. A shugenja who kneels to commune with a quelled thing finds no voice left to answer: a smooth river-stone with no memory of the water-kami of the stream it was lifted from; a lump of ore with no thread back to the earth-kami of its mountain; a coal that holds no last warmth of the Fire that made it; a fragment of pumice with no memory of the volcano that cast it up; a feather that has never known the wind; a bronze mirror that keeps no face it ever held. Nothing ill remains in the thing, but nothing at all remains - it has been stilled to silence, and what silence takes it does not give back.

The proof of it is an absence, and an absence is the hardest thing of which to be sure. A skeptic would say a smooth pebble carries little kami-voice to begin with, that a shugenja who kneels to a plain stone expecting to find nothing will find nothing, and that a night in clean water does no more than a night in clean water ought. Yet those who have set into the jar a thing of known and living connection - a stone still wet from a named spring, a coal lifted from a shrine-fire that morning - and drawn it out mute do not doubt the silence, and are troubled by it; for to still a kami's voice in a thing is a small violence, and no one has ever been able to say clearly which Fortune, if any, blesses the doing of it. The temple rolls that mention the jar guess at Suijin, for the working looks like a purification; as many rolls leave the line blank. In truth the jar has quelled its own history as thoroughly as it quells what is set inside it: it enters the records with no founder, no chain of custody, and no dedication anyone will vouch for.

What the jar returns answers no question about where it came from - and a thing that answers no question can be given any answer. That is its darker use, and the reason it was always kept obscure, taken out seldom and spoken of less: an object drawn mute from the jar will not contradict a story told over it, however the story runs. Daikoku's monks would not bless such a use, and did not; the jar sat on a low shelf among the temple's lesser holdings, half-catalogued and rarely disturbed.

It disappeared from the Sovereign Temple of Daikoku at Shiro Masao in the same season that the True Measure of High Monk Sadamu and the Sealed Sack of Doji Ryusen were carried off, and it is counted among the relics stolen from that house. But where those two were delivered openly to the Yasuki Estates under the monk Ryoshin's claim of a dream, the jar was not among them. Ryoshin denied ever having taken it - swore he had left it on its shelf - and then, the two great relics handed over and his pay in hand, he disappeared before the question could be pressed. The Masao rolls carry it now as stolen, taker unproven, whereabouts unknown: a relic whose provenance was a blank before it was lost, and whose very existence is now a rumor with a gap in it exactly the shape of the man who walked away.
