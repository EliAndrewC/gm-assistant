summary: "A heavy untarnishing silver coin from an unknown foreign mint, gifted by a Yobanjin captain to an honest Yasuki merchant; Yasuki magistrates summon it as silent witness in tax, customs, and cartel disputes, reading the face it shows as omen."
---

A single foreign coin, round, the diameter of a man's thumbnail, struck from what the temple's records describe as "silver mixed with another metal not known to Rokugani assayers." The coin is heavier than it looks, and the metal does not tarnish. On one face is the head of a foreign sovereign, crowned with what may be a sun-disc or may be a coil of flame - opinions among the temple's senior monks differ. On the other face is a single character in a foreign script which no one in living memory has read, though the temple's library holds three separate scholarly attempts at translation. The coin rests on a small velvet cushion within a glass-fronted box of black-lacquered wood, displayed at eye level in a niche off the temple's customs-petitions hall - the hall in which the temple receives the customs officials and merchant-house factors of the Yasuki harbor in their professional capacities.

Yasuki Tomomichi was a Yasuki harbor merchant of the sixth century who built his house by breaking the Pearl Coast cartel. The cartel had for two generations fixed the price at which pearls from the southern islands could be landed at the Yasuki Estates, and the Yasuki families who controlled the cartel had grown lazy and grasping. Tomomichi was a younger son of a minor Yasuki line, with no standing in the cartel, and he chose to deal honestly: he paid the southern pearl-divers what they asked, took no kickbacks from his factors, and reported every transaction at the customs post in full. His ships were heckled, his warehouses fired twice, and his factors beaten in the streets. Yet within nine years his house was the largest pearl-trader on the southern coast, and the cartel had collapsed because the divers would deal with no one else. This coin was the first payment Tomomichi ever received from a foreign merchant - a Yobanjin sea-captain who had been refused service by the cartel's houses and who paid Tomomichi in his own people's silver because he had no Rokugani coin to spare. Tomomichi kept the coin to the end of his life and willed it to the temple with a brief note: *let it be displayed where the customs officers may see it.*

When a tax dispute, a customs dispute, or a cartel investigation is brought before the Yasuki magistrate's court, the magistrate may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for a *witness-of-coin*. The Custodian carries the lacquered box from its niche to the courtroom and sets it on the magistrate's table. The disputants are not required to touch it or address it; the coin sits beside the proceedings as Tomomichi's witness, and the magistrate hears the case knowing that Daikoku - and Tomomichi through him - attends. After the verdict is given, the Custodian returns the coin to its niche, and the magistrate's clerk records in the court log that the coin was present. The temple keeps a parallel log of all such witnessings; the two records are compared each year at the new year audit.

Some petitioners are graced with finding that the coin, when the Custodian unlocks the glass front to confirm its presence at the start of the proceeding, lies with its sovereign-face upward - an auspicious omen that Daikoku looks kindly upon the honest party in the dispute. Some find the coin lying with the foreign-script face upward, which is taken as a sign that the dispute concerns a matter the petitioners have not yet correctly named, and the magistrate is gently counselled to ask further questions before ruling. Some find the coin balanced on its edge against the velvet, which has happened seventeen times in the temple's records, and which is held to be the strongest of the omens - the dispute is one in which both parties have something to confess, and a settlement is to be preferred to a verdict. Most often the coin lies as it was last set down, neither face dominant, and the magistrate proceeds with his own discernment.

The coin's presence is not the ruling. A magistrate who allowed the omen of the upward face to decide a case, against the weight of evidence and testimony, would be failing his office; the temple is careful to remind new magistrates of this each year. The coin witnesses; the magistrate rules. Yet over four centuries of the practice, the Yasuki court's records show that verdicts in which the coin lay with the sovereign-face upward have been appealed less often than the average, and verdicts in which the coin was edgewise have, with striking regularity, been the cases that later resolved in private settlement before any appeal could be lodged.

The coin cannot be removed from the temple except in the lacquered box, and even then only under the Custodian's escort, and only to a Yasuki magistrate's courtroom within the Estates. The Yasuki harbor-master has on three occasions over the past century asked that the coin be permitted to travel to subsidiary customs posts up the coast; each request has been refused, with the temple's reasoning recorded in the petition logs: *Tomomichi gave the coin to the central harbor, and to the central harbor it stays.* The lacquered box bears a small brass plaque on its underside, engraved with Tomomichi's family seal and the date of the bequest. The seal is worn smooth in two places, where the Custodians have rested their thumbs each year when carrying the box.
