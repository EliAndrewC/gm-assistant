A treasure-sack small enough to sit in two cupped hands, sewn of stiff old brocade - gold thread worked on a ground long faded from crimson to rust - drawn shut with a plaited cord and then stitched over the mouth, the stitches themselves aged and never once cut. It rests on a small ceremonial rice-bale on the storehouse-altar of the temple granary. Lifted, it has more weight than an empty sack should, and something within it shifts and settles, though no ear has ever caught it make a sound.

Doji Ryusen was a merchant-benefactor of the Natsu markets who built his house on the grain trade and gave much of it back to the Order of Daikoku that had blessed his measures. In his last years he endowed the temple granary against the lean years that come to every country, and on his deathbed he gave the monks this sack, sewn shut by his own hand, and bade them never open it and never empty it - that as the sack stood always full and never wanting, so the temple's storehouses should stand always full and never fail the poor of its hamlets in a hard season. The monks kept his word. The sack has not been opened since, nor is it known with certainty what he sealed inside it.

While the sack is kept whole and unopened, the temple's stores do not fail. In lean years the granary has always somehow found grain enough to see its dependents to the spring, and headmen, granary-keepers, and merchants provisioning long voyages come to petition before it that their own stores hold as the temple's do. The sack is not opened, for to open it would be to empty it, and to empty it would be to break Ryusen's charge and let the blessing out; and it is not weighed, though those who lift it in a failing year speak of finding it heavy in the hand, which is taken as Daikoku's own promise that the stores will reach the harvest. What such a promise weighs against a well-kept granary and a careful steward, the monks do not trouble to ask; Daikoku blesses the diligent, and a house that keeps faith with Ryusen keeps faith with its stores.

At the storing-in after each harvest, the first measure of the temple's new grain is carried before the sack and presented - never poured into it - and the granary-keeper lays a hand upon the brocade to ask a blessing on the year's stores before the storehouse is sealed. The sack cannot be duplicated, for a copy would be an empty bag and nothing more; it cannot be opened without ending it; and its loss would be counted a disaster past mending. So even those who suspect it holds only a fistful of old rice handle it as though it held the harvest of the Empire.

When the Natsu lands fell to the Lion in the recent war and became the domain of Matsu no Masao Agetoki, the sack passed with the other Crane Daikoku relics into the Sovereign Temple of Daikoku at Shiro Masao. It was carried off within a few seasons by the monk Ryoshin, keeper of that temple's relics, together with the True Measure of High Monk Sadamu, under Ryoshin's claim that Daikoku had charged him in a dream to restore the relics to a house that still loved him. The Grand Abbot of Daikoku in the Yasuki Estates received the sack as a restoration and the Imperial magistrate of the Yasuki Estates ruled the transfer lawful. It now rests on its rice-bale in the storehouse-shrine of the Temple of Daikoku in the Yasuki Estates - a great harbor city whose granaries provision fleets, and a fitting home, the Yasuki say, for a sack that promises the stores will never fail. The temple at Shiro Masao says only that it was stolen.
