A small wooden mallet, the size of a man's fist, carved from a single piece of paulownia wood. The head is squat and weighted, marked along its broad face with the character 福 (*fuku*, "good fortune") burned shallow into the grain. The handle is bound in red silk cord, faded now to a pale rose. The mallet rests upon a square of black lacquered wood, set within a shallow brass tray, on a low altar at the front of the temple's petitions hall. Two rice-bales — small, ceremonial, the kind a merchant might carry as a token of good harvest — sit one to each side of the tray.

High Monk Norikazu founded the sovereign Order of Daikoku at this temple in the latter half of the eighth century, when the Doji court was opening the city's eastern markets to inland trade and the question of where Daikoku's blessing should rest within the new city had not yet been settled. Norikazu was a third son of a minor Kakita lineage who had renounced his bushi training as a young man and walked to the city with only his teaching robes and this mallet, which had been carved for him by his grandfather, a paulownia-wood craftsman of the southern wards. He used the mallet for forty-one years to strike the daily bell at the small shrine he had been granted on the eastern road. Pilgrims came in such numbers, and the daimyo's favor grew so steadily, that within a generation the shrine had become a temple and the temple a sovereign house. After Norikazu's death the mallet was placed where it now rests, and the bell he had struck for forty-one years was retired beside it.

A merchant who has formed a new venture — a new shop, a new caravan route, a new contract with a domain treasury — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to be received at the mallet's altar. The merchant brings a sample of the goods or coin upon which the venture turns: a measure of rice from the first lot, a length of silk from the first bolt, a string of cash from the first day's takings. The Custodian receives the sample in the brass tray, kneels with the petitioner, and strikes the mallet three times against the black lacquered wood beneath it — once for the merchant's labor, once for the merchant's honest dealings, and once for the favor of Daikoku upon the work. The strokes are soft; the mallet has never been used to strike anything hard, and Norikazu's wishes in this are honored.

Some merchants are graced with a clear ringing in the air after the third stroke — though the mallet strikes wood and not metal, an auspicious sign that Daikoku has heard the petition and that Norikazu joins his teacher's voice to the merchant's. Some are graced with a sudden warmth in the handle's silk binding, which the Custodian also notes; this is taken to mean that the venture's profit will return in kind to the city that hosts it, and the merchant is gently counselled to give generously to the temple's almsgiving box in the years to come. Some are graced with nothing more than the three soft strokes — and even this is a blessing, for Daikoku is not hurried, and many ventures must walk before they may run.

The mallet's strokes do not produce good fortune in themselves. A merchant whose books are crooked, whose grain is short-weighted, whose laborers go unpaid — Daikoku will not bless such a one, and the strokes of the mallet sound dull and small to those whose hands have not earned the petition. The Custodian does not say so aloud; that is for the merchant's own conscience to know. Yet the temple's records of three centuries show that those who return to give thanks for a venture come most often after the warmth in the silk has been remarked upon, and those who do not return are often those whose ventures the Order has later heard came to grief.

The mallet may not leave the temple. Daikoku-blessed merchants of the city who travel to open distant markets may petition the temple workshop to commission a *replica mallet* — paulownia, the same red cord, the same shallow-burned character — to carry in their travel-chests. These replicas are common among the senior merchants of the eastern markets, and many a caravan-master will tap his replica thrice against the lid of a treasure-chest before opening it for inspection at a customs post. The original mallet, Norikazu's grandfather's gift, rests on its tray and is struck only by the Custodian, and only at the petition of a merchant who has come in person.
