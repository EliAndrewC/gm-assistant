---
name: The Preserved Rice-Bale of the Flood Year
japanese_romaji: Kouzui-no-Tawara
japanese_kanji: 洪水の俵
fortune: daikoku
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Daikoku, Kyuden Daidoji (Daidoji family, Crane clan)
named_entity: Daikoku himself, through the unnamed peasant boy who recovered the bale from the floodwaters of the Great Flood of the Daidoji lowlands
relic_type: vessel (rice-bale)
---

A single small rice-bale, *tawara*, of the kind a peasant household keeps for the year's own eating — a third of the size of the merchants' standard bale, bound in the older five-cord pattern that has not been the fashion in the Daidoji rice-country for two hundred years. The straw is dark with age, the color of old tea, and the cords are dry but unbroken. The bale rests on a square of pale silk on a low cedar plinth at the rear of the temple's main hall, behind the Daikoku statue and to its right. Twice a year — at the first day of spring planting and the first day of autumn harvest — the bale is lifted by the Custodian of Sacred Relics and weighed on a small bronze balance kept beside the plinth. The weight is recorded in the temple's weighings-book, which now runs to four volumes and is the second-oldest continuous record kept by the Order of Daikoku in Daidoji.

The bale came to the temple after the Great Flood of the Daidoji lowlands, three hundred and eleven years past. The summer rains had come early and hard, the levees of the lower Daidoji rivers had failed in the third month, and the rice country had been under water for the better part of two months. Whole villages had been swept down to the sea; the survivors had retreated to the high ground and lived on what the Daidoji daimyo of the time could send up from the inland granaries. When the waters finally went down, the rice that had been stored against the planting year had been ruined in almost every storeroom; the Daidoji could not have replanted the lowlands without grain bought at terrible expense from the Akodo, the Yasuki, and even the Yobanjin.

In the fourth month of the year that followed, a peasant boy of about eleven years — no one took down his name; the temple's records say only *a boy from the village of Higashi-Kawakami, since washed away* — came to the temple at Kyuden Daidoji carrying this rice-bale on his back. He told the monk at the gate that he had found it caught in the branches of a willow on the far side of the river, a full season after the floods had receded, and that the willow had been bare of leaves when he had last looked at it, but had leafed out in a single day in the night before he found the bale. He had untied the cords and looked inside, and the grain was dry and whole and smelled of the harvest of three years before. He gave the bale to the temple and went home; the monk at the gate did not ask him for the rest of his story. The boy's village was lost to a second flood the following autumn, and the boy with it.

The first Grand Abbot to study the bale opened its cords and counted the grain. He recorded the count in the first volume of the weighings-book: *grains within, forty-one thousand and three hundred.* The cords were retied. The weighings-book records every formal weighing of the bale since — six hundred and twenty-two weighings in three hundred and eleven years. The weight has varied by less than the width of a finger-bone on the bronze balance across the entire span of the records. The grain has not been counted again. Twice in the temple's history a Grand Abbot has proposed that the cords be reopened and the grain recounted; both proposals were withdrawn before the act, the second time because the Grand Abbot in question had dreamt three nights running of the unnamed boy standing at the foot of his bed and shaking his head.

A peasant or peasant village-headsman of the Daidoji lowlands may petition the Custodian to receive a blessing of harvest at the bale. The Custodian receives such petitions in the season after planting and the season before harvest. The petitioner brings a single grain of the seed-rice that has been set aside for the year's planting, or a single grain of the first cut of the year's harvest, and places it on the silk square beside the bale. The Custodian kneels with the petitioner, recites the temple's standard prayer for abundance, and then sets the petitioner's grain inside one of the bale's outer cords for the night. At dawn the Custodian returns, removes the grain, and gives it back to the petitioner, who carries it home and plants it back into the field or adds it to the household's eating-rice.

Some petitioners are graced with their grain coming back lighter than it went in — a hair's weight only, on the bronze balance, but enough to notice. This is an auspicious omen that the bale has accepted the petitioner's offering and that Daikoku attends the household's harvest. Some find their grain heavier, which is taken to mean that the bale has given of itself to the petitioner's planting, and that the harvest will be richer than the season's signs would predict. Some find their grain unchanged in weight, and this is the most common — the Custodian counsels that the grain was already blessed when it left the field, and that the petitioner has been heard without the need for sign. The temple's weighings-book records the weight-changes faithfully; the totals across each century are very nearly even.

The bale has not been opened in three centuries. The cords are checked at each weighing for fraying and have shown none; the straw is checked for the small green mold that takes old grain-straw and has shown none of that either. The temple does not assert that the bale will never decay, only that it has not yet decayed. Daikoku is the Fortune of wealth and of the abundance of the rice country, and the bale that came down from the Great Flood is held to be his gift to the Daidoji lowlands — a single bale of true grain in a year when no grain was true.

The bale cannot leave the temple under any circumstance. The Daidoji daimyo of fifty years past, during a year in which the rice harvest had failed across the southern Crane provinces, asked that the bale be brought to the family granary at Kyuden Daidoji in solemn procession as a public petition for abundance. The Grand Abbot of that generation refused, and the refusal was upheld by the Doji daimyo when it was appealed to him: *the boy from Higashi-Kawakami brought the bale to the temple, and to the temple it stays.* The temple's workshop produces small woven-straw charms — miniature *tawara* the size of a thumb, blessed at the bale's plinth — which are distributed to the peasant villages of the lowlands each spring and hung over the storeroom doors of their granaries. There are thousands of these charms across the lowlands; there is one bale.
