A rice-measure of pale paulownia, a hand's span square and a hand deep, the four staves joined without a single nail and darkened along the rims by three centuries of handling. Into the grain of one face is burned the character 真 (*shin*, "true"); the base carries the pressed seal of High Monk Sadamu. It sits on a low black-lacquered stand in the trueing-hall, a plain bamboo strike-rod - the *tokaki* used to level a heaped measure - laid across its mouth, and two small ceremonial rice-bales set one to each side.

High Monk Sadamu founded the Order of Daikoku in the Natsu domain of the Crane some three centuries past, in the years when the Natsu markets were becoming the busiest inland exchange in Crane country. Sadamu taught that an honest measure is the first prayer a merchant offers Daikoku - that a market which measures true is a market the Fortune of Wealth will bless, and that a house which shorts the grain has already turned its face from him whatever else it lays on his altar. The measure he consecrated became the master *masu* of the Natsu markets. Every measure used in those markets was brought once a year to be trued against it and stamped, and a *masu* that did not bear the temple's stamp had no standing before a Natsu merchant or a Natsu magistrate.

Rice heaped into a true measure and struck level with the *tokaki* settles level and full, the grain lying at rest under Daikoku's regard. A measure that runs short - its staves sprung, its base shaved thin, its corners quietly packed - cannot be trued: heap it and strike it beside the *shin-masu* and the grain will not lie flat, mounding or hollowing again however the hand passes, for Daikoku will not let a false measure rest quiet beside a true one. Merchants graced, on the year's first trueing, with the grain settling clean and level at a single strike take it as an omen that the Fortune's regard follows their house through the season; those whose measures fight the strike are counselled to look to their staves, and to their dealings.

The trueing is done each year at the storing-in after harvest. Market officials and merchants bring their measures to the trueing-hall; the Custodian of the temple's relics fills each in turn, strikes it level against the master, and stamps those that lie true. A copy of the *shin-masu* is only ever a copy - trued to the original, never itself the standard - and so the measure cannot be duplicated away from the temple, and the temple stays at the center of the market it serves. Contracts and tax-assessments in the old Natsu markets were written for grain "by the temple's true measure," and a bushel owed by that phrase could be settled in no other.

When the Natsu markets fell to the Lion in the recent war and the Natsu lands were made the domain of Matsu no Masao Agetoki, the *shin-masu* passed with the rest of the Crane Daikoku relics into the Sovereign Temple of Daikoku at Shiro Masao. It did not stay there long. A monk of that temple named Ryoshin, who had been given charge of cleaning and guarding the relics, carried the *shin-masu* and the Sealed Sack of Doji Ryusen out of Lion keeping, declaring that Daikoku had come to him in a dream and bidden the relics restored to a house that still honored the Fortune as the Crane had. The Grand Abbot of Daikoku in the Yasuki Estates received them as a restoration; the Imperial magistrate of the Yasuki Estates ruled the transfer lawful, a matter of the Fortune's will and not of theft. There the measure now rests, in the trueing-hall of the Temple of Daikoku in the Yasuki Estates, where the Yasuki - a Family Crane by blood for centuries before they turned Crab - keep Daikoku's worship with a convert's zeal, and where the measures of one of the Empire's great harbor markets are once more trued against it each year. The temple at Shiro Masao calls the restoration a barefaced theft dressed in a borrowed dream, and the grievance stands unsettled between the two houses to this day.
