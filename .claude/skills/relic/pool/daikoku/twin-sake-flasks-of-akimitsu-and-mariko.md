---
name: The Twin Sake-Flasks of Akimitsu and Mariko
japanese_romaji: Tokkuri-no-Tsui
japanese_kanji: 徳利の対
fortune: daikoku
clan: crane
temple: a temple of Daikoku in Crane lands
named_entity: Akimitsu and Mariko (peasant rice-brewers of the Kakita lowlands, fifth century, whose household prospered into a great Kakita-vassal sake-house)
relic_type: pair (sake flasks)
---

Two earthenware sake-flasks of the round, round-shouldered shape called *tokkuri*, each the height of a man's hand spread thumb-to-little-finger. One is glazed in pale celadon green, the other in a deeper iron-rust brown; together they sit on a low cedar tray on a side altar in the temple's household-prosperity hall, beneath a hanging scroll on which is painted the character 福 (*fuku*, "good fortune") doubled — once in the green of the celadon flask, once in the brown of the iron-rust. A small white porcelain cup rests between the two flasks. The flasks are filled once a year, on the first day of the new year, by the senior brewers of the Kakita-vassal sake-house that descended from the relic's founders; they are emptied each evening by the Custodian, who pours a thimble's worth into the porcelain cup as the day's offering and drinks it himself.

Akimitsu and Mariko were a peasant couple of the Kakita lowlands in the fifth century, rice-brewers in a hamlet two days south of the temple. They married late — both in their thirtieth year — and worked together at the craft Akimitsu had inherited from his uncle: brewing sake from the broken grain that the rice-merchants would not buy, and selling it to the dock-workers of the Kakita river-ports. Their first year of marriage they brewed thirty bottles. Their fifth year, three hundred. Their tenth year, three thousand. They had no children and built their house instead from their work; they brewed in pairs, Akimitsu mixing the rice and Mariko tending the yeast, and the practice in their house was that no batch was ever begun unless they were both present and both sober. When they died — Mariko first, in her seventy-second year, Akimitsu within the same month — the brewery passed to Akimitsu's nephew, who was granted vassal status by the Kakita house in recognition of the brewery's by-then-considerable contribution to the Kakita treasury. The nephew donated the founders' last pair of flasks to the temple, with the prayer that household prosperity be remembered as a thing of *two* hands and not one.

A married couple — newly wed, long-married, or estranged and seeking reconciliation — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to be received at the household-prosperity hall. The Custodian draws a thimble of sake from each flask into the porcelain cup, sets the cup between the petitioners, and asks them to drink — first the wife from one side of the cup, then the husband from the other, finishing the cup between them. They are then asked to speak together, in the Custodian's hearing, one matter of their household upon which they have not yet agreed: a purchase, a sending of a child to study, a debt to be paid, a guest to be received. The Custodian does not advise; he only listens. When the petitioners have spoken what they came to speak, the Custodian bows them out and refills the cup from neither flask, leaving it dry until the next morning.

Some couples are graced with the sake from the green flask tasting clearly sweeter than the sake from the brown — an auspicious omen that the wife's wisdom in the matter is the wisdom Daikoku favors. Some find the brown tasting fuller and rounder, which is taken to mean that the husband's view is to be honored. Some find no difference at all between the two, which is held to be the rarest and finest blessing — Akimitsu and Mariko brewed as one, and a household that drinks as one is a household Daikoku has already prospered. Some find one of the flasks bitter or sour, which the Custodian receives gravely; he counsels the couple to return after the next planting season, and the petition is logged as *deferred* rather than complete.

The flasks do not produce prosperity from nothing. A household whose accounts are slovenly, whose servants go unpaid, whose lands lie unworked — Daikoku will not gild such a household, and the couple who come petitioning to escape the consequences of their own neglect will find the sake teaches them nothing they did not already know. The temple's records of seven centuries show that couples who return year after year, in good seasons and bad, are the couples whose houses grow steadily — and the temple has long since ceased trying to determine whether the petitioning was the cause or the consequence of the steadiness.

On the first day of the new year, the flasks are carried in procession from the household-prosperity hall through the temple grounds and out into the surrounding streets, borne by two senior monks beneath a small silk canopy. The procession ends at the gate of the Kakita-vassal sake-house that descended from Akimitsu and Mariko's brewery, where the senior brewers refill the flasks for the coming year and bow the procession back to the temple. Married couples line the procession's route and bow as it passes; those whose households have prospered in the year past leave a single grain of rice on the lacquered tray that one of the junior monks carries before the canopy. The grain is brought back to the temple and added to the next morning's almsgiving bowl.

The flasks cannot be replaced. The Kakita-vassal sake-house has, twice in seven centuries, sent a master potter to the temple asking permission to replicate the pair for their own household altar; both petitions were refused, with the temple's reasoning that the green and the brown were chosen in Akimitsu and Mariko's own time and are not to be matched by later hands. The temple's workshop produces only the small porcelain cups, which it distributes to newlywed couples of Kakita lineage and to such Kakita-vassal couples as the Custodian deems worthy.
