A tally-stick of dark seasoned oak, as long as a hand and split cleanly down its length into two matched halves, each notched across the grain so that the notches of one align with the notches of the other only when the two are laid together face to face. Across the split faces, half to each side, is cut a single character 和 (*wa*, "accord"), which reads whole only when the halves are seated true and is meaningless on either half alone. The two pieces are never kept together: one is held by the Aki daimyo of the day, the other by the head of a cadet branch of the lineage, and both are brought to the door of the Aki's oldest granary when it is to be opened, for the lock of that store is cut to the tally and will turn to no key.

When the warlord Aki swore fealty to Lord Daidoji at the dawn of the Empire and was given the governance of Akishi, he raised a granary against lean years and, the story holds, split its tally between his two heirs with his own hand, so that neither could open the house's store without the other. He meant it, the Aki say, as a lesson cut in oak: that the wealth of the lineage is not any one Aki's to spend but is held in common across the whole blood, and that a house which sets its kin against one another over what they own together will find, when the hard winter comes, that it cannot feed itself. A thousand years of Aki have kept the two halves apart and brought them together only in the presence of both branches, and the granary has opened for the lineage through every famine since.

The halves brought together by kin at peace seat true, the notches meet, the character 和 stands whole across the seam, and the old lock turns. When the Aki are riven - a kinsman wronged, a branch set against the daimyo, blood spilled within the lineage for what the lineage holds in common - the halves will not seat, however they are pressed and turned: the notches ride false, the character will not close, and the granary stays shut, and the Aki read it as Daikoku's judgment that a house divided against its own blood has no claim on its own stores. Some who bring the halves together in a season of true accord are graced with the notches meeting at the first touch, clean and soundless, taken as a sign the Fortune keeps the house in his regard; most simply feel the pieces seat or fail to, which the lineage has learned to trust.

The Temple of Daikoku in Akishi keeps the granary and its rites, but it does not keep the tally: the two halves stay in Aki hands, apart, precisely so that no single hand - not the daimyo's, not the temple's - can open the store alone. In the worst of the lineage's quarrels the halves have been carried to the temple and laid before the Custodian to be tried together as a test of whether the breach had gone too far to mend, and more than one Aki feud has been settled, or judged past settling, by whether the notches would meet.
