A heavy bound ledger, the size of a man's spread hand, two fingers thick. The covers are of dark lacquered wood, joined along the spine with stitched silk, and pierced through each cover with a single small hole through which a brass ring passes — the ring is for hanging the ledger on its peg when not in use. The pages within are pale rice-paper, ruled in faint red lines, and the columns are filled in Tatewaki's narrow hand, with the dates of the entries running down the right-hand margin in cinnabar ink. The first page bears the seal of the Akodo daimyo of the fourth century, and beneath it the seal of the Order of Daikoku in Kyuden Akodo. The ledger rests on a low writing-desk in the temple's treasury-chamber, a small windowless room off the main hall where the temple's own books are kept. It hangs by its ring from a brass peg above the desk when not in use.

Akodo Tatewaki served as treasurer to the Akodo daimyo's house during the long convalescence of the Akodo lands after the Plague of the Twelfth Year, which had hollowed out the domain's revenues for almost a decade. He was given the unwelcome task of telling his daimyo, year after year, how much had been lost and how little remained — and then the still less welcome task of finding, in each successive year, how the domain might survive another. He kept this ledger as his private working-book, in which he set down not only the official accounts but also his own notes on the discrepancies he could not yet explain, the entries he suspected of error, and the corrections he intended to make in the next audit. He wrote in plain language, as a man writes to himself; the ledger has no flourishes and no excuses. After his death in his sixty-ninth year, the ledger was donated to the temple by his eldest son with the request that it be used to guide the audits of those who came after. Eleven generations of Akodo treasurers have consulted it; the Order has not allowed it to be borrowed beyond the chamber in which it rests.

On the first day of the seventh month — the day on which the Akodo house traditionally closes its annual books for review — the Custodian of Sacred Relics receives the treasurers of the Akodo daimyo's house, of the Order's own treasury, and of any of the great Akodo merchant-houses that wish to petition. Each treasurer brings the ledger upon which they have been working. The Custodian lifts Tatewaki's ledger down from its peg, sets it on the desk beside the petitioner's own book, and opens Tatewaki's ledger to a page at random. The petitioner then reads the page aloud — entries, dates, corrections — and afterward sits with the Custodian for a half-glass of incense, considering whether the page Tatewaki has shown him has any bearing on the work the petitioner has lately been doing.

Some treasurers are graced with a page that addresses the very dispute they have come to resolve — a category of expense Tatewaki had himself misjudged, a creditor he had failed to record until the third audit, a sum that should not have been entered into income at all. They leave the chamber resolved to correct their own books accordingly. Some are graced with a page that bears no relation to their work, and the Custodian counsels them to consider whether some larger pattern has been overlooked — Daikoku's lessons sometimes come slantwise, and the page may speak to a question they have not yet thought to ask. Some are graced with a page upon which the cinnabar of the date is faintly damp to the touch, as though newly inked; this is taken as an auspicious sign that Tatewaki himself has reviewed the petitioner's labor and judged it sound. Most pages are dry, and the petitioner finds in them only what diligence and reflection find together — which is itself the lesson Tatewaki spent his life teaching.

The ledger does not balance the petitioner's books. A treasurer who has carried an error of a thousand koku into the seventh month will still carry that error out, unless he has noticed it himself in the course of reading Tatewaki's page aloud. Daikoku does not relieve the careful of their care, only joins his hand to theirs. Yet the temple's records show that of the great Akodo accounting scandals of the past four centuries, none have arisen from the books of treasurers who kept their audit-day petitions to the ledger; the men and women who go before Tatewaki each year find errors before errors find them.

The ledger cannot be removed from the chamber. The Order's workshop produces, on request, *blank books* bound to the same dimensions and with the same cinnabar-ruled paper, into which a treasurer may copy whichever of Tatewaki's pages have spoken to him; many a senior Akodo treasurer's writing-desk holds such a copy beside his own current ledger. The original ledger hangs on its peg, and the brass ring through which the peg passes was bent slightly out of round in Tatewaki's own time. It has not been straightened.
