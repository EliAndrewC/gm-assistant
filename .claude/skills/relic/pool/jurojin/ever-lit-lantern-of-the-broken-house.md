---

A stone lantern of grey andesite, the height of a kneeling man, set on a low platform of river-rock in the temple's inner courtyard. The lantern's hood is carved with the seven petals of an autumn chrysanthemum; its body bears seven small windows, one for each of Genbei's lost children, and within burns a single oil-lamp wick of twisted hemp, fed from a brass reservoir at the lantern's base. The lantern has been continuously lit for one hundred and fourteen years. The Custodian of the Lamp, a senior monk appointed for life, sleeps in a small cell that opens directly onto the courtyard, so that the lantern may be tended at any hour. A second monk, the *vice-custodian*, sleeps in a cell on the opposite side; if both the lamp and one custodian were to fail in the same night, the second would yet be at hand.

Shosuro Genbei was a Bayushi-vassal merchant of Ryoko Owari, who established his trading house in his twenty-third year and lived to manage it until his hundred-and-third. He had seven children by his first wife and three by his second; the first wife predeceased him, the second wife predeceased him, and all seven of the first wife's children predeceased him as well - three to plague, two to childbed, one to a duel he had not been told of until the body was returned, and one to old age in her seventieth year, herself outlived by her father. The three children of the second wife survived him. In his ninetieth year, Genbei commissioned the lantern and the cell beside it, and gave to the temple a perpetual endowment from the trading house's books to maintain the lamp and the Custodian's sustenance. The terms of the endowment specify that should the trading house fail, the temple is to draw upon the principal until the principal is exhausted, and only then to extinguish the flame. The trading house has not failed; it is now in its second century, run by Genbei's great-grandson, and the principal has not been touched.

The lantern is the focus of the *Watch of Continuing Days*, a continuous vigil maintained by the temple. The Custodian of the Lamp checks the wick and the reservoir at the hour of the dog each evening and at the hour of the tiger each morning; the vice-custodian checks at the hour of the rat. The lamp has not gone out. Once, in the great storm of the fifty-eighth year of its lighting, the flame was reported as dim for nearly an hour; the Custodian of that generation knelt at the platform through the night and replaced the wick three times in succession, and the flame did not fail. He wrote in his journal afterward that Jurojin had not so much sustained the flame as sustained *him*, that he might sustain the flame.

A pilgrim who has buried a child - of any age, infant or grown adult, of their body or of their household - may petition the temple to keep a single watch at the lantern. The petition is granted in order of receipt, with no preference given to rank; the Bayushi daimyo himself, when one of his sons fell in the Lion border-skirmish of seventy years past, waited eleven days for his turn. The watch is kept from the hour of the dog to the hour of the tiger, the pilgrim seated on a low cushion on the courtyard stones, the Custodian present but silent. Jurojin, who himself has buried generations beyond counting, does not require speech of those who keep the watch.

Some pilgrims are graced with the flame steadying after their arrival, where before it had wavered in the night air - a singular omen that Genbei has acknowledged the pilgrim's loss and joined his grief to theirs. Some are graced with finding, at the hour of the tiger, that the seven windows of the lantern have each shown a clear unflickering light, where ordinarily one or two will flicker with the night's breath - and this is taken as a sign that the pilgrim's lost one has been received among Genbei's seven, and is no longer alone. Some hear, in the small hours, the soft sound of a brush moving across paper, as of an old man working over his ledgers, and this is the merchant himself attending to the accounts of the living. Most pilgrims feel only the cold of the stones and the slow turning of the night, and even this is enough - for a parent who has kept one watch at the lantern of the Broken House has been told, without words, that the bearing of such losses is a labor that has been borne before, and that one may yet rise from the cushion at dawn and continue.

The lantern cannot be extinguished. The terms of Genbei's endowment are unalterable in the temple's records, and successive Bayushi daimyo have confirmed them at the changing of each generation. Were the flame to go out, the temple is bound to relight it immediately from the secondary lamp kept ready in the Custodian's cell, and to record the lapse in the *Book of the Watch* - a book which contains, in one hundred and fourteen years, only the single entry from the great storm of the fifty-eighth year, and no other.
