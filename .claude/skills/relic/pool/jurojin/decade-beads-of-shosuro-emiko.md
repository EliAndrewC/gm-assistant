---
name: The Decade Beads of Shosuro Emiko
japanese_romaji: Junen-Juzu
japanese_kanji: 十年数珠
fortune: jurojin
clan: scorpion
temple: a temple of Jurojin in Scorpion lands
named_entity: Shosuro Emiko (Scorpion widow of the late third century, who outlived her husband by sixty-one years and kept the count of her widowhood on these beads)
relic_type: implement (prayer beads numbered by decade)
---

A string of one hundred and eight prayer beads, larger and more deliberately spaced than the ordinary monastic *juzu*, the beads carved from polished cypress and strung on a heavy silk cord the color of dried iris. Among the hundred and eight are nine larger beads of darker wood, set at regular intervals — one larger bead for every twelve smaller — and a single bead of pale river-pearl at the place where the cord knots into its tassel. The decade-markers are stained darker than the rest from being passed many thousands of times between Emiko's thumb and forefinger over the sixty-one years she carried them. The beads rest in a shallow cedar tray on a low table in a quiet alcove off the temple's bereavement hall, beside an unlit oil lamp that is lit only when the beads are in use.

Shosuro Emiko was a widow of the late third century. Her husband, Shosuro Hidetake, fell in his thirty-second year in a duel whose particulars are no longer recorded in the family register. Emiko was twenty-nine. She did not remarry, despite three serious proposals during her thirties and one again in her fifties; she kept her husband's household, raised their two daughters, served three Shosuro daimyo as a senior lady-in-waiting, and lived to her ninetieth year. She had commissioned the beads in her thirty-second year, the year after Hidetake's death, from a Shosuro craftsman who carved the cypress from a tree felled on the day of her wedding. She began the count of her widowhood on the night of the year-day of his death, telling one full circuit of the beads — one hundred and eight passes of the cord — and marking the first decade-bead with a single touch of red ink at the end of the year. She continued the practice each year for sixty-one years. The decade-beads bear faint traces of the red ink to this day, layered into a deep stain at each marker.

Emiko's last surviving daughter brought the beads to the temple after her mother's death, with a written note: *for those who must yet count out their own years*.

A pilgrim who has lost a spouse, a parent, or a beloved companion within the past year may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to take up the beads for one telling. The Custodian receives the pilgrim at the bereavement hall, lights the oil lamp, and places the beads in the pilgrim's hands. The pilgrim is asked to tell one full circuit — one hundred and eight passes — counting each bead aloud or in silence as they prefer, and naming the lost one once at each decade-bead. The Custodian sits across from them in silence and does not speak unless spoken to. A full telling takes most pilgrims between a quarter-hour and an hour, depending on the steadiness of their hands and the depth of their grief.

The beads carry the steadiness of Emiko's count. Some pilgrims are graced with finding the decade-beads warm beneath their thumb where the surrounding beads are cool — an auspicious omen that Emiko has set her thumb beside theirs and that the count has been joined to hers. Some are graced with the lamp's flame steadying noticeably during the telling, where before the pilgrim's breath had set it dancing, and this is taken as Jurojin's particular attention to those whose breath has begun to ease. Some find that the count seems shorter than they had expected, the hundred and eight passing more quickly than the time of an hourglass ought to allow, and this is taken as a sign that the patience of the years to come may yet pass more quickly than the bereaved had feared. Most pilgrims simply tell the beads and feel their grief no less than before, and even this is the gift — for to count to one hundred and eight in the presence of one who counted to over six thousand is to be reminded that grief has a tempo, and the tempo continues.

At the conclusion of the telling, the Custodian asks the pilgrim a single question: *will you return in a year?* The pilgrim is not required to answer. If they answer yes, their name is entered into the *Roll of Counted Years*, which has been kept since the beads came to the temple. Each year-day of the loss, the pilgrim may return and tell one further circuit; their name is marked in the roll with the new decade-bead they have reached in their own widowhood or bereavement. Those who reach the ninth decade-bead — sixty years of accumulated tellings — are entered in the temple's permanent records and remembered in the year-end prayers. In the four centuries the beads have been at the temple, eleven names have reached the ninth.

The beads cannot be removed from the bereavement hall. The cord has been replaced twice — silk does not last sixty years of daily handling, much less four centuries of intermittent — but the beads themselves are the same beads Emiko commissioned, the same cypress from the tree felled on the day of her wedding. The river-pearl at the tassel was added by Emiko's own hand in her eightieth year, the year she stopped her own daily telling and laid the beads aside; the dates of the cord-replacements are not recorded, for the cord is the servant of the beads and not the relic itself.
