name: The Knotted Cane of Old Tarobei
japanese_romaji: Fushi-no-Tsue
japanese_kanji: 節の杖
fortune: jurojin
clan: any
temple: a temple of Jurojin, in any city of the Empire
named_entity: Tarobei (peasant farmer of the Yasen plain, who survived three famines and lived to his ninety-seventh year)
relic_type: implement (walking cane)
---

A short walking cane of mulberry wood, dark and oil-blackened from a lifetime of handling, the length of a man's arm from elbow to fingertip. The wood is gnarled with eleven distinct knots along its length, and Tarobei is said to have rested his thumb on a different knot each year of his old age, beginning with the lowest in his eighty-seventh year and ending with the highest in his ninety-seventh. The grip-end is bound in faded indigo cloth, replaced by the wife of the village headsman each spring; the foot is shod with a small iron cap, the third such cap, the previous two having worn through. The cane stands in a wooden rack against the wall of a side chamber off the temple's almsgiving hall, beside a low cushion where elderly pilgrims may sit.

Tarobei was a peasant farmer of the Yasen plain who lived through three famines — the Long Snow of his eleventh year, the Yellow Summer of his forty-second, and the Locust Year of his seventy-fourth. He buried his parents, his wife, two of his three children, and four of his six grandchildren. He continued to work his rice paddy until his ninety-third year, when his great-granddaughter took the work from him and gave him the cane in its stead. He had cut the cane himself from a mulberry tree in his father's hedge in his sixtieth year, but had not used it until his ninety-third, having said that the cane was for his old age and his old age had not yet arrived. He died in his ninety-seventh year, sitting in the doorway of his hut at dusk, and the village said only that he had finally finished his work. The cane was brought to the temple by his great-granddaughter, who herself died two winters later in her sixty-third year, having outlived her brothers and her husband and three of her children.

A pilgrim who has buried a child, a spouse, or both parents, and who is uncertain whether they have the strength to continue living, may petition the *Shika* — the Steward of Hospitality, in the temple's old usage — to sit for an afternoon with the cane. The Shika opens the rack, lifts the cane down, and places it across the pilgrim's lap. The pilgrim sits on the cushion until the sun has moved one hand's breadth across the floor of the chamber. There are no prayers prescribed; Jurojin attends those who simply remain.

The cane carries the weight of Tarobei's work. Some pilgrims are graced with finding the wood warm where their hand has rested on it, though the chamber is cool and the cane was cold when placed across their lap — an auspicious omen, a sign that Tarobei has recognized the pilgrim's burden and lent them a measure of his endurance for the season to come. Some are graced with the smell of cut rice-straw in the chamber, though no rice has been harvested nearby in months, and this is taken as the old farmer's particular blessing on those who must yet return to their fields. Some weep quietly without knowing why, and the Shika does not disturb them; the cane has carried many such weepings, and it knows how to bear them. Most pilgrims feel only the wood and the slow turn of the light, and even this is a kindness — for a samurai or a peasant who has sat one afternoon with the cane of a man who outlived three famines has been reminded that survival is itself a labor, and a labor that has been done before may be done again.

The cane is also brought out at the *Year-Turning Petition*, observed at the temple on the first morning after the winter solstice. Elderly pilgrims of the city come in procession, each touching the cane once and naming aloud the year they have just completed. The Shika records each name and year in the temple's ledger, which has been kept since the cane arrived — three centuries of names, accumulating from the foot of the page to the top in each generation's hand. Those whose names appear in the ledger for ten consecutive years are given a small wooden token from the temple workshop, carved with the character 寿 (*kotobuki*, "longevity") and the year of their tenth attestation, to be placed on their family altar.

The cane does not leave the temple. The iron cap has been replaced when worn through, and the indigo cloth is replaced each spring, but the mulberry shaft is the same wood that Tarobei cut from his father's hedge in his sixtieth year. To replace the wood would be to lose what the wood carries; the temple's records do not name the dates of the cap-replacements, for such records would suggest a relic that is *maintained* rather than *given*.
