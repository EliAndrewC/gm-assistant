name: The Mortar of the Five Generations
japanese_romaji: Godai-no-Suribachi
japanese_kanji: 五代の擂鉢
fortune: jurojin
clan: unicorn
temple: a temple of Jurojin in Unicorn lands
named_entity: Iuchi Sarukichi (Unicorn apothecary who returned from the Burning Sands and treated five generations of the Shinjo household)
relic_type: implement (apothecary's mortar and pestle)
---

A wide-mouthed mortar of dark-fired clay, the size of a melon, glazed on the inside with the pale grey film of a thousand grindings of herb and bone. The pestle that rests within it is of pale unfinished oak, worn smooth and slightly curved by Sarukichi's right hand and the right hands of his three apprentices in succession. The grooves on the mortar's inner surface are barely visible to the eye but unmistakable to the touch, faint radiating lines worn by the pestle along its accustomed arc. The mortar rests on a wooden stand in a small chamber off the temple's hospice wing, where the sick of the city are received; the chamber smells of dried angelica and ginseng even when neither herb is being prepared, the long residue of Sarukichi's work having soaked into the wood of the walls.

Iuchi Sarukichi was an apothecary in the train of Shinjo Otokwan, one of the families that crossed the Burning Sands and returned in the great homecoming. He had begun his apprenticeship in the herb-gardens of the old Iuchi house before the crossing, and was already past his fortieth year when the families set out. He survived the Sands, returned with the Unicorn, and on his arrival took up the post of household apothecary to the Shinjo daimyo, a post he held for the next sixty-three years. He treated the daimyo who had received him, that daimyo's son, that son's son, that son's son's son, and the infant grandson of the fourth — five generations of the same line. He had taken three apprentices in succession, of whom two predeceased him and the third inherited his practice. He died in his hundred-and-eighth year, having missed only nine days of work in the sixty-three years of his Shinjo service, and the mortar he had brought back from the Sands was given to the temple by his last apprentice, who specified only that it be kept where the sick might be near it.

The mortar holds the long alignment of an apothecary's life with Jurojin's domain. Sarukichi himself, in the only writings of his that survive, recorded that the herbs of longevity must be ground by a hand that has touched the same grindings many times, for the spirit of the herb knows its own road and grows confused when the road is changed. The pestle is the same pestle Sarukichi shaped from a sapling on the eastern slopes of the Sands, after the original pestle was lost in the crossing; the mortar is the original mortar he brought from the Iuchi gardens before the crossing, and is therefore older than the Unicorn's return.

Before any longevity tonic is prepared for a difficult patient at the temple hospice — for an elder facing their eightieth winter, for a samurai whose wounds have not closed despite ordinary care, for a child whose breath has been weak from birth — the temple's senior physician, who serves as Custodian of the Mortar, performs the *First Grinding*. He takes a single root of ginseng, places it in the mortar, and works it once around its arc with the pestle. He then sets the pestle aside, removes the root, and proceeds with the rest of the preparation using a working mortar of the hospice's ordinary stock. Sarukichi's grinding has joined itself to the day's work, and the patient's tonic is thereby brought into the long road of his practice.

Some patients are graced with the recovery proceeding faster than the senior physician would have predicted from his examination — a sign that Sarukichi has recognized the patient as he recognized his old daimyos, and lent the practice of those treatments to the new one. Some patients are graced with the chamber smelling unusually strong of angelica on the morning of their preparation, though no angelica has been ground there in some weeks, and this is taken as Sarukichi's particular attention. Some recoveries do not proceed; Jurojin's patience is for those whose time he has chosen to extend, and the relic is not a guarantor but an attendant. The senior physician records each preparation in the *Hospice Register* with the date, the patient's name, and the herb worked at the First Grinding, but does not attempt to record outcomes; outcomes belong to Jurojin, and the register's purpose is the maintenance of the practice, not its measurement.

The mortar cannot be removed from the hospice chamber. Sarukichi's last apprentice was specific in his bequest: the relic is to remain where the sick may be near it, and is to attend the hospice's preparations as Sarukichi attended his daimyo's household. A traveling Shinjo physician who wishes the mortar's blessing on a particular case is given, instead, a small clay token from the hospice — fired from the same earth as the mortar's body, glazed with a single grey stripe — which they carry in their satchel and place beside the working mortar of their own practice. The pestle has been re-shaped twice in the centuries since Sarukichi's death, as oak does not last forever; the hospice's records do not name the dates of the re-shapings, for the curve of the pestle is given by the work it does, and a freshly shaped pestle returns to the same curve within a season of use.
