---
name: The Long Shell of Bayushi Akemi
japanese_romaji: Nagaki-no-Kora
japanese_kanji: 長きの甲羅
fortune: jurojin
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Jurojin, Kyuden Bayushi (Bayushi family, Scorpion clan)
named_entity: Bayushi Akemi (Scorpion courtier of the late fourth century, who outlived four daimyo and every rival she ever knew)
relic_type: natural object (turtle-shell amulet)
---

The shell of a mountain turtle, the size of a woman's open hand, polished so that the dark plates of its back catch the lamplight like wet stone. Along the rim a thread of pale silk has been knotted through five small holes drilled by Akemi's own hand; from the silk depends a single carved bead of bone, which the old courtier kept against her breast for sixty-two years. The shell rests on a black-lacquered tray in a narrow chamber off the temple's eastern colonnade, beside a brass stand on which a brazier of long-burning charcoal is kept warm at all hours. The chamber smells of pine resin and old ink.

Bayushi Akemi was a courtier of the late fourth century, and the longest-lived woman of her generation in the Bayushi house. She entered Kyuden Bayushi's court in her seventeenth year and did not leave it until her ninety-first, having served four daimyo of the Scorpion in succession. She had no children of her own, no husband whose name survives in the family records, and no enemies left living to mourn her — a circumstance she is reported to have remarked upon in her final winter with neither pleasure nor regret. The shell she carried she had taken from the riverbank of the Drowned Merchant's Tributary on the day of her gempukku, when an old turtle had hauled itself up onto the stones to die. Akemi remarked that the shell would carry her past her enemies as it had carried its first owner past many seasons, and she fastened it to her sash that same evening.

The shell came to the temple by the bequest of her granddaughter-in-law, who had received it from Akemi's hand on the night before the old courtier's death. The bequest noted only: *for those who would yet endure*.

A pilgrim who has come to know that their position is unsafe — a courtier whose patron has fallen, a magistrate who has ruled against a powerful house, a samurai whose name has begun to appear in the wrong correspondence — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to keep vigil with the shell for one night. The Custodian receives the pilgrim in the chamber at the hour of the dog, places the shell on a folded silk cloth before them, and withdraws. The pilgrim sits before the brazier and the shell until the hour of the tiger, neither speaking nor sleeping. Jurojin, Fortune of long life, attends those who would learn his patience.

Some pilgrims are graced with finding the shell warm to the touch at the changing of the watch, though the brazier is set far enough away that the shell should be only as warm as the room — an auspicious omen that Akemi has accepted the vigil and recognized the pilgrim as her successor in patience. Some are graced with a single clear thought about which of their adversaries will not outlive them, and they leave the chamber knowing where to direct their care and where to spend none. Some hear, in the small hours, the faint scrape of a turtle's claws against stone, and the Custodian when he returns finds them composed in a way they were not when they entered. Most pilgrims experience no remarkable sign, and the night of stillness is itself the gift — for a samurai who has sat one night with their own peril and not fled it has already begun to outlast it.

The shell does not permit haste. Pilgrims who have asked to keep vigil with it twice in the same season are gently turned away by the Custodian; Jurojin's patience is not a resource to be drawn upon, only a stream to be entered when one is ready to enter it. The bone bead has been replaced twice in the centuries since Akemi's death — the silk thread perishes with handling, and a replacement bead is carved by a senior monk and blessed at the brazier — but the temple's records do not identify which bead is current. What matters is the shell, which is the same shell that crawled out of the river the year Akemi came of age, and the patience of which the bead is only the small bright reminder.

The shell cannot be removed from the temple. The Bayushi house has petitioned three times across the centuries to have it travel to a daimyo's deathbed, and three times the Custodian has refused — Jurojin's gift is in the pilgrim coming to the shell, not in the shell coming to the pilgrim, and a daimyo who could not walk to the chamber has by then passed beyond the reach of the relic's particular consolation. The refusals were each accepted; in the Scorpion, even the daimyo understands that some gifts must be earned by the journey to them.
