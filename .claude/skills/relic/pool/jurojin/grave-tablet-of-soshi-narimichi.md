A wooden funeral tablet of dark walnut, the length of a forearm, narrow and unadorned save for the posthumous name *Joen-in Choju Koji* (淨園院長寿居士, "Layman of the Pure Garden, Long Life") inscribed down its face in lacquer ink, and the dates of Narimichi's birth and death cut into the reverse with a fine knife. The tablet stands upright in a small wooden tabernacle at the rear of the temple's only prayer hall, behind a low altar where a single stick of sandalwood incense is kept burning. The tabernacle door is opened at dawn and closed at sunset; the tablet is not removed from the tabernacle for any reason save the annual cleaning at the changing of the year.

The Kakkimine hermitage is a small Jurojin temple of fewer than a dozen monks, established eight generations past on the eastern flank of the highlands that wall off Ryoko Owari from the Dragon border. The temple was founded by Soshi Narimichi himself in his eighty-seventh year, after he had outlived every member of the expedition with which he had set out into the Sands six decades before. He had been a young Scorpion shugenja, one of those who had crossed westward in the first century of the Unicorn's absence, on a mission whose precise charge is not recorded in any surviving register. He returned alone in his fifty-fifth year, thirty-one years after his departure, walking out of the eastern desert with no clear account of where the others had fallen. He took no further commission from the Scorpion. He lived quietly in Ryoko Owari for thirty more years, and in his eighty-seventh year withdrew to the highlands and built the hermitage with the help of three young monks who attended him. He died in his hundred-and-second year, having lived more years after his return than he had lived before his departure.

The tablet was inscribed by Narimichi himself in his hundredth year, with his own choice of posthumous name and the date of his birth. The date of his death he left blank, to be filled in by the monk who would receive him; the cut on the reverse is in the hand of his successor, the first Custodian of the hermitage.

The hermitage is small, and its visitors are few. The Custodian of the Tablet — which office is held in this house by the senior monk after the High Monk, there being no distinction here between *Custodian of Sacred Relics* and *Custodian of the Tablet*, for there is only the one relic — receives pilgrims who have walked the four-day path from Ryoko Owari to the temple. The Custodian asks the pilgrim to spend a night in the prayer hall before the open tabernacle, to speak the names of their own dead aloud once each, and to sit until dawn.

The petition is offered most commonly by those whose families have outlived themselves — a samurai who is the last of their lineage, a widow whose children have all preceded her, a household elder who has signed the funeral documents of every cousin of their generation. Jurojin's gift to such pilgrims is not the restoration of what they have lost, but the patience to bear what remains; Narimichi, who walked alone out of the Sands and built the temple in his eighty-seventh year, understood this gift more deeply than any other servant of the Fortune in the Scorpion lands.

Some pilgrims are graced with a slow steadying of the incense smoke during the night — the smoke ordinarily curls and wavers, but during the watches before dawn it may rise straight in a single column from the stick, and this is taken as an auspicious omen that Narimichi has accepted the pilgrim's vigil and recognized in them a successor to his patience. Some are graced with the smell of damp sand and far-off wind in the prayer hall, the smell of the desert from which Narimichi returned, and this is taken as the shugenja's particular acknowledgment of those who have made similar long crossings of their own. Some pilgrims hear nothing and feel nothing through the night, and even this is no failure; Narimichi himself, in the writings he left to the hermitage, recorded that the gift of the night is the night itself, and that those who require further sign are not yet ready for the gift's deeper portion.

The pilgrim, on departing at dawn, is given a small wooden token by the Custodian — a sliver cut from the same walnut tree from which Narimichi's tablet was carved, of which the hermitage keeps a careful stock. The token is to be placed on the pilgrim's family altar beside the tablets of their own dead, where Narimichi will attend them.

The tablet does not leave the hermitage. The walnut tree from which it was cut still stands in the small grove behind the prayer hall, having outlived Narimichi by eight generations. The hermitage's records do not speculate on the tree's age; what is known is that Narimichi planted no tree, and that the tree was old when he arrived. The tokens are cut sparingly, that the tree may continue.
