A long bamboo-handled rake with a head of fifteen split-bamboo teeth, the bindings of waxed hempen cord. The bamboo of the handle has been polished smooth by hands; the teeth are slightly uneven in length, three of them shorter than the others where they have been replaced over the centuries. The rake is kept upright in a slim wooden rack at the threshold of the temple's gravel garden, beside the worn flat stone where the rake's user removes their sandals. The garden itself is a long rectangle of white river-gravel raked into slow curves around five standing stones, enclosed by a low wall of weathered cedar. From a small bench inside the threshold one can sit and watch the garden without entering it.

Isawa Sumire was an Isawa scholar of the late fourth century, born into a lineage of fire-shugenja and educated, from her sixth year, in the Isawa libraries. By her thirtieth year she had become the senior cataloguer of one of the great libraries — a position of considerable standing, held by appointment of the Council of Elemental Masters. In her forty-fourth year she fell into a long illness from which she emerged thin and quiet. She returned to her library, looked at the seventeen years of marginal notes she had written in her cataloguing journals, and felt nothing. She walked home, sealed the journals into a cedar chest, took the chest into her family's garden, and burned it. The fire took three days to reduce the journals to ash; Sumire sat with the fire the whole time, sleeping in short stretches against the garden wall, taking water and a little rice from her servants. When the ash was cool she scattered it under the persimmon tree, walked to this Temple of Hotei, and asked to be permitted to work in the gardens. She did not return to her lineage. She did not write again. She lived another thirty-six years in the temple, raking gravel and tending the persimmons, and her only known utterances during this time were short observations about the weather and the gravel — none of which were recorded, for she had specifically asked that none be.

The rake was Sumire's own working tool from her tenth year at the temple until her death. The Custodian of Sacred Relics in the year after her death moved the rake from the gardeners' shed into a place of honor, replaced its leather grip with a finer cord, and instituted the protocol by which it has been used ever since.

A pilgrim who struggles with attachment — to a book they cannot finish, to a position they cannot let go, to a grudge they have nursed beyond its season, to a love that was not returned — may petition the Steward of Hospitality, the *Shika* of the temple's archaic Sangō tradition, for the **Hour of Raking**. The Shika receives the pilgrim at the threshold of the gravel garden, hands them Sumire's rake, and instructs them only: *"Rake until the bell."* The temple's hour-bell is sounded an hour later. The pilgrim enters the garden, removes their sandals, and rakes the gravel for the hour. The patterns to be raked are not specified; some pilgrims attempt to restore the curves they found, some draw new shapes, some rake the gravel flat. When the bell sounds, the pilgrim returns the rake to its rack and leaves by the side gate.

Sumire's hand guides the hands of the troubled. Some pilgrims are graced with a single, simple thought arriving in the middle of the hour — the answer to a question they had not consciously asked, the name of the attachment they had not been able to name. Some find that they have, without noticing, raked the gravel into the shape of a character — most often 捨 (*sute*, "to discard") or 任 (*nin*, "to entrust") — and the Shika, when this happens, permits them to leave the character standing for the day. Some find that they have raked the garden into perfect emptiness, and weep at the threshold; the Shika brings them tea. Most pilgrims simply rake for an hour and leave with their breathing slower than when they came, and this is Hotei's customary kindness. The garden is raked smooth by the next day's first pilgrim, who finds whatever the previous one left and decides what to do with it.

A samurai of any rank may petition for the Hour of Raking; a peasant may also petition, and the temple does not turn away peasants who come barefoot. The fee for the petition is one stone the size of a fist, brought from the pilgrim's home and added to a low cairn outside the garden wall. The cairn is a hundred and fifty years tall and has become a small hill against the wall. Sumire did not begin the cairn; it began of itself, after her death, when pilgrims started bringing the stones unbidden, and the Custodian of that generation decided not to refuse what Hotei was clearly arranging.

The rake cannot be removed from the temple. Its teeth are replaced as they wear out, three of fifteen having been replaced in the present generation; the Custodian's office does not record which teeth are original, as Sumire herself often replaced her own teeth without ceremony. The handle and the binding cord have not been replaced. The temple workshop produces miniature *replica rakes* of cedar wood, the size of a writing-brush, which scholars and bureaucrats keep on their desks; these are not used to rake anything, but rather are looked at when the work has become too heavy, and Sumire is said to watch over the small lookings as well as the great rakings.
