---
name: The Patch-Cloth Sack of Mirumoto Hayato
japanese_romaji: Tsugihagi-no-Fukuro
japanese_kanji: 継ぎ接ぎの袋
fortune: hotei
clan: dragon
temple: a temple of Hotei in Dragon lands
named_entity: Mirumoto Hayato (retired Mirumoto general of the early sixth century, who laid down his banners and walked to the temple)
relic_type: vessel (cloth sack of small charms)
---

A worn cloth sack the size of a man's two cupped hands, sewn from forty-one squares of fabric in differing colors and weaves — some indigo cotton, some pale silk, some coarse hempen weave, some patterned in the old Mirumoto sword-and-claw design now faded almost beyond reading. The squares are stitched together with thick linen thread, the seams uneven, the patches not arranged by color or pattern but by no order at all. The sack closes with a simple drawstring of plaited silk. Inside it, on a bed of unbleached cotton, rest one hundred and seven small charms — wooden carvings of animals and household objects, tiny brass bells, polished river-stones with characters scratched on them, small folded paper figures, a few pieces of cheap blue glass smoothed by the sea. The sack hangs from a wooden peg on the wall of the temple's children's hall, at the height of a tall child's reach, beside a low writing-desk where the temple's literate novices teach the children of the city their characters.

Mirumoto Hayato was a Mirumoto general of the early sixth century, a commander of three companies in the long border skirmishes against the Phoenix over the disputed villages of the high passes. By the end of his thirty-seventh year of service he had been wounded eleven times, had buried two of his three sons in battle, and had earned the favor of the Mirumoto daimyo, who proposed to elevate him to general of the entire western front. Hayato traveled to the daimyo's house, accepted the gift of a banner, and that evening sat in the daimyo's garden and watched the daimyo's youngest daughter, a girl of perhaps five years, playing with a cloth doll. He thought a long time. At dawn he laid the banner on the doll's mat, walked out of the house without speaking to anyone, walked four days to this Temple of Hotei, and asked to be received. He took nothing with him but his under-robe and a small bag in which he carried his razors and a comb. The Mirumoto daimyo did not contest his refusal; some say the daimyo wept when he heard, but this is not certain.

Hayato spent the remaining twenty-nine years of his life in the temple, teaching children to read. He sewed the patch-cloth sack himself, slowly, over the first three years. The squares came from his old military garments, from the under-robes of monks who had died, from a torn obi that a passing peasant gave him, from the wrapping of a gift he had once received from his daimyo and saved. Into the sack he placed small charms — first the ones he carved himself from scraps of cedar, then the ones the children began to make and leave for him, then the ones brought by pilgrims who came to know his story. By the time of his death the sack held more than two hundred charms; the present count of one hundred and seven reflects what has been given out over the centuries.

On the first day of the Boys' Festival in spring and again on the first day of the Girls' Festival, each child who is being raised by the temple — orphans, the children of monks, the children of the city's poor whom the temple has taken in — may put a hand into the sack and draw out one charm. They may keep that charm for the year. At the next festival they return it to the sack and draw again, or they may keep what they have if Hotei has spoken clearly through it. Hayato's spirit guides the small hands; the charm drawn is the charm the child needed, and there is no shame in not understanding why for many years. Some children draw the same charm three festivals running and learn, slowly, what attachment looks like. Some draw a new charm each time and learn the lighter wisdom of letting go. Most simply receive their charm, hold it in their hand for a moment, and carry it for the year as Hotei intended.

A pilgrim may not draw from the sack — it is for the children of the temple. But a pilgrim weary of strivings may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to *lift* the sack from its peg and feel its weight in their two hands, for one breath. The sack is unexpectedly heavy for its size; the charms shift inside it, a soft wooden rattle. Some pilgrims are graced with the sense that the weight is not the charms but the burdens Hayato laid down, and that he holds them so the pilgrim need not. Some are graced with a single charm rattling near the bottom in a particular rhythm — three soft strikes against the cloth — and the Custodian, who has heard this in others, will say that this is Hayato laughing. Most pilgrims feel only the weight and the cloth, and this is Hotei's plenty; the relic is not given to ornamenting every petition.

The sack cannot be removed from the children's hall. Its patches are repaired, never replaced; when a square wears through, a senior monk stitches a new patch over it without removing the old, and so the sack has grown slowly thicker through the centuries. The Custodian's office keeps no record of which patches are original and which are additions — Hayato did not record his own sewing, and to do so would be to make the sack into a thing of pride.
