name: The Laughing Fan of Kakita Shibu
japanese_romaji: Warai-Ougi
japanese_kanji: 笑い扇
fortune: hotei
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Hotei, Kyuden Kakita (Kakita house, Crane clan)
named_entity: Kakita Shibu (iaijutsu prodigy of the Kakita Academy, late fifth century, who renounced the duel)

---

A simple folding fan of bamboo and unbleached *washi* paper, twelve ribs, the standard size of a Kakita teaching fan. On its broad face is painted a single character — 樂 (*raku*, "ease, joy") — in soft black ink. The brushwork is hesitant in places, the strokes uneven; Shibu painted the character with his off-hand, three days after he laid down his sword. The fan rests on a wooden display stand in a small alcove off the temple's main contemplation hall, beside a low window that looks out onto a moss garden.

Kakita Shibu was a young iaijutsu prodigy of the Kakita Academy in the late fifth century. He won every duel he entered from the age of fourteen onward; by his twenty-second year he had become a chief instructor and was being considered for appointment as personal champion to the Doji daimyo. On the morning of the duel that would have sealed his elevation, Shibu sat with his father — an old Kakita sensei — and shared tea. His father asked him: *"What will you do tomorrow?"* Shibu said: *"I will duel and win."* His father asked: *"And the day after?"* *"I will become the daimyo's champion."* *"And the day after that?"* Shibu found he had no answer — only that the day after, and the day after that, was a path he could see perfectly and did not wish to walk. He did not appear for the duel. He left his sword at his father's house. He walked the half-day to the Temple of Hotei in Kyuden Kakita, then a modest temple of no great repute, and asked to be made a monk. He took only the fan that had been his teaching aid in the Academy.

The scandal of Shibu's refusal was great, but he never explained himself further. He spent the remaining fifty-three years of his life teaching village children to read and write in the hamlets around Kyuden Kakita, laughing freely, and never accepted a position above novice within the temple. His fan he kept, but used only to cool himself in the heat — never to teach iaijutsu, never to settle a dispute, never as a courtly gesture. After his death the fan was placed on the display where it now rests, and the Temple of Hotei in Kyuden Kakita slowly grew in standing as pilgrims came to know the story.

A pilgrim weary of striving — a failed courtier, an aging duelist, a samurai who has lost their place or been demoted, a monk who has begun to suspect their ambitions exceed their wisdom — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to hold the fan for one breath. The Custodian opens the alcove, places the fan in the pilgrim's hand, and asks the pilgrim to breathe once with the fan in their grip. The fan is then returned to its rest. Hotei's wisdom is not given to grasping; the briefest contact is enough.

The breath of contact reveals what the pilgrim has been refusing to see. Some are graced with a soft chuckle from somewhere in the alcove — Hotei's laugh, an auspicious omen that the pilgrim's renunciation will be accepted with mercy. Some are graced with a single clear thought about what they must lay down to find peace, and they leave the temple already at peace. Some weep without knowing why, and this is Hotei's particular kindness — the burden was greater than they had let themselves know. Most pilgrims feel only the bamboo and the breath, and even this is no failure; Hotei is content to be unhurried with souls.

Those who try to hold the fan longer than a breath — and a handful do, every generation, samurai of strong will who think they may extract more from the relic — find that the fan is not in their hand when they look. It rests on its stand as before. The Custodian does not comment on this, nor punish the attempt; Hotei has already spoken by withholding what was not asked for properly.

The fan has not yellowed in three centuries. The character 樂 is as soft and uneven as the day Shibu painted it. The bamboo ribs are the same bamboo, though the *washi* paper has been replaced perhaps twice over the centuries — the Custodians of the past did not record the dates of the replacements, for such records would suggest a relic that is *made* rather than *given*.

The fan cannot be removed from the temple. Kakita who wish to honor Shibu's example may petition the Custodian to commission a *replica fan* from the temple's workshop, painted with the same character by a senior monk; the replicas are taken home and placed on writing desks or kept in sleeve-pockets, but the original fan does not leave its alcove. It is not a teaching aid. It is a witness.
