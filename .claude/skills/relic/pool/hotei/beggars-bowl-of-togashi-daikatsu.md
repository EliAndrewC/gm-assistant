A black iron alms-bowl the size of two cupped hands, beaten rather than cast, the iron pitted and uneven across its outer face. The inside is dark-glazed from forty years of rice, broth, and rainwater. Along the lip on one side is a shallow notch worn smooth where Daikatsu's thumb rested against it — the angle and depth of the notch fit a man's right hand, and pilgrims who hold the bowl correctly find their thumb falling naturally into the place. The bowl bears no inscription, no mark of maker, no decoration of any kind. It rests on a square of unbleached linen on a low cypress shelf in a small unadorned hall near the temple's western gate, the hall through which the Children's Festival procession passes each spring.

Togashi Daikatsu was the founder of this Temple of Hotei, in an age before the surrounding settlement had grown to its present size. He was an *ise-zumi* — a tattooed monk of the Togashi house — who in his early years had received the dragon-tattoos and trained in the mountain disciplines of the order. In his thirty-second year, in circumstances the temple does not record (Daikatsu himself, late in life, declined to explain them, saying only that "the mountain showed me what a man weighs and what he carries"), he walked down from the mountain in a single under-robe with this iron bowl and a wooden staff. He did not return to the order's main hall. He walked the roads of the Dragon lands, and afterward of the Phoenix lands, and finally of the Lion lands and back again, for forty years, accepting only what was placed in the bowl. He spoke little. He laughed often, and his laugh was loud and surprising — those who heard it once at a roadside fire remembered it for the rest of their lives, and several of the temple's earliest patrons came to the order because of it. In his seventy-third year he returned, set the bowl on a flat stone at the western edge of the pilgrim-settlement, sat down beside it, and announced that this would be the site of a temple to Hotei. He died the same winter. The temple was built around the flat stone, and the bowl was placed on the shelf where it now rests.

In ordinary days the bowl receives no offerings. Pilgrims may stand before it, kneel before it, leave a single grain of rice on the linen beside it (never inside it), and depart. The hall is open by day and closed only on the three festival days of summer; the Custodian of Sacred Relics passes through it twice a day to ensure that the linen is clean and that no one has placed a coin in the bowl. Coins placed in the bowl are removed and donated to the temple's school for poor children; the donor's name is not recorded. Daikatsu refused coins in his lifetime, accepting only food and water, but the temple acknowledges that pilgrims may not have rice with them, and the children are grateful.

On the third day of the third month, at the climax of the Children's Festival, the **Procession of the Empty Bowl** carries the iron bowl through the city streets from the western gate to the eastern, and back. The Custodian of Sacred Relics walks at the head of the procession bearing the bowl with both hands. Behind him walk the children of the temple's school, then the children of the pilgrim-city's families, then any monk who wishes to join. The bowl is held with its mouth open to the sky. As the procession passes, the families of the city come out and place into the bowl what they would give to a beggar — a grain of rice, a piece of dried fruit, a small coin, a folded paper crane, a wooden charm. By the time the procession reaches the eastern gate the bowl is heaped to overflowing, and the children carry baskets behind it for the contents that no longer fit.

What is collected on the procession is distributed at the eastern gate to the beggars of the city — and the city has many beggars, for it is a place of pilgrimage and pilgrims sometimes arrive impoverished and sometimes leave the same way. Daikatsu's spirit walks the procession alongside the Custodian; some children are graced with seeing an old monk in patched robes walking beside the bowl, smiling at them. Some adults are graced with their offering, once placed in the bowl, settling with a soft chime as of a small bell, though no bell hangs near it. Some are graced with their offering returning to them the same evening in unexpected form — a piece of rice given in the morning found that night on their threshold in twice the quantity, or a coin given that morning replaced by the gift of a neighbor returning a long-forgotten debt. The temple does not promise this; it is the kind of thing Hotei does when pleased.

A monk in late life — a senior monk or higher who is approaching their fifty-fifth year or the end of their active service — may petition the Grand Abbot for the **Forty-Day Walking**. The petitioner walks for forty days on the roads of the Dragon lands with the iron bowl in their hands, accepting only what is placed in it, sleeping under hedges and in temple courtyards, returning each day's offering to the bowl until it is shared with whoever else needs it. The walking is permitted only once in a monk's life. It is not penitential and does not earn rank; it is what it is. Few monks complete the walking without returning early, and the temple does not regard early return as failure — Daikatsu himself, in his early decades of walking, came back to the mountain three times before the walking became permanent. The bowl knows its own pace.

The bowl cannot be replicated for sale or for gift. The temple workshop produces no copies of any kind; iron bowls in the city markets that claim to be "after Daikatsu's bowl" are not blessed and the temple does not contest them, for to contest them would be to claim more for the original than Daikatsu would have claimed. The bowl cannot be removed from the temple except for the Procession and the Forty-Day Walking. In the present century the bowl has gone out only sixty-three times by procession and four times by walking. It has come back each time.
