A dried gourd of pale green-gold, the size of a small child's head, polished smooth by long handling. Its narrow neck is bound with a band of hempen cord; its mouth is stoppered with a plug of pale paulownia wood. Along one side of the gourd's broad belly is a long, faint scar where the rind cracked once in its growing and the gourd healed itself — the temple does not consider this a flaw. The gourd rests on a low cypress stand in a small chamber off the temple's autumn courtyard, beneath a hanging scroll that bears only the character 足 (*taru*, "enough, sufficient") in Akari's own hand. Beside the gourd lies a single shallow sake cup of unglazed clay, the cup small enough that a full draught of it is no more than a swallow.

Shiba Akari served as a war-captain in the long border war of the Yari Pass in the fourth century, when the Phoenix and the Dragon disputed three villages on the western slopes. She commanded a company of bushi through eleven engagements and ended the war with a reputation for steadiness under fire. In the year following the peace, she sat in her family's house and discovered that she could not remember any of her dead by their faces — only by their voices, or the way they had moved at certain moments, and even these were beginning to fade. She broke her bow over her knee, donated her armor to the nearest Shrine of Bishamon, and walked to a small farm in the foothills where she spent the remaining forty-one years of her life brewing sake from rice she had not grown and giving most of it away. She did not return to the army. She did not marry. She took on no students. She is reported to have laughed often.

The gourd was Akari's own brewing-gourd in the last decade of her life — a vessel into which she would decant her best sake when a pilgrim of suitable misery came to her door. She would pour a single cup, drink with the pilgrim in silence, refill the cup once, and then send the pilgrim home with the gourd's blessing but without the gourd. The gourd never left her farm in her lifetime. After her death the gourd was found stoppered and full, and the village headsman brought it to this Temple of Hotei, where the Custodian of that generation tasted from it — the sake was excellent, and undimmed by the years. The gourd has been refilled each autumn since, and at each refilling some portion of the new brew is poured back into the gourd's mouth to mingle with what remains.

On the seventh day of the ninth month, the Custodian of Sacred Relics performs the **Autumn Refilling**, in which the gourd is unstoppered and a new brew — sake offered by the local Shiba families whose bushi have died in the preceding year — is poured into it. The names of the dead are read aloud as the sake is poured. The gourd does not overflow, though more sake is added each year than is drunk from it; the Custodian's office does not test this directly, for to measure the gourd would be to ask it a question it has not chosen to be asked. After the refilling, the gourd is stoppered and returned to its stand.

A widow or widower of a Shiba bushi may petition the Custodian for the **Cup of Sufficiency**. The Custodian unstoppers the gourd in the petitioner's presence and pours a single small cup — never more, never less. The petitioner drinks. The Custodian asks only one question: *"Is it enough?"* The petitioner may answer or remain silent; either is a complete answer. Some petitioners are graced with the cup tasting unmistakably of the year of their marriage — the rice their lost husband or wife had grown, or the brew that had been served at their wedding feast. Some are graced with a warmth in the cup that lingers in their hand for the walk home, longer than wood and clay should hold heat. Some taste only good sake, and this too is Akari's kindness — she did not promise miracles, only that the cup would be enough. The Custodian does not ask further about the experience; what is given to the petitioner is for the petitioner alone.

The gourd is never poured for celebration, never for wedding feasts, never for any drinking but the Cup of Sufficiency. Pilgrims who request the cup for any other reason — to taste a famous sake, to test the relic's powers, to settle a wager — are refused without ceremony, and the Custodian does not record their names. Akari did not pour for those who were not yet finished with their wanting.

The gourd cannot be removed from the temple. The local Phoenix families have at times asked that the gourd be carried to the homes of dying old bushi for the Cup of Sufficiency to be poured in their last days — and the Custodian has, on rare occasion, permitted this when the bushi could not be moved to the temple. In each such case the gourd has returned the same evening, and the brew within has not lessened by more than the cup drunk. There is no replica. The temple workshop produces small wooden cups, sealed with the temple's mark, which Shiba families keep on the household altars of bushi who have died in service; these cups are filled with the family's own sake at festivals, and Akari is said to watch over the small drinkings as well as the great ones.
