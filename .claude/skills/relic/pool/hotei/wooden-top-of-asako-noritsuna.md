---
name: The Wooden Top of Asako Noritsuna
japanese_romaji: Noritsuna-no-Koma
japanese_kanji: 紀綱の独楽
fortune: hotei
clan: phoenix
temple: a temple of Hotei in Phoenix lands
named_entity: Asako Noritsuna (Asako scholar of the fifth century, who gave away his property after the death of his small son)
relic_type: implement (child's wooden spinning top)
---

A small wooden spinning top, the height of a man's thumb-joint, turned from a single piece of pale cherrywood and balanced on an iron point set into its base. Around its widest circumference, four faint bands of pigment circle the wood — once red, blue, green, and yellow, now so worn that the colors can only be told apart when the top is held close to a lamp. The point is slightly dulled with use, and the top no longer spins perfectly true; one watches it wobble slightly as it slows, and the wobble was already present in Noritsuna's last years. The top rests on a small square of dark silk inside a shallow cypress box on a low altar in the temple's family-petitions chamber. The box has no lid; the top is always visible, and may be touched by petitioners who have been received by the Custodian.

Asako Noritsuna was a scholar of the Asako family in the fifth century, a researcher in the Phoenix tradition of family genealogies and a man of moderate property — a small house in the provincial town, three rice fields rented to tenant farmers, a household of seven servants. He married late and had one child, a boy named only in the family records as "the small one," who lived four years and died in the summer fever of Noritsuna's forty-eighth year. The boy had loved his spinning top, which Noritsuna had carved for him with his own hands in the boy's third year — an unusual gift in that Noritsuna was not a craftsman and the top is not a fine piece of work. In the year after the boy's death Noritsuna gave away his rice fields, dismissed his servants with generous pensions, sold his house to the Asako lineage, and walked to this temple with the top in his sleeve. He did not become a monk; the Custodian of that generation suggested that he might, but Noritsuna said only that he had given enough away already and did not wish to give away his sorrow too. He lived in a rented room near the temple for the remaining nineteen years of his life, ate one meal a day, and walked the gardens. He spoke kindly to the children of the town, but he did not teach them, and he did not carve another top. On the day of his death he left the top on the altar of the temple with a folded paper that read only: *"For other small ones, and their parents."* The Custodian placed it in the cypress box where it now rests.

The petition of the top is for parents — almost always for parents, though occasionally for an elder sibling or a grandparent who has the rearing of a child. A parent who is exhausted by an unruly child, or who has lost a small child to fever or accident, or who fears they cannot love the child they have been given, may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for the **Spinning**. The Custodian receives the petitioner at the altar, lifts the top from its silk square, and offers it to them. The petitioner takes the top between their thumb and forefinger, sets it on the polished cypress board that lies beside the box, and spins it. The petitioner watches the top until it falls. The Custodian does not speak during the spinning.

Hotei's wisdom comes through the wobble. A top that spins long and falls late — many breaths, the wobble small until the final moment — is a sign that the parent's love is steady though they may not feel it; the child will outgrow what the parent fears, and patience is rewarded. A top that spins briefly and falls quickly is a sign that the parent is being asked to release some part of what they are holding — a hope, a fear, a grudge against a circumstance — and to find lightness with what remains. A top that wobbles wildly from the first turn and falls almost at once is Hotei's particular kindness to the grief-stricken: the parent is being told that they may set down the weight that is breaking them, and that the small one is already at peace and does not require their suffering. A top that spins, falls, and rolls some distance before stopping is a sign that the answer is not yet ready and the parent should return in another season. The Custodian interprets the falling, but does not add to it; the petitioner is free to receive the sign in whatever way Hotei has prepared them to receive it, and Custodians of the past century have noted that parents most often understand the falling before they are told.

Some parents are graced with the top spinning unusually long — twice or three times what a child's casual spin would give. Some are graced with the top, in falling, coming to rest with its iron point exactly upon a knot in the cypress board, in a balanced posture that should not be possible. Some weep before the top has fallen, without knowing why, and the Custodian brings them a cup of warm rice-water and sits with them. Most parents see a small wobbling top spin and fall as small wobbling tops do, and this is no failure of the relic — Hotei does not crowd every petition with marvels, and the spinning itself is often the gift, for the parent has been required by the petition to hold still and watch a small turning thing for the length of its turning, which is a thing most parents in their daily lives no longer remember how to do.

The petition is permitted once per year per parent. A parent who returns within the year is gently turned away; Noritsuna gave the top for sorrow that lasts, and a year's spacing is what Hotei has chosen. The fee for the petition is a single small object that the petitioner has carried — a hair-pin, a button, a folded paper, a loose tooth from the child, an unimportant coin — which the Custodian places in a tall cedar jar kept beside the altar. The jar is emptied once a generation into the river that runs past the temple, and the things are released; Noritsuna's spirit has charge of them, and tends to them in the river's slow Hotei-ish way.

The top cannot be removed from the temple. It is not repaired; the iron point is dulled and will continue to dull, the colors are worn and will continue to wear, and when the day comes — the temple does not yet know when — that the top can no longer spin, the temple will receive a new sign from Hotei about what to do. The Custodian's office produces no replica tops for sale. Parents who wish to take something home from the petition may, with the Custodian's permission, take a single shaving of cherrywood from the box's silk square — there is always a small handful of such shavings under the top, where they have collected over the centuries from the wood's slow surrender, and the temple does not know quite where they come from but accepts the supply as Noritsuna's continuing generosity.
