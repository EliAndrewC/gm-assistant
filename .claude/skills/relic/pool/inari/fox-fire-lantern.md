The Fox-Fire Lantern is a small stone lantern, no taller than a kneeling man's forearm and small enough to be lifted and set down by a single magistrate without ceremony. It is carved from a single piece of the pale grey granite quarried in the hills above Ochiba village, and is shaped as a squat hexagonal pillar with a flared cap. Each of the six faces of the pillar is incised with a single character - 稻, rice; 狐, fox; 公, public; 正, upright; 燈, lantern; 民, the people - the six together running as a short prayer that the magistrate's predecessors held to be the foundation of honest magistracy in a countryside whose tithes were rice. The cap rests loose on the pillar, lifted aside to refill the oil-cup and replaced gently; the cup itself is a small bronze dish, blackened with use, sitting in a shallow well at the pillar's top. A short wick of twisted hemp burns in the cup.

The lantern was carved by Kitsune Goro, a stonemason of the southern Fox lands, in the third year after his own acquittal on a false charge of having shorted the rice-tithe of his village. Goro had been imprisoned for thirty days awaiting trial, and on the night before his trial a fox-fire - a small blue flame burning in mid-air with no fuel - appeared in his cell and burned for the length of the night without consuming itself. It was witnessed by the jailer and by two other prisoners. The trial the next morning was disrupted when a neighbour of Goro's came forward to confess that he had himself shorted the tithe and had falsely accused Goro in revenge for an old grievance. Goro, acquitted, carved the lantern as a votive offering to Inari, and presented it to the County Magistrate of his county at the time with the request that it be used during the magistracy's grain-tithe hearings so that no other man should suffer the same false charge. The lantern has been used in this way by every county magistrate of Ochiba since.

The lantern is the personal relic of the County Magistrate of Ochiba county, currently Kitsune Tatsuya, who is also a senior priest of Inari and keeps her shrine in the eastern range of the magistracy's inner court. The lantern is kept on a low shelf beside this shrine when not in use, and brought to the magistrate's bench by Tatsuya himself, in his own hand, at the opening of evening proceedings. The lantern is not lent; it does not travel beyond the magistracy. Junior magistrates who hear grain-tithe disputes outside the magistracy hall hold those hearings during daylight hours, without the lantern.

Tatsuya conducts evening hearings of grain-tithe disputes - those that have outlasted the day's docket, or those that the parties have brought after the harvest workers' day is done - with the lantern set on his bench and the wick freshly lit. Before each witness speaks, Tatsuya invokes Inari's name once in the formal manner of an Inari priest and asks the witness to speak truthfully on the harvest. The witness then testifies. The lantern's flame burns through the testimony, and Tatsuya watches it as he listens.

When a witness is concealing what they know about a harvest - overstating the loss to weather, understating the yield, accusing a neighbour of theft they did not commit - the flame of the lantern is sometimes seen to flicker blue, a clear blue distinct from the ordinary yellow of an oil flame, lasting a single breath or two before returning to its yellow. This is the kitsunebi, the fox-fire of the lantern's name, and is taken by Tatsuya as a sign that Inari's foxes have noted the deception. Tatsuya does not interrupt the testimony when the flame flickers; he marks the moment and continues. He does not announce a verdict on the strength of the flame. The flame is evidence weighed alongside the village's tithe-ledgers, the witness's standing among their neighbours, and the testimony of the others called.

Oil lanterns flicker for many reasons - a draft from the door, an impurity in the oil, a fold in the wick. Witnesses also fall silent or hesitate for many reasons that have nothing to do with deception. Tatsuya is well aware of these mundane causes, and will say so freely if asked by a younger magistrate observing his proceedings. He will also say that Inari uses the means at her disposal to make her wishes known, and that an honest magistrate need not distinguish between the draft and the fox-fire if both serve to mark the same moment of doubt. The lantern's signal is one of many that Tatsuya weighs.

The lantern cannot be destroyed without releasing the kitsunebi that abides in it; this is what the priests of the Inari shrine in Ochiba village have said to every magistrate since Goro's day, and no magistrate has yet wished to test the claim. The lantern is not lit during daylight hearings or during proceedings that do not concern grain. The oil is supplied by the Inari shrine at the village's expense, never purchased by the magistracy from the general oil-market, so that the supplier of the oil cannot be suspected of having a stake in the testimony. The wicks are likewise braided by the shrine's two senior shugenja, and brought to the magistracy by them in a small wooden box once each season.
