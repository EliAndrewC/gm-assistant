The Akami Brush is a medium-sized brush in the calligraphy style, but used not for ink and paper but for cinnabar and stone. The handle is cherry-wood, smooth-worn to a deep amber by long use; the ferrule is wrapped with cord that was once natural hemp and is now permanently red from generations of pigment. The hair of the brush is goat-hair, but no white goat-hair remains visible - every hair has been so saturated by the cinnabar of the threshold-stone painting that the brush sits in its rest as a small upright flame of vermilion. The brush is kept on its own low stand at the eastern altar of the shrine - the altar of Inari as Myobu - between paintings.

The brush is used by the County Magistrate of Ochiba - by the magistrate's own hand, not by an assistant - to paint the fox-tracks onto each newly-dressed threshold stone before that stone is carried out to its checkpoint along the Imperial road. The painting is done at the colonnade off the eastern altar, in the daylight hours, with the stone wetted to take the pigment. The magistrate-priest sits on a small cushion, the stone before him, the brush in his right hand, and lays down the fox-tracks in a fixed order that the shrine's apprentices learn by watching.

The brush has been used by every County Magistrate of Ochiba since the Year of the Long Cutting, when the wardings along the Imperial road through the southern reach of Kitsune Mori were first set. The physical brush is not the original - by the shrine's records, brushes have been retired and renewed something between six and eight times in the line, the records varying as records of so much age will do. Retirement is by burning at the Setsubun fire, the smoke carrying the brush's accumulated work back into the line of magistrates who painted with it; the new brush is dipped for its first stones at the dawn ceremony of the first day of the new year, and the apprentices and the shrine's recordkeeper acknowledge it as the same Akami-fude in continuity. The brush in the current magistrate's hand is therefore both the brush of the present generation and the brush of the original - there is, by Fox reckoning, only ever one Akami-fude, no matter how many physical brushes have served.

The brush is held to know the hand of its previous magistrates. New magistrates posted to Ochiba spend their first season practicing strokes on river-stones at the colonnade, and the sitting magistrate watches their first painted stones for signs that the brush has accepted the new hand. The signs are not announced; the sitting magistrate marks them in his records, and after a magistrate has painted a stone that the brush has plainly accepted, that magistrate is reckoned able to paint without supervision.

The brush is also held to refuse stones that have not been properly blessed at the quarry - the wear of the brush feels wrong on the stone's face, and the pigment will not lay smooth. The shrine's stonemasons therefore observe their quarry-rite scrupulously, knowing that a stone the brush will not paint must be returned to the river and a new one dressed in its place. A skeptical visitor might note that an experienced calligrapher's brush develops a particular feel that suits or does not suit a given surface, and that this is true of brushes everywhere; the magistrate-priest would say that Inari uses the means at her disposal to refuse the unworthy stone, and that a brush is a fitting means for her purposes.

The brush is never washed. The cinnabar between paintings is allowed to set in the hair; before a new painting the hair is loosened by softening the brush in clean spring-water for the time it takes to recite the short prayer of the Three Fox-Tracks, after which the brush is ready to take new pigment. The brush is not lent, even within the magistracy. A magistrate of Ochiba who travels for ceremonial duty does not carry it; the brush stays at the shrine, and the paintings done elsewhere are done with the brush kept at that destination's shrine, or not at all.
