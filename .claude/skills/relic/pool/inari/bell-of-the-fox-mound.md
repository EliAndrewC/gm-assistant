The Bell of the Fox Mound is a hand-bell of dark bronze, no larger than a man's fist, fitted with a worn leather thong by which it is held. The bronze has darkened almost to black with the centuries of incense and prayer-smoke through which it has been rung; the inner surface, visible when the bell is lifted, still shows the lighter colour of fresh-cast metal. A single chrysanthemum is incised on the bell's shoulder. Opposite the chrysanthemum is a smaller incised character — 狐, *kitsune*, fox — too worn now to be read by anyone but those who have been shown where to look.

The bell was originally hung at the entrance of a kitsune-zuka — a burial mound of foxes — on the road south of Shinden Kitsune, beside which the founder of the Fox clan, Kitsune Ryosei, was sheltered during a winter storm by a vixen and her three kits. Ryosei swore to make offerings at every fox-mound he passed for the rest of his days, and the foxes of that particular mound were among the first to whom he made his offerings. The bell was rung when offerings were laid at the mound. In later generations, when the road was rerouted and the mound itself slipped into the river that had eroded its base, the bell was carried into Shinden Kitsune and hung in the inner shrine. It has been rung from that shrine each dawn and dusk for at least seven hundred years.

The Steward of the Inner Shrine of Shinden Kitsune is the bell's custodian. The bell is rung by the senior shugenja on duty for the dawn and dusk offices, and is taken in hand by no one else without the Steward's permission. The bell is not displayed; it hangs by its leather thong from a hook beside the inner altar, where it is reached for and rung without ceremony.

When pilgrims are received at the inner shrine, the Steward rings the bell three times after the petition is spoken. Some pilgrims are graced, after the third ring, with hearing a second bell-tone returning from the wooded hills to the east — a faint chime, sustained for the length of a breath, answering the third ring as if from a great distance. This is Inari's nod, given through her foxes: the petition has been heard. The sign is not common. A pilgrim who does not hear the second tone is not refused; the Steward will tell them that Inari speaks in many voices, and that the silence of the foxes is not the silence of Inari.

The hills east of Shinden Kitsune carry sound strangely on still mornings, and the bell of the small Inari shrine in the village two ri east — also rung at dawn and dusk — is occasionally heard at Shinden Kitsune when wind and air-pressure favour it. Those who would test the relic by ringing the bell at hours other than dawn and dusk find no answering tone, but the Steward will say that the foxes answer when they are asked at the proper hour, not when they are tested. The Steward does not rebuke the question; she simply does not arrange the test.

The bell cannot be removed from the inner shrine for any purpose, even ceremonial procession, and the Stewards have held to this since the original mound was lost. It is not lent to other Inari shrines for inauguration, though Shinden Kitsune does send blessed cords of plaited rice-straw to such occasions in its place. The bell is rung by hand and never by mechanical means. A previous Steward, in the last century, briefly experimented with a pull-rope to spare the senior shugenja the daily climb to the inner altar; she discontinued the practice after a season of unusually poor harvest in the lands south of Shinden Kitsune was attributed by the local farmers to the change. The shugenja resumed the climb. The harvest the following year was within ordinary range and the matter was not raised again.
