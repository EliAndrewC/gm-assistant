The Bowl for Going to the Mountain is a low, shallow bowl of black-lacquered yew wood, the size of a man's two cupped hands. The lacquer has been applied and re-applied over many generations; the underlayers, visible where the rim has been worn by handling, are the dark red-brown of old urushi, and the topmost layer is a glossy black so deep that the few grains of rice in the bowl at any time appear to float above a small pool of ink. The bowl rests in a niche cut into the trunk of an enormous cedar at the edge of the forest, set perhaps three feet from the ground at a height comfortable for a kneeling forager. A rope of plaited rice-straw, replaced each new year, hangs above the niche.

The bowl was carved by Kitsune Hosen, a hermit-monk of Inari who lived in the forests of the Fox lands during the famine year that has come to be called the Year of the Three Rices — the year in which the rice was eaten three times before harvest could be brought in: once by the cold, once by the locust, and once by the soldiers passing through. Hosen survived that winter by the bounty of the forest, learning from the foragers of the surrounding villages where the mushrooms grew, where the chestnuts ripened, where the deer crossed. He carved the bowl in the spring of the second year and set it into the cedar where the foragers gathered before going into the woods, so that they might leave a few grains of rice in offering and ask Inari's blessing on their day's foraging. Hosen lived another forty years and tended the bowl himself; after his death the practice continued, and the bowl has been at the cedar for, by the best reckoning of the villages that share it, four hundred years.

The bowl has no resident custodian; the cedar is alone in the forest and the nearest village a half-ri away. A wandering shugenja of Inari makes a circuit of seven such forest shrines in the southern Fox lands, walking the circuit in about ten days and revisiting each shrine in turn. She gathers the offerings each morning into a small sack and carries them to feed the foxes and forest birds at the temple's main grange. The shugenja presently in this office is Kitsune Mariko, the third of her line to hold it since Hosen's death.

Before entering the forest, a forager pauses at the cedar, takes from the small pouch at their belt a measure of rice — three to seven grains is customary — and places it in the bowl. They touch the rim of the bowl with the index finger of their right hand, bow once to the cedar, and proceed. Foragers who skip the offering are not refused entry to the forest; the forest is no temple and has no gate. But foragers who skip the offering often, in the villages' telling, find themselves returning empty-handed, or with their gathering-baskets torn by branches, or with mushrooms that turn out at home to be the wrong kind.

The foragers who keep the offering carefully are also the foragers who keep their other preparations carefully — who sharpen their knives, who walk the forest slowly, who learn the difference between matsutake and shoro by sight and not by guess. Mariko, when asked, will say that Inari rewards the diligent through her foxes' guidance, and that diligence in offering is diligence in foraging, and the two cannot be separated even if one wished to.

At dusk on the first day of each new year, a procession of villagers walks the forest path to the cedar and rebraids the rice-straw rope that hangs above the bowl. The old rope is burned at the cedar's base; the new rope is plaited from the new year's harvest by the villagers themselves, with the shugenja chanting the proper invocations. If the smoke of the burning rope rises straight, the year's foraging will be good; if the smoke drifts to the south, the forest will yield more sparingly. Mariko, asked by a young forager what to make of the year a sudden eastward gust took the smoke sideways into the underbrush and the rope had to be coaxed back into a column with a fan, told the boy that smoke drifts as the wind tells it to drift, and that Inari speaks to the wind, and the two together speak to those who listen.

The bowl cannot be removed from its cedar. Two generations past, in a season of unusual flooding, the villagers sought to lift the bowl from its niche and carry it to the village temple for safekeeping until the floods receded; the bowl could not be lifted. The lacquer had bonded to the cedar's wood over the centuries, and the niche had grown closed around the bowl's foot. The villagers built a small roof of bark over the cedar instead, and the bowl rode out the season where it had always sat. The roof was taken down after the floods, and the bowl is still in its niche.
