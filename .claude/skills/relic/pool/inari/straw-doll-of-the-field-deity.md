The Straw Doll of the Field Deity is a figure of plaited rice-straw, the size of a child of perhaps four years, with a body shaped from a bound bundle of straw and arms and legs of the same straw plaited into short braids. The face is suggested rather than rendered: two black-stained straws crossed for eyes, a single red-stained straw for the mouth. The figure wears no clothing; the straw itself is its garment. It rests on a low altar of unfinished cedar within a small Inari shrine set into the wall of the village temple, beside the larger shrine of whichever Fortune the temple was founded to honour. The shrine is no more than a niche, draped with two short curtains of unbleached hemp; the doll sits in the niche on the altar, hands resting on its straw knees, head slightly bowed.

The doll is not a single artefact. It is remade each year — by the village priest at the end of each harvest, from the first sheaves of the new harvest, in the same form as the previous doll. The old doll, having served its rotation, is burned at the village's New Year fire, and the new doll is dressed in its place at the dawn ceremony of the first day of the new year. The continuity of the figure across centuries is the continuity of the rite, not the continuity of the straw.

Ta-no-Kami, the field deity, is one of the many faces of Inari. Where Inari's great temples — Shinden Kitsune and its sister shrines in the Fox lands — venerate Inari in the cycle of foxes and the great offerings of the harvest court, the smaller villages of the Empire know Inari as Ta-no-Kami, the deity who descends from the mountain in spring to oversee the rice-planting and returns to the mountain in autumn after the harvest is in. The Straw Doll is the figure into which Ta-no-Kami is invited each year to dwell during her time in the village.

The village priest is the doll's custodian, regardless of which Fortune their temple primarily serves. In a temple of Hotei, the priest will be the same monk who oversees Hotei's rites; in a temple of Ebisu, the same. The Inari shrine is by long custom a guest-shrine within the host temple, and the host order tends it as a matter of courtesy to the village's farmers. The host order is not obliged to maintain the Inari shrine, but no village priest in living memory has refused to do so.

Three days after the first sowing of seed in the paddies, the priest takes the doll from its altar and carries it in a small procession to the paddies. The villagers carry rice-straw torches in the procession, lit from the temple's hearth. At the paddy that has been chosen for the year — rotated among the village's paddies on a cycle the priest keeps in his records — the doll is set on a small wooden platform raised above the water, where it will remain through the planting season and the growing season. The doll watches over the paddy. At harvest, after the last sheaf is cut, the priest returns the doll to the temple in a similar procession, and the doll resumes its place on the altar of the Inari shrine within.

The villagers watch the doll's posture as it sits on its platform through the season. A doll that sits upright through the rains and the high winds of the summer is held to be a sign of a good harvest. A doll that leans, or a doll that loses an arm to a storm and must be repaired, is taken as a warning. The priest interprets these signs in light of the year's rains, the temperature, and the local insect-counts; the doll does not speak alone.

A straw figure in a rice paddy is exposed to the same weather as the rice. A good harvest follows a year of favourable weather, and a year of favourable weather is also a year in which the doll holds together. The priests will say that Inari favours the year that favours her doll, and the harvest that follows is the proof. A modern reader hearing this might think only that a year of good weather produced both an intact doll and a good harvest; the village priest will say the same words in a different order.

The doll cannot be carried beyond the village's paddies. A doll that is taken from the village ceases to be a Ta-no-Kami figure and becomes mere straw. If a doll is destroyed before its annual burning — by storm, by accident, by malice — the priest must make a new one at once and consecrate it on the same day; a paddy left without its doll during the growing season is held to lose Inari's regard for the season. The villages of the Empire that observe this rite hold to it, and the rite has been observed in some villages since before living memory.
