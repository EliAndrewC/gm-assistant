A small drag-net of woven hemp cord, the size that a single fisherman would cast from the stern of a flat-bottomed coast-boat. The cord has gone the dark color of old rope, soaked through with sea-salt and fish-oil over generations. The leaden weights along its bottom edge are river-stone rather than lead — Rokuemon's village was too poor for metal in his time, and he tied each stone himself with a fisherman's knot, the knots still visible after these centuries. The net is folded carefully into a low square of unvarnished pine wood, lined with rice-straw, and rests on a stone plinth in a side-chamber of the temple that opens onto the harbor through a wooden lattice. The sound of the tide can be heard from the chamber at all hours.

Yasuki Rokuemon was a fisherman of one of the small coastal hamlets that pay rice and fish to the Yasuki domain, in the second century after the domain's founding. He was a tenant of no particular distinction until the famine year that the temple records simply as *the Hungry Spring* — the year a late frost killed the rice-shoots in their flooded paddies up and down the coast, and the inland granaries were already depleted from a hard winter. While the headsmen of the inland villages petitioned the Yasuki for relief, Rokuemon and the fishermen of his hamlet rowed out every morning, in every weather, and brought back what they could. Rokuemon's catch in that spring was such that no household in three hamlets went without a fish-meal, and his own family ate last of all. The boats of the other fishermen often came home empty; Rokuemon's net came home heavy. He gave away the bulk of his catch to the temple's almsgiving station and to the headsmen of the inland hamlets, and asked no payment. He fished for thirty-six years after that spring and was buried in a fisherman's plain shroud, and the net was brought to the temple by his eldest son.

A fisherman preparing for a voyage of difficulty — a long passage down the coast, a season of unfavorable winds, the first voyage of a new boat — may petition the *Shika*, the Knower of Guests, to be received at the chamber of the Net. The Shika opens the pine box, lifts a single corner of the net between his two hands, and lays it briefly against the shoulder of the fisherman in the manner of a blessing. The fisherman speaks aloud his voyage and the hold he hopes to fill, and the names of the household whose meals depend on the catch. The Shika folds the corner back into the box and closes the lid. Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, lays his regard upon the fishermen whose work is done in honest measure, and Rokuemon's net is the witness through which the regard is given.

Some fishermen are graced with the smell of fresh sea-water rising from the net when the Shika lifts it — an auspicious omen, signifying that Rokuemon has put his hand upon the voyage to come. Some are graced with a single drop of water falling from the net to the stone floor though the net has been folded dry for weeks, a sign that the catch will be full. Most experience nothing remarkable, and the blessing is no lesser for it; a fisherman who has honestly prepared his boat and his nets and his crew has already done the work that Ebisu favors, and the relic does not undo what the fisherman has not done.

On the first morning after the spring equinox, the Shika performs the **Telling of the Knots**. He kneels before the box, unfolds the net to its full extent in the chamber, and counts the stones along the bottom edge — the same one hundred and eighty-seven river-stones Rokuemon tied to it in his thirty-first year. Each year one or two of the knots are found a little loosened by the season's handling, and the Shika ties them again with the original cord. The count is set down in the chamber's register beside the count of the year before, and the temple's records of nine generations show that the count has never changed. Rokuemon tied his knots once and well, and the stones do not fall.

A fisherman who has lost his boat — to storm, to gaijin raiders along the Yasuki coast, to fire — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for a piece of cord cut from a *replica net* woven by the temple's monks each spring. The cord is tied around the wrist or the rope-belt and is carried until the next boat is launched, when it is tied to the mast. The replica nets are not the Net of Rokuemon; they are blessed at the Net's box and given freely, and the fishermen of the Yasuki coast carry them without ceremony, the way a man carries a coin in his sleeve.

The original net cannot be removed from the chamber. The harbor below has come to expect its presence; the Shika of the present generation has heard from the harbor-master that the older fishermen will not put to sea on a morning when the chamber's lattice is closed. The temple does not close the lattice. To take the net out to a boat would be to ask Ebisu to bless a single voyage rather than the whole work of the coast, which is not Rokuemon's gift to give.
