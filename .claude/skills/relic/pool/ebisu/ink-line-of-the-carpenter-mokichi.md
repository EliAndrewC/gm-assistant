A carpenter's ink-line - a *sumitsubo* - carved from a single block of zelkova wood in the shape of a carp caught mid-leap. The fish's open mouth is the ink-pot, packed with silk wadding kept dark and wet; from a small reel set in its tail pays out a fine line of old silk, re-spun many times over the generations, which passes through the inked wadding and comes away black. At the free end is a small iron pin - the *karuko* - by which the line is pegged. Drawn taut across a board and plucked, the line snaps down and leaves a single perfectly straight black mark, the true line by which a carpenter cuts. The carp's scales have been worn smooth by handling to the color of dark honey. The relic rests on a low plank table in the temple's penitents' chamber, and beside it always lies a length of planed cypress board, kept bare for the marking.

Mokichi was a master carpenter, a *daiku* of no high birth, who rebuilt this temple's main hall after a fire. The famous part of his story comes from the middle of that rebuilding. A great pillar he had already raised and finished - planed, fitted, and joined - he found to be sound on its face but rotten at the heart, a flaw that paint and good joinery would have hidden for thirty years. He reported the rot himself. He tore out his own finished work, re-cut the pillar true, and bore the loss from his own purse, saying only that Ebisu does not bless a hall raised over a hidden rot, and that a carpenter who marks one false line will in time mark a thousand.

The pillar is the story the temple tells, but it was not the pillar alone that ruined him. It was that he lived by the same rule on every job of his life, in a trade where a false line is how a man grows rich: the hidden rot painted over, the green wood billed as seasoned, the measure padded a finger's breadth on every beam. Mokichi would do none of it. He tore out his own errors at his own cost, named every flaw he found though the naming lost him the work, and would not underbid a rival by promising what he knew he could not honestly build. Within a few years of the temple's rebuilding he was bankrupt. He lost his shop, his tools, and his apprentices, and the men who cut the corners he would not cut took his trade and prospered on it.

He did not stop working. For forty more years he hired out his hands to other masters - an old master carpenter taking a journeyman's daily wage - and in all that time neither the temple's records nor, later, the guild's hold any account of a crooked line he set and let stand. Late in his life his name was remembered. The guild that had let him fail honored him; the temple lauded him; carpenters brought their apprentices to meet the old man who had never marked a false line. But the honoring put no shop back under him and no coin of consequence in his hand, and he died as poor as any hired man, in a rented room, with the regard of Ebisu and of every honest carpenter in the domain and with nothing else. His ink-line was given to the temple when his eyes failed him and he could no longer follow the mark, and the bare cypress board has lain beside it since.

A penitent who comes to the temple to leave a sin behind is first received before the Ink-Line. They kneel at the plank table with the board before them and speak their true name and their true deeds - the honest accounting of what they have done - while the Custodian of Sacred Relics draws the silk taut across the board and snaps it. Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, sets a true line for the honest tongue. A line that runs straight and clean from peg to peg is the witness that the confession runs straight, and the penitent may then be brought before the temple's vow. A line that bows, that frays in the snapping, or that breaks before it marks, is taken as a sign that something has been kept back, and the penitent is sent to sit with their conscience and is received again only when they are ready to mark the board true. The line is not the verdict. A true line wins a penitent the chance to swear the vow and take up the term of labor; it does not by itself absolve them, and the long proof of the work to come is the real test. The Custodian weighs the line as one thing among many - the penitent's bearing, the testimony of those they wronged, the record of the life behind them - and the judgment remains the Custodian's, which the relic serves and does not replace.

Some who kneel to confess are graced, as the line is snapped, with its clean carpenter's note - a soft, clear sound against a board that is only bare wood - an auspicious omen taken to mean that Mokichi has accepted the confession as a true one. Some find a single bead of ink standing on the board where no line was struck, read as a sign that the penitent has more yet to confess and will confess it before the work is done. Most experience nothing remarkable, and the confession is judged by the Custodian's discernment all the same; a man whose tongue runs true has marked his line whether or not he is granted a sign, and a man who has kept something back has not, though the board show a line straight as a temple beam.
