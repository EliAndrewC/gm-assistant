A simple cedar rice-measure (a *masu*) the size of a fist, hand-carved and unadorned, with one small character — 誠 (*makoto*, "honesty") — burned into the inside of one corner. The exterior is the pale color of cedar where the wood is smooth; the interior is darker, oil-soaked from generations of grain. It rests on a low lacquered stand in a side-chamber off the temple's commercial-petitions hall.

The masu belonged to Yasuki Bunzo, a Hida-vassal rice steward of the Reiji domain in the second century of its establishment. During the famine of his fourteenth year of service, Bunzo was tasked with redistributing the temple's stored grain to the starving villages of the northern provinces. He used this single masu for every measure, and the rice did not run out until every family had received their share. The chamberlain who audited the redistribution afterward could not reconcile the recorded measures with the volume actually distributed; he wrote in his journal that he simply trusted Bunzo, and that Ebisu must have multiplied the grain through his faithful service.

When a grain merchant in the Reiji domain is accused of fraud, the County Magistrate may petition the temple for a *measure-test* as one element of his investigation. The merchant brings a sample of his own grain. He pours it into the Honest Masu in the presence of the Custodian of Sacred Relics, levels it with a flat strike-stick cut from Bunzo's staff, and the result is observed. Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, steadies the hand of the righteous and refuses to steady the hand of the deceitful. A merchant whose conscience is clear pours calmly and levels the grain on the first stroke; a merchant whose conscience is heavy finds the grain piling unevenly, the strike-stick catching, the measure refusing to settle.

The test is not a verdict. The magistrate weighs the result alongside the merchant's account books, the testimony of his neighbors, and the records of his prior dealings. Some magistrates make a point of demanding the test from both parties in a commercial dispute, knowing that a calm conscience steadies the hand and a guilty one shakes it — and that Ebisu's hand is upon the room either way. The magistrate's discernment, supported by what the relic reveals about each party's standing with Ebisu, is what produces the ruling.

The masu has not measurably warped in its centuries of service. The character 誠 in its corner was burned in by Bunzo's own hand without flint or fire — Ebisu blessed the wood so that it smoldered to mark him. The strike-stick is one of seven cut from cedars of Ebisu shrines, rotated through use; the temple's records do not identify which is the original, and this is as Ebisu wills, for what matters is the work and not the tool.

The masu cannot be removed from the temple. Traveling tax officials carry blessed *replicas* commissioned from the temple workshop, but the original Bunzo-masu has not left Shiro Reiji in living memory. To lose it would be a grievous offense to Bunzo's spirit and to the long tradition of honest measure he sustains in the Reiji domain.
