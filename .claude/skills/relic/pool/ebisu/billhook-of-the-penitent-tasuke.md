A heavy single-edged woodsman's billhook - a *nata* - the blade a hand's length of thick beaten iron, the spine as broad as a man's thumb, ground to a cutting edge only along its lower third. The iron is dark and pitted with age. Near the heel of the edge a deep notch has been filed out and never re-ground; Tasuke filed it himself, with a stone, so that the blade could never again take an edge fit for a man's neck and would serve only for wood. The haft is white oak, bound at the grip with cord worn black by a working hand. It does not rest in a gilded chamber. It hangs in a plain wooden cradle on the wall of the temple's woodshed-shrine, beside the chopping-block where the temple's firewood is split, where the air smells of sap and cut pine and the floor is dusted with bark.

Tasuke was a brigand. The temple does not record the name he carried in those years, for Ebisu does not steady the names of the dishonest. He had taken lives on the road - how many, the records do not say - and he came to this temple in a hard winter not as a pilgrim but as a hunted man, and he asked not for sanctuary against the magistrate's men but for work. The Custodian of that generation set him to splitting wood, and he split wood. He stayed more than thirty years. He cut and carried firewood for the temple and for the poor of the surrounding hamlets, mended the fences of the woodlot, cleared the road of deadfall after every storm, and swore a vow to lay down forever the blade against men. He never broke it.

His death is the reason the temple keeps the billhook, and the reason it teaches what it teaches about honesty. In his old age Tasuke was recognized by a traveler as a man wanted for a killing on the road a lifetime before. He might have denied it, and the denial would have held, for the road-name he had once carried was long buried and thirty years of honest work stood in front of it. Instead he confessed the crime truly and in full, and named the man he had killed. The monks did not shield him. They would not lie for him and they would not hide him, though they knew what the confession would cost, for the temple that had made an honest man of him would not make a liar of itself to keep him. The Custodian went before the magistrate and pleaded for his life: thirty years of honest labor, an old man who fed the poor of three hamlets, a soul long since turned, and asked that a lifetime of service stand in place of death. The magistrate heard the plea and had him executed all the same, for the crime he had confessed with his own mouth carried death, and by confessing he had proved himself guilty of it. Had he kept silent he would have died a free old man in the woodshed. He kept nothing back, and it saved him from nothing, and the temple has honored him ever since for exactly that: an honesty that served no end of his own and bought him no mercy, and that he spoke for no reason but that it was true.

From his death the temple keeps its resolve to deal with its own penitents, and to stand between a faithful penitent and the magistrate as far as an honest house is able - to shelter the reformed, but never by a lie. His billhook was carried from the execution-ground to the woodshed-shrine, where it has hung ever since.

A person who would leave a sin behind - a gambler, a thief, a brawler, one who has shed blood - may petition the temple and swear, before the Billhook, a vow to abandon that sin. The vow is spoken with the right hand laid on the worn haft. With the vow the penitent binds themselves to a term of honest labor for the temple, measured in seasons: so many seasons of wood-splitting, of road-clearing, of carrying and mending and working the temple's fields. The term is the penance, and the work itself is the offering, for Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, knows a turned life not by its words but by the seasons of labor that follow. A penitent who keeps the vow and serves out the term is received as one whose debt to Ebisu is paid. A penitent who breaks the vow and returns to the sin is surrendered by the temple to the magistrate without protest, for the Billhook shelters the penitent and not the unrepentant, and the notch in its edge was filed for honest wood and not for the comfort of liars.

Some penitents, gripping the haft to swear, are graced with the smell of fresh-cut green wood rising from a blade that has cut nothing in long years - an auspicious omen, taken to mean that Tasuke has set his hand over the vow and that the labor to come will go true. Some find the oak warm beneath the palm where they close their hand to swear, a sign that the vow has been received. Most feel nothing remarkable, and the vow is no weaker for it; Ebisu knows the penitent by the seasons of honest work to come, and not by any sign granted at the block.
