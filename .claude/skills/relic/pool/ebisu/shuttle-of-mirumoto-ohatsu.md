A weaver's shuttle of close-grained mountain-cherry wood, the length of a woman's hand from wrist to fingertip and the breadth of two fingers. The wood has been polished to a soft glow by the passage of warp-thread and the oil of generations of palms; the carved channel for the bobbin is darkened where the silk has worn it. Along one side of the shuttle a single line of small characters has been incised by a thin blade — Ohatsu's own counting-marks for the bolts she finished in her last winter, seventeen marks in all. The shuttle rests on a folded square of unbleached hemp cloth, on a low table at the side of the temple's craftsman-petitions chamber. On the wall above hangs a single bolt of plain cotton, woven by Ohatsu herself in the autumn of her sixty-second year and given to the temple when she could no longer sit at the loom.

Mirumoto Ohatsu was a weaver of one of the river-towns that pay cloth and dye to the Kitsuki family, in the fifth century after the Kitsuki line's founding. She was a tenant weaver of no great household, and would have passed without record but for the long winter of her sixty-second year, when the Kitsuki river-towns were cut off from the cotton-merchants of the south by an early snow and the dye-houses had no work. The peasants of three towns came into that winter with patched and threadbare clothing, and the new season's bolts had not arrived. Ohatsu, who had given up most of her weaving by then on account of stiffening hands, set up her loom again. She wove every day from dawn to second-bell, on the cotton thread the temple still had in store from the previous summer, in the plain undyed weave that her mother had taught her. She finished seventeen full bolts that winter, and the temple's almsgiving steward distributed them to the village headsmen for cutting and patching. No family of the three towns went into spring in rags. She died the following autumn, at the loom, having woven the eighteenth bolt to within a hand's-breadth of completion. Her shuttle was placed beside the unfinished bolt and brought together to the temple.

A craftsman of any trade — weaver, carpenter, smith, dyer, paper-maker, cooper — who has been commissioned for a work of particular weight, may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for the **Blessing of the Shuttle**. The craftsman brings the first material of the commission: the cut warp-thread for a weaver, the first stave for a cooper, the unworked iron for a smith, the prepared mulberry pulp for a paper-maker. The Custodian receives the craftsman in the chamber, lifts Ohatsu's shuttle in his right hand, and passes it once — slowly, in the manner of a weaver throwing across the warp — above the material the craftsman has brought. The craftsman names the commission aloud, names the household or temple for whom the work is being done, and names the season by which the work must be completed. The Custodian sets the shuttle back upon its cloth and bows; the craftsman bows in turn and carries the material home.

Some craftsmen are graced with the soft sound of a loom — the brief, clear thump of a beater settling a weft against the warp — when the shuttle passes above their material. This is taken to mean that Ohatsu has accepted the commission into her sight, and is an auspicious omen that the work will rise from the craftsman's hands without crooked seam. Some are graced with the warmth of their material when they take it back into their hands, a sign that Ohatsu has set her labor beside theirs. Most craftsmen experience nothing remarkable, and the blessing is no lesser for it; Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, knows the craftsman by his work and not by the omen at the chamber.

The Custodian may refuse a blessing. This is uncommon, perhaps two or three times a year, when the craftsman names the commission in a way that the Custodian's ear judges to be incomplete — when the season named is impossible, when the household named has commissioned another craftsman already without releasing the first, when the materials brought are not the materials that the commission would require. The Custodian sets the shuttle back without passing it over the material, and asks the craftsman to return when the commission has been clarified. Ebisu does not bless a work whose terms the worker has not honestly stated.

A craftsman whose commission has gone badly — a bolt that has unraveled, a barrel that leaks, a blade that has cracked at the tang — may return to the chamber for the **Counsel of the Shuttle**. The Custodian receives the craftsman with no rebuke, lifts the shuttle, and asks the craftsman to speak each step of the work from the beginning. The shuttle is not passed over anything; it rests in the Custodian's hand while the craftsman speaks. Some craftsmen, in the speaking, find the fault themselves, and bow and leave to begin again; the shuttle's witness has been enough. Some are still uncertain at the end of the telling, and the Custodian sits with them in silence for as long as the craftsman wishes. Ohatsu's last winter is in the wood of the shuttle, and the craftsman who sits with it for a while is rarely without counsel by the end.

On the first day of the tenth month, when the dye-houses of the Kitsuki river-towns prepare for the winter's indigo work, the Custodian performs the **Reading of the Marks**. He kneels before the table, lifts the shuttle, and reads aloud the seventeen counting-marks Ohatsu cut into it in her last winter — one for each bolt she finished. The reading is short. The Custodian sets the shuttle back down and gives the day's blessing to the gathered weavers and dyers of the town, who have come in their work-aprons to witness. The Reading does not change from year to year, and the marks do not fade.

The shuttle cannot be removed from the chamber. The temple's workshop will, on request, weave a small **token-cloth** of plain undyed cotton in Ohatsu's weave — a square the size of a man's palm, woven on the shuttle of one of the senior weavers of the temple and blessed at Ohatsu's table before being given. Craftsmen of the river-towns keep these token-cloths folded in their tool-chests; some keep them tied around the handle of a favored tool. The original shuttle does not leave its hemp cloth, for Ohatsu's labor is not borrowed — it is honored where it lies, by being remembered.
