A long-hafted smith's hammer, the head a single block of forge-blackened iron with a slight asymmetry to its striking face — the right side a touch wider than the left, from the way Katsuro's grip wore the steel over a working lifetime. The haft is oak, darkened with sweat and quenching-oil to the color of old tea. A short hempen cord is tied around the haft just below the head; into the cord is braided a single thread of red, the color of the apron-stripe of the Hida foundry guild. The hammer rests on a pair of iron pegs driven into the wall of a small forge-shrine adjoining the temple's main hall — a working forge with a banked fire kept always alight, and a low altar of unworked stone beside it where the local smiths leave their offerings.

Hida Katsuro was a smith of the southern foundries in the fourth century after the great Crab castle's founding. He served the Hida family for forty-one years and is credited in the foundry records with the forging of more than a thousand spear-heads, several hundred plowshares, and the iron banding of three of the great gates of that castle itself. The famous part of his story comes from his thirty-eighth year, when a minor Hida-vassal lord — whose name the temple does not record, for Ebisu does not steady the names of the dishonest — demanded that Katsuro forge a set of executioner's blades for the punishment of his own peasants over an unpaid tax he himself had set in bad weather. Katsuro refused, saying only that his hammer was Ebisu's and would not strike for cruelty. The lord threatened him; Katsuro continued his ordinary work. The lord was eventually overruled by his own father, the matter passed, and Katsuro went on forging plowshares and spear-heads for the Hida bushi until he laid the hammer down in his sixty-third year. He gave it to this Temple of Ebisu in the year of his retirement, and the smiths of the surrounding lands have come to honor it ever since.

On the first day of the seventh month, when the local foundries bank their fires for the brief monsoon rest, the Custodian of Sacred Relics performs the **Blessing of the Tools**. Smiths and carpenters and stonemasons of the city bring their working hammers, chisels, adzes, and saws to the forge-shrine. The Custodian lifts Katsuro's hammer from its pegs and lays its head once, gently, against the head of each tool brought, while the smith or craftsman states aloud the work the tool is intended for in the coming season. Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, accepts the tool into his sight when its purpose is honest, and a tool so blessed is held to ring true through the season's labor.

Some craftsmen are graced with the brief, clear ring of the iron when Katsuro's hammer touches their tool — an auspicious sign, taken to mean that Ebisu has set his hand upon the work to come. Some are graced with the warmth of the hammer-head against their palm when they bear their tool away, a sign that Katsuro himself has put his weight behind their season. Most experience nothing remarkable, and the blessing is no less for it; the work of the season will be its own witness to Ebisu's regard.

The Custodian may refuse to bless a tool. This is uncommon, but it happens — perhaps once in each Blessing — when the smith pauses too long in stating the tool's purpose, or when the purpose stated rings false in the Custodian's ear. The Custodian does not give a reason; he sets the hammer back on its pegs and asks the craftsman to return the following season. Ebisu does not steady the hand of the deceitful. The craftsman who is refused is not shamed, but is given to understand that he should reflect on what work he means to do.

A smith who has been commissioned for a single great work of unusual importance — the iron banding of a new gate, the forging of a daimyo's farm-blade, the hammers and chisels for the rebuilding of a damaged temple — may petition the Custodian for a private blessing outside the seasonal ceremony. The smith comes alone with his hammer (or with the first iron of the commission, unfinished), and the Custodian lifts Katsuro's hammer and touches it to the smith's, and the smith speaks his commission aloud. The blessing of an honest commission is given freely. The blessing of a doubtful commission is refused as politely as the seasonal refusals, and as without explanation.

Katsuro's hammer cannot be removed from the forge-shrine. The fire beside it has not gone out in three centuries, and the smiths of the temple keep it banked through every season. Replicas are not made; the hammer is not a tool to be carried, but a witness to which tools are brought. To take Katsuro's hammer from the shrine would be to ask Ebisu to bless work he has not seen, which is not Ebisu's way.
