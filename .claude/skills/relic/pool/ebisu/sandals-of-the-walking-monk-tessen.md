A pair of straw sandals — *waraji* — the kind that any peasant or traveling monk wears for a day or two and then replaces. These are not replaced. They sit side by side on a low cypress shelf at the entrance of the temple's traveler-hall, where pilgrims leave their own sandals before entering the inner rooms. The straw of the original weave is so worn down that the soles are barely a finger's thickness in places; the cord ties are frayed but knotted, and the rear loop of the left sandal has been mended once, in coarser straw than the original. Above the shelf hangs a small wooden plaque painted with the character 旋 (*sen*, "circuit, turning") — the second character of the walking monk's name.

Tessen was a mendicant of the Order of Ebisu who came to Shiro Mirumoto in the year of the present temple's rebuilding, four generations past. He did not present himself as a teacher and he never accepted the rank of Senior Monk, though he was offered it twice. Instead, with the Grand Abbot's blessing, he walked. Every morning at first light he set out from the temple along the eastern road that climbs through the Mirumoto foothills, visiting in turn the seven hamlets along the road as far as the watchtower at the *Three Pines Pass*, and every evening he returned by the western branch, visiting the four hamlets along the river. He carried a beggar's bowl and a small bundle, and what he was given by one hamlet he distributed in the next. He spoke with peasants in their fields, helped repair their tools when his hands were able, witnessed births, prayed at the bedsides of the dying, and walked. He walked the same circuit for forty-one years. When the soles of his sandals wore through, he wove himself new ones at the temple's straw-shed; when his hands grew too stiff to weave, the temple's novices wove them for him. He died on the road between the third and fourth hamlets of the eastern arm, in his sixty-ninth year, in the company of a peasant family who had set down their planting to walk the last hour with him. The peasants brought his body back to the temple, and the sandals he had been wearing were placed on the shelf at the traveler-hall entrance. They have not been moved.

The temple's circuit has been walked by a successor monk every day since. The Custodian of Sacred Relics — for the Order of Ebisu of Shiro Mirumoto, as at Kyuden Hida, keeps the title — appoints the *Walker of the Circuit* from among the monks who have completed their second year of vows. The appointment is a quiet honor; the Walker carries no special markings and earns no special rank from the work. Each morning the Walker bows at the shelf, dons his own ordinary sandals, takes up a beggar's bowl and a bundle, and sets out along Tessen's eastern road. He returns by Tessen's western branch in the evening, having visited the same eleven hamlets, having spoken with whatever peasants and travelers wish to be spoken with, having given out what was given to him in the hamlet before. Ebisu, Fortune of honest work, walks the circuit beside the Walker; the work itself is the offering, and the sandals on the shelf are the witness that the offering has been continuous.

A pilgrim arriving at the temple may leave a single coin, a handful of rice, a folded sleeve-cloth, or any small thing in the bowl that rests on the shelf below the sandals. The Walker of the day takes the offering into his bundle and gives it out along the circuit. Some pilgrims are graced, when they place the offering, with the faint smell of road-dust rising from the old straw — an auspicious omen, signifying that Tessen accepts the offering personally and that the gift will reach a particular hamlet where it is needed. Some are graced with the brief sound of footfalls on the cypress floor when no monk is near, a sign that Tessen walks beside the day's Walker. Most pilgrims experience nothing remarkable, and the offering is no less for it; Ebisu's care for the eleven hamlets does not depend on the pilgrim's sign.

A young monk of the Order who has begun to suspect that the labor of the road exceeds his patience may petition the Custodian to be permitted to kneel for a single afternoon at the shelf. The Custodian permits this without ceremony. The monk kneels, the sandals rest before him, and the monk considers the forty-one years. Some monks rise and ask to be appointed to the next available Walker's turn. Some monks rise and ask to be released from their vows, and the Custodian grants the release without rebuke — Ebisu's labor is not for every hand, and a monk who cannot walk the road honestly is better laboring elsewhere. The shelf is a teacher whose lesson is given without speech.

On the first morning after the autumn equinox, the Custodian performs the **Counting of the Hamlets**. He walks the circuit himself, in Tessen's manner, and at each of the eleven hamlets he asks the headsman whether the year's circuit has been faithful — whether the Walker has come daily, whether the gifts have arrived, whether any sickness or distress went unwitnessed. The headsmen of the eleven hamlets have answered yes in every count for four generations. This is the Counting's quiet glory, and the Custodian sets the year's answer down in the register beside the years before.

The sandals cannot be moved from the shelf. The shelf cannot be moved from the traveler-hall entrance. The straw-shed at the back of the temple weaves replacement *waraji* for each Walker as they wear out their own; the temple gives these out also to pilgrims who ask, blessed but otherwise unremarkable, and the pilgrims wear them home along whatever road is theirs. The original sandals do not walk again. They have walked enough.
