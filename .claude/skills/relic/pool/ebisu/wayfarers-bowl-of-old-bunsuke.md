name: The Wayfarer's Bowl of Old Bunsuke

japanese_kanji: 旅人の椀
fortune: ebisu
clan: any
temple: a temple of Ebisu, in any city of the Empire

A simple wood-turned rice bowl, unglazed and undecorated, the diameter of a man's palm. The exterior is the pale color of natural wood; the interior is darker, stained by forty years of rice. A tiny chip on the rim of the foot — exactly the size of a single grain of rice — marks the spot where Bunsuke's wife dropped the bowl on the day of their wedding. It rests on a low table at the temple's almsgiving station, beside the threshold where the daily charity meal is distributed.

Bunsuke was a peasant farmer in a village three generations past. He was a poor tenant farmer with three children, but for forty years every wandering monk, ronin, beggar, or pilgrim who passed through his village received a bowl of rice from his wife at this exact bowl. Bunsuke held that feeding the homeless and the religious was the highest duty of an Ebisu-blessed peasant — that the Fortune of honest work expected its faithful to share the fruits of that work. After he and his wife died (within the same week, in their late seventies), the bowl was found clean and dry on a shelf in their hut. The village headsman donated it to the temple, where the Custodian of that generation said only: "this is a great relic; place it where it can continue its work."

There is no formal ritual. A pilgrim or visitor may leave a single grain of rice in the bowl. Each evening, the *Shika* — the Knower of Guests, the Steward of Hospitality in the temple's archaic Sangō tradition — empties the bowl and uses the rice in the next morning's charity meal for the poor of the surrounding city. The bowl is a perpetual almsgiving circuit, mediating between pilgrims who wish to honor Ebisu and the poor of the city who need to eat. Bunsuke's spirit oversees the circuit personally — the grain a pilgrim leaves is the same grain, multiplied many times through the seasons, that fills the bowls of the city's hungry.

Some pilgrims are graced with finding the bowl warm to the touch when they leave their grain — an auspicious omen, signifying that Bunsuke has accepted the offering and that Ebisu's favor follows the pilgrim into the coming season. Some are graced with the smell of cooked rice in the alcove though no fire burns there, a sign that the grain has already been blessed for the meal it will become. Most pilgrims experience nothing remarkable, and the gift itself is its own blessing whether or not the pilgrim is granted further sign.

On the first day of each month, the Shika performs the **Counting of the Grains**, a ritual observation kept since the bowl came to the temple. He counts the grains placed in the bowl by pilgrims during the preceding day, then marks one grain with a single stroke of black ink. The bowl is left undisturbed overnight in the sealed alcove. At dawn the Shika returns and counts again, and seeks the marked grain. The records of three generations of Shikas show that sometimes the count is higher than the day's offerings, sometimes equal, sometimes lower; sometimes the marked grain is found, sometimes it has gone into the bowl's blessing and cannot be recovered, sometimes it appears the next month after the count had recorded it lost. Each pattern is interpreted in light of the season, the festivals nearby, and the alignment of the pilgrims of that day. Bunsuke's spirit has its own rhythms, and the Counting is a way of attending to them rather than testing them.

The bowl cannot be removed from the almsgiving station; the daily charity circuit depends on it, and the poor of three nearby wards have come to expect the meal. To destroy the bowl would break Bunsuke's continuing gift to the city's poor, which Ebisu would not forgive.
