---
name: The Sealed Letters of Bayushi Renjiro and Shosuro Aiko
japanese_romaji: Fuuin-no-Koibumi
japanese_kanji: 封印の恋文
fortune: benten
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Benten, Kyuden Bayushi (Bayushi family, Scorpion clan)
named_entity: Bayushi Renjiro and Shosuro Aiko, an estranged samurai pair of the late sixth century whose reconciliation was discovered only in their letters after both had died
relic_type: written material (bundled love letters, partly sealed)
---

A bundle of forty-one letters on undyed *washi* paper, tied with a single faded red cord, kept in a black lacquered box on a low table in a small reading-room off the temple's eastern courtyard. The box has no lock. Twenty-three of the letters in the bundle are unsealed, their folds opened and refolded so many times the paper has gone soft at the creases; eighteen remain sealed, the wax red and unbroken, the recipient's name written across each seal in a careful hand. The reading-room is small enough for six listeners and one reader, no more, and the paper screens of its windows are kept closed by day so that the only light is the lamp at the reader's elbow. A second lamp is kept burning in a niche beside the box; this lamp is never allowed to go out.

Bayushi Renjiro and Shosuro Aiko were married in the late sixth century by arrangement of their families — Renjiro a third son of a Bayushi vassal lineage, Aiko a magistrate's daughter of the Shosuro. The marriage went badly in its first year. Aiko was posted to a magistrate's office in the western provinces; Renjiro remained at Kyuden Bayushi in the household of his father-in-law. They quarreled before her departure and did not speak again face-to-face for the remaining nineteen years of their lives. They wrote — first formally, in the matter of household affairs, and then, slowly, as the years went by, in matters that were no longer formal. The forty-one letters in this bundle are the love letters that grew from that correspondence: not the household letters, not the duty letters, but the letters between two people who had married in anger and then, across a great distance, found that they had been writing themselves into love with one another.

They never met again. Aiko died of a fever in her fourteenth year of separation; Renjiro learned of her death by Imperial post and survived her by five years, during which he wrote the last eighteen letters in the bundle — letters addressed to her, sealed, never sent, kept in the lacquered box she had given him at their wedding. After Renjiro's death the box was opened by his nephew, who read enough to understand what he held, then closed the box and brought it to the temple. The temple took the box and the letters, refused the nephew's offer of payment, and made a place for them in the reading-room where they now rest. The eighteen sealed letters have never been opened. They will not be.

Three times each year — at the opening of spring, at the Festival of the Long Cord in autumn, and on the seventh day of the second winter month, which is the day Aiko's last letter was written — the Custodian of Sacred Relics convenes a small reading. A pilgrim, or a married couple, or a group of betrothed cousins together, or a courtier sent at the request of a daimyo whose marriage is in difficulty, may petition to attend. The Custodian draws a single unsealed letter from the bundle by touch, without looking, and reads it aloud. The reading is slow. The Custodian does not gloss the difficult passages, and does not explain who is writing or in what year. The listener is left to receive the letter as if it had been addressed to them.

Some who hear a letter find that it speaks to their own marriage with such precision that they cannot afterward remember whether they were the listener or the addressee. Some hear a letter about a quarrel they themselves have not had, and the Custodian asks them to consider whether the quarrel is one they have been preparing without knowing. Some are graced with a faint warmth in the red cord that ties the bundle when the letter is returned, the cord briefly the color of fresh blood rather than faded; this is Aiko's grace upon a marriage that may still be rescued. Some are graced with the second lamp in the niche flaring without draft as the letter is finished, the small fire reaching upward as if to take a breath, and this is Renjiro acknowledging that the lesson has been received. Some hear a letter that does not speak to them at all, and this is no failure — Benten has spoken in other languages on other days, and the pilgrim may return when the season is more apt.

The eighteen sealed letters are read only by Benten. The temple does not open them. The wax is unbroken on all of them, the seals dark with age, the names of the addressee written across the wax in Renjiro's hand. The Custodian holds the sealed letters during the reading-out of the unsealed ones, and may, if the listener's burden is great, place a sealed letter against the listener's forehead for the length of one breath. The listener does not learn what is written. The letter speaks past the wax in whatever language Benten chooses, and the listener carries away what was given without knowing what it was. Pilgrims who have received this gift describe a curious lightness afterward — they speak of a thing they had been carrying that they no longer carry, but cannot name what it was.

The letters are also brought out, twice in a generation perhaps, in cases of estrangement among the Scorpion lineages where a marriage has broken and a daimyo wishes to know whether reconciliation is possible. The Custodian of Sacred Relics travels with the bundle to the household in question under formal escort. A single letter is read aloud to the estranged pair in the presence of their families. The Custodian does not advise, and does not offer interpretation. The Scorpion are a clan that hears well; they are trusted to hear what they are given.

The letters cannot leave the box, and the box cannot leave the reading-room except for the household readings described above. The temple workshop produces *replica letters* on request — short copied passages from the unsealed correspondence, transcribed by senior monks and blessed at the table, taken home by Scorpion couples to be read aloud on the seventh day of each month at their family altars. The replicas are common in Scorpion households throughout the southern provinces. The originals are read only here, only by Benten's appointed voice, only when the season is right.

When the bundle is returned to the box, the red cord is retied by the Custodian's own hand. The cord has not been replaced in the four generations the temple has held the letters. Some have asked whether the cord will outlast the paper, and the temple's answer is that the cord will outlast whatever it must.
