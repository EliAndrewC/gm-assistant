---
name: The Basin of the Lapping Wave
japanese_romaji: Sazanami-no-Tarai
japanese_kanji: 漣の盥
fortune: benten
clan: mantis
temple: a temple of Benten in Mantis lands
named_entity: Benten herself, whose emergence from the spring at Onogawa is locally attested; and the peasant pair Toshimi and Heigo, the salt-makers whose forty-year marriage outlasted shipwreck and exile
relic_type: water-feature (stone basin fed by a sacred spring)
summary: "A stone basin of brackish water fed by a sacred spring; betrothed couples drink from it to receive Benten's witness, and magistrates use it to weigh marriage and dowry disputes."
---

A round basin of dark-gray stone, knee-high to a standing man and broad enough that a tall woman cannot reach her hands across its diameter without leaning. The stone is rough on its outer face and smooth-worn on its inner; the lip is grooved where the water trickles over a single low notch on the basin's eastern side and runs down a stone channel to the temple's lower garden. The basin sits at the center of an open court paved with gray beach-pebbles, beneath a wooden canopy on four cedar pillars; no walls enclose the court, and the wind from the harbor passes freely through it. The water in the basin is brackish - fed by a small spring beneath the temple foundations that mingles with the sea-water that seeps through the island's stone - and the surface is rarely still, even on windless days, for the spring beneath sends up a slow, irregular bubbling that disturbs the surface in patterns the eye cannot quite predict.

The spring beneath the basin is the spring of Onogawa, from which Benten is said to have emerged in the second age of the world, riding the back of a sea-dragon, and on the shore of which she first wept for the love of the mortal she could not keep. The spring was at the time on the mainland; the island of Kaimetsu Toshi rose around it in a great upheaval of the sea in the fourth century before the Empire, and the Mantis fishing-villages that became the city were settled around the spring as their first holy site. The basin was set above the spring by the first Yoritomo who took the city, perhaps eight hundred years past, and the temple was built around the basin a generation later. The basin has never been moved. The water has never run dry, not in the worst summers of the Mantis lands' memory, and has never overflowed its notch, not in the worst typhoon seasons either.

The basin is also a memorial to Toshimi and Heigo, salt-makers of a small fishing village on the eastern shore of the island, who lived three centuries past. Toshimi was the daughter of a salt-maker; Heigo was a fisherman who took up salt-making after marrying her. In the seventh year of their marriage Heigo was lost at sea in a sudden storm and presumed drowned; Toshimi raised their two children alone for eleven years, mourning him each spring at this basin. In the eighteenth year of the marriage Heigo returned - he had been carried by storm to the southern islands, taken as a slave by gaijin traders, and made his way home over the course of more than a decade through means he could never afterward fully recount. The temple records the day of his return as the day Benten's grace was made most visible to the village: Toshimi met him at the harbor and brought him to the basin and washed his hands and feet in its water before taking him home. They lived together another twenty-two years, and were buried in the temple's lower garden where the basin's water runs down into a small pool above their graves.

A betrothed couple may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to be received at the basin. The Custodian leads them to the court at the hour of the rising tide - the time is determined by the temple's tide-tables, posted at the gate - and asks each of them to dip a single hand into the basin and bring up water to their lips. They drink. The Custodian does not speak during the dipping. The water tastes of salt and stone, and is not pleasant to the unprepared mouth.

Some couples are graced, on tasting the water, with the surface of the basin briefly stilling beneath the spring's bubbling - a flat circle of calm appearing for the length of a breath, then breaking again into ripples. This is Benten's nod: the marriage is in her water and will keep. Some are graced with the water tasting sweet rather than salt - the spring's mineral signing to the couple that the bond is the bond of fresh water and not of salt, a long bond rather than a short bond, a bond for many seasons. Some couples find the water bitter or simply briny, and this is no failure - Benten is not in the habit of remaking what she has already blessed, and the bond will keep on its own merits. The Custodian does not interpret the taste at length; the couple is given a cloth to wipe their mouths, a small token of pebble from the court to carry away in their sleeve, and a brief blessing.

The basin is also the relic to which marriage and dowry disputes are brought in Kaimetsu Toshi. The temple is by long custom a referee for such disputes, called in by the city's magistrates when a marriage contract has gone wrong - the dowry partly delivered and partly withheld, the bride's family alleging mistreatment, the groom's family alleging deception, a betrothal broken and the question of which family must bear the loss. The Custodian of Sacred Relics receives the parties at the basin in the presence of the magistrate. Each party speaks their case across the basin, the water between them. They are then asked to each dip a hand and drink, as the betrothed do.

The dispute is not decided by the drinking. The decision is the magistrate's. But the temple watches closely how each party handles the water - whether they drink without grimace, whether their hand trembles at the lip of the basin, whether they look down into the water as they drink or away from it, whether the surface of the basin reacts to their dipping with calm or with sudden agitation. These signs are reported to the magistrate and weighed alongside the documents, witnesses, and reputations the magistrate has gathered. Benten, the Custodian will tell the magistrate, knows what each party has done; she will not always tell the temple what she knows, but she will tell it as she chooses, and the magistrate is welcome to listen. The Yoritomo and Yasuki merchant houses send their marriage-contract disputes to this court regularly. The judgments stick.

Some parties - those whose case is clean - drink the water and find it sweet, and this is a sign read by the temple in their favor. Some parties find the water so bitter they cannot finish the dip without coughing, and this is a sign too. Some find the water unremarkable, and the temple takes from this only that Benten has chosen to be silent on the matter and that the magistrate must work the harder. The basin's signals are layered with the magistrate's own observation, the merchant ledgers, the testimony of go-betweens; the Custodian does not pretend the basin renders verdicts, and no verdict in living memory has rested on the basin alone.

The basin is also visited each spring on the seventh day after the equinox by the salt-makers of the island, who come in procession with small wooden boxes of new-season salt and pour a measure of salt into the basin in Toshimi's name. The water of the basin is then dipped out by the salt-makers in turn and used to wet the first rakings of the new salt-pans for the year. The temple does not record the yield each year, but the salt-makers do, and the yield in years when the basin's salt-blessing has been given on a day of unusual stillness is said by them to exceed the yield of years when the spring was restless.

The water cannot be removed from the basin in bulk; the spring will not give what is not asked of it properly. The temple workshop produces small clay bottles into which a single dipping of the water is poured, sealed with wax, and blessed; these bottles are carried by sailors of the Mantis fleet, by traders sailing from Kaimetsu Toshi to the mainland, and by couples about to be parted by long sea voyage. The wax is not to be broken until the voyager returns home and presents the bottle to their spouse at their own threshold. The bottles that have been broken before such a return are kept by the Custodian in a small chest beneath the basin's canopy; there are perhaps thirty of them, and the temple does not say what became of those who broke them.

The basin cannot be moved. The Yoritomo daimyo of three generations past, in a season of pride, proposed that the basin be relocated to a more impressive court on higher ground in the new wing of the temple. The temple replied, formally and politely, that the spring beneath the basin had been in its place since the second age of the world and that the temple had been built where it was for the spring's sake, not the spring placed where it was for the temple's sake. The proposal was withdrawn. The basin sits where it has always sat.
