temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Benten, Kyuden Kakita (Kakita family, Crane clan)

relic_type: implement (set of three lacquered wedding cups, nested)
---

Three lacquered cups of *urushi* over thin cedar, nested one inside the other, smallest at the top and largest at the base. Each cup is finished in deep red lacquer on the outside and pale gold on the inside; each is rimmed in fine silver. The smallest cup is the diameter of a child's palm, the middle cup the diameter of a woman's palm, the largest the diameter of a tall man's palm. On the gold interior of each cup, painted in the finest brushwork, is a single flying crane — the smallest cup's crane in flight rising, the middle cup's crane in flight level, the largest cup's crane in flight descending. The set rests on a low table of cherrywood in a side chamber off the main hall. A small ewer of unglazed pottery sits beside the cups; it holds the rice-wine used in the ritual when the cups are taken into service.

Kakita Hokuto was a ceremonialist of the Kakita family in the fourth century, the year-records do not name his parents but record him as the third of three brothers, all of whom served the family in court ritual. Hokuto codified the *san-san-kudo* — the three-times-three exchange of cups that is at the heart of every Crane wedding to this day. He did not invent the rite; the threefold exchange existed before him in older and looser forms. Hokuto's gift was to fix it: three cups, three sips from each cup, three exchanges between bride and groom, the order of sips and exchanges precisely arranged so that the rite would carry the bond from heaven to earth to heaven again in a single closed circuit. He served four Crane daimyo as their wedding-master across forty-six years, performed the cup-ritual at more than a thousand weddings, and presented these cups — the cups he had carried in his sleeve to every wedding of his career — to the Temple of Benten in Kyuden Kakita in his sixty-third year, three years before his death.

The cups have been used in Crane weddings since. They are not the only cups used; most weddings use cups commissioned for the occasion from the lacquer workshops of the lower city. The Three Cups of Kakita Hokuto are reserved for weddings of unusual significance — the wedding of the Doji daimyo's child, the wedding of a Kakita daimyo, the wedding of an Imperial cousin to a Crane, the wedding that closes a great peace between Crane and another clan. The Custodian of Sacred Relics carries the cups in a black-lacquered carrying case under formal escort to the wedding, performs the *san-san-kudo* with the bride and groom himself or hands the cups to the officiating priest of the family with appropriate ceremony, and returns the cups to the temple the same evening. They have been carried out one hundred and twelve times since Hokuto's gift; the temple has the record of each occasion in its ceremonial ledgers, with the names of the couples and the date of every wedding.

In the season between the first warm rain of spring and the equinox — which is the start of the Crane wedding season — the cups are also brought out for the **Blessing of the Engaged**. On the first dragon-day of that season the Custodian opens the case in the temple's main hall, sets the cups on a low table beneath the hall's central beam, and pours wine into the largest cup. Betrothed couples come to the hall in turn through the day, kneel before the table, and each sips three times from the largest cup — bride first, then groom, then bride again — before bowing and giving way to the next couple. The wine is not refilled between couples; the same wine is sipped through the day by every betrothed pair in attendance, perhaps two hundred couples in a busy spring. The cup is wiped between sippers with a square of white silk. The other two cups are not used in this rite; they sit beside the largest, lids off, witnesses to the day.

Some couples find, after their three sips from Hokuto's largest cup, that small habits of speech between them ease. They begin to anticipate one another's silences more skillfully than they had before; they find themselves laughing more often at the same small things; they find, when the marriage is concluded later that season, that the first months of it are gentler than they had been warned to expect. Benten settles into the household with the wine she has tasted, and there is much that is easier in such houses for some years afterward. Some are graced with feeling the lacquer of the cup warm against their lip as they sip — Hokuto's hand, the temple says, warming the cup that he carried so long. Some are graced with seeing the painted crane on the cup's interior seem briefly to turn its head toward them as the wine recedes. Many feel nothing in particular and only sip a fine wine from a fine cup, and this too is a blessing — Benten is content to be quiet, and Hokuto's rite carries her blessing as fully whether or not the cup performs theatrically.

When the cups are used at a wedding, the threefold exchange is performed exactly as Hokuto recorded it in his ceremonial papers, which are kept in the temple library and consulted before each carrying-out. The order of sips, the angle at which the cup is presented, the silk that lines the carrying case, the pattern of the ribbon that ties the case: all of these are reproduced as Hokuto left them. The temple does not deviate. Couples who request alterations to the rite for their own reasons — and such requests are made, especially among the modern Doji — receive replicas commissioned from the workshop. The replicas are good, and the wedding is honored, but the Three Cups are not bent to the convenience of any wedding.

The cups have been damaged once. In the fourth generation after Hokuto's death, the largest cup was dropped by a junior Custodian during a wedding at Kyuden Doji and chipped at its rim. The Custodian was reassigned to the temple library; the cup was repaired by the lacquer workshop with red urushi and fine gold dust along the line of the chip, and the repair is now a faint gold streak along the cup's rim, visible in lamplight if one looks for it. The temple has come to consider the gold streak a part of the cup, and a quiet teaching: that a marriage is not destroyed by a chip, and that a careful repair becomes part of the beauty of the long bond.

The cups cannot leave the temple except for the formal weddings described above. Replica sets are commissioned from the workshop in some numbers each year, and Crane lineages of any standing keep a set of *san-san-kudo* cups in their household altar; the family wedding-master uses these cups at the wedding of each child, and the cups pass down in the lineage. The originals are not the model for these replicas in any meaningful sense — they are the original from which every Crane wedding for nine centuries has descended in unbroken succession, and they sit in the side chamber as the still center of that descent.
