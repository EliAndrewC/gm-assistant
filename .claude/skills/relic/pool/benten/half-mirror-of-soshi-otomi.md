A bronze hand-mirror, broken cleanly down its middle along an old fault in the casting, the two halves now joined again and held together by a fine collar of silver wirework that runs around the rim and across the break. The mirrored face is dim with age and shows a clouded image rather than a clear one; the back is incised with a pattern of two cranes flying in opposite directions around a sprig of plum-blossom. The handle is wrapped in dark silk. The mirror rests on a black silk cushion in a shallow tray of polished river-pebbles, in a small chamber at the back of the temple beside the wall that faces the city's eastern river. A single candle burns in a brass holder above the tray. The candle is not relit when it goes out — a new candle is set each evening at the seventh bell, and the chamber takes its light from whichever candle is presently burning.

Soshi Otomi was the wife of Bayushi Tadatsugu, a Scorpion magistrate of the early seventh century, and was widowed in her twenty-ninth year when Tadatsugu was killed on circuit in the western provinces. Before he left on what would be his last journey, the two of them broke their wedding mirror in the manner of the old Scorpion custom — each took one half, the husband to carry on his travels and the wife to keep at her writing-desk, and the halves were to be rejoined at every reunion. Tadatsugu's half was returned to her with his other effects after his death. She did not rejoin the halves. She kept them apart on her desk, one before her and one behind her, for forty years; she did not remarry; she kept Tadatsugu's name and his household as she had received it; she took the tonsure of the Soshi shugenja and entered the temple of Benten at Ryoko Owari in her sixty-eighth year. On the night of her death in her seventy-first year she asked her attending novice to bring her both halves of the mirror. The novice fetched them. Otomi joined them, edge to edge, on the cushion beneath her hand, and died with the mirror whole.

The silver collar was added by the temple workshop the year after her death, to hold the join Otomi had made. The temple has never separated the halves again.

A pilgrim recently widowed, or long-separated from a spouse by exile, or estranged by a quarrel they wish to repair, or a couple who must be parted for years by Imperial posting, may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to be received at the chamber. The Custodian leads the pilgrim into the chamber, asks them to kneel beside the tray, and recites a short prayer to Benten. The Custodian then touches the silver collar at the seam of the mirror with one finger and asks the pilgrim to look into the mirror's face. The image is clouded; what the pilgrim sees there is what Benten wishes them to see.

The recently widowed are graced, in some cases, with a glimpse of the absent spouse looking back at them — not the spouse as they were at the moment of death, but the spouse as they were in some private moment of the marriage, a turning of the head or a half-smile across the breakfast table. The image is gone in an instant. The pilgrim is not asked to describe what they saw, and the Custodian does not record what they say if they choose to speak. Some pilgrims see no image at all in the mirror, and the Custodian counsels them that Benten is not done with the marriage, that the absent spouse is still close, and that the pilgrim is not yet a widow in the sense the temple uses the word.

The long-separated are graced with a different gift. A samurai whose spouse is posted to the far provinces, who has not seen them in years, may be granted in the clouded face a momentary clearing — the mirror going briefly bright, the brass shining as if newly polished. This is Benten signaling that the spouse, in that moment, has thought of the pilgrim; the pilgrim is to write to their spouse that night, knowing the letter will be welcome. Records of these signs are kept in the temple ledger, and on more than one occasion the husband and the wife have written to one another on the same night across a thousand *ri*, the letters crossing in the post.

The estranged are graced, sometimes, with finding their own face in the mirror split along the silver collar — half on one side of the seam, half on the other, the join of the silver running through their reflected forehead. This is a hard grace. It tells the pilgrim that the quarrel is not in the spouse but in themselves, that they have been carrying the broken mirror as Otomi did, and that the repair is theirs to make. The Custodian sits with such pilgrims at length afterward.

Some pilgrims see only their own face in the clouded mirror, no clearing, no doubling, no glimpse — and this is no failure. Benten has been content to look. The marriage is in her hand whether or not she shows it.

The mirror cannot be separated. The temple workshop has, on rare occasions, commissioned bronze hand-mirrors broken at the time of casting for couples about to be parted by long Imperial duty; these are sold to families who wish to keep the old Scorpion custom Otomi kept, and a senior monk blesses each pair at the chamber before they are taken away. The replicas are common enough in the Shosuro and Bayushi households of Ryoko Owari. The original is not one of them. It has been whole now for over a century, and will remain whole.

When the city of Ryoko Owari is troubled — when a great fire passes through the eastern wards, when the river floods the temple's lower steps, when the Shosuro daimyo or the Bayushi households suffer some severe and unexpected sorrow — the candle above the tray is said to gutter without draft, and the silver collar around the mirror's seam to dim. The Custodian inspects the chamber at the seventh bell every evening for this reason. In the four decades since the silver was set, the candle has guttered six times. Each time, within a season, the city has been visited by a sorrow of marriages — a great household broken, a notable widowing, a chain of failed betrothals among the ruling lineages. Otomi watches over Ryoko Owari's marriages from her side of the silver, and the temple takes her watch as a kindness, even when what she shows them is a sorrow on the way.
