---
name: The Marriage Stones of Hokke Tomotsugu and Sayuri
japanese_romaji: Chigiri-no-Ishi
japanese_kanji: 契りの石
fortune: benten
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Benten, Shinden Kitsune (Fox clan)
named_entity: Hokke Tomotsugu (Fox samurai of the ruling lineage) and Sayuri (peasant woman), seven generations past
relic_type: pair (river stones in lacquered case)
---

Two smooth river stones, each the size of a small peach, one slightly darker than the other. They rest in a small lacquered case lined with pale silk. The case is open by day and closed by night, displayed on a low table beside a stone water-basin in a small garden alcove off the temple's main hall — water is Benten's element, and the alcove is built around a slow trickle of stream-water that runs through a copper trough into the basin.

The stones were taken from a stream in the upper reaches of Kitsune Mori, where Hokke Tomotsugu and Sayuri first spoke to one another some seven generations past. Tomotsugu was a son of the Hokke lineage's ruling house, sent into the forest in his eighteenth year for meditation. Sayuri was a peasant woman of a hamlet near the stream, who came each morning to do her village's laundry on a flat stone there. They met by accident, spoke awkwardly, and then arranged to keep meeting — for three years, with the patience of those who know their love is not going to be permitted.

When they wished to marry, the Hokke daimyo refused the match, as both knew he would; Sayuri's village would not have honored an unblessed union, and the Hokke would not have honored a peasant daughter-in-law. The couple eloped to the Temple of Benten in Shinden Kitsune, which had even then a reputation as a sanctuary for marriages the families would not bless. They each took a stone from their stream, walked the three-day pilgrimage in silence, exchanged the stones at the temple as wedding gifts, and were married privately by a Benten priest. They lived as outcasts for two years before Sayuri bore Tomotsugu a son. The Hokke daimyo, by then older and quieter, accepted the boy, and through him the marriage. The couple lived to old age together. After both had died — Tomotsugu first, in his eighty-fourth year, and Sayuri eleven years later — the stones were brought to the temple by their grandchildren and donated.

A betrothed couple, or a long-married couple in difficulty, or two people who acknowledge an affection that cannot become a marriage, may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics to hold the stones. The Custodian opens the lacquered case in their presence and asks each to take a stone in their cupped hand — the darker stone is given to the elder of the two by tradition, but no one minds if the giving is reversed. The pilgrims speak the words of their bond aloud, or, if they cannot, speak silently with only Benten as their witness. They then exchange the stones, each placing their stone into the cupped hand of the other. The Custodian returns the stones to the case after a moment of stillness. The whole ceremony takes less than the time of a long breath, and the pilgrims leave by the garden gate rather than the way they entered.

Some pairs of pilgrims are graced with the stone they receive being warm to the touch — Benten's blessing on the bond they have spoken. Some are graced with the stone bearing a faint impression of the other's grip when it is returned to them, a singular omen of two becoming one. Some find the stones merely smooth and cool, no different from any other river stone, and this is no failure — Benten is content to witness without ornament, and a quiet ceremony is still a ceremony. Some are graced with an unease while holding their stone, a chill or a heaviness — the Custodian receives the stones back gently in this case, and afterward asks the pilgrims whether they have spoken their bond fully and truly. The conversation is not a judgment; it is a kindness.

The stones cannot be replicated. The temple workshop does not produce replicas, for Benten's blessing in this relic is the singular pair and not the class of paired stones. Couples who marry at the temple may, however, carry away a small carved wooden token from the workshop, into which a senior monk has written the date of the marriage and a benediction; these tokens are common in the wardrobe-chests and small altars of married couples throughout the Fox lands.

The stones are very rarely allowed to travel. When two parties of unusual circumstance are to be joined — a marriage between two former enemies whose families have agreed to peace, a reunion of two estranged Fox lineages through a child, the reconciliation of a couple long separated by a shameful event — the Custodian of Sacred Relics may accompany the stones to the wedding under formal escort and bring them home the same evening. This is rare. In the past century the stones have left the temple in this way only nine times. They have not failed to return.
