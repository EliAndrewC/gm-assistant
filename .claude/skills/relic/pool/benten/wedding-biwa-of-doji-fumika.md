A four-stringed *biwa* of paulownia wood, its body the shape of a teardrop and the color of pale honey, with a narrow neck of darker rosewood and a tortoiseshell plectrum-rest worn smooth by use. The five frets are tied in silk cord that has been replaced perhaps twenty times across the centuries, always by the same workshop in the lower city. The strings are silk, and are restrung at the beginning of each spring at the Mending Ceremony — the old strings are not discarded but kept in a small lacquered box beneath the display, for any one of them may have been the string that sounded a particular blessing in a particular wedding. The biwa rests upright on a low stand of cherry wood, behind a sheer silk curtain, in an alcove off the main hall of the temple. A shallow basin of water set into the stone floor before the alcove catches the candle-light and throws it gently up against the curtain.

Doji Fumika was a court composer in the early seventh century, the third daughter of a minor Doji lineage and a student of the Kakita musical academy. She composed for the wedding of the Doji daimyo's eldest daughter when she was twenty-three, and the songs she wrote for that wedding so pleased the assembled courts of both families that they were performed at the next great Crane wedding, and the next, until they became the marriage-songs which are still sung at Crane weddings throughout the Empire. Fumika herself never married. She lived sixty-one years, composed perhaps ninety songs of which thirty survive, and on the day of her death asked that her biwa be given to the temple of Benten — for Benten, she said, had given her every song, and the biwa belonged in Benten's house.

The biwa is played twice a year — at the Festival of First Promise in early spring, when betrothals are formally announced in the Crane courts, and at the Festival of the Long Cord in autumn, when long-married couples renew their bonds before Benten. A senior monk of the temple, chosen for her skill on the instrument as much as for her standing in the order, draws the biwa from behind the silk curtain and plays one of Fumika's wedding-songs through from beginning to end. The Custodian of Sacred Relics waits beside her, takes the biwa from her at the song's end, and returns it to its stand. The played string is then replaced before the next festival; the song is not played twice from the same string.

The biwa is also carried in procession at the weddings of the ruling Doji house, and at certain other Crane weddings of unusual import — the marriage of a Doji to a Kakita that joins two lineages, the marriage of a Crane daimyo's child to a Scorpion peace-bride, the marriage of a widowed Crane lord to a second wife who brings reconciliation between estranged branches. The Custodian of Sacred Relics travels with the biwa under formal escort, plays the chosen song at the wedding feast, and returns the biwa to the temple the same night. The biwa has been carried out of the temple in this way forty-seven times since Fumika's death.

Some couples are graced, at their wedding or at the festivals, with a particular sweetness in the played note — a clarity that catches in the breath of those listening, that sets the older guests weeping for the weddings of their own youth. This is Benten's voice running beneath Fumika's, and it falls on the couple as a blessing on the long years they will share. Some are graced with finding, in the days after the song, that small disagreements between them resolve more easily than they expected, that they speak more freely to one another, that they remember more often why they first agreed to marry. Some hear only a fine biwa well-played, and this is no failure — Benten is content to bless quietly, and many of the longest marriages in the Crane lands began in quiet.

When the biwa is to be played, the senior monk fasts for the preceding day and bathes in the temple's south spring at dawn. She does not speak from the time she rises until the first note of the song. She does not play the biwa at any other time, and she does not teach others to play it — when she grows old, the temple selects a successor from among the senior monks and the Kakita academy is consulted on her musical training, but the successor does not touch this biwa until the previous player has died or formally laid the office down. The biwa cannot be played by an unwed monk who has known no love; this is not a rule but a practical matter, for Fumika's songs were written from the heart of one who had loved without marrying, and they ask of their player the same depth. The temple has on three occasions left the biwa unplayed for a festival because no monk in the order was prepared to play it — a quiet sorrow each time, but a more reverent one than playing it ill.

The biwa cannot leave the temple except for the processional weddings described above. The replicas commissioned from the workshop — biwas of paulownia and rosewood, made to Fumika's pattern, blessed at the alcove before being sent out — are taken by Crane lineages and by Kakita musical houses, and Fumika's songs are taught from these replicas. The original is a witness to those songs, not their source.
