name: The Staff and Scroll of High Monk Jion
japanese_romaji: Jion-no-Tsue
japanese_kanji: 慈恩の杖
fortune: fukurokujin
temple_suggestion: sovereign Temple of Fukurokujin, Kyuden Kitsune (Fox clan)
named_entity: High Monk Jion (Fox monk three centuries past, famed for road-mercy)
relic_type: implement (staff with attached scroll)
---

A walking staff and an attached scroll, mounted upright against the back of an old wooden chair in a side chamber of the temple, beside a small altar where seven candles are kept lit at all times.

The staff is cut from the heart of a cedar that grew in the deep wood of Kitsune Mori, near the spring where the Vermillion Threshold Stones are first marked each spring. The wood is dark with age and the oil of generations of hands. Its top is bound with hempen cord around an iron ring, from which depends a short cord and a brass loop. Through the brass loop is tied the scroll — a long roll of rice-paper, blackened along its edges, the calligraphy within in Jion's own careful hand.

The chair against which the staff rests is small and plain, the size that an old man would sit in. The cushion is replaced each spring at the time of the Mending Ceremony; the rest of the chair has not been touched since Jion himself sat in it.

The scroll contains High Monk Jion's writings on mercy — meditations he composed during the forty years he served as High Monk of this temple. The writings are not theological treatises but observations: cases he had heard, judgments he had made, refusals he had given, doubts he had carried, and the wisdom Fukurokujin had granted him by the end of each. Jion was famous in his own age for walking the Imperial Roads through Kitsune Mori with his staff and granting clemency to bandits who had repented and to travelers wrongly marked by the wood's wardings. The Kitsune family of his generation did not always agree with his judgments, but they did not contest them; even the most exacting of the road-rangers acknowledged that mercy and justice are both Fortunes' gifts, and Fukurokujin's hand is upon mercy.

A pilgrim who has been marked by the forest — whose footprints have begun to show as fox-paw, who hears barking at night, who has dreamed of fur — may petition the Shika to be received at the chamber of the Staff. So too may a pilgrim who has not been marked but knows themselves to have done wrong, and seeks to understand whether mercy is available to them.

The Custodian of Sacred Relics — for the Order of Fukurokujin keeps that title as the senior officer beneath the High Monk — receives the pilgrim, kneels with them before the staff, and asks them to speak their burden in three sentences. The pilgrim speaks. The Custodian then unrolls a portion of the scroll and reads aloud the passage that lies in front of them. The portion is not chosen; the scroll is unrolled to whatever length the pilgrim's hand has indicated when they touched the brass loop, and the passage that lies before the reader's eyes is the passage given.

Some pilgrims hear a passage that addresses their burden directly, in words so apt they cannot speak for some moments afterward, and the Custodian then waits with them in silence. Some hear a passage about a case quite unlike their own, and the Custodian asks them to consider how it might apply — Fukurokujin's wisdom is sometimes given as a riddle. Some hear a passage that seems to refuse them, and the Custodian sends them home with the counsel that they should return when their understanding has deepened. Some are graced, in addition, with a faint trembling in the staff against the chair, an auspicious omen that Jion himself has heard the burden and judges it worthy of his successors' attention.

The mercy of Fukurokujin is not a release from consequence. A bandit who comes to the chamber with sincere repentance may receive a passage that points him to the act of restitution he must perform — to seek out those he wronged, to labor for them, to surrender himself to the County Magistrate. The forest's wardings, the Kitsune family's instruments, do not lift on their own. But a pilgrim who has performed the act the scroll required of him is, after some seasons have passed, permitted to walk the Imperial Road again — and the rangers, in case after case, have found that his prints are his own.

The staff and scroll cannot leave the temple. The Kitsune family of the present generation, with whom the Order has at times disagreed, has never asked for the staff to travel; what could be said by the staff in motion is said better by Fukurokujin's monks remaining in place, with pilgrims coming to them. The temple's workshop produces small *replica scrolls* for those who wish to take a portion of Jion's wisdom home — excerpts copied by senior monks and blessed at the chair before being sent out, taken home and kept on writing-desks, or carried in sleeve-pockets, or read aloud at family altars. The original scroll does not unroll for itself, and does not leave its loop.
