A heavy book bound in dark calfskin, the size of a man's two hands placed side by side, its pages of thick rice-paper inked in a careful but unornamented hand. The cover is unmarked except for a single character — 温 (*on*, "warmth" or "tenderness") — pressed into the leather with a hot iron. The book rests open on a slanted reading-stand of pale wood in the temple's magistrate-petition hall, beside a low cushion where the petitioner kneels. A small brass paperweight, cast in the shape of a sleeping fawn, holds the open pages flat. The page is turned once each morning by the Custodian of Sacred Relics, in order; the book is read through completely once each year, beginning at the spring equinox.

Shinjo Haruna was a provincial judge of the Unicorn lands in the fourth century, in the time when the lineages were still settling the disputes that had arisen from the Return. She was called the Bending Judge by those who came before her, because she was known to find the place where the strict reading of the law would have produced an outcome that no decent person could uphold, and there to bend. She bent only at that place, and not before it; her rulings on clear cases were as exact as any judge's. But she had a particular gift for the case where the letter of the law and the spirit of mercy pulled in opposite directions, and for finding the ruling that honored both. She wrote each of her significant judgments into this book over the forty-one years of her service, in her own hand, with a brief summary of the case, the strict reading, her ruling, and — most importantly — the reasoning by which she had arrived at the bending. There are three hundred and seventeen entries. When she died at seventy-three, the book was given to the Temple of Fukurokujin, which had blessed her appointment in her youth.

A magistrate facing a case in which the law's strict application would produce a result that troubles his conscience may petition the Custodian for a consultation. The petitioner kneels at the reading-stand and speaks the shape of his case aloud in three or four sentences: the facts, the strict reading, his hesitation. The Custodian then turns the brass fawn aside and turns the book to a fresh page — not chosen, but the page where the morning's turning has brought the reader, or, if the petition comes in the afternoon, the page the morning had just opened. The petitioner reads the entry that lies before him, slowly, twice if he wishes.

Fukurokujin's wisdom and mercy come together in Haruna's hand. Some petitioners find an entry that addresses a case so closely resembling their own that the path of bending is plain to them, and they leave the hall already at peace with the ruling they will write. Some find an entry quite different from their case in its facts, and the Custodian asks them to consider what Haruna's reasoning, not her ruling, suggests for the matter at hand — for the book is consulted for *how to think*, not for what to decide. Some find an entry in which Haruna refused to bend, and these petitioners are often the ones who go home and uphold the strict reading, having recognized that their case is not one where mercy is owed. Some find no clear guidance, and the Custodian sends them home with the counsel that they must rule by their own discernment, and that Fukurokujin grants wisdom to those who do not lean too heavily on the wisdom of others.

The book also serves a second use, observed by the Custodians of three centuries. A magistrate who has already issued a ruling, and who has been troubled afterward by doubts as to whether he ruled justly, may petition for a *retrospective consultation*. He kneels and reads the day's entry without speaking his case first. Some are graced with an entry that vindicates them in their own conscience, and they leave their doubts at the reading-stand. Some are graced with an entry that gently rebukes them, and these petitioners often arrange, in private, some quiet act of restitution to the wronged party — a gift, a recommendation, a delayed favor — that cannot undo the ruling but softens it. The book does not record what was read or what followed. The Custodian, who sees the rulings come and go but does not know what the petitioner has read in any given session, does not comment.

The book is consulted by the County Magistrate of Shinjo Province in advance of any capital case. By long custom the magistrate's first ruling on a young magistrate's first capital case is also read aloud before the book, with the young magistrate seated at the cushion — Haruna having herself written that no judge should pass sentence of death before the age of thirty-three without the counsel of an older voice, and that her own book might serve as that voice when no older judge was within riding distance.

The book is not allowed to leave the hall. The Custodian, who is by tradition a former magistrate herself, will accompany a senior judge to a particularly grave trial elsewhere in Shinjo lands once or twice in a generation, carrying instead a *traveling volume* — a slim companion book containing fifteen of Haruna's most consulted entries, copied by senior monks, bound in calf, and blessed at the reading-stand before it leaves. The original book has been read through ninety-six times by the temple's reckoning. The leather is supple, the pages unworn at their edges, and the ink as dark as the day Haruna laid down her brush. Fukurokujin's hand is upon the book, and the book is upon the magistrates of the Shinjo, and the magistrates are upon the people, and the people, in their many small mercies, are upon Fukurokujin.
