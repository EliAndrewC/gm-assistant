A bound manuscript of sixty-three pages, sewn in the simple Phoenix fashion with pale blue thread, the cover plain dark cloth. The interior pages are filled in a fluent, hurried hand with the dense calligraphy of a scholar writing rapidly toward a conclusion he can see and is racing to reach. The seventeenth page from the back is the last written page. The remaining pages — sixteen of them — are blank. The manuscript rests in a shallow open box of pale wood on a low table in a side chamber off the temple's library, beside a small inkstone and a single brush that have not been used in three centuries. The brush is dry; the inkstone is dry.

Shiba Mochizuke was a Phoenix Master of the eighth century, one of the senior advisors to the Shiba daimyo of his generation, and the holder of the lineage's traditional seat on the question of mercy in famine. In the third year of the long famine that struck the western Phoenix provinces, the daimyo asked his Masters to rule on whether the lineage's grain reserves — held against the threat of war with the Lion — should be opened to the starving peasantry. Mochizuke had been arguing for opening the reserves for two years, against the other Masters, who held that the reserves were the lineage's protection and could not be touched. The daimyo, finally, ruled with Mochizuke. The reserves were opened. The peasants were fed. And in the next spring, no war came, and the famine eased, and the lineage was praised throughout the Phoenix lands for the wisdom of its mercy.

Mochizuke renounced his seat that summer. He left the daimyo's court, came to this Temple of Fukurokujin, and asked to be received as an ordinary monk. The temple received him. The reason he gave, in the only public statement of his renunciation, was that he had been right to argue for the opening of the reserves, but that he had been right for the wrong reason — that he had argued from compassion for the peasants, but that his deeper motive had been the desire to prove the other Masters wrong, and that this motive, undetected by him for two years, had nearly led him to argue too vehemently to be heard. *"I won the ruling,"* he wrote in the only line of his renunciation that has been preserved, *"and I do not know whether I was the right person to win it."*

He spent the remaining nineteen years of his life as a monk of the temple. In his last decade he began this manuscript — a treatise, never titled, on the difference between a mercy that arises from the love of others and a mercy that arises from the love of being seen as merciful. He wrote in spurts, between his daily duties, and the manuscript grew. He intended, by all accounts of his attendants, to finish it. He died one evening in his sleep with the manuscript open on the table beside him and the brush still wet. The attendant who found him in the morning recorded only that the brush was on the inkstone where Mochizuke had laid it down, and that the page open before him was the seventeenth from the back. The temple's senior monks of that generation conferred, and decided that the treatise should remain as it was: unfinished, with the inkstone and brush as Mochizuke had left them.

A samurai who has issued an order he has come to regret — an officer whose command sent men into a place from which they did not return, a magistrate who has passed a sentence he has begun to doubt, an administrator whose policy has hurt those it was meant to help, a Master of any school who has won an argument and cannot afterward shake the suspicion that he won it for the wrong reasons — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for an *evening with the treatise*. The Custodian unlocks the side chamber at sunset, seats the petitioner before the open box, and leaves. The petitioner is alone with the manuscript until the temple bell rings for the close of the evening prayers. He may read as much or as little of the manuscript as he wishes. He may not write in it.

The treatise speaks plainly. Mochizuke wrote without disguise, naming the famine ruling, naming his motive, examining his own argument with the same exactness he had once turned upon the arguments of his rivals. Some petitioners are graced with a passage that addresses the precise question they have come carrying, and they leave the chamber having understood themselves better than they did at sunset. Some are graced with the manuscript opening to the blank pages and not to the written ones — the box is shallow and the manuscript shifts on its own as the petitioner kneels, and some find the blank pages turned uppermost. The Custodian, when this happens, asks the petitioner upon his departure whether he found his answer in the blank pages, and the answer is often yes; Fukurokujin's mercy is sometimes the gift of *not* receiving Mochizuke's words but being asked to write one's own. Some find only the written pages, and read, and leave. Most petitioners read for an hour and emerge quieter than they entered.

Fukurokujin's wisdom is not the wisdom of being right; it is the wisdom of knowing why one wished to be right. Mochizuke's treatise, unfinished, instructs by what it does not say as much as by what it does — for Mochizuke himself never reached the conclusion he was racing toward, and the temple holds that this is no defect but the lesson. The samurai who comes to the chamber seeking the answer to his regret will not find a verdict written there; he will find an examination, and he will be invited to continue it in his own life.

The manuscript may not be copied in full. Senior monks of the Order have for centuries copied individual passages — never more than two pages from any one petitioner's session — into small *companion books* that the temple workshop binds in pale cloth and gives to departing petitioners who have asked. The blank pages are never copied; what is blank in the relic must remain blank in the copy. The original manuscript stays in its open box, beside the dry brush and dry inkstone, in the side chamber off the library. Three centuries of dust have been brushed away by the Custodians; the manuscript itself has not yellowed beyond the warm cream it was when Mochizuke laid down his brush, and the ink is as black as a Master's regret.
