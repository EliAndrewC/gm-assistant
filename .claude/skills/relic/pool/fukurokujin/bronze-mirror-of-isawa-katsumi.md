A bronze hand-mirror the size of a man's palm, with a short handle wrapped in old silk cord. The polished face is darkened with age to the color of an autumn river at dusk; a viewer's reflection is visible, but softened, the features blurred at their edges. The back of the mirror is cast with a low relief of a stag drinking from a forest pool, the antlers extending past the rim of the disc. The mirror rests on a small folded square of pale silk on a low writing-desk in a side study off the temple's main scriptorium, where a single oil lamp is kept burning during the hours when the library is open.

Isawa Katsumi was the master of the Library of Two Doors during the long winter of the sixth century, when the great snows kept the Phoenix scholars at their books for nine months together and the disputes among them grew bitter. She was famed in her age not for any single treatise - she wrote only one, on the proper ordering of commentaries, and even that she left unsigned - but for being able to enter a room of quarreling shugenja and, by quiet questions, send each of them back to their desks no longer certain of their own argument. Her students said of her that she did not teach what to think but how to be uncertain. She kept this mirror on her writing-desk and consulted it before answering any difficult question. When a student of hers, frustrated, demanded to know what she saw in the mirror that the rest of them did not, she said only: "the face of someone who does not yet know."

A scholar who has come to a place in his study where he can no longer see his way forward - a senior monk whose commentary has lost its thread, a young initiate whose memorization has begun to harden into mere recitation, an Isawa shugenja whose theological argument has begun to bend toward a conclusion he is no longer certain he believes - may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for a session with the mirror. The Custodian unlocks the study, lights an additional lamp, and seats the petitioner before the writing-desk. The petitioner places his current work - the page he cannot finish, the passage he cannot interpret - on the desk before him. He then takes up the mirror and looks at his own face for the length of one slow breath, and sets it down.

Fukurokujin's wisdom is given to those who can hold their own ignorance steadily. The bronze does not show the petitioner anything beyond his own face; what the petitioner is asked to do is look at the face of the one who is stuck, and recognize that face as his own. Some are graced with a clear thought arriving in the moment they set the mirror down - an angle they had not considered, a question they had not asked. Some are graced with the mirror's reflection seeming, for a moment, to belong to someone slightly older than themselves, as though Katsumi were lending the petitioner her own eyes for the length of the breath. Some see only themselves and find no insight, and the Custodian sends them back to their books with the counsel that Fukurokujin's gift is sometimes a longer patience and not a faster answer.

The mirror is consulted in a quieter way during the *Audit of Commentaries*, held twice each year in the weeks before the snows close and after they open. The senior monks of the scriptorium gather at the writing-desk in turn, each bringing the work of the past season, and each looks once into the mirror before reading aloud what he has written. The audit is not a test of doctrinal correctness; it is a check upon the writer's clarity of mind. A senior monk who finds, after the mirror, that he cannot read his own passage without flinching is permitted to withdraw the passage from the season's record without explanation. Three withdrawals in a single audit are taken as Fukurokujin's signal that the monk should pass to a different task for a time, and the Custodian arranges it without comment.

The bronze has not lost its sheen, though it has not been polished within recorded memory. The silk cord on the handle has been replaced perhaps twelve times across the centuries; the temple's records mark only that each replacement was performed by a senior monk who had himself completed an Audit of Commentaries without withdrawal. The folded silk square beneath the mirror is changed each spring at the time of the Mending Ceremony. The mirror itself does not leave the writing-desk.

A scholar leaving the Library of Two Doors at the end of his career may petition for a *replica mirror* from the temple workshop - a smaller hand-mirror of polished brass, cast with the same stag-and-pool on its back, blessed at the writing-desk by the Custodian before being given. These replicas are kept on the writing-desks of senior scholars in Phoenix lands and beyond. They are not the relic; what they remind the bearer of is the discipline of looking once at his own face before answering a hard question.
