A small basin of dark river-stone, the width of a man's two hands, set into the floor of a covered alcove at the back of the temple's modest courtyard. The basin holds rainwater that the alcove's overhanging roof collects and slowly feeds in through a copper channel; the water is changed by the rains rather than by any human hand, and the level rises and falls with the seasons. The water is always still. The stone of the basin is smooth, worn dark by water and by the hands of generations of women who have knelt beside it. A wooden bench sits before the basin, just wide enough for two to sit together. The alcove is open at the front and screened from the wind on three sides.

Otsuyu was a peasant woman of Yoki-mura, a market town in the Iuchi Province six generations past. She was the wife of a tenant farmer, the mother of seven children, and the grandmother in time of nineteen more. She had no learning beyond the household and the rice paddy; she had never been outside the province and could read only the few characters needed for the village's accounts. But her counsel was sought, over the fifty years of her motherhood, by every other woman in Yoki-mura who could not work out what to do with a willful son, a daughter caught in folly, a husband grown cold, an in-law turned cruel. She kept this basin in the courtyard of her own farmhouse, fed by the eaves, and she would seat the visitor on a bench beside it and speak with her at length. The villagers said of Otsuyu that she did not give advice; she asked questions, and listened, and asked more questions, and by the end the visitor had usually answered her own question and gone home knowing what to do. Otsuyu credited the basin. *"I look at the water,"* she would say, *"and the water shows me what to ask."*

When she died at seventy-eight, the village headsman of that generation prevailed upon her sons to give the basin to the small Temple of Fukurokujin that had grown up in the town's market quarter. The basin was installed in the alcove where it now sits, and the eaves were extended to feed it as the eaves of her farmhouse had. The temple's senior monk of that generation, a woman herself, said only that Fukurokujin's mercy is also the mercy of a wise neighbor, and that the basin had been a temple in its own right before it ever came to one.

A mother whose child has begun to go wrong — a son turning to drink, a daughter refusing the match her family has arranged, a younger child whose troubles the mother cannot name — may petition the Custodian of Sacred Relics for a *sitting with Otsuyu*. The Custodian, who is by long custom a married woman or a widow of the town, walks the petitioner to the alcove, seats herself on one end of the bench, and asks the petitioner to sit on the other. She does not speak first. The petitioner speaks the trouble; the Custodian listens, and looks at the water; and after a while she asks a question. The petitioner answers; the Custodian asks another question. The water is between them.

Some petitioners are graced with seeing, in the surface of the pool, a faint reflection of an older woman beside their own — Otsuyu sitting where the Custodian sits, lending her face to the listening. Some are graced with the surface of the pool stirring once, of itself, in the moment when the right question has been asked, the moment when the petitioner understands at last what her child needs of her. Some see only their own reflection, doubled by the bench, and find their answer regardless; Otsuyu's gift in life was that she asked the questions a mother had not let herself ask, and the gift survives her in the asking, with or without omen.

The basin is also sat at by families who have come to a difficult choice that affects all of them — whether to send a younger son to a temple rather than to the fields, whether to refuse a marriage proposal that brings money but estranges the family from a beloved aunt, whether to forgive a runaway daughter who has returned with a child not her husband's. The Custodian seats them all on a long bench brought into the alcove for the purpose. The same protocol holds: she does not speak first; she asks questions in turn; the water sits between them. Decisions reached at the basin are by long custom not announced for three days afterward, so that the family may live with the decision before committing it; many decisions soften in those three days, and the Custodian holds that this softening is itself Fukurokujin's mercy at work.

A magistrate too may sit at the basin, though this is rarer; the temple is a small one and the County Magistrate of Iuchi Province sits elsewhere. But on the occasion when a magistrate has been called to rule on a family matter — a disputed adoption, an inheritance contested between siblings — and the law gives him no clear answer, he may come to Yoki-mura and sit with the Custodian as any mother would. The Custodian treats him no differently. The water is between them.

The basin's pool has never been emptied by human hand, nor has the temple's record-keepers found a season in which the rains failed it entirely. Twice in living memory the pool has gone very low, but did not dry; the Custodian of those years interpreted the low water as Fukurokujin's signal that the town's mothers were carrying heavier burdens than the basin alone could ease, and arranged for additional alms-rice to be distributed through the women's quarter for the season. The water rose with the next rains. The temple does not say what the connection was.

The basin cannot be moved; the alcove was built around it, and to lift it from its setting would be to abandon Otsuyu's gift. The temple workshop produces small *companion bowls* of dark stone, the size of a cupped hand, blessed at the basin and given to married daughters leaving Yoki-mura for households elsewhere. The companion bowls are filled with rainwater by the daughters and kept on shelves in their new kitchens, and consulted in their own private sittings when the daughters become mothers in their turn. Many a household in the Iuchi Province keeps such a bowl. The water in them does not stay clear for long, but the women who keep them do not mind; what they look at is not the water but the bowl, and the bowl reminds them of the kind of question to ask.
