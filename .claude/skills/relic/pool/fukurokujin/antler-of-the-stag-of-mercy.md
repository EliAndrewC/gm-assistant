---

A single shed antler of a great red stag, nearly the length of a man's arm, with five points. It is mounted on a polished slab of dark wood — the wood is plain, not lacquered, and the antler is held by two small bronze brackets that grip it loosely enough that it could be lifted off without effort. The slab rests on a low altar of pale stone in the temple's antechamber, the room that any visitor must pass through before reaching the inner hall. A single oil lamp burns on the altar at all hours. The antler itself is the color of old ivory along its main beam, darker at the points where the stag would have rubbed it against trees in the season before shedding.

The antler is held to have been shed by the stag upon which Fukurokujin rode in the founding age, when the Fortune walked among the magistrates of the early Empire and instructed them in the difference between justice and harshness. The stag, by the temple's account, shed its antlers each spring as ordinary stags do, and one spring left this antler in a clearing in the mountains east of Otosan Uchi. The antler was found by a charcoal-burner who recognized it, by the unusual length and the five points, as no ordinary stag's, and brought it to the temple then being built. This was nearly a thousand years past. The antler has been the temple's foundational relic ever since.

The temple at Otosan Uchi is small by the standards of the Imperial capital, but its standing is great because of its proximity to the Imperial Board of Justice. Senior magistrates of the Board — Emerald magistrates returning from the provinces with difficult reports, junior judges newly appointed and uncertain, magistrates summoned to argue particularly contested cases — make a point of passing through the antechamber before they enter the Board's halls. By long custom an Imperial magistrate touches the antler with the fingertips of his right hand as he passes, lightly, the way a man might brush a doorframe. The touch is brief, the gesture nearly invisible. The Custodian of Sacred Relics, who sits in the antechamber during the hours the Board is in session, notes who has passed and who has not, but does not speak of it.

Fukurokujin grants wisdom to the magistrate who acknowledges that he is about to rule on the lives of men, and grants mercy to the magistrate who acknowledges that he himself may have erred. The touch of the antler is the acknowledgment in physical form. Some magistrates are graced with a sudden coolness at the fingertips, as though the antler had given back a measure of the warmth a worried hand carries — and these magistrates often find, by their own account, that a small detail of the case they had been overlooking comes clear to them as they cross the threshold into the Board's chamber. Some are graced with the lamp on the altar flickering as they pass, an auspicious omen that Fukurokujin walks with them into the chamber. Most touch the antler and feel only the cool of old bone, and proceed about their business, and this is the gift the antler usually gives — the reminder that they are passing from one room to another.

The antler is also brought into the Board's chamber on the rare occasion when a *case of mercy* is to be heard — that is, a case in which a sentence already passed is being reconsidered on petition, whether of the family of the condemned or of the magistrate who passed the sentence and has begun to doubt it. On these days the Custodian carries the antler in his two hands, walking before the senior magistrate, and lays it on the chamber's central table for the duration of the hearing. The bronze brackets remain at the temple; the antler is set directly on the wood, on a small white silk cloth. Two of the petitioning Board's magistrates of the past century have written privately that they could not have brought themselves to commute a sentence without the antler before them on the table. One wrote that he could not have brought himself to uphold a sentence, either, without it. Fukurokujin's mercy is not soft; it is the mercy that sees clearly and rules accordingly.

The antler does not regrow what it has lost; a stag's antler, once shed, is finished. The Custodian shows the antler to junior monks each year on the festival of Fukurokujin and reminds them that the Fortune's wisdom is the wisdom of what has been let go, not of what is still being grasped. The five points of the antler are taken, by the Order's teachings, to be the five questions a magistrate must ask before he rules — but the temple does not write the questions down, holding instead that each magistrate must arrive at them in his own life, and that to fix them in writing would be to fail Fukurokujin's lesson.

The antler may not be carried beyond the walls of the temple precinct, save in the Custodian's hands and for a session of the Board. Junior monks who wish to make pilgrimage to the temple from the provinces are received with hospitality and may touch the antler once before leaving; some take home small carved tokens of stag-antler from the temple workshop, cut from the antlers of ordinary deer offered to the temple by hunters of the surrounding hills, blessed at the altar before being given. The original antler has rested in its brackets for nearly a thousand years. It has not yellowed nor cracked. The temple does not say why.
