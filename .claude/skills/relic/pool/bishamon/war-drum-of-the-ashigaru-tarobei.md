A medium *odaiko* of cedar staves bound with iron hoops, the drumhead of bull-hide so dark with age it appears black in shadow. The drum is the size of a large rice-cask and stands on a low cedar frame at waist-height, the striking-face turned toward the shrine's outer wall. The hide is patched in four places where it has been struck through; the patches are of paler leather and were sewn in by the drummer's daughter and her descendants, one generation at a time. The shrine is small - three rooms above the inner gate of the Sunda Mizu garrison-outpost - and the drum occupies most of the main room.

Tarobei was a peasant ashigaru drummer of the Kaiu Wall levy in the middle of the fifth century. During the long night that the garrison commanders remember as the Breach of the Twelfth Tower, when an oni and its tide of lesser things reached the parapet and the bushi line was broken back from the gate, Tarobei kept his drum at the cadence of the muster though the men around him fell. The ashigaru who could no longer see the line could still hear the drum, and they re-formed on the cadence. Tarobei was killed near dawn - the records do not say how, only that the drum was found upright with its drummer fallen against it, his sticks crossed on the head. The Hiruma captain who took the muster the next morning ordered the drum carried to the gate-shrine and a place made for it there. The Order of Bishamon at Sunda Mizu has tended it since.

The drum is struck once at dawn and once at dusk every day by the Custodian of the Drum - a Senior Monk who holds no other office and who lives in the shrine itself. The single beat at dawn is for the men who held the Wall the previous night, and the single beat at dusk is for the men who will hold it the coming night. The drum is heard along the parapet for half a li in either direction, and Tarobei's strength may pass into the men of the watch as the beat reaches them. Some on the line are graced with finding their fear quieter for the hour after the beat; others find that the beat lingers in their chest through the long hours of watch and steadies them when the shadow at the foot of the Wall stirs.

When the alarm is sounded - the long crooked horns of the garrison taking up the call from one tower to the next - the Custodian of the Drum takes up Tarobei's sticks and beats the **Cadence of the Muster**, the cadence Tarobei himself was beating on the night he died. The cadence is twelve strokes, a pause, four strokes, a pause, twelve strokes, repeated until the bushi line is formed or the alarm is recalled. Ashigaru who hear the cadence and find themselves moving toward their assembly point without thinking are well served by Tarobei; those who freeze in the moment are not abandoned, but Tarobei was a drummer and his gift is to the legs of the running, not the hearts of the still.

The drum has been struck through four times across the centuries; on each occasion the strike has been heavy enough to split the bull-hide, and on each occasion the strike has been delivered on a night of grave loss along the Wall. The records of the garrison list the four nights: the Breach of the Twelfth Tower itself, the Long Cold of the second century, the Falling of the South Lantern in the fourth, and the Three-Day Watch in the fifth. Tarobei strikes back when his men are dying, and the hide gives. The patches are the daughters' work - Tarobei left a daughter who lived in the garrison village, and his line has tended the patches in each generation since, the labor passing daughter-to-daughter through marriage and adoption alike. When a patch is needed the current daughter is summoned from the village, and the work is hers.

The drum cannot be moved from the shrine. The garrison commanders of the past have asked whether it might be carried to the Wall itself in time of need, and the abbots of three generations have refused - the drum is heard from the Wall already, and to carry it forward would be to expose it to the kind of stroke that breaks the hide, when the daughters are not always close to hand. Tarobei is content where he is. The Wall stands; the cadence is kept.
