A weathered post of dense red oak, the thickness of a man's thigh, planted upright in a square of packed earth within an open-air alcove off the temple's training yard. The post stands to chest height; its striking face is wrapped in many layers of rice-straw rope, the outermost layer fresh and the inner layers brown and brittle, the whole bound by a single hemp cord knotted at the back. The earth around the post is bare and hard from generations of bare feet. A simple cedar roof on four pillars shelters the post from rain, but the alcove is otherwise open to the air.

Ikoma Tadanari was an Ikoma bushi of the late second century who, after a campaign in his thirty-eighth year that cost him the use of his sword-arm, took lay-vows at this Temple of Bishamon and devoted the remainder of his life to the study of the striking-arts. He could no longer hold a katana, but he could still strike with the heel of his hand and with the open palm, and he developed in the last thirty years of his life a form of unarmed striking that the Ikoma school still teaches under the name *Tadanari-no-Te*. He struck this post — then a young post he had planted himself in the temple's yard — every morning for the last twenty-seven years of his life, a thousand strikes a day, the count tallied by an acolyte assigned to him by the temple. The post is the same post; the straw has been replaced by every Custodian since, the cord renewed each spring, but the oak heartwood is Tadanari's.

A samurai of any clan who is to fight a duel, contest a tournament, or take part in a *taryū-jiai* between schools may, by tradition that predates the temple's records, come to the alcove and strike the post one hundred times in the hour before the contest. The temple makes no formal grant of permission for this — the alcove is open during daylight, and the post is for those who come. The Custodian of Sacred Relics observes the visitors from the inner gallery but does not interfere. Bishamon's strength is in the hand that has struck the post; the strength that the post has gathered from Tadanari and from the thousands of hands that have followed his may pass into the striker for the work that follows.

Some samurai are graced with finding the post warmer to the touch than the morning air would suggest — an auspicious omen, signifying that Tadanari is present and lends his attention to the coming contest. Some find the straw wrapping unusually firm beneath their palms, as if the post answered back; this is read as Tadanari approving the form of the striking. Some find their hand reddening more quickly than they had expected, and the Custodian, if asked, will say that Tadanari has noted a weakness in the striker's stance and that the hand has paid for what the stance has not yet learned to carry. Most strikers feel only the post and the count and their own breath, and this is no failure; Tadanari taught a thousand strikes a day for twenty-seven years before he claimed to have learned anything, and the temple does not ask its visitors to do better than its founder.

The hundred-strike practice is the common rite, but the post serves longer purposes also. A novice of the Ikoma striking-school, at the time he is to be confirmed as a journeyman of the form, performs the **Thousand-Strike Vigil** at the post — a single day's work from before dawn to after dusk, the count kept by his sensei. The vigil is recorded in the school's roll. The roll has been kept for four centuries and contains every journeyman the Ikoma have produced; the temple's Steward of Records keeps the count and a copy of the roll is in the temple archive. The vigil is not given to every novice — some are turned away by their senseis before they are brought to it — but those who complete it carry the post's mark into the rest of their lives, and the rite of confirmation is performed at the post itself by the school's master in the presence of the Custodian.

The post cannot be moved. The Ikoma school has at times built training-halls in other cities, and the masters of those halls have asked whether a slip of the original post might be planted in their yards. The Custodians of three generations have refused. The post is the post Tadanari planted, in the earth of the temple where he took his vows, struck by the line of his school for four centuries; what the slip would be would not be the post. The school has accepted the refusal. Replicas of the form's name-plaque are produced by the temple workshop and hung in the daughter-halls, but the post itself stays where it stands.

The straw wrapping is replaced each spring at the time of the Mending Ceremony common to the temples of Bishamon. The old straw is not discarded; it is burned in a small brazier at the foot of the post, and the ash is mixed back into the packed earth of the alcove. The temple holds that the earth around the post is now as much Tadanari's as the post itself. Strikers who come to the alcove for the hundred-strike practice are asked to remove their sandals before stepping into the square. The earth remembers the feet that have stood upon it.
