A single river-boulder the height of a kneeling child and broader than a man can easily reach around, its gray stone worn smooth and darkened to the color of old iron along its middle, where uncounted hands have gripped and lifted it. Set into one flank, shallow and half-polished away by those same hands, is the character 力 (*chikara*, "strength"). The stone rests in a low court of raked sand at the side of the temple, ringed by a plain rope of twisted straw, with a wooden rack of straw sandals beside it for those who would try their strength barefoot on the sand. It is not an ornament. It is meant to be lifted.

Osato was a porter-woman of the Reiji domain in the fourth century, of a strength that passed into local legend in her own lifetime. She carried loads two ordinary laborers could not raise between them, and at the Imperial waystation by the river ford - where this stone then sat, as strength-stones sit at many a post-station for porters and travelers to try their arms - she lifted it clean over her head, though not one of the waystation's small guard could clear it from the ground. The stone came to be known by her name. What the temple remembers of her, though, is not the lifting. When the spring floods took the river dike below the town and the low quarter went under in a night, Osato waded the black water until dawn carrying the aged and the injured out on her back, load after load, and when a sodden roof-beam came down across the last doorway she set her shoulders under it and held it up - held it while the trapped crawled out beneath her arms, and did not let it fall until the street was empty. She lived to be old and revered, and in her last years the stone was carried from the waystation to the sovereign temple at her asking, that it be kept where anyone might try it and not shut away. Bishamon, she is remembered to have said, has no use for a strength that is only shown.

Those who would take up a hard labor or a hard duty come to the court to lift the stone under the eye of the Custodian of the Sacred Relics. A bushi before a long campaign, a laborer before a season of breaking stone, a young monk about to swear the vow of poverty and its hardships - each may kneel, set their hands to the worn grip, and raise the stone from the sand, and swear as they hold it that they will spend the strength Bishamon lends them as Osato spent hers: on the burden that serves others, and not on the burden that is only shown. Bushi and commoners lift the same stone; the Fortune of Strength does not ask a lifter's caste, and the temple does not either.

Some who lift are graced with the stone coming up lighter than its wont, rising almost gladly to the hands - taken as Bishamon lending his strength to a purpose he favors, and a sign the lifter's vow is well made. Others find it sits fast, heavy and unwilling, and are counselled that they are not yet ready for the thing they mean to take up, and should return when they have prepared themselves further. That the stone is heavy, and that a resolved arm on a good day lifts what a doubtful arm on a poor one cannot, is not a thing the temple troubles to say; the strength is Bishamon's to lend, and the reading of the sign is the Custodian's to make. Many a lifter who was told the stone sat fast has come back a season later to raise it clean, and gone away counting the failure the truer blessing of the two.

The stone is not moved, and could not easily be. It cannot be lifted away by any ordinary work of hands, and to try would be to mistake what it is for; it is not a weight to be carried off but a weight to be raised and set down again in its place. A vow sworn with hands upon it is held to bind as the stone is bound to its court. The Custodian keeps the sand raked, records who has lifted the stone and what sign was given, and turns away those who come only to prove themselves before a crowd - for that, Osato is remembered to have had no patience, and neither does the house she gave her stone to keep.
