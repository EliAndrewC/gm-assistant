A squared beam of old oak longer than a tall man, banded and shod at its ends with black iron, its faces scarred, splintered, scorched along one edge, and gouged deep in three places by blows that did not break it. It is mounted horizontally on two iron brackets across a plain doorway in a side hall of the temple - a doorway that leads nowhere, walled up behind, so that the bar bars a gate that is no longer there. The character 閂 - a single stroke drawn through a gate, which is the word *kannuki*, a gate-bar - is branded into the wood at its center. Below it, worn almost smooth, is the place where hands rest.

Hida no Reiji Munetoki was gate-warden of a Reiji border outpost seven centuries ago, in the years after the Yasuki forsook the Crane for the Crab and the affronted Crane loosed a long fury along the Crab borders - when raiding-parties came where for a lifetime almost none had come, and the small posts that watch the edge of the domain earned their keep at last. The worth of such a post is that the border cannot be crossed past it in silence. A raiding-party came down on Munetoki's outpost at dusk meaning to take it quickly and quietly, to put out its watch before it could rouse the countryside and so open the way for the raid behind them. They did not manage it. Munetoki barred the gate and held the outpost's few defenders within, and when the iron brackets that seat this bar tore loose under the battering, he took the bar up in his own arms and braced it across the leaves with his shoulder and his whole weight, alone, while his handful of men shot down through the ports above the gate and poured boiling water on the raiders crowded at the door. For better than an hour they beat and burned at the gate and could not break it. Then they broke off and withdrew - for the outpost had not gone silent, its watch was up, and its riders would now come out behind them to raise the alarm and harry their flank; a raid that lives by surprise was already a raid undone. Munetoki lived, though the blows that gouged this bar had crushed the hand he braced against it, and he never had its full use again. The daimyo of that generation had the bar carried to the sovereign temple rather than hung again on the gate, so that what it had bought - a whole raid turned back at a single door - would not be forgotten against the day another gate had to be held.

Those who take up a post they must not abandon come to lay their hands where Munetoki's rested and to ask Bishamon for the strength to hold. A gate-warden beginning a watch, a bushi ordered to hold a ground against odds, a magistrate's guard set over a thing that must not be lost, a young soldier of the Reiji levy brought before it on the day they take up the spear - each sets a hand to the worn place on the bar and prays not for victory but for the narrower and harder thing the bar stands for: to still be at the post when the relief comes, and not to have yielded it. Bishamon is the Fortune of Strength, and the temple holds that there is no greater strength than the strength to hold when holding is all that is left.

The bar is not lifted, and this is more vow than wonder. It is a heavy, awkward beam - a lift for two or three wardens together, as gate-bars are made to be - and no marvel holds it in its brackets; what holds it there is that no one of the temple will be the one to raise it. To lift it from its seat would be to open the gate Munetoki did not open, and so it stays barred, and the walled doorway behind it stays walled. Now and then a strong pilgrim, told the story, sets his hands to it in the idle way of strong people and puts his back into shifting it, and finds it a punishing dead weight that does not want to come, and lets it be; and the tale of that grows in the telling until strangers arrive half-expecting a beam no arm can move at all. The Custodian neither feeds the tale nor quite corrects it. What the temple will say plainly is only this: that the bar held once when it had to, that it is kept against the day a gate must be held again, and that some who pray at it before their own trial are graced with a steadiness that settles into the arms and will not be shaken loose that day - Bishamon's hand, they say, laid over theirs on the wood.

The Custodian of the Sacred Relics keeps the iron of the brackets sound and brings the young of the Reiji levy before the bar each year to hear what it is - for a bar that has been held once is a bar that may have to be held again, and Bishamon's strength, the temple holds, comes soonest to the hand that already knows where on the wood to place itself.
