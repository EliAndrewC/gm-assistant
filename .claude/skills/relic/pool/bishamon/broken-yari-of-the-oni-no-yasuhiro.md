A broken yari of unusual make. The haft is a man's height in length, of a dense black wood that has not been identified by the temple's carvers, snapped clean across two-thirds of the way to the head. The break is recent in appearance though it is over four hundred years old — the splintered fibers within the haft are still pale, as if the break had been made yesterday. The spear-head is of an iron darker than ordinary forge-iron, the edges nicked in three places, the tang bound to the broken haft by iron wire taken from the original binding. The weapon rests on a stone slab within a barred iron cage in the lower chamber of the temple, where the floor is cut from the bedrock and a single lamp of fish-oil burns at all hours.

The yari was taken in the late second century by Hiruma Tatsumune from a creature that had once been a samurai named Yasuhiro. The history is set down in the temple's records as Tatsumune reported it: a Hida vassal of the lower marches named Yasuhiro was lost on a patrol south of the line in his twenty-eighth year, returned in his thirty-first as something that wore his face and carried his name, and was killed by his cousin Tatsumune at the cost of three of Tatsumune's men. The thing that had been Yasuhiro fought with this yari, which Tatsumune broke across his cousin's shield in the final exchange before the killing stroke. Tatsumune carried the broken yari to this Temple of Bishamon rather than burn it with the body, on the counsel of a Kuni shugenja who held that the weapon's strength was Bishamon's by right and ought not to be lost to the southern fires. The Order has kept it under bar and lamp since.

The yari is brought out once each year, on the third day of the Festival of the Wall — the festival the Hiruma keep in honor of the line that holds the southern marches — for the **Blessing of the Arms**. The Custodian of Sacred Relics carries the broken yari from its cage to the temple's outer training yard, where the captains of the Hiruma patrols and the senior bushi of the Wall have assembled with their own weapons laid before them. The Custodian sets the yari on its slab at the head of the line, and the captains pass before it one by one, each laying his weapon for a moment against the broken haft before reclaiming it. Bishamon's strength is in the weapon that was wrested from the enemy and turned against him; the strength is shared with the weapons that touch it, and the captains who pass before the yari carry that strength back into the field.

The yari does not give its blessing to every weapon. Some captains find their own blades warmer in their hand when they reclaim them — an auspicious omen that Yasuhiro's strength has been turned against the southern fires in their hand for the coming year. Some find nothing remarkable, and this is not held against them; the yari gives its blessing as Bishamon wills, and a captain who is not graced this year may be graced the next. Some weapons placed against the haft are found to slip from their owner's grip when reclaimed, and the temple holds this as a quiet sign that the captain has been carrying his weapon poorly and ought to consult the smith before the campaign season. The captains who have heeded this sign have not regretted it.

The yari cannot be destroyed. The Kuni shugenja who counseled Tatsumune left a writing on this matter, kept in the temple's archive: the thing that had been Yasuhiro left some residue in the weapon, and to burn or break the weapon further would be to free that residue into the world rather than keep it bound. The temple does not invite Kuni inspection more often than once a generation, and on those occasions the inspecting shugenja has reported that the binding holds and the residue is quiet. The lamp of fish-oil is kept burning because the fish-oil is the lamp the second-century shugenja specified, and the temple has not seen reason to substitute.

The yari cannot leave the temple. The Hiruma have asked at moments of grave threat — most recently during the long winter of the present century, when the southern marches were pressed harder than at any time in living memory — and the abbots have refused. The yari does its work where it is. The Blessing of the Arms is its outward gift; the cage and the lamp are its inward labor. To carry the weapon out would be to risk breaking the binding the Kuni set, and no captain's strength is worth that cost.
