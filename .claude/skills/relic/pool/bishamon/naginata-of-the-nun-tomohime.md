name: The Naginata of the Nun Tomohime

japanese_kanji: 縋りの薙刀
fortune: bishamon
clan: lion
temple: a temple of Bishamon in Lion lands
named_entity: the nun Tomohime (Matsu-born widow who took monastic vows and was twice called to trial-by-arms)

A naginata of unusual length — the haft of dark oak rather than the more common ash, the blade longer than a man's forearm and curved more deeply than a soldier's pattern. The blade is set into the haft with a long iron collar bound by braided silk cord, the cord now the color of old straw though it was red when it was first wound. The haft is bare of lacquer; six centuries of hands have darkened it to nearly the color of the iron. The naginata rests horizontally on two cedar pegs against the back wall of a small chamber off the temple's judgment-hall, behind a low rope of plaited rice-straw that pilgrims do not cross.

Tomohime was a Matsu woman who had been widowed young, in the late fourth century, and had taken vows at this temple in her twenty-sixth year. She continued to practice the naginata in the temple's training yard as the senior monks permitted, the weapon being honored as a tool of Bishamon even within monastic hands. Twice she was called from the temple by writ of the magistrate to settle matters of dispute by trial-by-arms — both being cases in which the contending samurai had refused all other forms of judgment, and the magistrate had no remaining path. The first trial Tomohime won at the second pass of the blades; the second she won without striking, the contending samurai laying down his weapon when she had taken her stance. Both judgments were accepted, and the matters were settled. Tomohime returned to the temple after each and resumed her vows, asking no honor and refusing the offered stipend. The naginata was hers; on her death it passed to the temple, and the cord was changed once in the century after her death and not since.

A magistrate of the Matsu provinces who cannot resolve a matter by ordinary means — where evidence is exhausted, witnesses have failed, and the contending parties refuse any judgment but combat — may petition the Grand Abbot for the **Calling of the Naginata**. The Grand Abbot in council with the Custodian of Sacred Relics and the Steward of Arms considers the petition; the consideration is rarely brief, and the calling is not given lightly. If granted, a senior monk of the temple is appointed to bear the naginata as Tomohime's successor, and the trial-by-arms is held at the temple's outer training yard rather than in any clan court. The monk fights to Tomohime's standard — sparing where she would have spared, striking where she would have struck — and the result is taken as Bishamon's judgment, mediated through Tomohime's hand.

The Calling has been performed four times in the six centuries since Tomohime's death. Each calling is recorded in the temple's judgment-roll, with the magistrate's writ, the names of the contending parties, the appointed monk, the outcome, and the disposition of the matter afterward. Three of the four callings ended without a death, the contending party laying down arms before the second pass; the fourth ended in the contending party's death and the matter was settled at the funeral. The records are not consulted in deciding whether a new calling is to be granted, but they are read by the appointed monk before the trial is held — Tomohime's example is the standard.

The naginata is taken from its pegs only by the appointed monk, on the morning of the trial, in the presence of the Custodian and two senior monks as witnesses. The braided cord is checked for fraying and the iron collar is inspected for slack; if either is found wanting, the calling is postponed and the trial deferred until the temple's smith has put the weapon right. Tomohime would not have fought with a weapon she had not herself prepared. Some appointed monks find the naginata heavier in their hand than they had expected from its size — an auspicious omen, the temple records show, indicating that Tomohime's attention is upon the trial. Some find the weapon balanced as if their own; some find their stance settling into a posture they had not trained, which the senior monks recognize as Tomohime's own and which they do not correct.

The naginata cannot be carried into ordinary combat. It is a relic of trial, not of war, and to bring it into the field would be to spend Tomohime's judgment on the chaos of battle where no judgment can be heard. The Matsu commanders of the past have at times wished otherwise — particularly during the long campaigns of the present century — and the Grand Abbots have at each request returned the same answer: Tomohime gave her judgments by writ of the magistrate, and the temple will not lend her hand to any other purpose.
