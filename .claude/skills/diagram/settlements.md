# Mode B: Settlement maps (hamlets, villages, towns, provincial cities)

This file is the Mode B half of the /diagram skill: settlement maps built by the parametric generator ([`settlement.py`](settlement.py)) and gated by the validator ([`check_village.py`](check_village.py)).  Read [`SKILL.md`](SKILL.md) first for everything shared (principles, workflow, labeling, style conventions, render pipeline, output convention); this file adds the settlement vocabulary, the spec → validator → persona workflow, and the historical grounding behind the realism checks.

Settlement maps are landscape plans - a hamlet, village, or town sitting in its fields - not interior building plans. They reuse every shared convention in [`SKILL.md`](SKILL.md) and add the vocabulary and workflow below.

### Architecture: a shared library + thin per-settlement specs

The common machinery lives in [`settlement.py`](settlement.py) (the `Settlement` class). A specific settlement is a **thin spec** that imports it, declares its fields/water/shrine/houses, and calls `finish()`. Worked examples, smallest to largest:

- [`pool/moritono.gen.py`](pool/moritono.gen.py) - a **hamlet** (no headman/shrine/tax-free), three small fields fed by a cascading stream, plus two features beyond the usual vocabulary: a **forest** (`s.forest(west_edge, ...)`) filling the east, and the county magistrate's **walled manor** (`s.manor(...)`, a hunting lodge - a Mode A compound shown as a feature, a samurai estate adjacent to but not part of the hamlet).
- [`pool/kikuta-village.gen.py`](pool/kikuta-village.gen.py) - an average **village**, **pond-fed**, TWO staggered common fields, hill-top Benten shrine with 7 torii, fallow fields ringed by abandoned houses.
- [`pool/hikari-no-sato.gen.py`](pool/hikari-no-sato.gen.py) - an average **village**, **stream-fed** (no pond), ONE great V-shaped common field with an internal fallow patch (no abandonment), a ring of small fields fed field-to-field, a modest 2-torii Benten shrine at the southern torii gateway (NOT on a hill), and a second Bishamon shrine in the southwest.
- [`pool/hoshizora.gen.py`](pool/hoshizora.gen.py) - an **unwalled town** (county seat, ~1,200 people). Imperial Road spine, a stream running parallel to the road between field pairs, a dense urban core (merchants/laborers/servants aligned to the road), a town **monastery** with a torii, the magistrate's walled manor + samurai houses, a segregated burakumin quarter, a theater stage by the monastery, **hayfield/grazing pastures** ringing barns, and a small forest - terrain features (forest, pasture, one field) run OFF the map edge.
- [`pool/hirameki.gen.py`](pool/hirameki.gen.py) - a **walled town**. The rampart is an irregular hill-anchored arc (`s.wall(...)`) whose two ends **climb partway up the hill flanks**; the **north flank is a steep hachured hill** (`s.hill(..., steep=True)`) carrying the magistrate's manor, so the slope defends that side instead of a wall; a front gate with guard station + guardtower; and a **chrysanthemum field** (`s.flower_field(...)`, the Imperial flower) kept INSIDE the walls, abutting the inner face (the wall bulges out around it).
- [`pool/tango.gen.py`](pool/tango.gen.py) - a **walled provincial city** (the city scale). A closed `s.city_wall(...)` ring with N and S gates, an `s.moat(...)` fed by an off-map stream, the Imperial road straight through gate-to-gate with a guard house + inspection station + tower at each gate, `s.governor_mansion(...)` + six `s.ministry(...)` offices (Rites in the temple neighborhood), a crossing-grid street network of dense neighborhoods (~600 buildings - tight rows packed in the blocks), quartered districts (NW agricultural + in-wall burakumin, NE laborers, SW merchants + temple, SE government + samurai), and 5-15 varying-size walled samurai estates outside the SE.

**What is COMMON (lives in `settlement.py`, do not duplicate per village):** the palette and SVG defs; organic field outlines (bbox-based OR an arbitrary base polygon like a V) with non-uniform crop basins and per-plot angled rows; the south-facing pitched-roof house glyph; size-aware, corridor-aware, field-blocking house placement; hills + summit shrines + torii; ponds, off-map streams, and winding channels; tax-free plot picking (interior-validated); the manifest emission. When you need a NEW shared capability (a new field shape, a new building type), add it to the library, not to one village.

**What is SETTLEMENT-SPECIFIC (declared in the thin spec + echoed into `manifest["meta"]`):** field count/shape/placement; the irrigation **source** (pond vs stream vs field-to-field) - water *must exist*, but the kind varies by region (a pond/*tameike* suits a dry locale; a stream diversion suits a river valley - not every village had a pond); torii count; whether the main shrine sits on a hill or stands central on flat ground; whether blight left abandoned houses (Kikuta yes, Hikari no); additional shrines; the settlement scale and (for towns) the holding clan, walls, slope, and monastery dedications. The validator reads `meta` and each channel's `frm`/`to` anchors, so it adapts per settlement instead of assuming one layout - see the knob reference next.

### Settlement `meta()` and the per-settlement knobs

A settlement's identity and the rules the gate applies to it are declared with `s.meta(...)` (echoed into `manifest["meta"]`) plus two `Settlement` attributes. **This is also the persistence mechanism:** the knobs live in the `.gen.py` source, so every regeneration re-applies them and the gate fails loudly if a pass ever drops one - which is why these maps survive dozens of re-generation passes without losing their identity. Annotate non-obvious knobs with a comment explaining *why* (a changed-hands town, an intentional single monastery), so the reasoning travels with the data.

- `name=` - the place name (the title block).
- `scale=` - `"hamlet"` | `"village"` | `"town"`. Drives nearly everything downstream: headman/shrine presence, the house-count band, the religious tier, and which town rules switch on.
- `households=N` / `target_houses=N` - village sizing (occupied houses ≈ 0.7 per household, or an explicit count band).
- `walled=True` - a town has a rampart (turns on the wall / gate / main-street / theater-stage-inside checks).
- `torii_expected=N` - exact TOTAL torii count drawn: a village/hill shrine's gateway arches, OR the sum across a town's monastery avenues (a town monastery's avenue may be several arches - see `monastery_torii_scale_with_space`, so this is no longer "one per monastery").
- `shrine_on_hill=True` - the village shrine sits on the summit, reached by a torii ascent.
- `fallow_implies_abandoned=True` - fallow fields are ringed by abandoned houses (post-blight).
- `downhill=<cardinal name | [dx,dy]>` - the land's slope (so channels can be gated to run downhill); tag it when the gradient is clear (a hill on one side).
- `clan="<Great Clan>"` - the holding clan; sets the town's two default monastery dedications. **ASK the GM if not given.**
- `monastery_fortunes=[...]` - override the monastery dedications (a town that changed hands, or one with a single monastery).
- `theater_stage=False | "outside"` - omit the town theater stage, or allow it outside a walled town.
- `gate_market=False` - suppress the extramural gate market a walled town has by default (a purely military fort, or a depopulated / suppressed gate). See `walled_town_has_gate_market`.
- `fire_tower=False` - omit the fire-watch tower(s) a WALLED town or a city has by default (`walled_town_has_fire_tower` / `city_has_fire_towers`). An open road-town has none either way (see below).
- `windward="NW"` - the compass quarter the prevailing cold wind blows FROM, setting which side each farmstead's homestead grove (*yashikirin*) shelters. Default `"NW"` (the East Asian winter monsoon); override per map (`"N"`/`"NE"`/.../`"NW"`) when local geography - a sea, a mountain wall, the western Burning Sands - sets a different exposure. See `groves_on_windward_side` and "Homestead groves".
- `grove_prevalence=1.0` - the fraction of farmhouses that are homestead-grove candidates (UNIVERSAL by default - a grove is drawn on every farm with windward room). Lower it for a sheltered/atypical microclimate where fewer farms planted a windbreak. `groves_where_possible` honours the knob.
- `inwall_groves=False` - whether farms INSIDE a city wall get groves. Off by default (an intramural plot is sheltered by the urban fabric and on land too precious for a tree belt; `no_groves_inside_walls` enforces it). Set `True` only for an unusual in-wall homestead district.
- `granary=True` - mark a rice-TRANSIT town and require a distinct granary (`s.granary(...)`, gated by `town_has_granary`). OFF by default - a standard county seat's grain sits inside the magistrate's yamen, not drawn separately.
- `flophouses=N` - how many market-day flophouses a town must show (`town_has_flophouse`). Defaults to **1** (default-on); set higher for a busy market hub, or `0` to opt out.
- canvas is `Settlement(W, H, seed=...)` (echoed to `meta.W/H`); **`s.bscale`** (≈ 0.82 for a town) shrinks the urban glyphs so a larger settlement packs at a finer grain. **Farmhouses share the same grain** - `s.ring(...)`/`try_place` scale the outside-the-walls farmhouse footprint by `bscale` too, so a peasant farmhouse never renders LARGER than the samurai/merchant houses inside the walls (at city `bscale` ≈ 0.42 the farmhouses shrink to roughly laborer/merchant size). Because smaller footprints pack the field rings more easily, dropping `bscale` may raise the farmhouse count - at TOWN scale farmhouses count toward `population_consistent_with_housing`, so thin the rings if it overshoots (a CITY excludes farmhouses from its population entirely, so ring freely there).

### Workflow (spec → validator → persona)

1. Pre-design conversation with the GM (scale, fields, water source, civic features, the residing NPC), grounded in the setting numbers below. **For a town, also settle:** the **holding clan** (sets the monastery dedications - ASK if not given), **walled or unwalled** (border/contested towns are walled), the **elevation slope** if there's a clear gradient (a hill), and any exceptions to the defaults (single monastery, no theater stage, a town that changed hands).
2. Write a thin spec by copying the closest existing one: a **village** from `kikuta-village.gen.py` (pond, two fields) or `hikari-no-sato.gen.py` (stream, one V-field); a **hamlet** from `moritono.gen.py`; an **unwalled town** from `hoshizora.gen.py`; a **walled town** from `hirameki.gen.py`. Running it writes the `.svg`, the `.json`, **and the `.png`** next to itself - `s.finish()` rasterizes the PNG automatically (via `rsvg-convert` at 2600px), so the `.png` can never drift from the `.svg`. Only touch `settlement.py` if you need a genuinely new shared capability.
3. Run `python3 check_village.py <manifest>.json` - the machine gate. It must report ALL CHECKS PASSED before going further. (Running the test suite, `python3 -m pytest`, re-runs every gen and so refreshes every pool `.png` as a side effect - if you ever hand-edit an SVG, re-run the gen or the suite rather than calling `rsvg-convert` yourself.)
4. Read the PNG yourself - the persona pass the gate cannot do (coherent village? legible? balanced?). Per Constitution Principle I, get an independent review if you both built and reviewed.
5. Show the GM. Save the `.svg`, `.png`, `.gen.py`, and `.json` in `pool/`.

### Scale and density (ground in the setting numbers)

Pull from `/gm-assistant/setting/median-domain.md`, `demographics.md`, `village-headsmen.md`:

- Average village: 200-500 people = 40-100 households (median household ~5 over 2-3 generations). Declare `meta(households=N)`; the gate's **`households_consistent`** check requires the OCCUPIED farmhouse count to be **~0.7 per household** (extended families share a roof - the GM's rule: "~70 households ... at least 50 houses"). So ~70 households → ~50 occupied houses, a few larger (the ~5% wealthy-landowner farmers) plus the headman's (largest). **Abandoned houses do not count toward households** (they're former homes).
- **Population is DWELLINGS x ~5, never total buildings** (`population_consistent_with_housing`, town + city). A town/city's **businesses** (`shop`, and the dual-use `merchant` house+shop) and its **civic/government** buildings (ministries, the governor's yamen, flophouses, kura, gate furniture) house no one, so counting them inflates the count. Declare `meta(population=N)`; the gate counts only DWELLINGS times 5, within a tight tolerance of N (default 7%, override with `meta(population_tol=...)`) - so you declare the figure you actually want and the gate verifies the map delivers it, rather than passing a loose band. **What counts as a dwelling differs by scale:** a TOWN counts farmhouses + the residential kinds `laborer`/`servant`/`burakumin`/`samurai`/`merchant` (its depicted farmhouses ARE its partial county farmer cohort). A CITY counts the **urban residential kinds ONLY** - **no farmhouses at all**: a city's declared ~3,000 is its urban castes (budgets.md lists ZERO farmers in the city tables), and the farmers who feed it - whether in the surrounding county villages OR the unusual in-wall agricultural district - are simply not part of that figure. So a 3,000-resident city places ~600 dwellings (Tango does), and its TOTAL building count is *higher* (the shops/civic are extra). City maps depict the full city; town maps depict a representative slice of the ~1,200 county, so their declared `population` is the depicted figure, not the documented 1,200.
- **A city's CASTE MIX must match budgets.md, not just the total** (`city_caste_counts_in_band`). The total-population check alone lets the mix silently DRIFT: the classic failure is one quarter's pack (the laborers) absorbing everyone else's slots while **servants get starved to near-zero because they were appended to the END of a pack list** (`["laborer"] * 445 + ["servant"] * 50` places 445 laborers and then has no room left for the servants). A provincial city is roughly **~40% laborer / 20% servant / 25% merchant / 10% samurai / 5% burakumin** of its ~600 households, and each caste is held within **+/-30%** of that target. Two rules for hitting it: (1) **INTERLEAVE servants** into the laborer and merchant packs (`(["laborer"] * 3 + ["servant"]) * N`), since they live among the households they serve - never tack them on the end. (2) The samurai and merchant **walled ESTATES are households too** - the check counts `M["manors"]` (extramural samurai estates, who commute in) toward samurai and `M["merchant_estates"]` toward merchants, so the in-wall *houses* need only make up the remainder (which is why `city_samurai_housing_sufficient` wants ~39 in-wall samurai houses, not the full ~60). Space-bound quarters (the SE samurai quarter is crowded by the government compounds) lean on their estates to reach band.
- To seat ~50 houses you usually need them **two-deep** around the fields, not a single necklace - call `ring()` twice per field, an inner pass (small gap) and an outer pass (larger gap, e.g. ~55). A single ring saturates a field's perimeter around ~40 houses.
- Hamlet (`meta(scale="hamlet")`): 50-100 people = 10-20 households. A hamlet has **no headman of its own** (it falls under the village district's headman - so omit `s.headman(...)`), **no village shrine**, and **no tax-free plot**. The gate enforces this asymmetry: `hamlet_has_no_headman` for hamlets vs `village_has_headman` for villages; tax-free and the orientation checks simply don't apply at hamlet scale. A hamlet typically has just 1-3 small fields.
- Villages and hamlets are peasant-only - no resident samurai (samurai live in the county town).
- **Non-standard size:** for a larger-than-usual village (say 500 people / ~80 houses), declare `meta(target_houses=N)` and the gate's count band follows it (~N ±15%). House count is bounded by field perimeter (houses ring fields at ~56px spacing), so to actually *seat* that many: enlarge the canvas (`Settlement(W=..., H=...)`) and give bigger/more fields, then raise the `ring()` counts. A second concentric house ring is a small library addition if one ring around the fields can't hold them.
- **Town (`meta(scale="town")`):** a county seat, ~1,200 people / ~238 households (per [`budgets.md`](https://github.com/EliAndrewC/l7r/blob/main/setting/budgets.md)). A town is **not** peasant-only: it has ALL castes, and the map must place the **actual documented number of each non-farmer household** (the Town caste table's Families column - one building each), not a token sample. Per `budgets.md`: **~24 merchant houses, ~29 laborer dwellings, ~13 servant households, ~12 burakumin, ~4 samurai families**, plus ~156 farmer households. There is **no peasant headman** at town scale; the state is the magistrate, in a **walled manor** (`s.manor(...)`, walls only - its interior is a separate Mode A diagram). Three modelling conventions adjust what is actually DRAWN (and the gate's target bands): **servants** mostly live inside the samurai/merchant compounds they serve, so only the ~5 "miscellaneous" households stand alone (the rest aren't drawn); **shops** are additional business premises counted separately from the ~24 merchant dwellings (not household-gated); **samurai** show as 5-10 houses with the ~15-strong working platoon barracked inside the manor (not drawn). The gate enforces these counts per caste (`town_caste_count[...]` - which sums the size variants below), farmer plurality (`town_farmers_plurality`), and the magistrate's manor (`town_has_magistrate_manor`). **Merchant and laborer housing varies in SIZE by wealth, exactly like a provincial city's** (`town_merchant_housing_varied`, `town_laborer_housing_varied`): per the `budgets.md` Town wealth tiers a MINORITY of merchants are very-rich/rich and live in large homes (kind `merchant_large`, ~4-5 of ~24) and a few laborers are 'master/rich' (kind `laborer_large`, ~2-3 of ~29), the rest in small/standard dwellings - the town is never one uniform house size. The `_large` footprints are too big for the tight frontage/pack grids, so place them **hand-set in cleared gaps AFTER all the seeded packs** (`s.building(x, y, *s._dims("merchant_large"), "merchant_large")`), which also perturbs no RNG. Mind `town_farmers_plurality`: the farmhouses must stay the largest single group, so the laborer total (small + large) cannot exceed them - Hoshizora caps its `laborer_large` at 2 for this reason. **The residential housing is also BANDED by distance from the road** (`merchant_residences_behind_businesses`, road-fronted towns only - those with a trunk `M["road"]`, e.g. unwalled Hoshizora; a walled town's interior grid is laid around cross-streets, not one radial axis, so the single-axis test is skipped there): a merchant family lives over or directly behind its own shop, so the **merchant residences** (`merchant_house`/`merchant_large`) sit in a tight band **directly behind the storefronts** (no merchant home may fall within the shops' own road-distance band), and the **laborer warren** sits **further back still, with a clear radial gap** between the two bands (the nearest laborer must be >= 35px deeper than the deepest merchant residence). The read is **shops front the road -> merchant homes -> gap -> laborers**, never the two house-types intermixed. Place the hand-set `merchant_large` in cleared gaps just behind the frontage (Hoshizora's sit at road-distance ~106-116px, the laborers begin at ~156px) rather than scattered through the laborer pack. **A home tucked DIRECTLY BEHIND a storefront must also share its ORIENTATION** (`housing_aligned_behind_storefronts`, same road-fronted-town scoping): a merchant family lives over/behind its own shop, so the dwelling lies PARALLEL to the shopfront, never askew - the pair reads as one aligned unit. "Directly behind" is judged in road coordinates (project onto the road): a home at nearly the same position ALONG the road as its nearest shop (in its radial shadow) and one building DEEPER (~15-74px). When the road runs at an angle the storefronts tilt to it, so the homes behind them must tilt to the SAME angle - pass `rot=` to `s.building(...)` set to the local road tangent (Hoshizora's diagonal Imperial Road gives `rot` ~-27deg; the merchant homes match, vs the default `rot=0` which left them visibly askew). Angles are compared mod 180 (a 180deg-flipped footprint is still parallel), and only the home-over-its-shop case is implicated - pack-edge laborers/burakumin that merely pass near a shop (deeper than ~74px, or offset along the road) are not.
- **Provincial city (`meta(scale="city")`):** **~2,000-4,000 inside the walls, average ~3,000 / 600 households** (per `budgets.md`): ~120 servant, ~240 laborer, ~150 merchant, ~30 burakumin, ~60 samurai households - and, structurally, **ZERO in-city farmers** (the farmers who work the city's land live in the surrounding villages/hamlets, counted in other totals). The samurai run ~10% (the governor R8, six provincial ministers R7 + their R6 deputies, R3-5 clerks, bushi) plus a small **foreign** Imperial yoriki sub-station auditing gate tariffs. A city map **places these households densely - near the full ~600** (Tango runs ~600 buildings) - on a crossing street grid, with a smaller building grain (`s.bscale` ~0.5) so the big merchant/samurai footprints fit. The gate checks the required **structures + neighborhood presence + the street network**, not an exact per-caste headcount, but the neighborhoods are sized to the real numbers (~240-310 laborer, ~120-150 merchant, ~50-60 samurai, ~30-60 burakumin). Domain capitals (~12,000) have their own numbers and are a **separate future mode**.
- **Farmland is sampled, non-farmers are not.** We deliberately draw only some of a town's ~156 farmer households (the rest implied by the off-edge field, below), but we draw **all** the non-farmer households at their documented counts - so the map answers "where does each caste live?" faithfully. Farmers must still be the largest single group on the map (`town_farmers_plurality`), so ring the fields deep enough that farmhouses out-number the laborers (the largest non-farmer group, ~29).

### Fields

- Outlines are terrain-following: organic lobes (outgrowths) and bays (indentations) off a semi-rectangular core. Never clean rectangles.
- Interior paddies are a mosaic of bunded basins of **non-uniform size** (vary the cell widths - break the grid), with wavy bunds, not straight. Each cell reads as its crop: flooded rice basins show transplant rows, soybeans grow as rows on the raised bunds (nitrogen-fixing), and ~1 in 6 plots is a dry/hedge crop (furrowed) guarding against blight.
- Crop rows are **regular within a plot but angled differently between plots** - each basin has its own row orientation, never one global east-west direction.
- Field arrangement is village-specific - it might be two staggered fields of differing orientation (Kikuta) or one great V-shaped field ringed by small ones (Hikari). The universal rule: when there ARE two large common fields, do not make them similar size and orientation side by side (vary and stagger); and spread the smaller fields around the settlement.
- Every field is ringed by farmhouses, all field-adjacent. Whether blight leaves **abandoned houses** is village-specific: Kikuta's fallow fields are ringed by abandoned houses; Hikari's blight is an internal fallow patch of the main field with no abandonment. (Declared via `meta.fallow_implies_abandoned`.)
- No paddy on hills (upland rice exists but is not chosen when flat land is available). Hills carry grass, woods, and shrines only.
- Strip-allocation (usufruct, per `village-headsmen.md`): the headman doles non-contiguous strips across the common fields; show via subtle subdivision and the religious figure's tax-free allocation highlighted as non-contiguous **plots** (a vermillion tint + outline - high-contrast against the greens, and thematically the shrine's sacred color; a thin gold outline blends into the tan bunds and was hard to spot). That allocation is law-bounded to ~2 households worth (**2-3 plots**, scattered across the common fields) - gated by `check_village.py`.

### Houses

- Stylized pitched-roof glyphs: two shaded roof planes (darker north slope, lighter sunlit south slope) with a ridge highlight along the long axis, entry on the south face. NOT flat envelope/box icons.
- South-facing convention: ridge runs E-W, facade faces south (historical minka sun orientation).
- **A fireproof *kura* grain storehouse is a WEALTH MARKER on ~30% of plain farms - an ATTACHED annex, never a free-floating shed** (`farm_sheds_attached`; recorded `M['farm_sheds']`, a `_CROP_HARD` feature). It abuts the house wall and is drawn WITH the house in `farmsteads()` so a move-procedure can never strand it (the regression that prompted the check: the kura got detached and drifted into the courtyard). The decision is position-seeded (`_hjit(x,y,3.0) < 0.30`, no RNG ripple) so it is stable across regenerations, and the kura's footprint is RESERVED in the homestead bundle bbox so a neighbour never lands on it. **Which wall:** a DISPERSED farm puts it on the WEST; a NUCLEATED-cluster house puts it on the shaded NORTH (back) wall - the cluster hugs the field to the east so the sunny walls are the garden's, and the north is the one side the garden never takes, so a north kura stays clear of it (`gardens_clear_of_sheds` reads the recorded footprint and guards this). Distinct glyph from the shared draft-animal byre below. The headman's house is the largest and carries no kura.
- Houses never overlap and never sit on a road/lane or have water running through them.

### Water, religion, state markers

- Villages **must** have a water source, but its KIND is village-specific (regional). Historically not every paddy village had a pond: a reservoir **pond** (*tameike*, never "tank") suits a dry/rain-shadow locale (Kikuta); a **stream diversion** from off-map feeding the main field, which then feeds the smaller fields field-to-field, suits a river valley (Hikari). A pond, if used, must be larger than a house, on flat ground, set back from steep hills (erosion). Channels route AROUND buildings (never through the headman's house), take a near-direct path, **wind gently** (not dead straight), and must be **anchored at both ends** - each end starts/ends inside its source (pond / off-map edge / another field) and well inside its target field, so the field paints over the end (a visible connection, not a line stopping in the gap). Anchors are declared per channel as `frm`/`to` in the manifest. Channels are **no-build corridors**: the generator reserves ~33px around each, which is enough to keep a farmhouse's whole **footprint** off it (its half-diagonal is ~26px), not merely its center - and `no_structure_on_channel` gates the rendered map with the *same rotated-rect footprint test the streams use* (a corner clipping a channel fails it, not just a center on it). The earlier center-only guard (`houses_off_corridors`, still kept for the lane) let corners slip through - that is how Hikari once shipped farmhouses sitting on its field-to-field channels.
- **Water flows downhill.** Streams and channels follow the land's slope. If a map declares its slope with **`meta(downhill=<dir>)`** (a cardinal name like `"south"`, or a `[dx,dy]` vector in map coords where `+y` is south), every channel must run *with* it - its source (the tap on the stream/pond, `poly[0]`) sits **uphill** of where it feeds the field (`poly[-1]`), gated by `channels_flow_downhill`. So when a north-flowing stream feeds a field to its east, tap the stream **upstream/north** of the field and angle the channel down to it; a channel run flat or angled back uphill would carry the water *away* from the field. Hirameki's hill is in the north, so it sets `downhill="south"`. Maps without the tag are exempt (slope unknown) - tag a map when its elevation gradient is clear (a hill, a falling valley).
- **The religious building scales with the settlement** (gated by `religious_matches_scale`): a **hamlet** has NONE; a **village** has a Shinto **shrine** ("Shrine to <Fortune>" / "village shrine"); a **town** has **monasteries** (each "Monastery of <Fortune>", with a torii in front); a **city** would have full **temples**. Never label a village site a "temple" - temples belong at town scale and up (see the `/temple` skill). If the GM says "temple" for a village, silently correct it to "shrine."
- **A town has TWO monasteries by default**, one for each of the **2 patron fortunes of the clan** whose holdings include it. Declare the clan with `meta(clan="Lion")`; the gate then expects exactly those two dedications (`town_monastery_count`, `town_monasteries_dedicated`). Patron pairs: **Crab** Bishamon+Ebisu, **Crane** Benten+Daikoku, **Dragon** Hotei+Ebisu, **Lion** Bishamon+Daikoku, **Phoenix** Fukurokujin+Hotei, **Scorpion** Benten+Jurojin, **Unicorn** Fukurokujin+Jurojin. **When generating a town, if the GM has not named the clan, ASK** - the dedications can't be chosen without it. Override the default with `meta(monastery_fortunes=[...])` (an explicit list, any length) for special cases: a town that **changed hands** (Hirameki is Lion-held but keeps a small older Benten monastery from Crane rule, so `monastery_fortunes=["Bishamon","Benten"]`), or a quiet town with just **one** monastery (Hoshizora, `monastery_fortunes=["Bishamon"]`). `town_declares_monasteries` requires every town to set `clan` or `monastery_fortunes` - this is also how the data persists across regenerations (it lives in the `.gen.py` source, re-run each pass). The same rule will extend to **city temples** at provincial-city / capital scale. Mark the principal monastery `primary=True`.
- **A town monastery fronts a torii AVENUE sized to its approach space** (`monastery_torii_scale_with_space`, walled towns only). Torii lead up to a hall along a processional way (*sando*); how many arches you draw is set by how much open approach there is in front of the monastery - the clear run from its front edge to the public street it faces, or to the field / wall / map-edge that cuts the approach short. A monastery with a long, open run to the market street shows **several** arches; one wedged hard into a corner shows a **single** arch. In Hirameki: Bishamon has a clear ~220px run south to the cross-street, so it fronts a 4-arch avenue; Benten is pinned between the west rampart and the Imperial chrysanthemum field (~50px), so it gets one arch. The check measures that span - deliberately **ignoring buildings**, since the avenue is a cleared way that displaces dwellings rather than being stopped by them - and wants roughly one arch per ~55px, within a +/-1 band. So you cannot under-build (1 arch where the approach has room for 5) or overflow (5 arches crammed into a corner). **This is a town-monastery rule ONLY; village shrines are exempt** - they carry 0-1 torii regardless of space. `meta(torii_expected=N)` is then the TOTAL arch count across all monasteries (Hirameki: 4 + 1 = 5), not the monastery count.
- The main shrine's placement is village-specific: it may sit on a **hill** (Kikuta's Benten, reached by a torii ascent) OR stand **on flat ground** reached by a torii gateway (Hikari's Benten sits at the southern entry path - a recent addition belongs at the village edge/gateway where there is room, not displacing an established structure). If on a hill it sits ON the summit, never overhanging the edge, with the torii ascent winding across the open face. Torii **count varies by village** (Kikuta 7, Hikari 2); whatever the count, they stay clear of the shrine hall and spread out. Each village district has one tax-free religious figure whose dwelling may double as the shrine.
- Fallow field: dry stipple, dashed organic outline (post-blight). Abandoned house: grayed and dashed with a collapse mark, clustered around the afflicted fields. Lanes and irrigation channels are no-build corridors.
- **No legend/key box, no descriptive subtitle, and do not label what is visually evident.** A settlement map should be self-evident: just the place name as a title (no "an average farming village..." subtitle/summary line), and inline labels only for the few non-obvious elements (fallow fields, the tax-free plots, named buildings). Do NOT state counts the reader can see (e.g. "2 torii" / "7 torii") - the torii are drawn. **No two body labels may overlap** (gated by `no_label_overlaps`). **No label may run off the edge of the image** (`labels_within_image`): plenty of things rightly run off the frame - farm fields, roads, samurai country estates, farmhouses, the countryside continuing beyond - but a label that spills past the edge is clipped and unreadable, so every label's bounding box must sit inside the frame. The frame is the cropped **view** (a village crops to its content, a city crops tight to the walls, so the `viewBox` bounds, not the full canvas) or, uncropped, the canvas; the title is placed directly (not in `M["labels"]`) and sits inside by construction. **The title is auto-placed over BLANK space** (`title_clear_of_features`): `s.title(name)` scans the framed window top-first for a text box that clears every feature (buildings, fields, water, groves, the pond) and drops the name there - so the place name never sits on the map content (it records its box in `M["title"]` and the check verifies it). Call it **AFTER `crop_to_content`** so the search runs over the final window. **There is NO compass** - the maps are always north-up (drawn convention), so a non-diegetic N/S rose added clutter for no information; it was removed project-wide. The fix for an off-subject label is to move the `s.label(...)` to where the thing it names is actually visible - e.g. Tango's "farmland" label moved from a spot west of the crop onto the visible moat-irrigated south field that runs off the bottom edge. **Label text renders ON TOP of everything** (`labels_render_on_top`): no part of a label may be covered by anything. `s.label(...)` / `s.title(...)` draw into a dedicated **topmost label layer** (`s.add_label`, above even the TOP layer of gate furniture / kido / torii), so the architecture guarantees it; the check guards the invariant by flagging any label whose bounding box is overlapped by a structure drawn over it (a higher draw-z) - the structures it knows the footprint of are the gate guard-houses/towers (`M["gate_structs"]`), the kido ward gates (which now record a `z` + `bbox`), and the torii. (This caught the kido ward gates painting over the "Ministry of Retainers" / "Ministry of Revenue" labels.) **A label must also not sit on a feature it does NOT name** (`labels_clear_of_other_buildings`, **town + city scale**): a label may cover the feature it labels - its own building/compound, or (for a zone/neighborhood label) any building of that cluster - and may clip a street-fronting shop or an interleaved servant house (those line every quarter, so they are never treated as a victim), but where it spills onto a DIFFERENT-identity building it tells the reader that building is something it is not. Each labelled structure (buildings by kind, flophouses, temples, ministries, the governor's yamen, gate furniture, merchant estates, manors) carries a GROUP; the label text declares which group(s) it may overlap (`"flophouse"`->flophouses, `"guard house"`/`"inspection"`->gate furniture, `"Ministry of..."`->ministries, `"Temple of..."`/`"shrine"`->temples, `"samurai..."`->samurai houses + estates, `"graveyard"`/`"cremation"`/`"mausoleum"`/`"ossuary"`->the FUNERARY group (the funerary structures cluster, so a funerary label may cover any of them, but NOT a temple), a `"...street"`/`"...road"` label->the merchant frontage it runs along, etc.; a farmland/market/title label names no building, so it must clip none). Funerary structures (cemeteries, mausoleums, cremation grounds, ossuaries) are themselves victims now, so a temple/monastery label may not sit on a graveyard and vice-versa - the Hoshizora "Monastery of Bishamon" went `label_below=True` and its graveyard `label_above=True` so neither sits on the other (`s.shrine_hall(..., label_below=True)`). Anything else its bbox overlaps fires. The fix is to nudge the label onto its own subject and off the neighbor - move the `s.label(...)`, or pass `label_below=True` to `s.flophouse(...)` so a flophouse jammed against the gate labels south into its cluster instead of up over the guard house. (This caught a "flophouse" label over a merchant estate, a "gate guard house + inspection" label over a flophouse, and the "Temple of Benten" label over a merchant house.) **A finer companion catches the SAME-GROUP case** (`city_civic_label_on_its_own_building`): because `labels_clear_of_other_buildings` lumps every ministry into one `ministry` group, it permits a ministry label to sit on a SIBLING ministry - so a NAMED civic building's label (a ministry by name, the governor's yamen, a named temple) must additionally not overlap any OTHER named civic building. (This caught the "Ministry of Justice" label sitting on the "Ministry of Works" office.) Fix by separating the two offices (which the abut rule below already wants) or moving the label onto its own building. **Naming convention (not gated, but apply it): reserve "quarter" for a genuine ~1/4-of-the-settlement division** - a CITY has quarters - and call a small TOWN sub-area a **"neighborhood"** (or "enclave"/"row"), never a "quarter": a town's outcaste cluster is a "burakumin neighborhood", its warrior cluster a "samurai neighborhood". (The city, Tango, already uses "neighborhood" throughout; the towns were the offenders.)
- **A building's subtitle must add information, never restate the label or the obvious.** Drop subtitles that repeat the label - a "Monastery of Bishamon" needs no "(town monastery)" note (`religious_subtitle_not_redundant` gates this for religious halls: the subtitle may not contain "shrine"/"monastery"/"temple") - and drop subtitles for the visually evident, like "atop the hill" under a manor that is plainly drawn on a hill. KEEP subtitles that genuinely add context: "(still tended)" on a shrine, "hunting lodge by the Shirin Forest" (what the compound actually is). Do NOT label a manor with the settlement tier ("county seat", "provincial capital") - that names the town, not the building, and is redundant on a town map.

### Towns and walled towns

**The unifying principle for towns: Rokugan's geography is modeled on China, its administrative culture on Japanese samurai society.** That is the *why* behind almost every town decision below - the Chinese **walled county-seat** layout (the wall defines the town; the magistrate's *yamen* on the gate-to-citadel axis; the whole urban population inside; farmland outside), zoned and defended *jokamachi*-style (samurai by the castle, merchants on the streets, outcasts at the edge). When a town choice is unobvious, reach for "what would a Chinese county seat administered by samurai do?" A town spec adds urban vocabulary on top of the field/house machinery:

- **Urban core - businesses front the streets, housing sits back.** The `shop` and `merchant` glyphs are **businesses** (drawn with a striped awning + hanging sign so commerce reads as visually distinct); `laborer`/`servant` are **housing**. Three gated rules tie them to the street network: businesses must **front a street** (`businesses_front_streets`), every street-fronting building must **face the street it lines** (`buildings_face_street`), and **housing stays off the main street's frontage** (`housing_off_main_street`, set back behind the shops). Place businesses with **`s.frontage(street, items, rows=2)`** - it lines a street with shophouses, each rotated to face it (pass `skip=<full road poly>` when fronting a sub-stretch of a longer road). Fill block interiors with **`s.pack(bbox, items, face_streets=True)`** - each building turns to face its nearest street, and spots that wouldn't front any street are skipped. Place housing with plain `s.pack(...)` in set-back bboxes (>92px from the main street). The **burakumin quarter** is its own pack, segregated at an edge (exempt - not part of the commercial frontage).
- **Streets and town layout (history).** Chinese towns - Rokugan's geographic model - were *planned*, not organic: a main avenue ran from the principal (usually south) gate straight to the **government office (yamen)**, which sat on that axis facing south, with the grid divided into blocks by cross streets. Early (Tang) cities walled those blocks into curfew *wards*; by the Song the wards opened into **shop-lined streets** (merchant shophouses fronting the roadbed), and commerce spilled **outside the gates** along the approach road (the *guan-xiang* suburb). Japan copied the Tang grid for its imperial capitals (Nara/Kyoto) but laid out castle towns (*jokamachi*) as zoned districts - samurai by the castle, merchants in trade-blocks, temples at the edge - with deliberately kinked, defensive streets. **So a town should read as blocks fronting streets, not a scatter of houses.** An unwalled town gets this for free if a road runs through it (Hoshizora's Imperial Road is its spine). For a walled town, draw the streets explicitly (below).
- **Road** - `s.road(pts, label=...)`, an Imperial or trunk road as a spine; a no-build corridor (wide clearance). Like streams, **a road runs OFF both map edges** (`edge_features_run_off_map`).
- **Where a road (or town street) CROSSES water, a BRIDGE carries it over** (`roads_bridge_water`, universal): a road does not simply run through open water. After laying ALL roads/streets and ALL water (streams, irrigation channels, the city moat), call **`s.bridges()`** once - it scans for every place a carried path intersects a watercourse and drops an `s.bridge(...)` (a planked timber deck with side rails, drawn on the TOP layer so it sits above the water and the roadbed) at each crossing, oriented along the road. The classic case is a **walled city's approach road crossing the moat at each gate** (Tango gets two, N and S); a country road crossing a stream gets a timber bridge the same way. The check re-derives the crossings from the polylines and fires if any lacks a bridge within ~40px - so a watercourse added *after* the `s.bridges()` call (leaving a fresh unbridged crossing) is caught; put the call last. A road merely running *alongside* water (never intersecting) needs no bridge and is left alone.
- **Where WATERCOURSES meet, they MERGE like a confluence** (`waterways_merge_at_crossings`, universal) - they must not stack opacity into a dark seam where they cross. Water is semi-transparent, so two overlapping strokes drawn independently double up to a darker patch at the junction (the old bug). The fix mirrors the road crossroads: `s.stream` / `s.channel` / `s.moat` / **`s.pond`** / **`s.field_channel`** (the comb-net irrigation channels) all defer through a shared **`_water`** block that renders, bottom-to-top, **all the pond RIMS (edges - the only water feature with a border), then all the beds (the blue water bodies, one colour) inside a single opacity group, then all the lighter sheens in another group above** - so overlapping beds composite as one continuous body of water (an X-crossing or a Y feeder-into-moat reads as a real confluence), and the check enforces it by recording each course's bed/sheen draw position (`bedz` / `sheenz`) and firing if any course's opaque bed is drawn *over* another's sheen at a crossing or feeder junction. Just route every watercourse through those methods (don't hand-draw a water `<path>` with `s.add`) and it holds automatically.
- **A pond JOINS its feeders at the rim, the same way** (the "`_| |_` not `_|_|_`" rule the streets already follow). The pond is not a standalone ellipse painted over its inflows; it goes through the SAME `_water` block, with its **rim in the bottom EDGE layer (below every bed)** and its **fill in the shared bed group as the TOPMOST bed** (`pond_fill=True`). So a feeder brook's or irrigation channel's bed, sitting in that bed group above the rim, **covers the rim where they meet** - leaving a clean gap in the shore exactly at the mouth, so the water reads as continuous (channel water flowing into pond water) instead of the rim drawing a hard border across the junction; and because the fill is drawn LAST among the beds, it paints over any feeder's small **inside-the-rim overshoot** - an irrigation channel's round end-cap bulging a few px past the rim, which was showing as a darker nub on the open water when the channel was drawn *after* the pond (a feeder drawn *before* it is hidden by the fill anyway). So the open water stays clean whatever the draw order. The other half of the trick is that a feeder must **stop AT the rim, not draw across the open pond**: `_clip_to_pond` snaps a pond-anchored course's endpoint onto the rim - trimming a run that lies *inside* the pond (a channel whose sluice sits within the water) or extending one that sits *just outside* (the sluice foot), for both the bed and the sheen. This is **deferred to flush time** (a course carries a `clip` spec through `_water`), so it works even when the feeder is drawn BEFORE the pond in the gen (the pond geometry, `M["pond"]`, is not yet known at call time). A course is treated as a feeder when its `frm`/`to` anchor has `kind=="pond"` (`_pond_anchored`) - so declaring the anchors (which the connectivity checks already require) also drives the clean join, no extra call. The visible result: on Ueda / Hoshigaoka / Kikuta / Moritono the inflow brook merges seamlessly into the pond and the supply canals branch out of its downhill foot, none of them laying a coloured stripe across the open water.
- **We do not draw all the farmland.** A town's fields are a representative sample; the rest is implied off-map. So **at least one field must run OFF the map edge** (`town_has_field_off_edge`) - a partial field at the margin signals "more farmland beyond." Off-edge fields are exempt from the ringing and irrigation checks (their houses/water are off-map too).
- **Every town has a THEATER STAGE** (`s.theater_stage(cx, cy, w, h, label=...)`; `town_has_theater_stage`) unless `meta(theater_stage=False)` - a roofed performance stage facing an open viewing ground (the East Asian troupe/festival venue: a temple OPERA STAGE / shrine NOH-kagura stage, NOT a Greco-Roman seating bowl). It **belongs to a temple/monastery precinct**: it must sit ADJACENT to a religious hall (`theater_stage_by_temple`, centre within ~260px of the nearest one) AND its viewing ground must **OPEN TOWARD that hall, not away** (`theater_stage_faces_temple`) - the stage faces the hall, the audience gathered between. Orient it with `rot`: the glyph's open side is local +y, so `rot=0` opens the ground **south**, `180` **north**, `90` **west**, `270`/`-90` **east** - pick the one that points the ground at the temple (Hoshizora's monastery is north of its stage, so `rot=180`; Hirameki's stage sits north of Benten facing south, `rot=0`; Tango's likewise). For a **walled** town it sits INSIDE the walls (`theater_stage_inside_wall`) unless `meta(theater_stage="outside")`.
- **The town granary is NOT a separate building by default - it lives inside the magistrate's compound (the yamen).** A county seat collects and forwards tax rice, so it has substantial grain storage, but at this scale that store sits within the administrative compound (both the Chinese ever-normal granary and the Japanese domain rice storehouse were kept at the seat of government), which on the map IS the magistrate's manor. So treat the granary as **implied by the manor** and do not draw a standalone one. The exception is a **rice-TRANSIT hub** - a town at a road/river junction where grain from many counties is gathered and forwarded up the kick-up chain - which historically grew a distinct fortified storehouse district. For that case only, set `meta(granary=True)` and draw a row of fireproof *kura* with `s.granary(cx, cy, n=...)` near the gate or transport route; `town_has_granary` then requires the granary to be present. The flag is **opt-in** (the inverse of the gate market / theater stage / monastery defaults): absent it, no granary is expected or drawn.
- **Several merchant houses keep an attached storehouse (*kura*).** Because most Rokugani farmers are **tenants**, their (often absentee) landlords' rent-rice and bulk goods are stored in town - over and above the ordinary inventory storeroom a shop already has. So a noticeable **minority** of businesses run the classic narrow-front / deep-lot merchant compound with a fireproof *kura* behind the shopfront. Call `s.merchant_storehouses(count=6)` AFTER placing the businesses: it tucks a small kura behind several merchants (the **back**, opposite the street-facing awning), drawn as an annex like the farmhouse `shed`, so it needs no open ground in the maximally-packed quarter (there is none). `town_has_merchant_storehouses` requires **>= 3** - more than a token one or two, but still a minority.
- **Draft animals live in the farmhouse, not a barn; rural settlements have no hay barns.** East Asian wet-rice farming is not herd-based, so there are no European-style barns. The peasant farmhouse - specifically its *doma* (earthen-floored work bay) - stalls the draft ox or horse **under the same roof**, including for winter, so the farmhouse glyph (with its optional attached `shed` storeroom) already implies both the storeroom and the animal housing. Do **NOT** add barns to a village or hamlet. Barns + grazing pasture (`s.pasture(...)`, `s.building(..., "barn")`) appear ONLY for a specific reason: a **road / post town's horse-relay infrastructure** (Hoshizora, on the Imperial Road - remounts + hayfields to feed them) or a **horse-breeding domain** (Unicorn / Moto). Town **stables** are likewise not drawn separately - warhorses are inside the magistrate's manor, travellers' mounts at the (unlabelled) inns.
- **A town has a market-day flophouse** (`s.flophouse(cx, cy)`; `town_has_flophouse`, default-on). A county seat exists partly *as* the periodic-market center for its hinterland: most peasants come from within a day's round-trip, but those at the far edge of the catchment stay over on market eve in cheap communal lodging - a *kichin-yado* ("firewood-fee inn"), where you sleep on straw under a roof for a sen a night. It is a deliberately **large, plain, barn-like dormitory** (no awning, a long row of plain doorways), distinct from a shophouse, because its whole value is volume at rock-bottom price - it beds dozens on market eve. Put it where travellers ARRIVE: the **gate market** of a walled town (outside the wall - the gate shuts at dusk, so a peasant arriving late literally cannot get in and sleeps outside until dawn), or **along the road** of an unwalled one. Default is one; `meta(flophouses=N)` requires more for a busy hub, `meta(flophouses=0)` opts out.
- **A town has ONE caravan inn + stables** (`s.inn(cx, cy)` + `s.stables(cx, cy)`; `town_has_caravan_inn`, always-on). A county seat sits on the trade route, so - exactly like a provincial city's gate caravan facilities, but a single one instead of several - it needs a prominent two-storey **inn** (lodging the wagon-train merchants, drivers and guards) with a large **stables** next to it (within ~150px) and **OPEN GROUND beside the stables** - a pasture for the oxen and horses (the check fails if >4 dwellings crowd within ~75px of the stables). The inn must also sit **ALONG the road - FRONTING the Imperial road (unwalled) or a town street (walled), with nothing between it and the roadbed** - so the caravans pull up to it, not hunt for it behind the shop rows (the `_fronts_route` test: within ~115px of a route and no building blocking the line to it). An **unwalled** town sites it along the road where caravans pass (Hoshizora uses the quiet SW approach); a **walled** town keeps the inn + stables INSIDE the rampart, fronting the main street (caravans enter through the gate). The inn must also **FACE the road and lie PARALLEL to it** (`inn_faces_the_road`): pass `s.inn(x, y, rot=...)` so the noren front faces the nearest roadbed point and the long edge runs along the road - a diagonal road needs a tilted inn (Hoshizora's is `rot=150`, parallel to the -30deg SW road; Hirameki's is `rot=90` to face the vertical main street). `s.stables(...)` takes a `rot` too if you want it to match, but only the inn's facing is gated. Place it (fixed coords, no RNG) after the seeded packs so it perturbs nothing.
- **A magistrate's manor sits at the EDGE of its settlement, and its gate faces what it fronts** (`manor_gate_faces_town`, hamlet + town): the town/hamlet it administers (the dwelling centroid) OR the Imperial road it sits beside. **There is no fixed default direction** - it depends on where the town is - so pass `s.manor(..., gate_dir=...)` intentionally; **`s.manor`'s fallback is `"south"`** (the auspicious/formal orientation), NOT a blind `"west"`. Moritono faces **west** (the hamlet is west of the forest-edge lodge); Hirameki faces **south** (down its hill to the town); **Hoshizora is TILTED** - `s.manor(..., gate_dir="north", rot=-30)` so its front wall runs **parallel to the diagonal Imperial Road** it fronts, gate opening onto the road. `s.manor(..., rot=<deg>)` physically tilts the whole compound (and its overlap footprint rotates with it). One caveat: a tilt changes the manor's footprint/block, which reshuffles a dense settlement's **seeded packs** and can knock the population off its mark - the fix is to **pick a seed that lands it back in band** (each of the dense towns/city carries a hand-picked seed for exactly this), which is cleaner than whack-a-mole on individual pack counts.
- **Pastures and large terrain** - hayfields/grazing (`s.pasture(...)`), forests (`s.forest_patch(...)`): a large area feature near an edge must **cross** that edge rather than sitting wholly on the map (`edge_features_run_off_map`). Barns sit within the grazing.
- **Streams stay between fields, never through them** (`streams_avoid_fields`): run a stream parallel to the road between field pairs. **Every fully-on-map field must SHOW a water source** (`fields_show_water_source`) - a channel ending inside it, or the field directly abutting a stream/pond. Being merely *near* water with no visible connection does not count, so tap the stream with a channel (`s.channel(stream_pt, field_pt, {"kind":"stream"}, {"kind":"field","name":...})`). Fields that run off the map edge are exempt (their water may be off-map too).
- **Nothing overlaps the manor, road, stream, or irrigation channel** (`no_structure_on_manor` / `_road` / `_stream` / `_channel`): the manor blocks a rect expanded by 36; roads, streams, and channels are corridors tested by the full rotated-rect footprint. Fields stay clear of the road (`fields_clear_of_road`). The "structures" every overlap check tests are houses + urban buildings **+ the standalone civic buildings** (the flophouse and granary *kura*) - those are real buildings and must not sit on a road / stream / wall / street / channel either. The one exclusion is the **merchant storehouses**, which are drawn as annexes *deliberately* abutting their shop, so they are not in the overlap set (they would trip the structure-overlap test by design).

**Walled towns** (`meta(walled=True)`) add a rampart:

- `s.wall(pts, gate=(gx,gy))` draws an irregular thick polyline (a wide no-build corridor - clearance ~46, so even a large building's corner stays off the rampart) with a gate gap, flanking posts, a guard station, and a guardtower. The gate records to the manifest. The wall must be **irregular** - `wall_sections_irregular` requires **>= 5 sections and a section-length coefficient of variation >= 0.25** (a spread test, so a wall may hug a feature with several short segments while a lazy near-regular polygon still fails); `walled_town_has_wall` requires both a wall and a gate. No structure may overlap the rampart stroke (`no_structure_on_wall`).
- A wall need not enclose a full ring. It can be an **open arc anchored to a steep hill**: the hill's back/sides defend that flank, so the rampart ties into the slope. Draw the hill with `s.hill(..., steep=True)` (emphasized hachures on the steep north back/upper flanks), and **run the wall's two ends partway UP the hill flanks** so the slope, not a blunt wall-end, closes each side. The magistrate's manor sits on the summit.
- A **chrysanthemum field** (`s.flower_field(...)`, the Imperial flower - "Heaven with the defenders") is kept INSIDE the walls, abutting the rampart. A field may **abut** the wall but must never cross it (`fields_clear_of_wall`). To make it read as genuinely flush (not a thin tan sliver): flatten the abutting edge with `flower_field(..., flat_west=True)` and run a **straight wall segment** right along it - center the wall ~5px outside the field's flat edge so the rampart's inner stroke (half-width ~5) lands flush on the field boundary. A wavy field edge against a straight wall always leaves a visible gap; flatten one, straighten the other, and they touch.
- **The whole urban core lives INSIDE the wall** (historical: a Chinese walled county seat / Japanese *jokamachi* enclosed its townspeople; the *graph* for "city," 城, means "wall"). Inside, zoned around the magistrate's hilltop citadel: samurai by the manor, then the merchant/shop streets, then the **laborers and servants in back-lane blocks** off the commercial avenue, plus the monastery - and open ground the surrounding farmers retreat into during a raid. **Outside the wall, only:** the farmland and farmhouses, the **segregated burakumin quarter** (outcasts on marginal land - historically correct), and a *small* **gate market** (*guan-xiang*) of a few shophouses along the road just outside the gate. The gate market is a **default, not an option**: a walled town is gated to have a few businesses (shops/merchants) outside the wall near the gate (`walled_town_has_gate_market`, >= 3 within ~420px), because the gate is a chokepoint where the rural population trades without entering the walls, travellers buy services, and vendors dodge the intramural tax and market regulation. Suppress it only for a real exception with `meta(gate_market=False)` (a purely military fort, a depopulated or suppressed gate). Do NOT push the laborers/servants outside to save room - that inverts the real pattern.
- **Fire defense - a WALLED town gets a fire-watch tower (an OPEN town gets none).** The packed wooden core of a *walled* town is where fire is catastrophic, and the rampart that keeps raiders out also traps a blaze in, so a walled town draws one (an open road-town, strung along the highway with field-gaps for natural breaks, gets none - that is the walled/unwalled split). Place a **fire-watch tower** with `s.fire_tower(x, y)` - a *hinomi-yagura*, an open braced-timber tower with an alarm bell, the magistrate's watch - among the **laborer/merchant blocks** (`walled_town_has_fire_tower`; `fire_tower_in_commoner_quarter` keeps it in the dense commoner warren, not the samurai quarter, and it is an overlap-checked struct so it must stand clear of the wall - distinct from the guard towers ON the rampart). Place it **LAST, after the packs and `farmsteads()`**, on a cleared seam the finished map actually has, so it perturbs no placement. Suppress with `meta(fire_tower=False)`. See the "Fire towers" historical grounding below.
- **Town scale ≠ village scale.** A town is ~1,200 people vs a village's ~350, so it needs a genuinely larger drawing: a bigger canvas (Hirameki is `Settlement(2600, 1820, ...)`) AND a finer building grain - set `s.bscale ≈ 0.82` so the urban glyphs pack denser (town buildings are smaller dots than village houses; the `bscale` factor only applies where set, so an unwalled town like Hoshizora can keep `1.0`). Sizing the wall like a village's is what forces the population outside - give the rampart room for the whole core.
- **Streets carve the interior into blocks.** Draw `s.street(pts, width=26, main=True, label="main street")` for the **gate-to-yamen axis** (extend it *through* the gate so the gate market frontage outside is on the same avenue) and `s.street(pts, width=20)` for one or two market cross-streets - *before* the frontage/packs. `walled_town_has_main_street` requires a `main=True` street running inward from the gate; `no_structure_on_street` keeps buildings off the bed. Then line the streets with `s.frontage(...)` (businesses, two-deep, facing the street; `face_streets=True` packs skip deep spots so commerce only lines frontage) and fill the laborer/servant **back-lane blocks** with `s.pack(..., face_streets="fill")` - the `"fill"` mode faces buildings near a street but still fills the deep block cores (tenement housing, which is `>92px` from any street and therefore exempt from the facing check). Give the laborer quarters their own back-lanes off the avenue so their dwellings front a *non-main* street (`housing_off_main_street` only bars them from the main commercial frontage). Because `frontage()` sits *against* the street it fronts (it skips that street's corridor), several streets no longer starve each other the way plain `pack()`-into-blocks did - a main axis plus a couple of cross-streets and back-lanes is fine.
- **No "street to nowhere": every street earns its existence by the buildings along it** (`streets_have_buildings` - no inside-the-walls stretch longer than ~130px may be empty of buildings that **front this street**). The rigor that matters: a building counts toward a street only if that street is the one it actually **fronts** (its nearest street, the one it was rotated to face) - a building sitting beside a north-south lane but fronting the east-west cross-street it is nearer to does NOT keep the lane alive. (An earlier, looser version counted any building within proximity and so missed lanes that dead-ended past their last real frontage.) **A building walled off from a street cannot front it either:** if a ward fence (`s.ward`) or the city wall lies between a building and the point it would front, that building serves its own side, not this street. (This was the Tango government-avenue bug - the gap-band laborers NORTH of the samurai ward fence appeared to "serve" the avenue SOUTH of the fence, papering over a 150px bare approach between the ward gate and the first ministry; the avenue had literally zero houses on either side. The fix lined the avenue's west end by fronting the Temple of Bishamon onto it. The threshold was a red herring - the check was counting frontage across a fence; excluding it surfaced the real 150px gap.) The *why* is historical: a street is **access infrastructure for the buildings it serves**, and it is paved or worn into the ground by the foot traffic to and from them - Beijing's *hutong* alleys "emerged as access routes lined by contiguous courtyard residences," and a desire path forms only between real destinations. A planned grid line that never gets built up simply **isn't drawn** (an undeveloped block). Note the asymmetry: **buildings with no street are fine** (the poor can't afford street frontage - they sit in the deep block cores), but **a street with no buildings is not**. When the check fails, fix it by **building demand**: (1) if there are buildings the lane could serve - dwellings sitting off in the cores, or documented households still owed - **route the lane to them or move them onto it** (lower-status dwellings readily line lesser back-lanes); (2) if the population is already fully housed (all the `town_caste_count` bands are met), the street has no reason to exist - **trim it back to its last building, or remove it** (this is what Hirameki's redundant lower cross-lane got: deleted, with no loss of laborer capacity since `face_streets="fill"` had already seated them in the cores). Don't add a street first and hope buildings find it; add streets where the buildings (or the demand for them) already are.

- **The wall must HUG the built town - don't wall in empty space** (`wall_hugs_the_town` - no contiguous stretch of rampart longer than ~280px may run more than ~140px from the nearest building, feature, or terrain). The *why* is pure economics: a rampart is the single most expensive thing a town builds (rammed earth or stone, maintained for centuries), and its cost scales with its **length**, so a town walls in exactly what it must defend and no more - the line is drawn to skirt the built-up area, not to inscribe a tidy circle around a lot of empty ground. Historically this is why town walls are *irregular*: they kink in to exclude a gully and bulge out around a quarter, following the settlement's actual footprint (and the terrain). Two kinds of slack are legitimate and the check allows for them: (1) **terrain** - a wall climbs or skirts a hill rather than levelling it, so the hill counts as filled "occupancy" (Hirameki's north rampart rides up the citadel hill and is exempt there); (2) a **modest gate forecourt** - the open *masugata* assembly/defensive square just inside the gate is real, so a short open dip at the gate is fine. What is NOT fine is a long empty margin along a whole face (Hirameki's first town-scale draft enclosed empty ground at three of its four quadrant edges - the east rampart ran ~260px from anything). When the check fails, the fix is almost always to **pull that face of the wall inward** to ride just outside the last buildings (this is what Hirameki's S/SE/E faces got - and the cross-street and east quarter were drawn in to match, since a tighter wall means a smaller interior); only rarely do you instead **fill the margin** with displaced buildings. Note this composes with `streets_have_buildings`: streets earn their length from buildings, and the wall earns *its* length from the whole built area - both reject infrastructure drawn around emptiness.

### Provincial cities (`meta(scale="city")`)

A provincial city is the next tier up from a county town - a provincial governor's seat, ~3,000 inside the walls. Like towns, some are walled and some unwalled; the worked example **Tango** (`pool/tango.gen.py`) is walled. The drawing vocabulary adds a few city-only methods; the gate adds a city block of checks. (Building counts are representative, not the full 600 households - see Scale and density above.)

- **The wall is a CLOSED ring with multiple gates.** Draw it with `s.city_wall(pts, gates=[...])` - a full loop (not the town's open hill-anchored arc), with a gap at each gate where the wall runs ~horizontal. Records `M["wall"]` (the ring), `M["gates"]` (all), and `M["gate"]` (the first, for checks shared with towns). The town-walled checks (`wall_sections_irregular`, `streets_have_buildings`, `wall_hugs_the_town`, the gate market, the monastery torii avenue) are **town-only** and do NOT apply to a city - a city's wall is a regular defensive ring with deliberate open ground inside (drill grounds, refuge space), not a cost-minimised skirt. `walled_city_has_wall_and_gates` requires a closed wall + **>= 2 gates**.
- **A MOAT rings the wall, fed from off-map.** `s.moat(wall_ring, gap=...)` draws the wall ring pushed outward - water all the way around. `city_moat_surrounds_wall` requires every wall point inside the moat ring; `city_moat_fed_offmap` requires a **stream from a map edge** reaching the moat (the off-map source). **That feeder must be as WIDE as the moat** (`city_moat_feeder_matches_width`): a trickle cannot keep a full moat supplied, so a stream reaching the moat must match its width within ~25% - pass `s.stream(..., width=<moat width>)` (the moat's `width`, default 26, is recorded in `M["moat_width"]`; `s.stream` records each stream's `w`). Cities with moats **often** irrigate their outside farmland by tapping the moat (optional, not gated). A stream may legitimately terminate at the moat (the `stream_runs_off_edge` check now allows a moat terminus, as it already allowed a pond). (Waterways - streams, the moat, the pond feeder - render as solid water with a lighter mid-current sheen, **no dashed centerline**: a dash denotes a road's lane, which makes no sense on water.)
- **The Imperial road runs straight N-S through the city, gate to gate** (`city_imperial_road_through` - the road must run off both the top and bottom edges). It quarters the interior together with the implied E-W axis. The **`"Imperial Road"` label must sit OUTSIDE a gate** (`city_road_label_outside_walls`): inside the walls the same roadway is a *city street* the city maintains, not an Imperial responsibility, so pass `s.road(..., label_xy=(x, y))` a point beyond a gate (the default label anchor is the polyline midpoint, which for a city is the center - inside the walls).
- **A city ON the Imperial road LINES that road with commerce** (`city_imperial_road_has_commerce`). The through-road is the city's prime frontage: caravans and travelers pass along it, so it carries a **commercial ribbon** of shops + traveler services (inns), not bare housing-backs or empty ground. This is a **general rule for ANY city with an Imperial road, walled OR not** (a city *without* a road skips it, its commerce staying in the market district). The check measures the road's portion running *through* the city - bounded by the **wall** if there is one, else by the **urban footprint** (the bounding box of the city's buildings) - and wants commercial buildings (`shop`/`merchant`/`inn`) fronting it at roughly **>= 1 per 130px** of that length - a floor that catches a bare spine. Build it as a true **two-sided market street**: `s.frontage(<road sub-segment>, ["shop", ...], skip=<the road poly>, both=False, rows=2)` for the west side and a second call (a north-running sub-segment) for the east, plus a `s.pack(...)` to deepen a road-market block and a `s.inn(...)` or two for travelers. Capture the road polyline in a variable so you can pass it as `skip=` (a frontage row sits *against* the corridor it fronts). The dead frontage to watch for is the stretch between two quarters that both turn their backs to the road (Tango's was the band between the burakumin quarter and the merchant avenue) - fill it. Place the ribbon LAST in the gen (after every quarter's pack) so it flows into the gaps and perturbs no RNG-seeded placement. **General guidance on commercial intensity:** a provincial city's merchant/artisan caste is ~25% of households (~150 of 600, the highest commercial share of any tier), so a healthy commercial face is roughly **1 storefront per ~25-30 residents** (Tango ~3,000 -> ~105 `shop`+`merchant`+`inn` frontages); a road-city sits at the denser end of that band, with the increment concentrated as the road ribbon. (Domain capitals, all of which have an Imperial road, always carry this ribbon.)
- **Each gate has a GUARD HOUSE + attached INSPECTION STATION + GUARD TOWER**, drawn automatically by `s.city_wall(...)` (top layer, so the road passes under). Caravans entering submit cargo to inspection and pay the Ministry of Revenue's import tariff (the "Yasuki Taka system"), audited by the Imperial yoriki sub-station. Gated by `city_inspection_station_at_each_gate` (an inspection station within ~160px of each gate) and `city_gate_has_guardhouse` (>= 2 gate structures - the guard house + tower - within ~180px). Like the towers, the guard house + inspection station sit **SQUARE TO THE WALL / ring road** they line - rotated to follow it, not axis-aligned (`city_gate_furniture_aligned`). Each piece is WALKED along the curving wall from the gate (`_wall_walk`) so it picks up the wall's **LOCAL** tangent *where it actually sits* - NOT the gate vertex's tangent, which has already curved away by then (Tango's gates are on ~8 deg wall stretches, not the flat top/bottom, so getting this wrong leaves the road slicing diagonally across a flat box) - then pulled in radially to the **ring road centerline** (inset matching `s.ring_road`), so the patrol road runs lengthwise *through* each building. `s.city_wall(...)` records each piece's `rot` and `kind` (`"guardhouse"` / `"inspection"` / `"tower"`) in `M["gate_structs"]`; the check compares the furniture's `rot` to the nearest wall edge's angle **mod 180** (these are rectangles whose long axis runs along the wall, so a 180 deg flip is the same but a 90 deg turn stands them across the road the wrong way), within **6 deg** - tighter than the towers' 15 deg, because the furniture is set from the exact local edge angle (not the towers' chord-through-neighbours approximation), so it matches near-exactly and a 15 deg window would wave through the shallow ~8 deg gate slope. The angle is folded to (-90, 90] so the two gates render alike whichever way the wall runs through them.
- **The wall is TOWERED at regular intervals** (`city_wall_towers_spaced`), not just at the gates. A fortified city - Chinese walled seat, Roman colonia, Japanese castle town - spaced its mural towers about a bowshot apart all the way round, for enfilading fire along the wall face (the towers also housed the stairs up to the parapet); towers are a function of the **wall's defensive geometry, not the interior street grid**. `s.city_wall(...)` places them automatically at every other wall vertex (skipping the gate vertices, which already have a tower), recorded in `M["wall_towers"]`. `city_wall_towers_spaced` wants >= 6 towers with no bare arc wider than ~75 deg around the wall centroid. The towers also sit **SQUARE TO THE WALL** (`city_wall_towers_aligned`): each is rotated to the wall's tangent there - a tower on a 30 deg stretch slants 30 deg, not an axis-aligned box - recorded as `rot` in `M["wall_towers"]`. The check compares each tower's `rot` to the nearest wall edge's angle, mod 90 (a square reads the same every quarter-turn), within ~15 deg.
- **A RING ROAD hugs the inside of the wall** (`city_has_ring_road`) - the Chinese "follow-the-wall street" (順城街), the wall-clear patrol zone a fortified city keeps for moving troops along the rampart. Call **`RING = s.ring_road(WALL, inset=34)`** right after `s.city_wall(...)`, then **`s.bound = RING`** so the quarters pack INSIDE the ring (off the wall), leaving the clear zone. It is deliberately NOT a `town_street`: a fortification road is exempt from the must-be-built-up rule (its wall side is bare, and stretches run behind fields/compounds). Pulling the bound in off the wall costs ~one perimeter row of buildings (~8% area), so re-check the population/caste bands after adding it - usually a small density bump on one or two packs. **Streets AND ALLEYS MEET the through-lanes cleanly at a T-junction** (`city_streets_meet_through_lanes`): a street OR a gravel alley running toward the **Imperial road OR the ring road** must reach the lane's bed and END there - neither a sliver SHORT of it (an undershoot: the lane appears to dead-end in open ground; note `city_streets_connected` is satisfied by merely touching the lane's outer *edge*, so a lane can be "connected" yet visibly stop short of the bed) nor a sliver PAST it (an overshoot: a stub poking through to the far side instead of stopping at the junction). It covers alleys as well as paved streets (the laborer warren's east E-W alley once stopped ~15px shy of the east ring road - an alley heading straight at a through-lane must reach it too). A genuine 4-way crossroads, where the street truly continues well past, is fine. The fix is to extend an undershoot to the lane's centerline (mind clipping a nearby civic compound - extend a street ALONG ITS OWN axis to the lane, not diagonally to the nearest point) and trim an overshoot back to just inside it. This applies to ALL streets, the government quarter's included - the ring road runs inside the ward too (its SE stretch along the wall), so the gov avenue gives onto its own stretch of the patrol road. That works because **where the ring road crosses a ward fence it is GATED** (a kido at each crossing), sealing the ward's patrol stretch from the commoners' - so the ring road is part of `city_samurai_ward_sealed`'s netlines (find the ring x fence crossings and add a kido at each via `s.ward(..., gates=[...])`). **And the ring road stays CLEAR** (`ring_road_kept_clear`): the whole point of a patrol road is an unobstructed lap of the rampart, so NO building, civic compound (ministry, the governor's yamen, temple), or field footprint may sit on its bed. The wall furniture that SHOULD line it - the gate guard houses, inspection stations, and mural towers - is exempt (those records aren't in the building/civic/field lists), and a ward fence may cross it (gated, per above). The engine keeps the packs off automatically: `s.ring_road` lays a no-build corridor `width/2 + 17` wide along the loop, so even a large or rotated footprint's nearest corner stays well off the bed (Tango's closest dwelling clears it by ~14px). The check fires if any footprint's corner falls within the bed half-width (~4.5px) of the ring centerline or the ring crosses it - the failure mode it caught on Tango was 2 ministry offices and an in-wall farm field straddling the loop; move them inward off the ring (mind that nudging a ministry can land it on the gov avenue - clear both). If you ever shrink the corridor or hand-place a compound near the wall, this is the backstop.
- **The provincial government is in the interior** (every provincial city, walled or not): the **governor's mansion** (`s.governor_mansion(...)`, a grand walled compound; `city_has_governor_mansion`), the **six provincial ministry offices** (`s.ministry(x, y, name)`; `city_has_six_ministries` - Rites, Revenue, Retainers, War, Works, Justice), and a **samurai neighborhood** (`city_has_samurai_neighborhood`). **The Ministry of Rites sits in the temple neighborhood** (`city_has_ministry_of_rites`, exactly one) because it oversees temples and shrines; religious buildings at city scale are **temples** (`religious_matches_scale`, all of `kind="temple"`), and a city can have **several**. Where a **city street runs straight UP TO a temple** (a street that terminates at its front, within ~28px), a **torii arch must stand on that street just in front of the temple** (`city_temple_approach_has_torii`) - pass `s.shrine_hall(..., torii=[(tx, ty)])` a point on the approach (the `torii=` param both draws the arch and reserves a no-build block around it). Torii render in the TOP layer, so the street passes *beneath* the arch (`city_torii_over_streets`); each torii records its draw-z in `M["torii"]` as `[x, y, z]`. Temples set back from the grid (no street running up) need no torii - Tango has a Temple of Benten and a Temple of Daikoku in the SW temple neighborhood and a Temple of Bishamon among the SE samurai (the warrior fortune). **And a temple's torii AVENUE should FILL the open approach space** (`city_temple_torii_fill_approach`): where a temple HAS torii (an established sacred approach) and there is still clear room along its street for another arch - measured AFTER all the housing is placed - it should be filled (Tango's Bishamon gets three arches down the samurai-quarter street, not a lone one). This NEVER asks a building to move: the next arch-slot is tested only against EXISTING open ground, so a temple with no torii, or one whose approach is built up right to the avenue's end, is left alone (0-1 torii is perfectly fine depending on where it sits). The torii block reserves only the arch's own footprint (`bm=20`, kept *smaller* than a street corridor) so adding arches along a street doesn't shove the frontage houses off it. **A temple NEIGHBORHOOD (>= 2 temples clustered within ~400px) should be dotted with a smattering of small wayside SHRINES** (`s.small_shrine(x, y)` - a vermilion-roofed shed with a little torii, non-residential, recorded in `M["religious"]` as `kind="small_shrine"`: it is not a dwelling, needs no torii avenue, and is exempt from `religious_matches_scale`). `city_temple_neighborhood_has_shrines` wants >= 3 of them near the clustered temples; a LONE temple among houses (the Bishamon temple in the samurai quarter) is not a neighborhood and needs none. Place the shrines BEFORE the packs (they reserve a no-build block) and clear of the temples/wall/streets. The merchant district and laborer neighborhoods are checked for presence too (`city_has_merchant_district`, `city_has_laborer_neighborhoods`). **The samurai neighborhood must be SIZED to the cohort**: a provincial city is ~10% samurai (~300 of ~3,000, budgets.md) = ~60 households, most housed here as individual houses (the governor's compound + extramural estates hold the rest), so `city_samurai_housing_sufficient` wants >= ~65% of the expected household count (~`0.10 x pop / 5`) drawn as samurai houses - a token cluster of a couple dozen is far too few. Pack them with `face_streets="fill"` (NOT `"core"`: the samurai quarter is laced with government streets, and "core" skips the near-street band, leaving almost everything unbuilt). And unlike the poor, **samurai LINE their streets** - `city_samurai_partly_front_streets` wants at least a third of samurai houses within ~90px of a lane (deeper lots sit behind, but the quarter fronts its streets). **In-city samurai houses VARY IN SIZE by rank, and NONE are walled** (`city_samurai_housing_varied`): budgets.md's provincial-city rank table splits the resident cohort roughly ~25% senior (R5-7) / ~75% junior (R1-4), so the neighborhood mixes a **minority of large senior houses** (`kind="samurai_large"`, the bigger urban dwelling) among **many small junior houses** (`kind="samurai"`). Interleave them in the pack (e.g. `["samurai"] * 4 + ["samurai_large"]` repeated) so the large ones front streets prominently. The check wants >= 3 large houses, the small houses a strict majority, **and NO `s.manor(...)` inside the wall ring** - samurai walled ESTATES sit OUTSIDE the walls (see below); the only walled samurai compound within the city is the governor's mansion. The **governor's mansion is legitimately LARGE** - the historical provincial *yamen* / governor's compound was a whole city block (main hall, residence, retinue quarters, jail, granary, gardens - dozens of buildings), drawn here walls-only with the interior implied; so its size is correct (`city_governor_mansion_large` enforces that it is the grandest compound - a city block at least as large as any estate and >= 3x any ministry), but like the ministries and temples it must sit INSIDE the rampart, clear of the wall stroke and moat (`city_government_clear_of_wall_moat`). **The five other ministries cluster AROUND the governor's yamen** (the government district, threading into the samurai quarter - as in a Chinese provincial seat / Japanese castle town), not strewn about (`city_ministries_cluster_at_government`, within ~480px of the governor); only Rites sits apart, with the temples it oversees. In a *planned* city the offices are not scattered: the yamen sat where the main streets crossed and the bureaus LINED the avenues around it (the Chinese 'official street'; the jokamachi grid where compounds align tightly along the streets), so **every ministry must FRONT a city street** (`city_ministries_front_a_street`, within ~85px of one) - lay a short government avenue (an L wrapping the yamen works well) and seat the ministries along it. A street fronted by ministries counts as served (the `streets_have_buildings` helper now credits civic buildings - ministries, the governor's yamen, temples, flophouses - as frontage, not just houses and shops). **A government office stands in its OWN ground** (`city_government_offices_dont_abut`): a ministry or the governor's yamen is a large, important compound and must keep a clear gap (~14px) from every other building/compound - ordinary city houses may touch each other, but these may not abut a neighbor (this caught the Ministry of Works touching the Ministry of Justice, and the Ministry of Rites touching the Temple of Daikoku). When two offices are too close, separate them - and mind the knock-on (the Rites/Daikoku fix needed the SE caravan inn nudged east to make room, since Rites is wedged against the ring road). The **internal streets** must likewise stay inside the wall (`city_streets_clear_of_wall_moat`) - the elliptical wall is a *polygon* whose chords cut *inside* the smooth ellipse, so size streets/compounds against the actual wall points (or just inset them), not the ideal ellipse.
- **A walled provincial city must keep >= 1 burakumin neighborhood INSIDE the walls** (`walled_city_has_burakumin_inside`): a city under siege cannot be without its burakumin (corpse-handling, leather, sandals, executions), so however many live outside, a neighborhood always sits within. Not all burakumin need be inside.
- **City streets form a crossing GRID of blocks** (`city_streets_have_buildings`, the same "no street to nowhere" rule as towns). Per quarter, draw a few crossing `s.street(...)` lines (2 N-S x 2 E-W = nine blocks is a good unit), then fill the whole quarter with **one dense `s.pack(bbox, [...], face_streets="fill")`**: the pack faces near-street buildings toward the streets (so every street is lined) and packs the block interiors with tight rows, while respecting the street corridors (so nothing sits on a street). **Density is what lines the grid** - the failure mode is a *sparse* pack leaving a street stretch empty, so pack to the real per-caste counts. Two rules of thumb learned building Tango: (1) don't draw two streets on the same line (a doubled corridor pushes buildings out of fronting range); (2) the big footprints (merchant 54x36, samurai 56x40) need `step >= ~0.8 x footprint` or they collide and place *fewer* - a larger step paradoxically packs more. (`s.frontage(street, [...], rows=N)` - front row plus tight rows behind - also works and is good for a single avenue, but multi-row frontages collide at grid intersections, so prefer dense `pack` for a true crossing grid.) **A larger city street must be LINED with buildings close to it** (`city_larger_streets_lined`): roadside land on a commercial/residential through-street is prime real estate, so no long stretch (>~140px) may run with the nearest building more than ~58px off it - stricter than `city_streets_have_buildings` (which tolerates a building up to ~105px away and so misses a street with a wide bare margin). Narrow gravel ALLEYS are exempt (the "small streets" threading block interiors need no frontage), and so is the GOVERNMENT avenue (its frontage is the spaced ministry compounds, governed by `city_ministries_front_a_street`, not shops/houses - the check exempts a street fronted by >= 2 ministries / the yamen). **Frontage `skip`-object GOTCHA** (this caused the very bug the check now guards): `s.frontage(street, ...)` sits tight against the street by *skipping that street's own corridor*, but it matches the corridor by **object identity** (`poly is skip`). If you pass `s.street(...)` one list literal and `front(...)` a *fresh* literal of the same coordinates, the skip will NOT match the registered corridor, the frontage gets blocked off the street by its own corridor, and the storefronts silently fail to place - leaving a bare avenue. **Capture the street in ONE variable and pass that same object to both** `s.street(AVENUE, ...)` and the frontage call. The grid must also be **one connected network wired to the Imperial road** (`city_streets_connected`) - run at least one street per quarter so its paved bed actually OVERLAPS the road's or another street's. Connectivity is **width-aware**: two lanes count as joined only when their centerline gap is less than the sum of their half-widths (the beds physically touch, so you can walk between them) - a street that stops even a roadbed short of the next reads as a *separate* network and fires (this caught the laborer grid ending 40px shy of the road). Extend the road-facing street of each quarter until it MEETS the road (to its centerline). Kido ward gates do not break connectivity - the street centerline runs on under the gate. Streets must also never come ALMOST together without meeting (`city_streets_no_near_miss`): a pair that closes to within ~2-30px but does not cross reads as a mistake (the classic case is a grid corner where a cross-street stops a sliver short of the through-street) - make the streets actually CROSS (extend one past the other) or pull them clearly apart, never leave a sliver gap. The mirror-image fault is a **stub** (`city_streets_no_intersection_stub`): a street that crosses another and then STOPS a sliver (~3-50px) past it. Fine to cross and run on (to the next block or the edge), or to terminate AT the junction (a clean L/T corner), but not to overshoot it by a little - so at a grid's outer corner, end the two outermost streets at their shared corner point rather than extending each past the other. **A related, more general rule covers ALLEYS too** (`city_lanes_meet_when_aligned`): if two lanes - streets OR alleys - are **heading straight at each other and stop just short**, with a CLEAR path between (no building, ward fence, or wall in the gap), they should simply **connect**. Unlike `city_streets_no_near_miss` (which only compares street-vs-street segment *proximity*), this fires on an *aligned endpoint* - a lane end whose outward direction points within ~37 deg at another lane within ~80px - so it catches collinear lanes that resume a sliver further on (the classic case: a quarter's vertical lane and a continuation lane in the adjacent strip, ending 10-56px apart on the same line). The fix is to extend one lane to meet the other (mind any check that scales to lane length, like `alleys_serve_buildings` - if extending a lane tips its served/length ratio, close the gap from the *other* lane's side instead). A gap that a building/fence/wall genuinely blocks is exempt (the lane is correctly routing around it). The rule is generic to any city with lanes, walled or not. **Lane RENDER LAYERING is enforced** (`city_lanes_layered_by_width`): where two linear features cross, the WIDER renders on top - the Imperial road over the city streets it crosses, streets over the alleys. The engine handles this automatically (`s.street`/`s.alley`/`s.road` defer to one ground layer flushed in width order, so a narrow lane never paints over a wider one); the check guards it via each feature's recorded draw-z. **And lanes meet as clean CROSSROADS** (`intersections_are_crossroads`): the engine flushes the ground block in three sub-layers - all EDGE strokes (the dark kerb-borders), then all BED strokes (paved surfaces), then all centre-marks - so every edge sits below every bed and the beds merge into a continuous junction. Without this, one lane's edge-border paints a line across the other's mouth where they meet (a street looks like it dead-ends at the road instead of flowing into it). The check guards the invariant: `M["ground_edge_zmax"] < M["ground_bed_zmin"]`. **WALLS render OVER the ground lanes** (`city_lane_under_wall`, `city_lanes_under_ward_fences`): a road/street/alley that runs INTO a wall - the exterior city/town rampart OR an internal neighborhood (ward) fence - must pass **under** it, not paint over it. The engine draws ramparts in a dedicated **wall layer** (`s.add_wall`, recorded `wall_z`) above the ground block and below the TOP layer, so this holds by construction; the city wall (and town arc) are drawn with a **genuine gap** at each gate (`_gapped_ring`) rather than a land rect painted over the stroke - so the wall can sit on top yet the road still shows THROUGH the opening (a painted-over rect would erase the road once the wall is on top). A lane only breaches a wall at a gate, where there is no stroke to render over, so touches/crossings within ~50px of a gate are exempt. The checks fire if any lane that meets a wall has a draw-z >= the wall's (i.e. it would render over it) - so if you ever draw a rampart with plain `s.add` instead of `s.add_wall`, or a lane ends up in the wall layer, it is caught.
- **Shops front the streets; poor housing fills the block INTERIOR, reached by alleys** (`businesses_front_streets` - almost every `shop`/`merchant` within ~85px of a street; `poor_housing_mostly_interior` - most `laborer`/`burakumin` are NOT). The historical pattern: a street grid divides a quarter into blocks, businesses line the paved streets (`s.frontage(street, ["shop", ...])`), and the residential majority jams the deep interior (`s.pack(bbox, ["laborer", ...], face_streets="core")` - "core" leaves the street-facing band for the shop frontage and packs dwellings only DEEP, off the street). **The more mercantile a quarter, the more streets**, but **merchant HOUSING is varied and roomy - the deliberate contrast to the laborers** (uniform, small, jammed tight). Behind the storefronts the homes mix by wealth band (`city_merchant_housing_varied`; budgets.md provincial-city merchants, of ~600 households: very rich 8% -> **walled estates** via `s.merchant_estate(x, y)`, a fenced court with a large house inside, recorded in `M["merchant_estates"]` NOT `M["manors"]`; rich 12% -> **large houses** `kind="merchant_large"`; poor+other 80% -> **small houses** `kind="merchant_house"`). And they are **SPREAD OUT** - `city_merchant_housing_spread` wants the merchant homes' median neighbor gap >= 1.3x the laborers' (pack with a bigger `step`; a few denser blocks are fine, the median is robust). Walled estates need DEEP block cores, so do NOT over-street the merchant quarter into shallow strips: a couple of N-S streets off the avenue leave top-to-bottom blocks the estates and roomy homes fit into. **The estate COURT (the fenced rect, not just the house inside it) is checked for clearance** like any compound: it must sit clear of the wall and moat (`city_merchant_estates_clear_of_wall_moat`) and not overlap any other building - temple, compound, house, or another estate (`city_merchant_estates_clear_of_buildings`; the estate's own inner house, centered in the court, is exempt). So keep estates in the block CORES, well inside the rampart and clear of the temple neighborhood - not jammed against the west wall or sitting on a temple. It IS fine (and historically common) for an estate WALL to abut a neighboring building, but then its **GATE must open onto open ground, never into that building** (`city_merchant_estate_gate_clear`): pass `s.merchant_estate(x, y, gate_dir=...)` the side facing clear space (the estate abutting the Temple of Benten opens NORTH, not south into the temple). The gate point + direction are recorded in `M["merchant_estates"][i]`, and the check rejects a gate whose threshold fronts a compound (temple, ministry, the yamen, or another estate court). A laborer quarter, by contrast, has only a couple of through-streets and very deep blocks. Those deep blocks are subdivided by **unpaved gravel/plank ALLEYS** (`s.alley(pts)` - thinner than a street, a pale speckled lane, a narrow no-build corridor so the dense core leaves a gap), the lanes the interior housing is actually reached by. **An alley is checked like a street** - it must not run over a building (`no_structure_on_street` covers alleys too), nor cross the wall or moat (`city_streets_clear_of_wall`, `city_streets_clear_of_moat`); place alleys BEFORE the frontage so no building sits on a lane, and clip them inside the wall (the elliptical rampart curves in). **No GIANT cluster may be cut off from circulation** (`no_isolated_dwelling_cluster`): housing packs deep, but a big contiguous block of dwellings with no street OR alley within ~95px has no way in or out - lace every deep block (including any space-filling band, like Tango's strip between the laborer and government quarters) with alleys so every dwelling is reachable. Small deep cores are fine; the check flags only a large (>30) isolated cluster. And an alley must EARN its length by UNIQUELY serving dwellings (`alleys_serve_buildings`): each building is credited to its NEAREST lane only (the one it actually fronts), exactly as `streets_have_buildings` scores streets, so a lane counts only what no other lane already reaches. This catches BOTH a lane running off into a half-empty corner (a lane to nowhere) AND a **redundant** lane - one laid beside or across another that already serves the same block. The rule of thumb is **one lane per block**: a block's interior needs a single spine to reach it, so don't add a perpendicular cross-arm the spine already reaches (Tango's west laborer blocks share one continuous vertical spine at x1810 and carry no horizontal cross-alley; only the east block keeps its own L), and don't shadow a street with a parallel alley a few tens of px away (the street already fronts those dwellings). The threshold scales to the buildings (~1 uniquely-served dwelling per 30px of the alley's own length), so it bites at the city's dense small-footprint grain rather than relying on a fixed town pixel-gap. **Exception: a gate-access spur.** An alley that runs out to a **ward kido gate** past its last building is providing gate access, not block-service, so that terminal stretch (from the gate to the nearest building the alley fronts) is subtracted from its length before the ratio - extend a commoner alley to its ward gate freely without tripping this check. Why nearest-lane and not raw proximity: a redundant alley still sits *near* plenty of buildings (raw proximity is fooled into passing it), but those buildings are nearer the spine or street that truly serves them, so the redundant lane's UNIQUE tally collapses toward zero and it fails. Samurai (not poor) may front their streets; merchants front as businesses AND count as dwellings (dual-use). **Laborer HOUSING varies by wealth too** (`city_laborer_housing_varied`): like the samurai (senior/junior) and merchant (estate/large/small) tiers, the hinin are not uniform - budgets.md's provincial-city laborer cohort is ~12.5% **"master" (rich) laborers**, the rest standard. So a clear minority get **larger homes** (`kind="laborer_large"`, ~50x34) among the overwhelming majority of small standard `laborer` dwellings (~34x24). Place the large ones on the **prime back-street FRONTAGE** (the wealthier laborers take the roadside lots, with room around them) via `s.frontage(...)`, and keep the standard poor laborers jamming the deep interior cores (`face_streets="core"` packs). The check wants `laborer_large` at roughly **6-20%** of laborers (a generous band around the 12.5% target, since the big homes are room-limited to street frontage) and a strict standard-laborer majority; `laborer_large` is exempt from `poor_housing_mostly_interior` (it is the wealthy tier, so it *should* line streets).
- **`s.bound` makes placement fill the wall's SHAPE.** Set `s.bound = [list(p) for p in WALL]` after drawing the wall; every `pack`/`ring`/`frontage` then rejects positions outside it, so a dense pack over a generous bbox fills the elliptical ring cleanly (partial blocks at the rampart) instead of a rectangle with empty corners. Reset `s.bound = None` before placing anything OUTSIDE the wall (the estates, off-map fields).
- **A small city GATES its samurai/government ward; it does not WALL every neighborhood** (`city_samurai_quarter_gated`, `city_samurai_ward_sealed`). The walled-ward (Chinese *fang*) system - a continuous internal wall around every quarter - was a great-capital, Tang-era feature (Chang'an's wards were 30-80 hectares each, one outsizing a whole provincial city) and was torn down by the Song. At provincial-city scale the samurai/government quarter is **sealed by a light earthwork/palisade fence** (`s.ward(name, boundary, gates)`) whose ends abut the city wall, pierced ONLY at **kido** wooden ward gates (`s.kido`) where a street crosses it - so the gates can't simply be walked around and the road network has no ungated samurai<->commoner path. The gen must run the quarter's access streets THROUGH the fence line (extend them to the road) so each crossing is a gate. The check wants a ward declared, >= 2 kido near the yamen, every street/road/**ring road**/alley crossing the fence gated within ~32px, and both fence ends within ~45px of the wall. (The ring road is a closed loop near the wall, so it crosses the fence at the two points where the fence ends approach the rampart - gate both.) Keep commoner packs/alleys OUTSIDE the fence. **A commoner lane that APPROACHES the ward fence must reach it and END AT A KIDO GATE** (`city_lanes_reach_ward_gates`) - laborers come in to work in the samurai quarter, so the lanes they use pull right up to a gate rather than stopping a sliver short. (`city_samurai_ward_sealed` only catches lanes that *cross* the fence ungated; this catches lanes that stop *short* of it.) A lane endpoint within ~60px of a ward fence, heading at it (within ~37 deg) with a clear path (no building or the main wall between), must sit on the fence (within ~6px) AND have a kido within ~34px, else it fires. Two important distinctions: (1) **the MAIN city wall is exempt** - a lane may stop short of the outer rampart (that is the city's own edge, not a neighborhood boundary); only internal ward fences pull a lane in. (2) **Interior ward lanes are exempt** - a government/samurai lane near the fence from the INSIDE is not an entry; the check uses an interior anchor (the governor's yamen, else the fence centroid) and skips any endpoint on the same side as it. When you extend an alley to a gate, the run-out *past its last building* is a legitimate **gate-access spur** and does not count against `alleys_serve_buildings` (that check now subtracts the spur for an alley ending at a kido). Add the gates via `s.ward(..., gates=[(x, y, horizontal), ...])` - `horizontal=False` for an N-S lane crossing an E-W fence leg. **The fence ends must MEET SOLID wall** (`city_ward_fence_meets_wall`): not merely near the wall *polygon* (a point can be 40px inside the rampart yet read as on it, since `poly_dist` is 0 for interior points - use `edge_dist`), and NOT landing in a **gate opening** (where the wall is cut, so the fence meets nothing and the ward can be walked around) - extend the fence to solid rampart, jogging clear of the gate if need be. **And the wall renders ON TOP of the fence** at the junction (`city_ward_fence_under_wall`): the fence runs UNDER the rampart, not painting over it - `s.ward` lays a short wall cap (recorded with the fence's z in `M["wards"][i]`) over each end so the rampart wins. (Full walled wards become plausible only at the future capital-city tier, ~12,000.)
- **Crop the frame to the CONTENT so the image is only as big as what it shows** (`s.crop_to_content(margin=30)`; a nonstandard image size is fine and expected). This is the GENERAL village-layout step (the city's tight-to-the-walls crop below is the same mechanism, hand-sized). WHY: a fixed canvas leaves dead margins - a village's paddy runs off to one side, the settlement sits in a corner - so `crop_to_content` computes the bounding box of the **HARD** features + `margin` and `set_view`s to it, tight to `margin` on every side. The frame is set ONLY by hard content: `_CROP_HARD` + the fields' **visible** extent + the pond. The fields use a recorded `vis_bbox` (the drawn plots), NOT the house-blocking envelope polygon - that envelope can have an invisible tail past the last plot, and framing to it re-introduces dead space (a real bug: the south margin was once ~210px too low by the envelope's tail).
  - **Everything else clips at the frame; it never extends it.** The **commons** scrub AND the linear/off-map **runners** (streams, channels, lanes) are drawn and simply CUT at the crop edge, trailing off as "more wild ground / more map this way". We used to *extend* the frame to preserve ~2/3 of a trailing commons ("bleed"), but that repeatedly held the frame open on empty back-slope grazing - past the graveyard on the west, ~130px past the lone pond on the east - which is exactly the dead space the crop is meant to remove. So the commons now clips like the marsh: **the frame hugs the real settlement (houses, fields, pond, groves, graveyard), never the scrub around it.** (If a map ever *wants* a wilderness feature to widen the frame, add it to `_CROP_HARD`, don't reach for a bleed rule.)
  - **Call order matters for SET-APART hard features.** Anything the frame must CONTAIN has to be placed BEFORE `crop_to_content`: the large features (water, fields, houses, groves) and also any **outlying** hard feature that sits beyond the cluster - a back-slope **graveyard**, an outlying **shrine** - or the tight crop will clip it (this bit Ueda: its west-set graveyard, placed after the crop, got sliced once the frame stopped being padded by scrub). Only features that DROP INTO the framed space (wells among the houses, monk plots) and the **title** go AFTER the crop - the title is placed last so it lands inside the window (there is no compass - see the label note above). **`hard_features_within_frame` gates this** (village/hamlet): every hard feature - houses, groves, the graveyard, the shrine, wells, the torii - must lie INSIDE the cropped `meta["view"]`, so a deferred feature that lands off-frame and gets silently clipped is caught rather than only spotted by eye.
  - Linear / off-map **RUNNERS** (streams, channels, the connector lane) are ignored - they clip at the frame, and their off-edge checks already read the crop (`meta["view"]`) as the map edge, so a connector/feeder/brook that ran to the old canvas edge still "runs off" the new one.
  - **Crop advisory (SOFT default, `crop_relocatable_singletons` / the `ADVISORY crop_could_tighten` line):** unlike the hard invariants, this NEVER fails the gate - it prints a hint. It flags the case where a SINGLE, freely-relocatable feature is the ONLY thing holding a crop edge out, and there is EMPTY space to move it into - so moving that one feature crops the image significantly smaller without disturbing anything else. Fires only when ALL hold: (1) a village/hamlet that crops to content (a city/town frames bespoke); (2) removing the feature pulls an edge in by **>= 150px** (it is the unique extreme, big slack); (3) the feature is not **hydrologically or terrain anchored** - a **pond that sources a field** (a channel `frm=pond -> to=field`) is a valley-head reservoir that belongs uphill of the field, and a shrine whose centre sits on `M["hill"]` can't move to flat ground; both are exempt automatically; (4) an equal-size EMPTY landing, clear of all SOLID occupancy (buildings, fields, groves, forest, marsh, hill - **but not the sparse commons scrub, which a pond can simply replace**), fits INSIDE the tighter frame. Because the field-sourcing-pond exemption covers every irrigation pond, the advisory currently fires on **no** pool map - it stays a general detector for a genuinely free feature (a standalone/decorative pond, a mis-sited cemetery). A map that still WANTS to silence a fire on an intentional placement can set `meta(crop_advisory=False)`. Not a manifest-recorded check, so no regression fixture - covered by `test_checks` unit tests.
    - **The valley-head pond, and the NUDGE (the right way to reclaim a pond's poke).** The check now *auto-exempts* a field-sourcing pond (above), because moving it into the frame would drop it downhill of the field's water-entry - backwards for a gravity feed. So you do **not** relocate it. **But you can still reclaim the cheap part of the slack with a small NUDGE.** A pond only needs to *contain its sluice* to feed the field, so draw it decoupled from the `build_comb` anchor (keep `POND`/`SLUICE`/`build_comb`/feed-anchor untouched - the field is unchanged - and draw at a separate `POND_DRAW`), sliding it a little so its far edge sits **flush with the field/dry-plots** instead of poking past. The sluice stays inside the drawn ellipse, so the `frm=pond` topology still anchors. Ueda's pond is nudged ~157px W (E edge flush with the dry-field strip, ~9% smaller image); its remaining N poke is the irreducible valley-head cost and is simply accepted. Re-orienting the whole field to dodge the poke is **not** worth it: a pure mirror (NE-high -> NW-high) just flips which side the pond pokes and erases the deliberate variance between sibling villages.
    - **Where a nudged pond's channels meet it, they must JOIN at the RIM, not overlap the water.** Nudging the pond so it *contains* the sluice means the head-race would otherwise be drawn starting *inside* the pond (over the water). Clip each channel's in-pond vertices to the pond boundary before drawing (Ueda's `_clip_pond`) so it emerges cleanly from the rim - the same "join, don't overlap" rule roads/streams already follow. Clip the DRAWN geometry only; the recorded channel keeps its sluice endpoint so `frm=pond` still anchors. (An inflow brook drawn *before* the pond is already covered by the water, so it needs no clip.)
- **A city map crops TIGHT to the walls** (`s.set_view(ox, oy, w, h)` directly, rather than `crop_to_content` - a city is framed to its WALL, not its scattered outside estates). A town map is as much about its surroundings as the town; a **city** map is about the city - the countryside around it matters little, so the rendered window should *just barely encompass the walled city* (wall + moat + a ~50px margin for the title), zooming the city itself as large as the canvas allows. Compute it from the wall: `s.set_view(CX-RX-52-M, CY-RY-52-M, 2*(RX+52+M), 2*(RY+52+M))` (52 ≈ moat gap + wall stroke, `M` ≈ 50). Placement still uses the full coordinate space, so the estates, outside farmland, and the far rows of the gate market simply **run off the cropped edge** - they need not be fully rendered. Crucially, the **checks read `meta["view"]` and treat the crop, not the full canvas, as the map edge**: "runs off the edge" (road, moat-feeder stream, off-map fields) means off the *view*. So size the view to clear the moat but let the estates fall outside it. The title sits top-left *inside* the view (it follows automatically). The off-wall estates remain recorded (`s.manor`), so the 5-15 / SE / no-overlap estate checks still pass even though most estates are cropped from the picture.
- **Size the wall to HOLD the city - no large empty swaths inside** (`city_no_large_empty_space`, ported from the town's `wall_hugs_the_town`). A wall encloses what it must defend; an oversized ring with a big empty perimeter would not have been built. The fix is the inverse of the town's: rather than pulling a wall in around a fixed built area, **size the ring to the ~600 households and fill it densely** (drop `s.bscale` to ~0.42 so the footprints are small enough to pack ~600 into a hugging wall). A city keeps SOME open ground (the road's right-of-way, a drill/leisure ground), so the check is generous - it flags only a *large contiguous* empty region (no buildings, civic structures, fields, or pond) - currently a cluster of cells each > ~120px from anything, e.g. a band left between two quarters that do not meet. Fill such a band (extend a quarter / drop in a neighborhood) rather than leaving it open.
- **Civic amenities port up from the town tier** (a city is a bigger version of the same market center): merchant houses keep fireproof *kura* (`s.merchant_storehouses(...)`, `city_has_merchant_storehouses` >= 5), there is market-day lodging (`s.flophouse(...)`, `city_has_flophouse`) - one INSIDE the walls (`city_flophouse_inside_walls`) AND one just OUTSIDE EVERY gate (`city_flophouse_outside_each_gate`, within ~520px), for travelers who reach a gate from either direction after it has shut. The **in-wall flophouse is a humble doss-house and belongs in a humble quarter** (`city_flophouse_in_humble_quarter`): the laborer section, or - Tango's case - the agrarian sector, never within ~110px of a temple/merchant/samurai (the nicer neighborhoods) and never within ~150px of (in or beside) the burakumin quarter. **Each city gate gets a CARAVAN transit cluster just inside it** (`city_gate_caravan_facilities`): a town inn can hide its small stables, but a wagon-train arriving at or passing through a city brings **many dozens of draft animals (oxen, packhorses) plus guards, porters, and drivers**, so within ~340px of each gate there must be all THREE of a **flophouse**, a prominent **inn** (`s.inn(x, y)` - a warm building with a red hanging sign), and a **large stables** (`s.stables(x, y)` - stall-division lines), with **open ground around the stables** (the check fails if more than 4 dwellings crowd within ~75px - the animals need room to be tied up or penned; no need to draw the fencing). This caravan cluster is the one place an in-wall flophouse may sit beside an inn/stables rather than alone (the humble-quarter check exempts a flophouse that has both an inn AND a stables within ~170px). There is also a theater stage (`s.theater_stage(...)`, `city_has_theater_stage`), which - like a town's - must sit ADJACENT to a temple (`theater_stage_by_temple`); a **CITY theater stage is bigger than a town's** (`city_theater_stage_larger_than_town`, viewing-ground width >= 185, vs the town baseline ~120-150). Tango's sits in the Temple of Benten's precinct (the two merchant estates that crowded Benten were moved to the freed central ground). Place the theater stage/flophouse BEFORE the dense packs so the packs flow around them. (Not ported: the farmer-plurality - a city has zero in-city farmers (but it DOES have caste-count bands of its own, `city_caste_counts_in_band` - see Scale and density above); the monastery clan-patron system - a city has temples; `wall_sections_irregular` - a city wall is a regular defensive ring; the hill and gate-market rules.)
- **Cramped city lots push the wealthiest samurai to walled ESTATES OUTSIDE** the wall, which they commute in from - they can afford grander houses than the limited intramural space allows. A provincial city has **5-15** of them (`city_samurai_estates_outside`, `s.manor(...)` beyond the wall) and they **vary in size** (`city_samurai_estates_vary_in_size` - the largest at least 1.5x the smallest; some estates are far grander than others). The estates all lie to the **southeast** (`city_estates_in_southeast`) and are scattered (spread out, varying sizes). They must **not overlap each other** (`city_estates_no_overlap`) nor **cross the wall or moat** (`city_estates_clear_of_wall_moat`). **Their gates do NOT all open the same way** (`city_estate_gates_vary`): each scattered country estate fronts its OWN approach lane (not drawn at this scale), so pass `s.manor(..., gate_dir=...)` a MIX - the formal gate favours the auspicious **south**, others face the cityward (NW) approach (west/north) - rather than letting them all fall to the default. (The depicted gate is the formal one; the cityward service gates, like the governor's unshown secondary gates, are omitted at this scale.) Because the view crops tight to the walls, place the estates so **at least two fall (even partly) inside the window** (`city_estates_multiple_shown`) - estates straddling the SE edge, cropped, signal *several* estates rather than one; the same barrier rule applies to the **temples** - all of them sit INSIDE the walls (`city_temples_inside_walls`) and clear of the wall stroke and moat (`city_temples_clear_of_wall_moat`).
- **The surrounding farmland is close, farmhouse-worked, and (if moated) irrigated from the moat.** A city grows up around fertile land, so its outside fields sit **close** to the walls (`city_fields_close_to_city`); every outside field is worked by villagers, so each has **farmhouses ringing it even if it runs off the map edge** (`city_outside_fields_have_farmhouses`, >= 2 houses - draw the field, then `s.ring(bbox, n, gap, ["plain"])`; some fields still run off-edge per `city_has_outside_farmland`). Crucially, the SHOWN portion of each outside field must carry farmhouses at roughly **village density** (`outside_fields_farmhouse_density`, town + city): the check clips the field to the rendered window, measures the **on-map field edge** (the real boundary inside the view, excluding the map-edge cut), and requires **in-view** farmhouses at >= ~7 per 1000px of that edge (village fields run ~4-19, aggregate ~12). A field ringed only around its full, mostly-off-map bbox leaves almost nothing in view (Tango once showed 3) and fails - **ring densely so enough farmhouses land on the on-map edge** (most fall off-map, which is fine). At **city** scale **no farmhouses count toward the city population** (`population_consistent_with_housing` counts only the urban residential kinds for cities - the farmers, surrounding OR in-wall, are not in the documented ~3,000 urban castes, budgets.md), so you can ring as densely as the view needs without touching the figure; at town scale the depicted farmhouses ARE the (partial) county farmer cohort and do count. Fields must not cut across the wall stroke or the moat (`city_fields_clear_of_wall_moat`) - they abut the moat, not overlap it (mind the polygonal wall/moat, which curve in near the gates). A **moated** city always irrigates **>= 3 large fields from the moat** (`city_moat_irrigates_fields`): tap the moat ring with a channel - `s.channel(moat_point, field_point, {"kind":"moat"}, {"kind":"field","name":...})` (a new "moat" channel anchor; capture `MOAT = s.moat(...)`). **The moat has a flow direction, and channels must run WITH it** (`moat_channels_flow_with_current`): the feeder stream enters from one side and that heading, snapped to a cardinal, is the way the moat water flows (Tango's feeder enters from the north, so the moat flows **south**). Tap a moat vertex **upstream** of the field (north of it, for a southward flow) so the channel runs downstream to the field; a channel whose field is upstream of its tap reads as water flowing *from the field into the moat*, backwards. A field that is itself upstream of the whole moat (e.g. NORTH of a north-fed moat) is uphill of the current and **cannot be moat-fed** - run it off the edge with its water implied off-map, or feed it from the source stream, and make up the moat-irrigated count with a downstream (south/east/west) field instead.
- **Neighborhood/zone labels must sit WITH the cluster they name** (`city_labels_placed_with_subject`). A label like `"samurai neighborhood"`, `"merchant district"`, `"burakumin neighborhood"`, or `"samurai estates"` must be (1) **entirely on the same side of the wall** as what it labels (the laborer/burakumin/samurai/merchant/agricultural labels INSIDE the rampart, the extramural-estates label OUTSIDE), (2) **among its buildings** (the nearest of its subject within ~130px for a house cluster, more for sparse manors/temples/fields), and (3) **not floating over a foreign field or compound**. The subject is inferred from the label text (`estates`->the extramural manors, `agricultural`->the IN-WALL fields, `temple`->temples, else the matching building kind). The fix is always to move the `s.label(...)` onto its own cluster - not the quarter's edge over a neighbor, not out over the moat. The label TEXT is recorded in the manifest (`M["labels"][i][5]`) so the gate can match label to subject.
- **Public WELLS dot the commoner warren** (`city_neighborhoods_have_wells`, `city_wells_in_block_interiors`). Ensuring every commoner could draw water was a defining civic concern of a premodern city; the communal neighborhood well (井戸, the *idobata* social hub) served a courtyard / cluster of ~10-20 households, so the warren is sprinkled with them - one within a short walk of any home. They are the ONLY visible part of the water system: the aqueducts, underground cisterns, and rain barrels that fed the shafts are too small or literally subterranean and stay OFF the map. A wellhead (`s.well(x, y)`) is a stone curb under an open-sided roof, SMALLER than a house, and sits in a block INTERIOR off the lanes. Scatter them with **`s.place_wells(bbox, spacing)`** (a grid that tries a few offsets per cell, dropping any candidate on a lane / compound / the bound via the same `_fits` test the houses use) BEFORE that quarter's `s.pack(...)` so the houses flow around them; for a cramped, lane-laced quarter the grid can't reach (Tango's shallow burakumin lane-strip, the gap-fill band between the alleys), hand-seed a candidate list through **`s.well_at(x, y)`** (places only if the spot is clear). Tango runs ~47 wells (roughly the literal count for ~600 households at ~1 per 10-20, in the same draw-everything spirit as the houses). Two checks enforce this from different angles. **Coverage** (`city_neighborhoods_have_wells`): fires if any commoner dwelling (laborer/burakumin/merchant kinds) INSIDE the walls is more than ~290px from a well - samurai have private wells in their compounds (excluded), servants are interleaved and share the commoners' wells (ride along), and a transient gate market OUTSIDE the wall is not housing (excluded). **Density** (`city_well_density_sufficient`): assigning each commoner dwelling (servants included) to its NEAREST well, fires if any well is the nearest for more than **26** households - because coverage alone can look fine on AVERAGE while the busiest wells in the dense laborer warren are swamped (the 290px reach is satisfied by one well per radius, but a dense warren packs far more than 20 households into that radius). The ceiling is a touch above the historical 20 because the nearest-well split over-counts where two wells are nearly equidistant. The dense laborer/gap-fill quarters therefore need tighter `place_wells` spacing than the merchant quarter. `city_wells_in_block_interiors` is the placement backstop: a wellhead must sit clear of the lanes and not overlap a building/compound. **The SAMURAI/GOVERNMENT quarter has NO public wells** (`city_samurai_quarter_has_no_public_wells`): samurai drew from PRIVATE wells inside their own walled compounds, and gathering at the communal *idobata* was a commoner-district institution (beneath samurai status) - so a public wellhead must not sit among the samurai dwellings. The check is RELATIVE (a well is "in the samurai quarter" if most of its nearest dwellings are samurai), so it correctly ignores a commoner well sitting a block from the quarter across the ward fence rather than relying on a brittle absolute distance. Don't call `place_wells`/`well_at` in the samurai quarter. The drawn wellhead **scales with the map grain**: like buildings and farmhouses, `s.well` multiplies its curb-and-roof size by `self.bscale` (city 0.42 -> ~10px diameter; town 0.82 -> ~20px; village/hamlet 1.0 -> ~24px) and records the drawn radius as `vr` on each well. A fixed-pixel wellhead looked right in a dense city but shrank to a quarter of a house at village/town scale, where buildings are drawn at full size - the well must track the same grain. `wells_sized_to_buildings` enforces this at EVERY scale: mean well diameter over the median dwelling's larger dimension must land in **[0.35, 0.85]** (a well is naturally smaller than a house, but not a dot). The reserved footprint and block-poly also scale with `vr`, so at every scale the well still reserves only a TIGHT courtyard (houses ring it closely) - each well costs roughly its own footprint, not a whole house-plot, so keep that reservation small or a city's worth of wells visibly deflates the population. NOTE wells still displace SOME housing, so re-check the population/caste bands after adding them (to keep Tango's depicted population a little above its declared 3,000 - dwellings x5 - the laborer packs are stepped tight, and the busiest west edge of the warren is given a few extra `well_at` wells so no well exceeds the density ceiling).
- **The RURAL tiers need wells too** (`settlement_has_wells`, `settlement_dwellings_watered`, for `town` / `village` / `hamlet`). Every settlement keeps communal wells - one per ~20-25 households - so `s.place_wells(<dwelling bbox>, spacing=..., near=85)` AFTER the farmhouses. **Pass `near=` for the rural tiers** (the city omits it): the bare grid would scatter wells out into the open fields and countryside between the field-rings, but `near=N` only keeps a candidate that has a dwelling within `N` px, so wells sit AMONG the houses. (A well must serve households around it; `wells_among_dwellings`, below, enforces this at every scale.) A dispersed farm household may instead draw from the **irrigation network it sits beside**, so a dwelling counts as *watered* if a well **OR** a channel / pond / stream / moat is within reach (~380px at the rural grain): the well count makes wells PRESENT, the coverage check makes sure no household is left dry. Tune the spacing per map (the hamlet shares ~1-2, a 60-household village ~6-9, a town ~16) so the worst-served dwelling - usually a far field-ring farmhouse - is within reach; the city tier keeps its own finer suite (density, block-interior placement, the samurai-quarter exemption) instead.
- **A well must sit AMONG the dwellings it serves** (`wells_among_dwellings`, every scale): a communal well is the draw-point for the households around it, so one out in open ground with no building within ~95px is unreal. A city's `s.pack(...)` runs after `place_wells` and fills in around the wells (so they end up surrounded); the rural tiers must use `place_wells(..., near=...)` (their grid runs after the houses and would otherwise drop wells into the fields). This is the bug it caught: Hirameki's wells scattered into the empty countryside.
- **A set-apart SHRINE keeps its OWN WELL close by** (`remote_shrine_has_own_well`, rural tiers). A shrine/temple set sufficiently apart from the village (its nearest dwelling is >150px away) is too far to use the village's shared wells, so it needs its OWN ablution well (*temizu*) right beside it - and specifically a WELL, not just a nearby ditch/pond (those are not ablution sources). A shrine among/near the houses shares theirs (exempt). Place it with **`s.shrine_well(cx, cy)`** AFTER the hall + houses + village wells: it tries widening rings until a well fits clear of the hall/torii/graveyard/lanes, so a large hall pushes its well onto an outer ring; the "close by" test is measured to the hall's EDGE (a big monastery's well sits further from its centre), as is `wells_among_dwellings` (a shrine's own well stands beside the shrine, not among houses, so religious halls count as served). **A HILLTOP/mountain shrine is exempt** (it sits on `M['hill']`) - it draws from a spring/basin, and you cannot dig a well on a hill (kikuta's Benten hill-shrine). Hoshigaoka's water-mouth shrine sits past the graveyard, well south of the houses, so it keeps its own well.
- **A WELL stands BESIDE a shrine, never ON its hall or UNDER its torii arch** (`wells_clear_of_shrine_and_torii`). A well close to a shrine is REQUIRED (above), but its disc must not overlap the hall footprint or the torii arch's box (`x +/-19, y -10..+18`) - a wellhead painted on the hall or in the gateway reads wrong. The fix is ORDER: place `s.shrine_hall(..., torii=[...])` **BEFORE** `s.place_wells(...)` / `s.shrine_well(...)`, so the hall + torii block-outs already exist and the wells (both the scattered village wells and the shrine's own) are laid clear of them. (This caught Ueda, where `place_wells` ran before the shrine and scattered a wellhead under the torii.) Watch the knock-on: `place_wells`'s fallback pass seats an underserved house's well at `~0.6*near` px, so keep `near` modest (~150, not 210) or that fallback can land in open ground and trip `wells_among_dwellings`.
- **A shrine AND its TORII arch NESTLE in a CLEARING within the sacred grove - never UNDER the trees** (`shrine_clear_of_grove_trees`, `torii_clear_of_grove_trees`). The village fengshui/water-mouth grove often surrounds the shrine (the grove IS the shrine's sacred wood), but the hall and its arch must sit in a cleared opening, not painted over tree canopy (which reads as a building lost in the wood). Mechanism: `village_grove` skips any tree clump within an occupied feature's keep-out (houses, yards, gardens, byres, kura, wells at the ordinary `0.35*clump` reach; **religious halls, shrines, and torii at a LARGER `0.90*clump`** so a full clearing opens around the sacred features) - **provided the shrine + torii are placed BEFORE the grove**. So call `s.shrine_hall(..., torii=[...])` FIRST, then the `village_grove(...)` calls. **Canopy, not nominal clump:** the recorded clump `r` is the nominal footprint, but the DRAWN crowns overhang it, so the visible canopy reaches ~1.7x `r`; both checks use that CANOPY radius (a circle-rect test against the hall footprint and against the torii arch's `x +/-19, y -10..+18` box) so they match what the eye sees. (This caught Ueda's shrine + torii buried under the water-mouth grove: the old occ-skip used `0.35*clump` and the checks used the nominal `r`, so canopy that overhung by ~8px slipped through both. A well record carries a radius, not w/h, so it keeps its own occ branch.)
- **DRAFT-ANIMAL BYRES** (`s.draft_byres(fraction, gap)`; recorded `M['byres']`, a first-class overlap struct). Wet-rice plowing/puddling turns on an ox or water buffalo, but a buffalo was a costly asset that poorer households SHARED or hired - so a village keeps only a MINORITY of byres (~one per 4-5 households, `fraction~0.2`), shared open-fronted sheds standing in the COURTYARDS among the homesteads, NOT one per farm. HOUSE-DRIVEN placement: for the wealthier homesteads (buffalo owners) in turn, spiral out from the house to the nearest clear gap just past its reserved footprint (fits-checked off every other footprint / lane / block / crop, kept `gap` px apart), skipping any homestead boxed in - a grid scatter like the wells finds almost nothing because the nucleated homestead bboxes pack the cluster. Call AFTER `farmsteads()` + `place_wells` and BEFORE the grove (which then skips the byres). The glyph is a small plank-roofed shed with a dark stall mouth, distinct from the solid grey *kura* and the dwellings. **Sizing (1px = 2ft at village scale):** the shed footprint is ~15.5x10.5px = ~31x21ft = **~16 m^2**, right for a single-bay lean-to stalling 1-2 draft animals (an ox/buffalo needs ~6-8 m^2 of stall plus fodder/passage room); it must NOT read as a European multi-stall barn (there are none - see the draft-animals-live-in-the-house rule above), so keep it small.
- **PLANK BRIDGES - two kinds.** (a) A **carried-way crossing** (`s.bridges()`; `roads_bridge_water`): where a road, town street, or village LANE crosses a watercourse (stream, channel, field-ditch, moat) it must be carried over on a railed timber deck oriented ALONG the way; `bridges()` auto-spans every such crossing, on the TOP layer, and the check fails on any unbridged one. (b) **Standalone ditch FOOTBRIDGES** (`s.channel_footbridges(spacing, min_len)`; `long_ditches_have_a_footbridge`, opt-in via `meta.field_footbridges`): the planks field-workers use to cross an irrigation ditch while walking the paddy BUNDS - **NOT tied to any lane** (people reach them along the bunds, so NO path leads to them). Any ditch stretch longer than `min_len` gets a plank about MIDWAY; a long stretch gets one roughly every `spacing` px; each crosses PERPENDICULAR to the ditch. **Deck width (1px=2ft): a *dobashi* footplank is a single-file crossing (~3-5ft), so `plank_w=2.5` (~5ft) - kept NARROWER than a cart lane (~5-6px).** The wider `bridges()` carried-way deck matches the lane it carries (a road bridge is road-width); a footplank is not, so don't let it read as a road bridge. Because each ditch is a separate manifest entry, the northern trunk (several segments) gets several planks, each branch into the paddy gets one, the village-edge channel gets one (Hoshigaoka adds a second on its heavily-trodden northern stretch), the drain gets a couple. **A lane must NOT run through the flooded paddy** - the village-to-field spur STOPS at the paddy edge; people cross into the fields on foot along the bunds. Call `channel_footbridges()` AFTER the ditches are placed. **A plank must never sit ON a farmhouse** (`bridges_clear_of_houses`): a bridge is overlap-exempt in general (it deliberately sits on the water it spans) but a rotated-rect SAT of its deck against every house must be clear - so `channel_footbridges` SLIDES each plank along its own ditch (a few arc-length offsets each side of the midpoint) until its deck clears every house, keeping it ON the channel while dodging a home near the village edge.
- **Field-adjacency is TUNABLE by settlement form** (`meta.nucleated`). DISPERSED (default): every farmhouse fronts its own fields, so each must be within ~165px of a field (`all_houses_field_adjacent`). NUCLEATED (`meta(nucleated=True)`): the houses cluster and the fields radiate from the cluster edge, so interior houses are legitimately a cluster-span back - per-house adjacency is wrong. Instead the whole CLUSTER must ABUT its fields (`cluster_abuts_fields`): the nearest house is field-adjacent AND no house is farther than the cluster's own diameter past that edge. Set `nucleated` to pick the invariant.
- **Tax-free (temple/monk glebe) plots are OPTIONAL, not required** (`taxfree_plots_in_range`). Denoting them is a choice - the check only validates the count (~2-3) when a map actually draws some or `meta(taxfree_expected=True)`; a village that does not mark them at all is fine.
- **Every farmhouse-bearing map needs a HARVEST-PROCESSING layer** (`harvest_yards_present`, `harvest_yards_smaller_than_farmhouse`, `harvest_yards_on_sunny_side`, `harvest_yards_clear_of_paddies`, `harvest_yards_clear_of_structures`, for `town` / `village` / `hamlet` AND `city`). A rice settlement does not just grow the crop - it threshes and dries it, and that working landscape is as characteristic as the paddies. Historically Japan processed rice at the **farmstead**, not on a central communal floor: drying happened on *hazakake* racks erected in the fields, and threshing/hulling/winnowing on each household's own packed-earth work yard (the *niwa* / *kadoniwa*). So the model is **per-farmstead, not communal**, and the work yard was **UNIVERSAL** - every farming household processed its own rice, so **every farmhouse gets a yard** (a firm 100%). The yard is **independent** of the ~30% that carry a shed (a farmhouse may have neither, either, or both - the shed/storehouse, by contrast, was NOT universal: a fireproof *kura* was a wealth marker and a simple *naya* was common but not on every plot, so it stays a minority). The yard is a small earthen apron with a straw mat + a little drying rack. **Architecture (important): farmstead drawing is DEFERRED.** `try_place` / `headman` reserve + record a farmhouse but do NOT draw it; the gen then calls **`s.farmsteads()` LAST** (after every other feature) which, for each farmhouse, finds a yard spot, NUDGES the house a little if needed (with every obstacle now known), draws the yard then the house over it, and **drops** any farmhouse that truly cannot host a yard anywhere nearby. This keeps base placement identical to a yard-free map (so population/plurality/household counts are unaffected) while guaranteeing the yard. Wells go AFTER `farmsteads()` so they sit among the final houses; `place_wells` has a coverage pass that guarantees no dwelling is left in a well-gap. Five rules: (1) **present (100%)** - EVERY farmhouse has a yard (`harvest_yards_present`: no house lacks one, matched by the yard's `of` = parent centre). Because farmsteads drops the rare un-yardable house, ring counts are set a little generously so enough survive (e.g. plurality/household floors still hold). (2) **smaller than its farmhouse** (`harvest_yards_smaller_than_farmhouse`) - the niwa is a small dry apron, not a second dwelling; it scales with `bscale` so it stays smaller even on a compressed city map. (3) **on the sunny SOUTH/front side** (`harvest_yards_on_sunny_side`) - the yard is the *maeniwa*, the south-facing front work yard: rice must dry in the SUN and minka face south (`houses_face_south`), so the yard sits on the house's south/front side, NEVER the shady north back. (+y is south.) This follows the *sun*, not the field - so when the paddy is to the south the yard falls between house and field, but a northern paddy does NOT pull the yard onto the shady side. (4) **clear of the paddies** (`harvest_yards_clear_of_paddies`) - a DRY floor, footprint out of every flooded field. (5) **clear of every OTHER structure** (`harvest_yards_clear_of_structures`) - the yard abuts only its OWN farmhouse, never another building (placed before OR after it - which is why farmsteads runs last, when all obstacles are known). Applies to the **city** too (its in-wall agricultural district + the outside farm rings); only a map with no farmhouses at all is exempt.
- **Every settlement above a hamlet buries its DEAD - a full funerary geography.** Rokugan always **cremates**, then inters the bones, so a graveyard is a compact field of stone markers, not a body-burial sprawl. Four kinds of site, each with its own setting logic:
  - **Temple graveyards** (`s.cemetery(cx, cy, w, h, label=...)` - rows of *sotoba* markers + a couple of taller memorial stupas). The state merged Shinsei (the Buddhist strand) and Fortune/Shugendo worship, so **ANY temple may host a graveyard** - a temple opts out with `s.shrine_hall(..., graveyard=False)` for a new or special-purpose hall (Tango's Temple of Bishamon, founded in a former samurai estate, keeps none). A graveyard is a parish ground, so it **sits in a temple precinct** (`cemetery_in_temple_precinct`, within ~230px of a `monastery`/`temple`; at city scale only an *intramural* graveyard must - an extramural common ground is exempt). **A village SHRINE hosts its graveyard too - the churchyard rule** (`village_graveyard_by_shrine`). Real-world Shinto shuns death-*kegare*, but **L7R's country shrines are officially Shinseist** (the Buddhist-analog order) even though they house Fortune deities, and their resident **Brother of Shinsei performs the funerary rites** - so the shrine *is* the death-handling institution (the *danka*/*bodaiji* logic), and its graveyard is its churchyard, sitting IN the precinct (within ~250px), NOT held away. So the village graveyard goes BY the shrine - EXCEPT a **hilltop** shrine (`M["hill"]`), which is exempt (graves do not climb the sacred hill; a prominent hill-shrine is not the humble earth-god monk's funerary base). Only the sacred **hall + torii themselves** stay clear of burials (`cemetery_clear_of_shrine` now means "graves fill the yard AROUND them, never ON them" - it fires on a grave/mausoleum overlapping the hall or arch, not on mere proximity). This unifies burial across scales: graves by the monk-run building everywhere (shrine at village, monastery/temple at town/city). **Size the burial grounds to the population they serve** (the core "roughly to scale" principle): a centuries-old settlement's MAIN common ground should read as a substantial, monastery-scale field, not a token patch - and a **town has no ossuary** (city-only) to soak up the poor, so its common ground carries proportionally MORE and should be drawn larger (Hirameki's ~1,200-soul town has a 120x88 extramural common ground that dwarfs its 88x62 intramural parish plot). The intramural parish plot beside a temple is naturally smaller (one parish's families); the extramural common ground is the big one.
  - **Clan mausoleum** (`s.mausoleum(cx, cy, w, h, label=..., gate_dir=..., label_below=...)` - a walled crypt precinct with a stone hall + memorial stupas). The ruling clan's ancestral crypts, **inside the walls by the samurai/government quarter** (`city_has_mausoleum` - a prestige monument near the seat of power; ancestor veneration is central to samurai identity). City only. When a wall of such a walled compound **abuts a neighborhood (ward) fence**, the compound *yields* that wall to the fence: `s.mausoleum`/`s.manor` re-stamp the ward fence over the coincident wall so the **fence renders ON TOP and IS that side of the precinct** (the compound's own wall runs underneath) - no doubled, clashing parallel walls. The yielded sides are recorded in `ward_walls`, and `walled_structure_yields_to_ward_wall` gates it: any axis-aligned mausoleum/manor wall inside the city that runs along a ward fence must be recorded as yielded (in Tango the samurai-ward fence IS the mausoleum's north wall).
  - **Cremation ground** (`s.cremation_ground(cx, cy)` - a scorched clearing with a stone pyre platform, a wisp of smoke, and the officiants' shelter). Smoke, fire, and pollution put it **OUTSIDE the walls** (`city_has_cremation_ground`; `town_has_cremation_ground` wants one at a town's edge, clear of the dwellings). It **adjoins an EXTERNAL (outside-the-walls) burial ground** (`cremation_ground_by_external_cemetery`, edge gap <= ~70px): the body is burned and its cremated bones interred next door, so the crematory and the extramural common ground form one funerary complex (an in-wall graveyard does NOT satisfy this - the crematory's neighbour must itself be extramural). And it **stays set back from the main road** (`cremation_ground_set_back_from_main_road`, >= ~130px from `M["road"]`): the pyre is marginal, polluting land reached by a minor funeral path, not high-street frontage. Beyond that floor, it must not sit **between its temple and the road** (`cremation_ground_not_between_temple_and_road`): if a monastery/temple is nearby (within ~400px), the crematory must be at least as far from the road as that temple is (less a ~40px tolerance for "beside, a little away"), so worshippers and funeral-goers don't walk past the pyre to reach the hall - put it BEHIND or beside the temple, never on its road-side approach. (Note the danka split: a temple's own *graveyard* sits beside the temple wherever it is - like a Christian churchyard, and may sit road-side by the temple - but the *cremation ground* is the marginal exception kept apart from the road and off the temple's ceremonial approach; minor town streets don't trigger either rule, only the trunk road.) Monk-run with burakumin assistants - a religious order stands outside the caste system, so handling the dead does not pollute its caste.
  - **Pauper ossuary** (`s.ossuary(cx, cy)` - a low earthen mound with one weathered stupa). The communal bones of the poor and the *muenbotoke* (unconnected dead), **outside the walls by the cremation ground** (`city_has_ossuary`).
- **The scale schedule** (centuries-old settlements): a **CITY** shows **2-4 graveyards** (`city_graveyard_count`), **every non-exempt temple has one in its precinct** (`city_temples_have_graveyards`), plus the mausoleum (inside) and the cremation ground + ossuary (outside) - an extramural funerary complex beyond a gate. A **TOWN** has 1-2 temple graveyards + a cremation ground at the edge. A **VILLAGE** has one burial ground clear of its shrine. A **HAMLET is exempt** (its dead go to the village district's ground, like it has no headman or shrine).
- **Any WALLED settlement (town or city) keeps a graveyard inside AND outside the walls** (`walled_graveyards_inside_and_outside`), **and the exterior common ground is noticeably larger than the cramped intramural one** (`walled_exterior_cemetery_larger`, outside area >= 1.3x the largest inside - there is room beyond the walls, while the intramural temple plots are hemmed in). So a walled town has at least two graveyards (one of each); an unwalled town's graveyards are all parish grounds (no exterior-common-ground exemption). Intramural temple plots are deliberately **small** (Tango's are 46x34 / 56x40, cramped beside their halls), the extramural common ground **large** (104x74).
- **Every solid map feature is a first-class structure for overlap purposes, and a NEW feature must not overlap anything unless the overlap is explicitly declared OK.** This is the governing principle (each new feature historically shipped overlapping streams, channels, fields, walls...), and it is *enforced*, not just documented: `check_village.py` carries a classification registry (`_OVERLAP_STRUCTS` / `_OVERLAP_TARGETS` / `_OVERLAP_LINEAR` / `_OVERLAP_EXEMPT`) and the **`every_feature_classified_for_overlap`** guard, which FAILS when a generator emits a footprint feature whose manifest key is in none of those sets - so a new feature can never silently skip the overlap rules. The DEFAULT is must-not-overlap: a solid feature (houses, civic/urban buildings, the **funerary structures**, wayside shrines, ministries) joins the `structs` set and is automatically caught by `no_structure_overlaps` and every `no_structure_on_*` clearance (building, **wall**, **moat**, **road/street/alley**, **stream**, **channel**, the **pond**, religious hall, gate furniture, torii, and the **ring road** via `ring_road_kept_clear`); every solid feature is normalised to a w/h rect for the test. Overlaps are **opt-in**: a feature that is *meant* to overlap is named in `_OVERLAP_EXEMPT` with its reason - a **label** (text overlay), a **bridge** over water/road, a **guard tower** ON the wall, an **inspection post** at the gate, a **storehouse** annex abutting its shop, a **threshing yard** annex abutting its farmhouse, a **well-head** dropped into a courtyard gap. (`shrines` duplicates the primary religious halls, so it rides with `religious` as a target.) Place solid features AFTER the dwellings (they block placement) in clear pockets; a placement scan must mirror **all** the clearances (wall stroke, moat, ring road, streets, water, fields, torii, gate furniture, and existing structures' *rotated* footprints), and keep extramural funerary sites **outside the wall but inside the cropped view** (Tango clusters its common burial ground + cremation ground + ossuary just west of the wall).
- **Burial grounds sit a SET-BACK from open water that SCALES with the waterway's size** (`funerary_set_back_from_water`, helper `water_setback(width)`) - a burial ground by big water floods out, so the bigger the watercourse the further back the dead must lie - but even narrow open water floods graves, so there is a solid **floor (~75px)**: a stream gets the full ~75px, a **moat** (the heaviest watercourse, ~26px wide) ~130px, a river or canal most (capped ~140px). It applies to the **moat, streams, ponds, and rice-paddy field edges** - a flooded paddy is real standing water (rice grows submerged), a genuine flood hazard, so a burial ground keeps a solid **~50px** margin from any field edge (NOT a token creek distance - graves right against a paddy flood out); thin irrigation channels are not open water and don't trigger it. Two refinements: the **CREMATION ground may sit nearer the water** (a fixed modest ~30px, and exempt from the paddy set-back entirely - fire and ritual want water access, and a fire site is not flood-sensitive like graves), so the graveyard naturally lands *beyond* the cremation ground (Tango's common burial ground sits well back, on the far side of the cremation ground from the moat); and an **INSIDE-wall ground is exempt from the moat term** (the moat is outside the wall, the rampart shields it - streams/ponds still apply on either side). The **walled mausoleum is exempt** entirely (a crypt compound, not a flood-sensitive open plot).
- **A graveyard can be marked NON-parish** (`s.cemetery(..., parish=False)`) - a village-style burial ground not attached to a temple, exempt from the temple-precinct rule. Used for a one-off like **Tango's in-wall agricultural-district burial ground** (a graveyard serving the farm quarter, away from the temples - a Tango-specific extra, not a general rule). bunkotsu (divided remains - a portion at a great head-temple, the rest in the home village) is real but **not drawn** - it isn't visible in this view.
- **A gate market sits outside one gate** (`city_has_gate_market`, >= 3 businesses outside a gate within ~520px) - the same extramural *guan-xiang* commerce as a town's, here beyond the moat by a gate.
- **Fire defense ports up too, at city scale.** A city's quarters each need a **fire-watch tower** (`s.fire_tower(...)`, `city_has_fire_towers` >= 2; `fire_tower_in_commoner_quarter` keeps each in a commoner quarter, not the samurai ward; `fire_towers_dispersed` keeps any two towers at least one watch radius apart, and `fire_tower_amid_its_district` makes each tower stand amid the rooftop district it is nearest-tower for - so the towers actually spread across the commoner quarters instead of bunching in one quadrant). Because the commoner warren packs wall-to-wall, a tower stands where it actually has SIGHTLINES: a cleared block or an open seam between building clusters. Because the towers annotate existing gaps, place them **LAST, after the packs and `farmsteads()`**. `meta(fire_tower=False)` to suppress. See the "Fire towers" grounding below.
- **Extensive farmland lies OUTSIDE the walls** (`city_has_outside_farmland`, >= 1 field running off the map edge - the rest implied off-map). The in-city farmers are zero, so these fields are worked from the surrounding villages; draw them mostly running off the edge rather than ringing them with city farmhouses.
- **By default a city has NO farms inside the walls** (`city_no_inwall_farms`). Tango is *uncharacteristic* in having an **agricultural district** (its NW quarter): in-wall water sources plus dimensions that made it save ~nothing to wall the farms out. Opt in with `meta(agricultural_district=True)`; that quarter also holds the city's in-wall burakumin. **Most cities will NOT set this.** When a city DOES farm inside its walls, those fields are **real farmland**. Unlike a surrounding field - mostly off the cropped map, so only a sparse FLOOR is enforceable on its shown sliver - an in-wall field sits **entirely in view**, so its **whole perimeter must read as worked**: ring it **densely all the way round**, not a sparse few on one side that leaves long bare edges. `city_interior_fields_farmhouse_density` holds in-wall fields to a much higher floor than the extramural `outside_fields_farmhouse_density` - **16 houses per 1000px of edge** (the dense end of village ringing) vs the outside floor of 7. Ring with a tight inner row plus one or two denser outer bands at finer spacing (Tango lands ~24-37/1000px). Two wrinkles: (1) placement uses a single seeded RNG, so adding ring calls early in the gen shifts every later draw; if your samurai/laborer packs are tuned near a threshold, **append the extra interior-field rings at the END of the gen** so they perturb nothing upstream. (2) These in-wall farmhouses do **NOT** count toward the city population (city farmers are never in the ~3,000 - see the population rule above), so ring as densely as the field wants without watching the figure.
- **Tango's quarters** (a specific layout, not a universal rule): the road + E-W axis split it into NW (agricultural district + in-wall burakumin + a pond), NE (laborers), SW (merchant district + the temple neighborhood with the Rites ministry), SE (governor's mansion + the other five ministries + the samurai neighborhood, with the samurai estates just outside the SE wall). Other cities arrange their quarters differently; the gate checks the *contents*, not which quarter holds them.

### The validator (`check_village.py`)

The generator emits a manifest the validator asserts against; it must pass before a settlement map is presented. It works for **any** village/hamlet, not just Kikuta: the UNIVERSAL invariants are always checked, while the VILLAGE-SPECIFIC expectations are read from `manifest["meta"]` and from each channel's `frm`/`to` anchors.

- **Universal (always):** no farmhouse overlaps (rotated-rect SAT); nothing on a lane / **irrigation channel** (`no_structure_on_channel`, same footprint test as streams) / **road** (`no_structure_on_road`) / **stream** (`no_structure_on_stream`) / manor / **wall** (`no_structure_on_wall`) / **moat** (`no_structure_on_moat`) / **town street** (`no_structure_on_street`) / **religious hall** (`no_structure_on_religious`) / **gate guard station + tower** (`no_structure_on_gate`) / **torii arch** (`no_structure_on_torii`) - and **every solid feature (the funerary structures, wayside shrines, ministries) is checked by all of these exactly like a building** (see the overlap-classification principle above; **Three disciplines this gate has taught us:**

1. **Any rule that can be machine-checked belongs in `check_village.py`, not only in this prose.** When a new Mode B rule emerges, add it as an assertion.
2. **The manifest must record what is actually RENDERED, not the generator's pre-render inputs.** The fields render as a smoothed (Catmull-Rom) curve that bows inward of the raw outline vertices; recording the vertices let a channel "connect" in the manifest while visually stopping in the gap. The generator now records the *sampled smoothed boundary* (`smooth_points`). A check is only as honest as the geometry it asserts against - sample the drawn shape, and still eyeball a crop, because a green check on the wrong geometry is worse than no check. **The checks run against the JSON manifest, never the rendered SVG/PNG** - `s.finish()` writes the picture and the manifest from one in-memory model so they cannot drift, and the gate does pure geometry over coordinates (exact and fast) rather than re-deriving objects from pixels (lossy and ambiguous). The PNG is the human deliverable; the manifest is the machine-checkable truth.
3. **Test the checks themselves, with negative fixtures, and enforce 100% coverage.** `gate(M)` takes a manifest dict and returns the failed-check names (`main(path)` wraps it for the CLI). The suite has **four** legs: **`test_villages.py`** proves the real maps PASS the whole gate (integration, running the gens in-process so they count toward coverage); **`test_checks.py`** proves each check FIRES on a deliberately-broken synthetic manifest (unit); **`test_settlement.py`** reaches the generator branches the maps don't; and **`test_regressions.py`** replays the captured-failure corpus in **`pool/regressions/*.json`** - actual bad manifests, each tagged with a `_regression.fires` list of the checks it MUST still trip. The integration/unit pair is not redundant: if a refactor silently neuters a check, every real map still passes and `test_villages` stays green while the check sits dead - only a negative fixture catches that. **Coverage is gated at 100%** (`pyproject.toml`: `[tool.coverage.report] fail_under = 100`), so this isn't aspirational - the suite *fails* below 100%. Run the **whole** suite (`python3 -m pytest` from the skill dir; needs `pytest-cov`); a subset under-reports and trips the gate. The few genuinely-unreachable defensive fallbacks carry an explained `# pragma: no cover`. **When you add a check or a generator branch, add the test that exercises it** - the gate will fail until you do. Writing "here is a map that SHOULD fail this" also forces you to enumerate the ways the thing can be wrong, which is how *logic gaps* (not just regressions) surface - the streets-to-nowhere miss was such a gap.

   **STANDING REQUIREMENT - capture every "bad" map as a regression fixture.** 100% line coverage does NOT prove a check has teeth: a check whose condition is silently always-true still runs its `check(...)` line on every passing map, so coverage stays green while the check is dead. The cure is a manifest that actually *trips* it. So: **whenever you add OR refine a check - or you eyeball a generated map and find a flaw the gate let through - save the manifest of that bad map into `pool/regressions/<name>.json` with a `_regression` block** (`{"fires": ["the_check_that_should_catch_it"], "source": "..."}`), then make the check catch it. The maps we generate while iterating toward one we're happy with are exactly this corpus: each "oh, that one wasn't good enough" is a free, real negative fixture - don't throw it away, drop its `.json` (manifest only; the PNG re-renders from it) into `pool/regressions/`. `test_regressions.py` globs the directory, so a new file is picked up with no wiring. The backfill of the existing in-test fixtures into this corpus was produced by `make_regressions.py` (re-run it to regenerate the auto-captured set); going forward the discipline needs no tooling - just save the manifest. Note the corpus currently pins the ~80 checks that have a negative fixture; the remaining checks are still only exercised on their PASS path (real maps), so prefer adding a captured failure for any check you touch that lacks one.

## Historical grounding: the "why" behind the realism checks

**Requirement (project rule, restated here):** whenever historical research drives a check, a generation rule, or a magic number, record the *reasoning* here next to the rule - not just the rule. The check is the "what"; this section is the "why," so a future reader (or a future us, after the research has fallen out of memory / context) can revisit a decision without redoing the legwork. Source citations are optional; the reasoning is mandatory. Each entry: what the research found, the decision it drove, and any deliberate departure from literal scale (we draw small features oversized for legibility - see "Core principle: roughly to scale" in [`SKILL.md`](SKILL.md) - but keep *relative* sizes roughly honest).

- **Threshing / drying yards are PER-FARMSTEAD and UNIVERSAL** (drives `harvest_yards_present` = 100%, `harvest_yards_on_sunny_side`, `_smaller_than_farmhouse`, `_clear_of_paddies`, `_clear_of_structures`; the near-square irregular footprint also feeds `garden_plots_are_quads`).
  - *Per-farmstead, not communal.* Japan processed rice at the individual farmstead, on the household's own packed-earth work yard - the *niwa* / *kadoniwa* / *maeniwa* (front yard). This is a real divergence from **many European farming villages, which shared a common threshing floor / threshing green**, and from the Mediterranean/Chinese communal threshing floor. So we do NOT draw one big village threshing ground; every farm has its own.
  - *Universal.* Every farming household had to thresh, hull, and winnow its own crop (hand tools: *senba-koki* threshing comb, *tōmi* winnower, *dō-usu* hulling mill), so a work yard was an operational necessity, not a luxury - hence 100% of farmhouses, density permitting (the generator drops only a farmhouse too boxed-in to host a yard at all).
  - *Sun, not the field, sets its side.* Rice must dry in the sun and *minka* faced south, so the yard is the south-facing FRONT yard. When the paddy is south it lands between house and field (the classic gradient); a northern paddy does NOT pull the yard onto the shady back.
  - *Relative size.* A household's real work yard was small - on the order of ~100-300 m² against a ~1-*chō* (~1 ha = 10,000 m²) holding, i.e. **~1-3% of the cultivated area** - so a threshing yard is much smaller than the paddies, never larger. On the map we draw it a touch smaller than the farmhouse; since the farmhouse is itself drawn oversized relative to its fields (for legibility), the yard-vs-house ratio stays honest even though yard-vs-field reads larger than the true 1-3% (the same legibility exaggeration the houses already carry). Drying *racks* (*hazakake* / *inagi*) historically stood in the fields; we fold a small indicative rack into the yard glyph rather than tracking field racks separately.
  - *Size varies (DOWN from a maximum), shape stays near-square.* The base yard dims are the household's MAXIMUM apron (sized to the harvest it must dry), so they jitter DOWN (a smaller holding needs less drying floor) - the inverse of the garden, whose base is its minimum and jitters up. The footprint is a slightly-irregular inscribed quad like the garden but with **LESS** corner jitter: a threshing floor is a deliberately-swept, tamped work surface kept roughly rectangular for flailing and mat-drying, not a hand-dug bed, so it reads near-square (`garden_plots_are_quads` validates both). At 1 px = 2 ft the generator lands ~40-255 m² per yard (the largest is the headman's capped apron), squarely in the ~100-300 m² band.
- **Dooryard kitchen gardens (*saien*) are PER-FARMSTEAD and UNIVERSAL** (drives `gardens_present` = 100%, `gardens_on_sunny_side`, `gardens_smaller_than_farmhouse`, `gardens_clear_of_paddies`, `gardens_clear_of_channels`, `gardens_clear_of_structures`, `gardens_clear_of_sheds`, `garden_plots_are_quads`, `garden_area_within_norms`; placed by `farmsteads()` alongside the yard).
  - *Universal, and a SIDE feature - not the south front.* Every farmstead kept a small intensive vegetable plot (*saien*) for the household's daily greens (*daikon*, onions, beans, herbs), so it is drawn on 100% of farmhouses (the generator drops only a farmhouse too boxed-in to host BOTH a yard and a garden). Crucially it does NOT sit on the south front: that ground is the threshing/drying **work apron** (the *niwa*, which must stay clear hard-packed earth for flailing and sun-drying rice). The garden takes a **sunny SIDE** instead - we prefer the **east**, the *doma*/kitchen end where the cook steps out to it, then the sunny **SE/SW corners** (each tried flush AND a little further out, to slip the garden past the south yard into the corner), and the **windward WALL itself LAST** (the west, for the default NW wind) so that wall stays free for the homestead grove's belt; **never the shady north back**. The windward CORNER (SW) is fine - it tucks below the grove's arm - it is only the windward *wall* the garden yields. This matters most for a farm whose EAST faces the paddy: its garden can't go east, so it takes the SW corner rather than the west wall, leaving the west for the grove (a west garden vetoing the west grove arm was the #1 reason a windward arm went missing). (So the historical homestead reads: grove on the windward N/W, house, garden on a lee side, work yard on the S front, paddy beyond - all four drawn, and the homestead SOLVER shifts the whole farm to a nearby spot where they all fit without overlapping rather than stacking the garden under the grove.)
  - *Three appurtenances, three sides - they never collide.* The farmstead's yard (south front), its **storehouse/*kura*** (WEST wall for a dispersed farm, the shaded NORTH wall for a nucleated-cluster house, on the ~30% of plain farms that have one - see the kura bullet above), and its garden must sit on DIFFERENT sides of the house. The garden yields whichever wall the kura holds (`gardens_clear_of_sheds` enforces no overlap). The kura is a **recorded annex** (`M['farm_sheds']`) reserved in the homestead bundle, so the check reads its footprint straight from the manifest.
  - *Off the water, too (`gardens_clear_of_channels`).* A raised-bed *saien* is dry-ground - it must not sit in a running irrigation line (a feeder CHANNEL, an in-field/drain DITCH, or a stream). `gardens_clear_of_paddies` covers the flooded basin, but the **drain outfall and feeder channels thread the DRY village margin** where the gardens are, so they were the gap this check closes. The fix is in the SOLVER, not just the check: `_rect_blocked` now treats every water polyline as a keep-out for the SOLID bundle parts (house/yard/garden/kura), so the homestead self-heals off a ditch (the grove stays exempt - it may hug a bund). Found on Ueda, where two field-edge gardens sat on the SW drain.
  - *Why a side and not the front.* The south apron is occupied by the work yard and must stay open; a kitchen garden tolerates partial sun better than grain-drying needs full sun, so it can take an east/west side. This is the historically dominant *nōka yashiki* layout (idealized - real plots bent to terrain/wealth), which is why the generator encodes *preferences* (sunny sides, east first) and places "where there's room," not fixed slots.
  - *Relative size.* Small - smaller than the threshing yard and well under the farmhouse (a kitchen plot, not a second field); drawn as a tilled bed with tidy planted rows, distinct from the tan work yard and the blue-green paddy quilt.
  - *Size varies, and is held to a HISTORICAL AREA band (`garden_area_within_norms`).* No two households worked an identical plot, so the garden's base dims (its MINIMUM - the least ground that feeds a household's daily greens) jitter UP, by a different fraction in each dimension (which also varies its proportions). Unlike the house/yard - drawn oversized against the fields for legibility - a dooryard garden at **1 px = 2 ft is near its TRUE size**, so its area is a real quantity we can pin to history: the *saien* is the small INTENSIVE daily-greens bed by the kitchen (*daikon*, onions, greens, herbs); the household's bulk vegetable and dry-crop growing happened out in the **hatake dry fields**, not here. Historically that dooryard bed ran **a few *tsubo* up to ~1.4 *se*, i.e. ~10-140 m²** (1 *tsubo* = 3.31 m²; 1 *se* = 30 *tsubo* ≈ 99 m²). The check sums ALL of a household's beds (a fragmented plot is still one household's garden) and requires the TOTAL in that band - bigger reads as a field, tinier as no garden at all. (China-first per the core principle: rice-south Chinese farmhouse dooryard vegetable plots were the same small intensive kitchen beds; the bulk growing was likewise out in the household's fields.) The generator lands ~35-115 m² per household, centred ~55 m² (~1/2 *se*), comfortably inside.
  - *Shape - an irregular hand-worked quad, not a surveyed rectangle (`garden_plots_are_quads`).* Real dooryard beds were bent to paths, walls, and awkward soil, so the garden is drawn as a slightly-irregular **4-sided polygon** - each corner pulled INWARD from the reserved rect by a small position-seeded fraction, losing the perfect 90° corners while staying **entirely inside** its reserved w×h (so the shape can never create an overlap the rect-based placement/checks already cleared). The manifest records the `poly`; the check validates it is a non-degenerate inscribed 4-gon. (A swept threshing yard gets the SAME treatment but LESS jitter - a work surface stays near-square.)
  - *Occasionally FRAGMENTED into two beds, in THREE arrangements (`_garden_beds`, nucleated only).* Sometimes the land just works out that way - you garden the good topsoil where it lies rather than dig up a rocky patch when there is better ground a few feet over - so ~1 in 4 households (position-seeded), where each bed still reads as a real garden, shows TWO beds. WHERE the two beds sit varies: ~half the splits **FLANK the house on OPPOSITE walls** (a bed on the E and one on the W, or one in each south corner - the workable ground fell on both sides); the rest share ONE side, either **SIDE-BY-SIDE** or - for a south garden, where the upper bed stays sunny - **STACKED above/below**. It is the larger (well-off / headman) plots that fragment (each half must stay ~12 ft wide), which is also where a household works enough ground to bother. **Crucially the beds are decided in `_bundle_geom` and returned in `geom["gardens"]`, so they are RESERVED and collision-checked as part of the whole bundle** - an opposite-side bed grows the bundle bbox and the packer spaces the homestead out for it, so a flanking bed can never overlap a neighbour or a paddy (this is why the split is nucleated-only: a dispersed farm's W wall carries its windbreak grove, and its E-only garden has no clean opposite side). Every bed sits on a sunny band (never the north back), and all beds of one house carry the same parent - so `gardens_present` still counts one garden per house and the AREA band sums them. (Gardens can therefore outnumber houses in the manifest; that is expected.)
- **Storehouses / sheds are a MINORITY (~30%), not universal** (drives the `shed` flag rate, deliberately left unchecked - a visual flourish - and kept a minority).
  - A fireproof **kura** (土蔵) was an expensive **wealth marker** - only well-off farmers and landlords had one. A simpler **naya** (納屋) / **koya** (小屋) outbuilding was more common but still not on every plot: many households stored inside the *doma* (the farmhouse's own earth-floored work room), and the landless *mizunomi* had little to store. So a separate storehouse is a *portion* of farms (~30%), independent of the threshing yard (a farm may have neither, either, or both).
- **Settlement form: NUCLEATED cluster (the DEFAULT) vs DISPERSED ring (drives `s._nucleated`; researched + source-verified 2026-07, this is the record).** WHICH is more common: **nucleated clustered villages predominated in China** (Knapp, *Chinese Landscapes: The Village as Place*) - houses grouped together, "planted like islands among the fields," worked outward. The regional split is SIZE, not form: North China = large compact villages (1,000+ households); **the South-China rice regions** (Middle-Lower Yangzi - our analog, since Rokugani density anchors to Song/Ming rice China) = **small nucleated villages of ~30-60 households** (rice's intensive labour keeps farmers clustered by their paddies; 200-500 villages per 100 km2). **Dispersed** scattered farmsteads appear only in **mountainous/hilly terrain** (Sichuan-type, where fragmented arable forces homesteads apart). The Japan tiebreaker agrees (wet-rice Japan = *shuson*, nucleated). So the **default is NUCLEATED**; dispersed is the hill/upland TERRAIN mode (and dovetails with the multi-block-fields note - broken ground gives both scattered farmsteads AND several field patches). The ~30-60 rice-village band validates Hoshigaoka's ~70 households (a healthy single rice village, just above the band).
  - *The consequence for groves.* A nucleated cluster has **NO per-house windbreak grove** - the houses stand close and shelter each other, and a tight cluster of grove-bundles literally cannot nucleate (each ~6x-house grove-bundle forces the farms to RING the field, i.e. the dispersed pattern). Per the grove bullet below (and Knapp), the nucleated windbreak is instead a **village-EDGE belt** on the windward side (a *fengshui*/shrine grove), placed as one feature - NOT one yashikirin per house. So `_nucleated` swaps the homestead bundle: house + south threshing yard + a dooryard garden on an **adaptive sunny side** (SE/SW tucked beside the yard first - sunny and tight - then the E/W walls), the side chosen by the placer for **fit + no shading** (a garden with a farmhouse close to its SOUTH is shaded, so the placer avoids sandwiching it between houses; this also varies the gardens instead of all-east). The compact grove-less bundle lets the cluster pack into a real nucleus; the placer hugs each homestead to the paddy edge then packs it against its neighbours.
  - *DISPERSED (the shipped ring-village bundle) is unchanged* - house + windward L-grove (*yashikirin*) + yard + east garden, spaced so all fit; it is the terrain mode for upland/fragmented country. The existing ring villages (Hikari, Kikuta) stay dispersed; the water-first base case (Hoshigaoka) sets `s._nucleated=True`. The **windward village-edge belt** for nucleated clusters is a deferred TODO (the cluster currently renders grove-less pending it).
  - *Headman = just a LARGER plain farmhouse (tunable size), NOT its own logic.* Farmhouses are individually sizeable: `try_place(x, y, kind, role, size=(w,h))` overrides the footprint (base px). In nucleated mode `s.headman()` is a thin wrapper - it places a **plain** farmhouse of **~46x28 px** (~92x56 ft at 1px=2ft) through the STANDARD collision-checked bundle path, `role="headman"`. So it gets its (capped) yard + garden like any farm, packs without overlapping a neighbour, and carries no special reservation or drawing. It is the grandest house (clearly larger than a plain 23x14 or well-off ~26x16 farmhouse; the old 108x68 ~216x136 ft was a pre-scale fortress; `headman_is_largest` holds) purely by SIZE. The yard/garden dims are **capped** in `_bundle_geom` (yard ~34x22, garden ~22x17) so they read as an ordinary farm's beside his grand house (span ~the adjacent wall but shallower/narrower); plain houses are well under the cap, so they are unaffected. NOTE the `"big"` kind's glyph draws a small storeroom WING to its north that is NOT in the reserved footprint (it overlapped a neighbour's yard when used for the headman) - so the nucleated headman uses `"plain"`, not `"big"`.
  - *The cluster HUGS the paddy (algorithmic, not per-map seed tuning).* Villages sat as "islands among the fields," abutting their paddies - not floating with a bare buffer. So the nucleated placer, after shoving each homestead toward the nearest paddy edge, packs it against its neighbours with a **field-distance-monotone** constraint (`_slide_nuc(keep_field=True)`): a neighbour-pack move is rejected if it would drift the bundle FURTHER from the paddy than where it started. The cluster therefore glues its field side to the paddy and builds outward in rows (a blob anchored on the field), regardless of where the seed disk is centred - the fix lives in the ALGORITHM, so a map need only say roughly where the village sits, not hand-tune it onto the field edge.
  - *Lanes lace the cluster, and a spur bridges it to the fields.* A nucleated village is threaded with lanes. Lay them with `s.lane(pts)` **BEFORE** the houses - a lane lays a no-build corridor, so the homesteads FRONT it (place lanes after the water/fields, before `try_place`). The pattern: a **main spine** through the cluster (the headman fronts its head) plus a **spur running out to the paddy edge** - the spur is the village-to-field transition the bare buffer should carry (its field end is computed from the field geometry via `_nearest_field_point`, not hand-placed). Remaining house-to-field bareness is later filled by near-homestead hatake (a deferred layer). **Run the spine along the cluster's FIELD-FAR side** (not down the middle) when the field is on one flank: the placer hugs houses TOWARD the field, so a mid-cluster spine catches the houses it pulls across it (`houses_clear_of_lanes`); a spine on the far side lets them pack cleanly between it and the paddy.
  - *Name-informed siting: read the village NAME and let it place the settlement (GM, 2026-07, a GENERAL rule).* A Rokugani place name often encodes the settlement's position relative to its fields, water, or terrain - so before seeding the cluster, ask what the name SAYS and honour it. Worked example: **Ueda (上田, "upper paddy")** - the village's paddies are the UPPER (upslope) ones, so the dwellings sit DOWNSLOPE of them, facing up-valley toward the fields; on Ueda's NE-high slope that puts the cluster on the LOWER (SW) flank with the bulk of the paddy rising above it (NOT the default "village on the high flank"). Other cues to watch for and site to: *shimo-* (下, lower) -> the opposite; *kawa-/-gawa* (river) -> hard by the watercourse; *yama-/-take* (mountain), *saka* (slope), *hara* (plain/moor), *tani/-dani* (valley floor), *sawa* (marshy stream), *hama* (shore), *guchi* (a mouth/entrance/pass). When the name is neutral, fall back to the default (nucleated village on the dry high flank hugging its paddy). The point: the map should be able to be READ back into the name.
- **Village lanes and connecting paths - UNPAVED (or narrow-paved) NARROW single track (researched + source-verified 2026-07; CHINA-first per the core principle, Japan corroborating).** The conclusion is the SAME in both cultures - a village lane is a **NARROW single track, not a wide 2-lane road** - so the "dashed-centreline 2-lane" reading was wrong either way. The reasons, China leading:
  - **CHINA (the guiding star):** the Chinese deliberately built a **network of NARROW PATHS for the WHEELBARROW** (the characteristic Chinese one-wheel vehicle, which carries 3-6x a European barrow and needs only a footpath), plus **carrying-pole porters, packhorses, and litters** - NOT wide cart roads. Two-wheeled **cart-tracks predominated only in the flat NORTHERN/eastern plains**, which is NOT our rice-south analog. So the vehicle to picture is the **wheelbarrow and the shoulder-pole porter**, and the track is one-wheelbarrow / one-packhorse wide. *Paving nuance (a real China/Japan difference):* China had MANY narrow **stone-slab / flagged** paths, especially in the wet rice south (a paved causeway keeps porters and barrows above the mud) - MORE paving than Japan, but still **narrow foot/wheelbarrow paths, never wide cart roads**. A modest village's internal lanes are earth; an important inter-village or pilgrimage path may be a narrow stone causeway (an available upgrade, `worn=True` earth is the humble default).
  - **WATER as the freight highway - true for NATURAL water, but ARTIFICIAL canals are a Rokugan EXCEPTION (GM canon).** In Ming China bulk goods (above all tribute rice) moved by **canal and river boat**, and the man-made Grand Canal + dense artificial canal net were central. **Rokugan deliberately DIVERGES here:** artificial transport canals are NOT Empire-wide - they are a **LION-lands feature** (the Lion deliberately embrace the Chinese precedent), while the other clans rely on NATURAL water or land and simply do not need canals: **Crane** lands are coastal (never far from the ocean), **Dragon** has the Drowned Merchant River, **Unicorn** has the Firefly River valley and is intensely horse-focused, **Scorpion** lands are smaller, etc. So the RULE for a Rokugani village: where there is **navigable NATURAL water** (a river, the coast), that water is the main freight route and the village gets a **boat landing / jetty** as its primary link; but do NOT default to an artificial **transport canal** - add one only for a **Lion-lands** map or when the GM says so. This is a case where explicit GM setting canon **overrides** the China-first historical default. Make the transport canal + boat landing a **tunable, opt-in feature**, not the rule. (Hoshigaoka: irrigation pond + a minor drain, NOT navigable and NOT canal-served, so its land track legitimately IS the main connector - no canal here.)
  - **JAPAN (corroborating):** wheeled carts were near-absent rurally - ~70% mountains, horses too small to pull carts, carts even prohibited in places to prevent rutting and opposed by the packhorse/porter guilds; peasants travelled on foot, goods by packhorse/porter, heavy freight by boat. Paving was highways-only (even the Gokaido was stone-paved only on steep passes). Same bottom line: narrow, unpaved, single-track.
  - *Render:* `s.lane(pts, width=5, clearance=18, worn=True)` = a narrow packed-earth tread + soft worn shoulder, **NO centre marking**. Widths at 1px=2ft: a village lane ~5px (~10 ft), the out-track ~6px - clearly narrower than any constructed road. **`clearance` must be wide enough that a house CORNER clears the tread** - a plain house's half-diagonal is ~14px (a well-off one ~15), so `clearance=18` keeps houses fronting the lane rather than sitting on it. `houses_clear_of_lanes` gates this (fires if any house footprint corner falls within a lane's tread half-width): a lane is a no-build corridor, houses FRONT it, so **lay lanes BEFORE the packs** and give the corridor real width (11 was too tight - house corners overlapped the tread).
  - *The connecting path to the wider world IS marked, RUNS OFF THE EDGE, and the main lane does not dead-end.* A village connects to the nearest district / Imperial road (or, in the rice south, a **canal landing**) by a **trodden dirt track** worn by feet, wheelbarrows, packhorses, and porters - NOT a constructed road. Mark it `s.lane(..., connector=True)`: the village's main lane **continues OUT** of the cluster as this track and **runs OFF the map edge** (labelled e.g. "to the road") - it must NOT stop mid-landscape (that reads as a path to nowhere). `connector_lane_runs_off_edge` gates this: a lane flagged `connector` must have an endpoint at/off the map boundary. Internal lanes (the spine, the field spur) are exempt - they legitimately end in the cluster or at the paddy. The field-ward spur (village to paddy) and the road-ward connector are the two ends of the same through-route.
  - *Rokugan nuance (setting, not history).* The Imperial TRUNK network is unusually well-kept - `government.md`: ~50,000 mi of Imperial roads, ~5,000 staffed waystations, maintained by **corvee** (peasants owe ~10-20 days/yr, "most often called for road repair after the spring thaws and autumn storms"), and Hantei X outlawed road tolls (see [[project_rokugan_unusually_well_run]]). But that is the trunk network; a village's own lanes and its local connector are still humble narrow tracks. An actual Imperial road, if shown, may read as a maintained (earth/gravel, wider) road distinct from the village track.
  - *Legacy:* `worn=False` (the old wide dashed lane) is kept ONLY so the not-yet-rebuilt dispersed pool maps (Hikari, Kikuta) render unchanged; new/rebuilt maps use `worn=True`. `M["lanes"]` records each lane (classified LINEAR for overlap - structs avoid it).
- **Homestead groves (*yashikirin*) are a UNIVERSAL-where-possible windbreak L-belt on the WINDWARD side - the largest homestead appurtenance** (drives `groves_on_windward_side`, `groves_clear_of_paddies`, `groves_clear_of_structures`, `groves_are_substantial`, `groves_where_possible`, `gardens_clear_of_groves`, `yards_unshaded_by_groves`, and `no_groves_inside_walls`; the homestead SOLVER spaces each farm so all its features fit, then groves are placed by `farmsteads()` in a second pass).
  - *What it is.* The **yashikirin** (屋敷林) is the cluster of shelter trees the farm homestead planted around its house - regionally *igune* (Tohoku), *kainyo* (the Tonami plain). It is a **windbreak** (*bofurin*) against the cold prevailing winter wind, and it doubled as a **larder of wood**: firewood and fallen-branch fuel, construction timber (prized cedar/zelkova, often on the west), bamboo, fruit/nuts (persimmon, chestnut), leaf-litter for compost, summer shade, and a buffer slowing fire jumping between homesteads. Drawn as a **size-adaptive L-BELT** wrapping the windward N + W walls (one `groves` record per belt ARM), each arm a **dense, overlapping MIXED stand** of three species - tall **evergreen** conifer (dark, dense apex; the windbreak backbone, cedar/pine), **deciduous** broadleaf (mid green; timber/fruit), and a **bamboo** clump (*take*; fine culms with leafy tops) - so it reads as a real grove, not a few scattered trees. Each arm's canopy count scales with its area (grain-invariant, so a grove reads the same at town and city scale), with a few large emergent crowns over many small; the whole belt is sized to be the LARGEST homestead feature (bigger than the house), distinct from the big `s.forest` area feature and the striped kitchen-garden bed.
  - *Historical scale - the real numbers (research grounding, for calibrating the glyph).* A homestead grove is a substantial STAND, not a few trees. The best hard data is a 1987 survey of Kashima in the Tonami plain (the classic *kainyo* dispersed-farmstead country, 46 households): **~33 trees of trunk diameter >= 10 cm per homestead**, of which cedar (*sugi*) was ~48% (**~16 cedars per house**), the rest spread over ~83 other species; **~6 species per homestead** (range 1-14); a large/notable homestead ran **200+ trees across 31 species**. That count is trunks >= 10 cm ONLY - it EXCLUDES the bamboo stand (hundreds of culms), saplings, and the trimmed hedge layer - so the honest figure for a typical grove is **~30-40 mature trees + a bamboo grove + understory**, and a big one **100-200+**. The grove canopy footprint is therefore the LARGEST homestead appurtenance - **bigger than the farmhouse**, and far bigger than the garden or threshing yard - wrapping the N/W as a belt several trees deep. The map need not draw every tree (houses/yards are already oversized symbols), but per Principle "relative sizes roughly honest" the grove glyph must read at the RIGHT relative scale: clearly the dominant homestead feature, a dense stand suggesting dozens of trees - not a garden-sized clump of 5-10. *(Cross-check on the windward rule: Okinawa's homestead groves sit on the E/N sides, because the islands' damaging wind is the typhoon/NE monsoon, not the mainland NW - same logic, different geography, which is exactly why `windward` is a per-map knob.)*
  - *Prevalence - near-universal in dispersed-settlement country.* In a *sankyoson* (dispersed-farmstead) landscape - Tonami, Izumo, the Shonai/Sendai plains - the grove was **effectively universal, one per farmhouse (~100%)**; it IS the defining feature of that landscape (Tonami: ~7,000 grove-ringed homesteads over ~22,000 ha, about one grove per ~3 ha, farmhouses ~150-180 m apart). In **nucleated** villages (the more common East-Asian/China-leaning pattern) the per-house windbreak is less universal and the landscape instead carries **village-edge / shrine *fengshui* groves**. Our farmhouse-ring villages are semi-dispersed, which justifies a HIGH per-house prevalence. So the grove is **universal where physically possible**: `meta(grove_prevalence=N)` defaults to **1.0** (every farm is a candidate; lower it for a sheltered/atypical microclimate). The homestead SOLVER shifts each farm to make windward room, and a **minimal-clump fallback** means any farm with even a sliver of windward room gets *some* grove (small is fine - the spacious farms carry the big stands). The only farms left grove-less are those whose windward side is genuinely blocked - all paddy/edge, the sun-corridor south of a neighbour's yard, or intramural - which **`groves_where_possible`** verifies (it fires if a grove-less farm actually had clear windward room).
  - *Intramural farms (inside city walls) carry NO windbreak groves (default).* The *yashikirin* is the shelter of an ISOLATED farmstead alone in open fields; an in-wall plot is embedded in the urban fabric (surrounding buildings already break the wind) and sits on land too precious for tree belts - so a walled city's interior agricultural district draws NO groves (the grove pass skips any farm inside `M['wall']`; `no_groves_inside_walls` gates it; `meta(inwall_groves=True)` to override). The driver is "the house is not isolated + land is precious", NOT "the wall blocks the wind" (a wall shelters only a narrow downwind band).
  - *Why the WINDWARD side (N/W by default), never the sunny side.* The grove shelters the wall the wind hits and stays OFF the sunny lee, because the house faces the sun and the work yard needs it. The default windward is **N/W**: the **East Asian winter monsoon** (the Siberian high draining cold air southeastward) blows from the **NW across China AND Japan alike** - China is the source region, not an exception - so N+W is windward and the S/E is the sheltered, sunny side. The grove places a deep belt ARM on each windward face (N **and** W for NW), wrapping the corner into an L; a cardinal windward gets one deep band. **Since Rokugan leans continental (China-like), the wind is the same NW but drier and dustier** (continental dust, not maritime snow) - the orientation is unchanged; only the flavor shifts.
  - *Per-map geography overrides the default.* A map keys the grove off its own exposure with **`meta(windward=...)`** (`"N"`/`"NE"`/`"E"`/.../`"NW"`, default `"NW"`) - e.g. farmland downwind of a specific sea, mountain wall, or the western Burning Sands/steppe. The check reads the same `meta.windward`, so the geometry gate follows the declared wind.
  - *Size-adaptive, merging, never drops a house.* Each windward arm is tried at full depth, then shallower, then a shorter partial belt, then a **minimal clump** to slip into any sliver. Arms are kept OUT of `placed`, so a neighbour's grove may **MERGE** with this one - clustered homesteads read as one continuous windbreak (historically each grove was individual, but adjacent ones ran together; we do not model *shared* groves). Groves are placed in a **second pass after every farmhouse + its yard + garden**, so an optional grove can never block a neighbour's mandatory appurtenances and drop that house. The checks gate GEOMETRY per arm (windward / off-paddy / off-buildings), the typical grove's SCALE (`groves_are_substantial`: median grove >= **0.5x** its house - the spacious farms carry real stands, small clumps on cramped farms are fine), and presence (`groves_where_possible`, above).
  - *The homestead SOLVER - one rule that keeps the whole farmstead coherent.* `_solve_homestead` searches a **widening spiral of house positions** (the placed spot first, then shifts) for one where the threshing yard, the dooryard garden, AND room for a windward grove all fit, non-overlapping - keeping the farm **field-adjacent** and dropping it only if even its yard+garden won't fit anywhere (rare). This **spaces a crowded homestead out** instead of stacking features (so the grove no longer needs to overlap the garden - `gardens_clear_of_groves` gates that the two never stack), and it leaves grove-room so the second pass can place a grove on nearly every farm. To help it, the **dooryard garden prefers the LEE sunny side** (east, then the SE/SW corners) and takes the **windward wall LAST**, leaving that side free for the grove.
  - *And a grove never shades a yard.* **A grove never sits in the sun-corridor directly south of a threshing yard** (`yards_unshaded_by_groves`): the yard dries rice in the southern sun, so a *neighbour's* grove immediately to its south would shade it (a grove is N/W of its own house, so this only steers it off a neighbour's yard).
- **Village windbreak - the Chinese FENGSHUI FOREST (风水林), a COMMUNAL grove, is the NUCLEATED village's windbreak (NOT per-house groves)** (drives `village_windbreak_present`, `village_windbreak_on_windward_side`, `village_groves_clear_of_paddies`, `groves_clear_of_lanes`, and the nucleated SKIP of `groves_where_possible`; drawn by `s.village_grove(poly, role=..., dense=...)`, recorded in `M['village_groves']`; researched + source-verified 2026-07, this is the record - do not re-research).
  - *Trees stay OFF the paths (`groves_clear_of_lanes`).* A lane/street/road is bare trodden earth - you do not plant trees on it - so `village_grove` skips any clump within a corridor's keep-out (lanes + town streets + the road, via `_corridor_buffers`), just as it skips houses/yards/gardens/paddy. Each `village_grove` records its **actual drawn clump centres + radius** (`clumps`, `r`), and the check verifies none sits on a corridor (the recorded bounding `poly` covers the whole scatter region, so the check must test the real clumps, not the poly). The per-house *yashikirin* groves (`M['groves']`, a rect each) are checked the same way. This caught the leafy copse scatter landing on the village spine lane.
  - *Trees stay OUT of the SOUTHERN SUN of yards + gardens (`village_trees_unshade_yards_and_gardens`).* The drying yard and the kitchen garden need the sun, which comes from the SOUTH (+y), so no village-grove clump may sit in the strip directly south of either - `village_grove` skips any clump in a yard's/garden's southern sun-corridor (a touch wider than the check, so it stays clear), and the check verifies it against the recorded clumps. (This is the communal-grove companion to `yards_unshaded_by_groves`, which does the same for the per-house groves; the scatter avoids sitting ON a yard/garden, but that is not enough - it must also avoid being just SOUTH of one.)
  - *Shape - ORGANIC and EMBRACING, never a ruled wall (a correction the GM caught).* Real groves hug the terrain and WRAP the settlement; a rectangle + a square read as machine-planted. So `village_grove` takes an **irregular polygon footprint** (ragged, terrain-following) and FILLS it with clumps on a jittered grid, SKIPPING any clump that would land on a house / threshing yard / garden / paddy - which lets the belt nestle right up to the village edge without ever drawing trees on a building or in the crops. The 背山面水 ideal ("back to the hill, face the water") drives the whole layout: the DENSE wood sits on the high/windward BACK and wraps the flanks (the 玄武 back-forest + 青龙/白虎 embracing arms), while the FIELD/water side stays OPEN. The back belt is drawn as a CRESCENT embracing the windward edge, deepest in the middle and tapering into horns that wrap the corners.
  - *Why communal, not per-house.* The per-house *yashikirin* (the bullet above) is the DISPERSED-farmstead windbreak; a NUCLEATED cluster (the China-leaning default, per 'Settlement form') shelters itself and cannot fit a ~6x-house grove-bundle on every farm. So the nucleated windbreak is a single **village-scale grove**, exactly the dominant Chinese pattern: the **fengshui forest**, a grove protected by taboo (sacred "dragon" woodland, cutting forbidden - which is why these survived as the most mature forest stands in southern China). This is the CHINA-first answer per the core principle (surveys across Fujian, Jiangxi, Guangdong, Hunan, Anhui - our rice-south analog; Chen & Coggins et al.).
  - *Three parts, sited by role - the DENSE wood is concentrated, but the village is LEAFY, not bare (a second GM correction).* The greenery is STRUCTURED (not a uniform forest), and specifically does NOT line the main road or shade the paddies/drying yards (the field/downslope side stays open - same crop-and-drying logic as the *yashikirin* sunny-lee rule). But a south-China village was NOT bare tan ground with a few tree-blobs: houses nestled in scattered **bamboo and dooryard fruit** (litchi, longan, persimmon, citrus, mulberry - some villages effectively bamboo-encircled). So the three parts are:
    1. **Back-village belt (`role="windbreak"`, 后龙林)** - the big one: a dense grove crescent EMBRACING the high **WINDWARD back edge** (default NW - the winter-monsoon wall), nestled against the cluster and wrapping the NW/SW flanks. Villages backed against the high ground, so where the high side is windward the back grove IS the wind wall (Hoshigaoka: the WEST, away from the E/SE paddy). `village_windbreak_present` requires it; `village_windbreak_on_windward_side` requires its centroid windward of the house-cluster centroid.
    2. **Water-mouth cluster (`role="water_mouth"`, 水口林)** - a smaller irregular cluster of big old trees at the LOW entrance / water-mouth (where the stream/track exits down-valley), "locking in" the water and qi and marking the entrance.
    3. **Leafy scatter (`role="copse"`, `dense=False`)** - bamboo + dooryard fruit clumps filling the OPEN gaps THROUGHOUT the cluster (not a light dusting - the village reads as embedded in green), the conifer-free 'dooryard' species mix. Drawn by passing the whole-cluster region as one sparse-filled polygon; `village_grove` drops a clump into every gap that is clear of a house/yard/garden/paddy, so the houses end up nestled in bamboo and fruit trees while the lanes, yards, and field side stay open.
  - *Scale - the real numbers (research grounding, for calibrating the glyph).* Field surveys of southern-China village fengshui forests: **~2 groves per village** on average; **stem density ~3,400 woody stems/ha**, basal area ~49 m²/ha (genuine closed-canopy forest, not scattered trees); patch AREA is highly variable (famous lineage-village groves exceed 20 ha, e.g. Lingtou, 800 years old), but a TYPICAL village grove is a small forest patch, **~1-2 ha for the back grove** (modest villages <1 ha, big clan villages much larger) and **~0.1-0.5 ha for the water-mouth cluster**. So a ~1-2 ha back belt is *thousands* of woody stems in total, of which roughly **100-300 are mature canopy trees** over a dense bamboo/shrub understory; the water-mouth is **a few dozen big old trees**. Per "relative sizes roughly honest": the back belt reads as a real small FOREST (clearly the largest vegetation feature, a wall of dozens-to-hundreds of crowns - `village_grove` fills its polygon with overlapping dense clumps so a big belt does not read as a handful of lone trees), the water-mouth as a distinct smaller cluster, plus the bamboo/fruit scatter through the village. Hoshigaoka draws a ~1-2 ha embracing windward belt + a ~0.3 ha water-mouth + the leafy scatter.
  - *Why the sizing is honest at this scale.* At 1 px = 2 ft a ~1-2 ha grove is ~27,000-54,000 px² - genuinely large relative to the ~11 ha built cluster (~10-18%), so the belt reads as the dominant feature without being cartoonishly oversized. Sources: Fengshui woodland (Wikipedia); Chen & Coggins et al., "Fengshui forests and village landscapes in China" (57-village survey); Hu et al., "Values of village fengshui forest patches" (Pearl River Delta, 32 patches, the density/basal-area figures); "Village Fengshui Forests as Cultural and Ecological Heritage" (Forests 2020).
  - *The FAR side of the back-grove is NOT more forest - it is degraded FUEL-AND-FODDER COMMONS + the back-slope waste (`s.commons(poly)`, `M['commons']`; drives `commons_clear_of_paddies`, `commons_beyond_the_windbreak`).* The lush grove is a THIN protected band; the taboo is exactly *why* it is green when the surrounding hills are not. South China's hills were progressively stripped for firewood/timber over ~1,000 years, so past the grove the ground is cut-over and open - "a monotonous succession of hills either deforested or carrying open stands" of masson pine / China-fir, with severe erosion. So `commons` draws the far/upslope side deliberately OPEN and SPARSE - coarse grass tufts, low brush, and a FEW scraggly individual pines - VISUALLY DISTINCT from the dense dark closed-canopy grove (it is a *commons*, not a field, and never the flooded paddy). It has **NO solid fill and NO hard outline** (an early filled quad read as a literal rhombus): the scrub is a FEATHERED scatter that thins to nothing at the margin, so the ground has no boundary at all, just scrub petering out onto the open slope. It is drawn as a ragged BAND that PARALLELS the grove's back at a jittered width and is drawn UNDER the forest, so the grove's canopy overlaps its inner edge and the two ABUT seamlessly (no bare gap between wood and scrub). The land TOPOSEQUENCE is **village -> back-grove -> fuel commons -> (off-map domain boundary)**, so the commons sits on the windward/high side and FURTHER out than the windbreak (`commons_beyond_the_windbreak` gates the ordering; `commons_clear_of_paddies` keeps it off the wet crop). Sources: South China degraded lands (millennium of fuel/timber cutting); FAO forest-rehabilitation (deforested hills, open pine, erosion).
  - *`commons(poly, role="grazing")` for the OTHER dry margins.* The same scrub glyph fills any dry, marginal, non-paddy flank of the SATOYAMA toposequence (grass/scrub grazing hill) - the NW pond CATCHMENT, the NORTH up-valley head above the dry-field (*hatake*) margin, the NE flank, the SW/south back-slope behind the village. `role="grazing"` is general marginal hill-grazing, so it is **exempt from `commons_beyond_the_windbreak`** (that windward-back-slope ordering only applies to the fuel/fodder commons proper, `role="commons"`, the default); `commons_clear_of_paddies` still applies to all. **Fill the WHOLE margin as a CONTINUOUS RING, not isolated corner patches** (the GM's rule): every "empty" edge of the map is DRY marginal land in a NW-high/SE-low valley - so it is a continuous band of the toposequence (pond-catchment -> dry fields -> grazing scrub -> grove -> graveyard -> marsh), never open plain or forest-everywhere and never a few little rectangles floating in tan. Broad polygons that span a whole edge (e.g. one north band across the entire top above the dry fields, dropping at the east edge to meet the SE marsh; one south band filling the back-slope below the village) read as real hillsides; small disconnected quads read as patches. Keep each `grazing` polygon OFF the crops: `commons` auto-skips the paddy envelope, but NOT dry-plots/structures - so hold the polygon ABOVE the dry-field tops and WEST of the paddy edge (query those extents; don't eyeball) so scrub never dots the rows. On a fully-filled map the auto-title has no blank margin to land in, so leave it a small tan CLEARING (dip the band's top edge over the title's x-range - a natural col in the ridge) and call `s.title()` AFTER `crop_to_content()` as usual so `title_clear_of_features` still passes.
  - *DRAW ORDER for the margin fill (the GM's rule): place the grazing/scrub AFTER the farmhouses + groves but BEFORE the graveyard + shrine.* After the houses/groves so the scrub fills the gaps THEY leave (not drawn over them); before the funerary + religious features so those sit ON the scrubland, not on bare tan - burial + *kegare* belong on the waste back-slope, and a shrine at the water-mouth sits in its grove/scrub. In the gen this means: farmhouses -> village_grove -> `commons(role="grazing")` margin bands -> `crop_to_content()` -> `shrine_hall` / `cemetery`.
  - *Scrub/reeds are ground-cover UNDER the trodden road, never over it (the GM's rule: "the road should not look overgrown").* A constantly-walked lane stays BARE - so `commons` and `marsh` skip any scatter point on a lane's tread (`_on_lane`, clearing the tread half-width + ~3 px, off `M['lanes']`). The connector track that leaves the village always crosses the back-slope scrub; without the skip, scrub tufts drawn AFTER the lane sit on top of it and it reads as overgrown. The skip is per-tuft (the lane must already be recorded, i.e. drawn *before* the ground-cover - which it is: lanes lay their no-build corridor before the houses), so the tread reads clean ON the scrub regardless of z-order, and it generalises to a marsh causeway. No manifest-level check (scatter points are not individually recorded, same as the paddy-skip) - covered by `test_settlement` unit tests.
  - *Scrub/reeds are also kept OFF every building footprint.* `commons` and `marsh` skip any scatter point that falls on a `block_polys` no-build footprint (houses, wellheads, and especially the **shrine hall + its torii arch**) - a commons polygon that OVERLAPS the shrine (Ueda's water-mouth commons contains the shrine) must not dot grass tufts and scraggly pines over the hall and gateway. Same per-tuft mechanism as the lane skip (the structure must be drawn *before* the ground-cover; place `shrine_hall` before `commons`), same no-manifest-check caveat - covered by unit tests (`test_commons_keeps_scrub_off_a_shrine_and_torii`, `test_marsh_keeps_reeds_off_a_building`). Distinct from `shrine_clear_of_grove_trees` / `torii_clear_of_grove_trees`, which gate the dense *grove* clumps (those ARE recorded, so they carry a check); the loose commons scatter is handled purely at draw time.
  - *The village GRAVEYARD is the shrine's CHURCHYARD (built); the back-slope carries two still-deferred slices.* (a) the **village GRAVEYARD / cemetery (BUILT)** sits in the **shrine's precinct** - its resident Brother of Shinsei performs the funerary rites (see the churchyard rule above, `village_graveyard_by_shrine`), so `s.cemetery(cx, cy, w, h, parish=False, organic=True)` (a village plot ~0.2-0.3 ha, NOT a temple parish ground) goes in the clear ground of the shrine's yard, OFF the hall + torii themselves. **`organic=True` for a village burial ground**: a peasant graveyard was never surveyed into a ruled rectangle - it grew as an unbounded earthen patch, so `organic` draws an IRREGULAR blob (a jittered polygon inscribed in the `w x h` footprint) with the markers filling that blob, instead of the default rounded rectangle (which is right for a walled temple *parish* precinct). The recorded bbox AND the no-build block stay the `w x h` rectangle, so every placement / clearance / set-back check is unaffected - only the DRAWN ground is organic. It still keeps a set-back from OPEN water and the paddy (`funerary_set_back_from_water`) - the shrine sits at the water-mouth but the yard has dry ground off the field; and cremation means the old "graves foul the water source" body-burial worry is weak anyway (Rokugan inters *bones*). **Placement CROP bonus:** moving the graveyard from the far back-slope INTO the shrine precinct means it is no longer the map's outlying westernmost feature, so `crop_to_content` tightens that edge automatically (Ueda's west pulled in ~85px). The high/back slope beyond the grove still carries the two DEFERRED slices: (b) **dry hill-fields + orchards** (the unirrigable "hill crops": sweet potato, tea, mulberry, tung-oil, bamboo, dry grains - the *hatake* / 旱地 margin); (c) **rocky / eroded / poor-soil waste**. So the back-slope zone, in order of workability, is: dry-field + orchard patches -> fuel/fodder scrub commons -> waste. (The `commons` feature above is the scrub-commons slice; the dry-field/waste texture is the remaining deferred slice.)
  - *The village SHRINE sits at the WATER-MOUTH ENTRANCE (built; drives `religious_matches_scale` = village -> a `"shrine"`).* A Chinese village's tutelary / earth-god shrine (土地庙 Tudi Miao) stood "near the entrance, in the most strategic feng-shui location" - which is the **water-mouth** (水口), the feng-shui-critical entry/exit point where the connector track leaves for the road (and where the water-mouth grove already grows). So the village's one Shinto shrine nestles there in its sacred grove (*chinju no mori*), a small `s.shrine_hall(x, y, "", w=30, h=24, kind="shrine", primary=True, torii=[(tx, ty)], graveyard=False)` fronted by a torii facing the village. **SIZE it small** (~30x24 px ~ 270 m², a touch bigger than a plain farmhouse) - a village earth-god shrine is a modest HALL, not a temple; `shrine_hall`'s default 120x82 is a town-monastery / city-temple size and reads as a mansion on a village (pass small `w, h`). **No label** (`label=""`): the vermilion hall + torii glyph reads as a shrine on sight, and the graveyard's marker rows read as a graveyard, so both are left unlabeled per "do not label what is visually evident". `graveyard=False` on the `shrine_hall` call only suppresses its DEFAULT attached parish plot; the village graveyard is placed SEPARATELY (its own `s.cemetery`) in the shrine's yard - the churchyard, since the shrine's monk tends the dead (see the churchyard rule above). This is the CHINA-first answer (earth-god shrine at the entrance); the Japanese surface is the tutelary *chinju* in its grove. A village gets a SHRINE, never a temple (see the village-shrine-not-temple rule + `religious_matches_scale`: hamlet none / village shrine / town monastery / city temples).
- **Farmer wealth + homestead variation - houses come in a modest range of sizes and PROPORTIONS** (drives `farmhouse_sizes_vary`, `farmhouse_aspect_in_range`). Real holdings ranged from the landless *mizunomi* to *honbyakushO* landholders, and no two farmsteads were copy-pasted, so a plain farmhouse varies TWO ways depending on the settlement mode:
  - *DISPERSED (ring villages): a wealth TIER scaling the whole glyph.* A plain farmhouse takes a modest **wealth tier** (deterministic in its position): ~30% poor (0.9x), ~50% typical (1.0x), ~20% well-off (1.12x), recorded as the house's `wealth`. The tier scales the **rendered house** and, with it, the **homestead grove** (a richer farm reads bigger across the board). **Scope limit:** the wealth factor is applied to the rendered glyph ONLY - the footprint is reserved and the yard/garden placed at BASE size, then the house is drawn (and grove sized) at `base x wealth` - so it never perturbs packing/household counts. Here the yard/garden/shed stay uniform (scaling them coupled into placement and dropped houses), so the wealth signal rides the two largest features.
  - *NUCLEATED (clustered villages): the FOOTPRINT itself jitters, LENGTH-dominant (minka bays).* A clustered house varies its base `w,h` directly (position-seeded, wealth stays 1.0): the **length** (E-W long axis) jitters more (~0.85-1.35x), the **depth** (N-S) far less (~0.90-1.10x) - because a *minka* grew by **adding bays along its length** (a *hisashi* aisle, an extra *ma*), staying "longer but not much wider," never ballooning into a square or a 4:1 shed. `farmhouse_aspect_in_range` guards the result (fires above ~2.7:1; a minka norm is ~1.3-2.5:1). And here the **yard and garden vary too** (see their bullets - garden jitters up from its minimum, yard down from its maximum, both gaining irregular-quad shape and the garden an occasional split), because the nucleated bundle reserves each appurtenance's footprint before packing, so varying them within that reservation does not tip a boxed-in farm over.
  - *One check, both encodings.* `farmhouse_sizes_vary` measures the **effective rendered area = w × h × wealth²**, so it catches a regression that flattens houses under EITHER path (uniform footprint + wealth tiers, OR jittered footprint at wealth 1.0). The **headman** is exempt and stays the largest (`headman_is_largest`). All the jitter is a deterministic sin-hash of position (`_hjit`), so it never draws from the placement RNG and never ripples household counts.
- **Farmhouses face SOUTH** (drives `houses_face_south`, and the yard's south-front placement). *Minka* were oriented south for sunlight (warmth and drying); we render the long axis E-W with the entrance on the south (+y) side.
- **Commercial frontage, residential depth, wealth-varied housing** (drives `businesses_front_streets`, `buildings_face_street`, `housing_off_main_street`, `poor_housing_mostly_interior`, `merchant_residences_behind_businesses`, `housing_aligned_behind_storefronts`, `town_merchant_housing_varied` / `town_laborer_housing_varied` / `city_merchant_housing_varied` / `city_laborer_housing_varied` / `city_samurai_housing_varied`, `city_merchant_housing_spread`). In premodern Japanese castle-towns (*jokamachi*) and Chinese cities the valuable street frontage is COMMERCE: shops and workshops line the high street, each facing the road, while dwellings sit behind them or in the block interior reached by alleys. A merchant family lived over/behind its own shop (so merchant residences sit directly behind the storefronts, sharing their orientation). Housing was **stratified by wealth** - a few rich households in large homes or walled courtyard estates, most in modest/uniform dwellings, never one size - and the merchant quarter SPREADS (courtyard compounds) while the laborer warren packs dense and uniform.
- **Communal wells: density, siting, and the samurai exception** (drives `city_well_density_sufficient`, `city_wells_in_block_interiors`, `city_neighborhoods_have_wells`, `wells_among_dwellings`, `settlement_dwellings_watered`, `city_samurai_quarter_has_no_public_wells`). Commoner *machi* shared **public wells in the block interiors** (courtyard wells), roughly **1 per ~10-20 households** - enough that no quarter lacks water, not one on every corner. The samurai/government quarter shows **NO public wells**: noble and official households drew from **private wells inside their own walled compounds** (a status + security distinction), so a communal well there would read wrong.
- **Caste geography & status zoning** (drives `walled_city_has_burakumin_inside`, `city_samurai_quarter_gated` / `city_samurai_ward_sealed` / `city_ward_fence_*`, `city_flophouse_in_humble_quarter`, `city_samurai_estates_outside` / `city_estates_in_southeast`, and the burakumin-neighborhood siting). Castes lived apart. Burakumin cluster at the margin (a town's edge, or outside a city) - but a WALLED city keeps at least one burakumin neighborhood INSIDE, because it cannot do without their labor during a siege. The samurai/government quarter is sealed off the commoner streets by **kido ward-gates shut at night** (status separation + night security; a small provincial city uses kido, not the continuous walled wards of a great Tang-style capital). The cheap flophouse (*kichin-yado*) belongs in a humble laborer/agrarian quarter, not beside the temples, merchants, or samurai. Wealthy samurai kept walled country **estates outside the walls** and commuted in, for roomier houses than cramped city lots allowed.
- **Funerary geography: pollution, fire, and the two faiths** (drives `cemetery_clear_of_shrine`, `funerary_set_back_from_water`, `cemetery_in_temple_precinct` / `city_temples_have_graveyards`, `cremation_ground_set_back_from_main_road` / `cremation_ground_not_between_temple_and_road` / `city_has_cremation_ground`, `city_has_ossuary`, `walled_graveyards_inside_and_outside` / `walled_exterior_cemetery_larger`). Death-pollution (*kegare*) is a Shinto concern: no grave sits hard against a **shrine**, and burial grounds keep well back from **water** (a flooding/leaching grave fouls the supply). Buddhism manages the dead, so a graveyard is a temple **parish (*danka*) ground** sitting in a temple/monastery precinct. The **crematory (*kasoba*)** sits OUTSIDE the walls (smoke, fire risk, pollution), set back from the main road, and never between a temple and the road (no one should walk past the pyre to reach worship); monks officiate with burakumin assistants (a religious order stands outside the caste system, so handling the dead does not pollute it). A pauper **ossuary** holds the *muenbotoke* (unconnected dead). A centuries-old city splits graveyards inside + outside the walls, the main extramural ground the largest.
- **Religious building by settlement scale** (drives `religious_matches_scale`, the `torii_*` / `city_temple_approach_has_torii` checks, `town_declares_monasteries` / `town_monasteries_dedicated`, `city_temple_neighborhood_has_shrines`). Type and size track scale and the Shinto/Buddhist split: a **hamlet** has no religious building of its own (it shares the village district's), a **village** a Shinto **shrine**, a **town** a Buddhist **monastery** (dedicated to a patron Fortune), a **city** full **temples** (its temple neighborhood dotted with small wayside shrines). A **torii** (Shinto gateway) fronts any shrine/temple a street runs straight up to. (See also the village-SHRINE-not-temple rule in the project memory.)
- **Government & commerce of a seat of administration** (drives `town_has_magistrate_manor`, `town_has_caravan_inn`, `town_has_granary`, `city_has_governor_mansion` / `city_has_six_ministries` / `city_has_ministry_of_rites`, `city_ministries_cluster_at_government` / `city_ministries_front_a_street` / `city_governor_mansion_large` / `city_government_offices_dont_abut`, `city_inspection_station_at_each_gate` / `city_gate_caravan_facilities`, `city_has_gate_market` / `walled_town_has_gate_market`). Following imperial Chinese *yamen* and Japanese *jokamachi* practice, government offices cluster around the **governor's mansion** (the grandest compound) and FRONT an official avenue, never floating mid-block. A provincial city carries the **Six provincial Ministries** (one being Rites, sited in the temple neighborhood). The state taxes trade: **inspection/tariff stations at every gate**, **caravan facilities** (inn + stables + animal pasture) on the trade route, a **tax-rice granary** at a collecting magistracy, and **markets** springing up just outside the gates. A county-seat **town** is run by a magistrate from a walled manor.
- **Paddy plot grain + IRREGULAR PATCHWORK: fields are a fine mosaic of small, odd-shaped bunded plots** (drives `paddy_field`'s recursive `_paddy_plots` subdivision, the `plot` grain, and the `bund` stroke). A premodern wet-rice field was not one green expanse but a mosaic of *many small irregular bunded plots* (each a separately-flooded basin walled by low raised-earth ridges, *aze*, that double as footpaths); a single 1-cho (~1 ha) holding was subdivided into **dozens**, and pre-consolidation paddies were often just a few *se* (~100-300 m^2). Two rules:
  - *Shape - irregular patchwork, NOT a grid.* Pre-modern paddies were fitted to the land and water by piecemeal reclamation and inheritance, so plots are odd-sized and odd-shaped with bunds meeting at **T-junctions**; the tidy rectangular grid is a **modern (Meiji/Showa) land-consolidation (*kochi seiri*) artifact** and reads as anachronistic. So `_paddy_plots` builds the field by **recursively splitting** it - cut each plot's LONG axis with a straight, slightly-angled *aze* at a jittered 0.36-0.64 fraction, down to the target grain (with size variation) - giving the genuine irregular-parcel look. (An earlier jittered *grid* was still too regular; a proper recursive split fixes it.)
  - *Size - to scale.* At village scale (1 px = 2 ft), `plot=34` -> ~0.10 acre/plot (with the split variation ~0.05-0.17 acre), squarely the real pre-modern range (~1-5 *se*). The default `plot=46` (~0.19 acre) is the coarser legibility floor for older/denser maps; villages pass `plot=34`. *Departure:* true plots are finer still, but the bund network + planting-row hatching must stay legible at the 2600 px render. The **bund stroke thins with the grain** (`0.03*plot + 0.35`); per-plot colour = staggered flooding/planting, a fraction dry = barley/soy margins.
  - NOTE: the patchwork draws from the shared placement RNG, so changing `plot` shifts every downstream house position - the pool gens carry hand-picked seeds; a grain change means re-finding a clean seed (and placing big fixed buildings BEFORE the packs, which then flow around them).
- **Non-topographic relief: show slope by its CONSEQUENCES, never by shading** (drives the pictorial `hill()` mound, the `irrigation_ditch` + drainage water direction, and the deliberate ABSENCE of hillshade/contours). These maps are deliberately **not topographic** - no contour lines, no spot heights. Relief is shown three ways, which stack for a rice village:
  - *Pictorial hills.* A hill is a shaded **mound** (`hill()`: concentric tiers + radial hachures + a wooded cap) - the old *ezu* / estate-map convention, a picture of high ground, not survey lines.
  - *The farming shows the slope.* Water enters HIGH and leaves LOW; on genuine slopes paddies become **terraces** (*tanada*: long, narrow, CURVED contour-following strips, stacked with a riser between). Terracing is a **STEEP-slope feature** - a **gentle** valley tilt produces NO visible terraces, only a flat patchwork with imperceptible level steps, so we do NOT draw terraces on a gently-tilted village (it would misread as mountainous). Kikuta is a gentle tilt: flat patchwork, slope carried by water alone.
  - *Water direction is the honest gentle-slope cue.* Source high, drainage low. Kikuta: **NW = uphill** (the cold NW winter-monsoon wind pours off the high ground, so windward and uphill coincide) - pond/source at the NW corner, irrigation (*yosui*) distributary ditches (`irrigation_ditch`, drawn OVER the paddy since the deferred water layer sits UNDER the fields) fanning water in from the high NW, and a **drainage (*haisui*) ditch leaving the LOW SE corner** off-map (a hairline `channel(..., {"kind":"field"...}, {"kind":"offmap"})`). Wet rice needs water IN **and** water OUT - irrigation + drainage are the two halves of every paddy system (often separate networks) - so a drainage outlet is a real, expected feature, and it doubles as the downhill cue.
  - *What we REJECTED and why:* directional **shading / cast shadows** to imply the gradient. On a plan map, shaded relief reads as a **light source** ("the sun is in the NW"), not as a rising gradient - it fights the flat-plan convention and misleads. Slope is shown by water + (steep-only) terraces + pictorial hills, never by shading.
- **Marsh - where the paddy STOPS, the low ground is reed WETLAND, not dry plain** (`s.marsh(poly)`, `M['marshes']`; drives `marsh_on_low_ground`; researched + source-verified 2026-07). The empty ground beyond a village's paddy is not "open plain" by default - a wet-rice paddy stops where flooding the land stops being worth it, and *if that land were good paddy it would already be in the district* (the village would be larger). So the ground beyond is UNSUITABLE for paddy, and WHAT it is depends on WHY: **too HIGH to irrigate** -> dry fields (*hatake* / 旱地, the NW margin we already draw); **too STEEP** -> the wooded/scrubby back-slope (grove + graves); **poor/saline soil** -> waste / rough grazing; and - the key one for the LOW, downstream toe - **too LOW / WET / undrained** -> **reed MARSH / wet meadow**. This is grounded in the fact that **wet rice is reclaimed FROM marsh**: the lower-Yangzi / Tai-Lake paddies are embanked **polders (圩田/围田)** diked out into marsh and lake, and where reclamation stops (or the ground is too wet to manage) it *stays* reed wetland (abandoned paddy reverts to marsh). So `s.marsh` draws the low SE/downstream toe as WET and SPARSE: a faint blue-green wet tint (soft translucent patches), scattered reed/sedge tufts, and a few standing-water glints - FEATHERED to nothing at the margin (no hard edge, like the commons), a distinctly wet palette vs the dry tan scrub commons. It SKIPS any point on a paddy, so a generous region abuts the field's low edge (the polder embankment) and only fills the ground beyond, trailing off the map corner. `marsh_on_low_ground` verifies it sits DOWNHILL of the field (higher `fall` per `meta.down_deg`) - marsh is the low valley toe, never the high/dry side. **Do NOT default the beyond-the-paddy ground to dry "plain" or to forest** (valley floor is wet/cultivated, trees are on the hills); it is blank ("edge of our district") or, more accurately for the low end, marsh. **`marsh(poly, role="pond_fringe")`** draws the same reeds as a **water-edge fringe** around a POND's shallow shore (the reservoir sits in the hill catchment - *satoyama*; its shore is reedy): `marsh` skips the open pond water (and the paddy), so a ring polygon rims the shore; `role="pond_fringe"` is EXEMPT from `marsh_on_low_ground` (it is a water margin, wherever the pond is, not the valley toe). The pond's CATCHMENT hill (the cut-over slope that feeds it) is just more `commons` scrub placed behind/around it. So a pond need not float in blank tan: a reed fringe + a little catchment scrub read it as a valley-head reservoir in its watershed. Sources: rice domesticated in "naturally marshy areas" + paddy-as-reclaimed-marsh (AAS "Rice, Technology, and History"); Tai-Lake polders diked from marsh/lake; abandoned paddies revert to wetland.
- **Crop mix + paddy SURFACE: an irrigated valley is rice-dominant, and a wet paddy is a flooded sheet, not ruled rows** (drives the `paddy_field` crop roll and `_paddy_surface` vs `_rows`).
  - *Ratio - ~85% wet rice, dry crops on the MARGINS.* An irrigated valley village exists BECAUSE its land is waterable for rice, so its watered commons run overwhelmingly PADDY (~85%); dry upland crops (barley/*mugi*, soy, vegetables) cluster on the **higher, harder-to-water rim** - so dry/soy probability RISES toward the field edge (`edge_dist`), the well-watered interior all paddy, not scattered evenly. This share is deliberately ABOVE the ~55-60% paddy of Japan's *national* Edo arable - that average is dragged down by whole dry-upland regions (Kanto uplands, mountains) a watered valley common does not represent.
  - *One stage, not a rainbow.* A village transplants TOGETHER - shared irrigation water (released on one schedule) + exchanged labour (*yui*) - so at any moment its paddies are **largely a SINGLE growth stage** (default high-summer green, `PADDY_SHADES`), with only MINOR spread: a few early/late-variety plots, the odd low-lying plot still flooded (`FLOODED_SHADES`, ~6%), a rare early-ripening gold (`RIPE_SHADES`, ~5%). Do NOT scatter every stage evenly - that reads as a crop *mix*, not a rice village. (Corrected error: an earlier 22% flooded / 12% gold looked mixed.)
  - *Nitrogen - the paddy makes its OWN, so soy is food not fertiliser.* A flooded paddy is near self-sustaining for nitrogen: the standing water hosts N-fixing **cyanobacteria + *azolla*** and the **irrigation water carries in silt/nutrients** from upstream - which is why paddies crop continuously for centuries where dry-field monoculture exhausts the soil. Legumes entered as **winter green manure grown IN the drained paddy** (*renge* / Chinese milk vetch, plowed under before spring flooding) + applied night soil / ash / fish-and-oilseed cake - NOT soy on the margins washing in. So the ~6% soy is a **food crop** (dry fields, and characteristically on the paddy bunds - *aze-mame*, "ridge beans"), NOT the paddy's nitrogen supply.
  - *Surface - flooded, not rows.* A wet paddy is a flooded, mottled sheet (irregular hand-transplanted shoots + a water sheen), NOT the crisp parallel rows of **checkrow planting (*seijoue*), a Meiji improvement** - premodern transplanting was irregular, so ruled rows on a paddy are the same era-tell as the consolidation grid. At 2 ft/px a real ~30 cm transplant spacing is sub-pixel anyway, so rows would be invisible; `_paddy_surface` gives rice a sparse shoot-mottle + sheen. **DRY plots keep `_rows`** - barley/soy/veg WERE ridge-and-furrow row-cultivated, so rows are correct there, and the wet/dry contrast is itself realistic and legible.
- **In-field ditch geometry must be VALID, not just drawn** (drives `water_channels_obtuse_turns` + `field_ditches_terminate`; `water_field` records each ditch in `M["field_ditches"]` with its role). Two rules a real ditch obeys: (1) **obtuse turns only** - a canal/ditch never makes an acute (<90 deg) bend; an acute hairpin implies impossible topology (water can't flow round it). Checked at every interior vertex of every watercourse (channels, streams, and the in-field ditches): the incoming and outgoing segments must not fold back (dot >= 0). This is why the MAIN/DRAIN follow the SMOOTHED field boundary (gentle curves) rather than a jagged per-column scan. (2) **every ditch terminates at a ditch that leaves the field** - no channel runs to the middle of a paddy and dead-ends; each LATERAL's two ends sit on the MAIN or DRAIN, which are in turn fed by a pond channel / emptied off-map, so the whole net exits to the pond or the map edge. (3) **the whole net traces to a source AND a sink** (`field_ditches_reach_source_and_sink`): the watercourse graph (channels + streams + ditches, joined crossing-aware) must put every ditch in a component holding a POND-fed segment AND a runoff (off-map/stream) segment - a ditch tied to nothing outside the field, or one that never drains, fails. IMPLEMENTATION TRAP that motivated the termination check: a lateral must END *on* the main/drain, NOT overshoot it - an earlier version stubbed 0.5*plot past them, leaving dead-ends poking at the field edge that read as "ditches that don't drain". Negative fixtures: `pool/regressions/water_ditches_acute_turn_and_dead_end.json`, `.../water_lateral_stubs_overshoot_kikuta.json`.
- **Water-first fields (`water_field`): the irrigation network is the SKELETON; plots, crops, and colours are all DERIVED from it** so the map actually communicates the hydrology (drives `water_field`; the villages use it instead of `paddy_field`). WHY this replaced the layered approach: doing the patchwork, the crop roll, and the ditches as three INDEPENDENT random processes read as random no matter how each was tuned - the eye only sees structure when the pieces descend from ONE model. `water_field` takes a `source` (the pond, high side) and `drain` (low side / a lower field to cascade into) and builds, in the contour/fall-line frame: a SINGLE MAIN canal along the high edge fed from the pond at ONE point, SPARSE lateral ditches (every ~4-6 plot-columns) branching DOWN off it to a DRAIN ditch at the low edge - a connected TREE; it records a feed channel (pond->field) and a drain channel (field->drain). Then: (a) PLOTS are carved to the network (aligned to the laterals) - so they cannot help but follow the water; (b) CROP is a function of water-position - rice through the irrigated body (the paddies BETWEEN laterals are fed plot-to-plot, **田越し tagoshi CASCADE** - so MOST paddies having no direct ditch is CORRECT, not a bug), DRY only ABOVE the main canal / BELOW the drain (gravity cannot flood there) + the field MARGINS - ~85-90% rice. HISTORY: pre-modern used cascade + sparse branching distribution ditches; a ditch beside every paddy (用排水分離) is a MODERN (Meiji 耕地整理) improvement, anachronistic here; (c) COLOUR is ~ONE green (`RICE_GREENS`, three near-identical) so a rice field READS as one crop, flooded blue-green only along the head/drain where water pools, gold rare. RNG-isolated (decorative, no placement ripple). Kikuta: pond -> west+central (drain off-map SE) + midnorth (cascades into central). KNOWN residual: the grain holds a uniform ~45deg (the fixed NW-SE fall line) - real valleys let it DRIFT; and the settlement still necklaces the fields + the field OUTLINES are still blobs (the two structural items not yet done).
- **Water-first fields v2 - the WARP-THREAD engine (`waterfields.py`; SUPERSEDES the v1 bullet above).** WHY v2 exists: v1 still started from a field SHAPE (bbox -> blob) and fitted water to its boundary - and boundary-following water is a RING by definition, which is why the GM kept seeing orphan ditches and ring-shaped mains no matter how v1 was tuned. The construction was wrong, not the tuning. v2 inverts it: the water network is grown FIRST and the field is EMERGENT - every plot-column boundary is a thread marched downhill in lockstep fall-steps, clamped so threads can never cross and never pinch below one plot width; the blue delivery ditch is a thread's dug PREFIX (plain bund below); offtakes SPAWN from their parent thread mid-march so they always take off on the parent's real path; the drain is a gently-descending line fitted under the DEEPEST dug ends (shallower columns just cascade further to reach it). Crossings, orphan ditches, and acute turns are impossible BY CONSTRUCTION, not repaired after. Plots are carved between adjacent threads (contour-parallel bund rows; boundary falls back to the parent canal above a takeoff and to the drain below an end, so plots hug the canal and reach the collector without ever spilling past either). The build process is BY-EYE-FIRST: each assembly stage is approved by eye, then the checks are backfilled as ratchets around the accepted look. New water-first maps stage in `pool/wip/` (outside the test glob) during that build, then GRADUATE into `pool/` once complete - the base case **`pool/hoshigaoka`** has graduated (it now runs in `test_villages` and passes the full gate). `waterfields.py` itself stays outside the coverage gate (the emergent geometry is validated by the manifest checks + a regression corpus, not line coverage).
- **Water-first v2 historical grounding (researched + source-verified 2026-07; this is the record - do not re-research).**
   - **Pond**: a valley-head *tameike* behind an earthen dike, sitting ABOVE its fields ("located at a valley head and constructed by dividing off the valley mouth with an earthen dike... at elevations higher than the surface of the paddy fields they serve" - Tabayashi 1986, Geographical Review of Japan 60(1)). ONE outlet: an inclined intake (shahi) feeding a bottom conduit (sokohi) through the dam; the spillway is flood-safety, never distribution (Kagawa pref. tameike docs). Parent/child pond linkage (oyaike/koike, Kagawa; "melon-on-the-vine" in China) is attested flavor for larger systems.
   - **Distribution**: sluice -> head-race -> division point (bunsuiguchi) -> a branching TREE. "Main canals **gradually decrease in size as they are tapped by branch canals**" (Tabayashi) - hence the drawn taper. The smallest ditches "are often considered parts of the paddy fields they serve" - hence ditch-as-plot-boundary. SPARSE is correct: a village digs the minimum network; a ditch beside every paddy (yohaisui bunri) is a Meiji land-readjustment (1899/1905) anachronism.
   - **Layout modes** (terrain-driven; the GM wants all three eventually):
     - **COMB (the default)**: supply canals along the HIGH margins, delivery ditches perpendicular down-slope, one drain along the low line. Grounding: the Edo Kishu-school layout (Minuma-dai 1728: supply on the elevated margins, drainage channel on the lowest line, water reused downstream) AND codified Chinese canal doctrine (mains along contours/ridges on high ground, field channels perpendicular to contours). Chinese *beitang* pond systems - the direct tameike analogue - were THE dominant village-scale mode in rice China (8.3M ponds serving ~39% of irrigated area into the 1950s, ~71% in hilly regions); the GM chose the Chinese default deliberately (Rokugan demographics anchor to Song/Ming China).
     - **FAN (supported option, not default)**: gently-descending canals radiating from a valley-mouth apex - the Dujiangyan / Tedori-alluvial-fan geometry. Correct where the land fans out below the pond.
     - **JORI GRID (future option, recorded on GM request - NOT implemented)**: from the 7th century much of Japan's long-settled PLAINS carried an astronomically-oriented 109 m grid (jori-sei: 1-cho squares in 6x6-ri blocks, cut into ~12 x 109 m tan strips). A plains village in an ancient core province shows semi-regular GRIDDED paddies, not organic patchwork - Rokugan analog: ancient heartland provinces (e.g. Crane/Phoenix cores). The organic warp-thread patchwork is correct for terrain-following villages like Kikuta.
   - **Delivery-ditch SPACING - sparse, min-gapped, real Y-junctions.** The cascade reaches ~4-5 plots laterally, so a ditch every ~6-9 columns waters everything - the SPARSE pre-modern norm (a ditch beside every few paddies drifts toward the Meiji ditch-per-plot grid we avoid). Two placement rules keep it honest: (1) **min-gap** - a delivery ditch that takes off within ~2 plot-columns of another is DROPPED (`min_gap = 2*plot_across`; two ditches watering the same ground twice read as an artifact, not design); (2) **Y-junctions diverge** - a sub-branch takes off HIGH on its parent and heads off STEEPLY (bigger heading, longer run) so the two channels end up >~2 columns apart, a real fork serving a distinct sub-block, NOT two ditches running adjacent for a stretch. Only the INTERIOR (widest) blocks split. `offtakes_a`/`offtakes_b` control the count - keep the net sparse (Hoshigaoka: 3 canal-A offtakes + 1 sub + canal B + 1 canal-B offtake = ~6 ditches, gaps ~4-6 columns). Irregular gaps are CORRECT (terrain, fragmented holdings); evenly-gridded is the anachronism.
   - **Delivery ditches TAPER as they feed the paddies (`delivery_ditches_taper`).** A delivery ditch sheds its water into the paddies ALL ALONG its length, so the flow it carries dwindles - and so must its width, narrowing from full at the canal takeoff to a THREAD at the point where it has delivered the last of its water and stops. This is Tabayashi's "channels gradually decrease in size as they are tapped" rule (already applied to the supply canals) extended to the delivery ditches themselves - which are tapped continuously by the plots they feed. A blunt constant-width ditch that stops dead reads as jarring AND is less accurate. Implementation: `waterfields` tags each delivery ditch with `w` (head) + `w_tail` (thread), the gen draws it as decreasing-width stroked pieces (round caps soften the joins into a smooth narrowing) and records both widths on the field-ditch. `delivery_ditches_taper` gates it: every `role:"branch"` ditch with recorded widths must have `w_tail < ~0.85*w` (older `water_field` maps record no widths and are exempt). NOTE: canal B was reclassified `role:"main"` (it is a SUPPLY canal that narrows past its offtakes, like canal A) so the taper check targets only the true delivery ditches.
   - **Cascade (tagoshi)**: water passes plot-to-plot through bund cuts; MOST paddies touch no ditch; delivery ditches serve block heads and the water walks down the columns. Keep every plot within ~4-5 plots of a ditch laterally, AND cap the vertical tail: a dug ditch extends until no column cascades more than ~8-11 rows past its end to the collector (the engine enforces both). Ditches ENDING MID-FIELD is correct and intentional - the end is the delivery point to the block below, not a bug HEAD PLOTS must ABUT the supply canal (they take water directly from it through bund cuts - the first cascade link); the 'don't plant above the canal' guard is judged by the plot CENTROID, never per-vertex (a per-vertex test silently discards every canal-touching head plot, since their top edge sits ON the canal - this was a real bug, 8/552 plots touching the canal until fixed). (the string-depth bound is judgment, not a sourced number - the only unsourced quantity in this section).
   - **Drain (akusui)**: a dug collector below the DEEPEST delivery ends, exiting downstream; separate supply/drain at SYSTEM level is Edo-attested (Kishu school), per-plot separation is Meiji. Downstream reuse (next village / child ponds) is historical. FLOODED (blue) accents are reserved ENTIRELY for the closing rank that sits ON the collector, so every blue plot ABUTS the drain and reads as pooling-before-outfall, never a stranded mid-field reservoir (blue = flooded rice, not a separate water body); the closing rank's bottom vertices are SNAPPED onto the drain line (sample the drain fall at each vertex's own u, not the row top) so the field edge sits on the ditch. The whole field edge MUST abut the collector: the CLOSING RANK hems every column down to its OWN drain fall (the drain is diagonal, so the triangle between the last uniform row and the drain varies across a sector - filling only the deep side near each ditch leaves a sliver), backed by a final HEM PASS that walks the drain and fills any residual gap up to the existing field.
   - **The drain's two ENDS - water IN and water OUT, and neither dangles.** A drain cannot just STOP in bare ground - conservation of flow. (1) The HEAD (high end) begins AT the field's low SW corner (the westmost delivery-ditch bottom), NOT extended out to the boundary thread and beyond into open land - a collector starts where the first field drains in; the hem pass covers any SW-corner sliver. (An earlier `- uniform(12,26)` stub poked past the field into bare ground and read as "the ditch just stops" - removed.) (2) The OUTFALL (low end) discharges to a runoff SINK: historically paddy drainage is **reused by the next village downstream** or **returns to the valley's natural stream/river** (a standalone "drainage pond" is NOT the typical destination). So the akusui empties into a natural valley **BROOK** (`net["brook"]`, drawn `s.stream(..., frm={"kind":"drain"}, to={"kind":"offmap"})`, meandering, a touch wider than the dug ditch) that carries the water off-map DOWNHILL - **water IN via the pond feeder, water OUT via the brook**. The brook is grown only when the outfall sits INSIDE the frame (else the drain discharges off-map directly). A stream anchored `frm:"drain"` is a first-class case (the `anchored()` helper + `stream_runs_off_edge` accept a brook that starts at a drain outfall).
   - **Water SOURCES come from the map EDGE, not out of nowhere.** The pond does not generate water - its FEEDER must flow IN from off-map. Draw it as a real recorded `s.stream([edge_pt, ..., pond_pt], frm={"kind":"offmap"}, to={"kind":"pond"})` that starts at the map boundary (NOT the old decorative `pond(stream_curve=...)` stub, which floated above the pond and escaped every check). `pond_fed_from_edge` gates it: any stream whose end sits in the pond must have its FAR end at the map edge (a sole-storage / rain-fed pond with no feeder is exempt). So the whole valley reads coherently: feeder in from the edge -> pond -> sluice -> canals -> field -> drain -> brook out to the edge.
   - **The drainage matches the SLOPE, and the FIELD is BOUNDED in the frame (`field_fall`).** The march used to grow the field downhill until the threads left the MAP CORNER (`f_stop = max fall over corners + 300`), so the field filled the frame to the low corner and then SPILLED off the edge - and the drain outfall landed at that clipped far-corner, reading as "the water just stops." `build_comb(..., field_fall=N)` **caps** the downhill march at N fall-units, so the paddy is sized to the **population** (~83 acres for Hoshigaoka) and bounded WITHIN the frame with a low-side margin. Then the outfall lands at the field's true **downhill (max-fall) corner** - verify it: the outfall's fall value must EXCEED the head's (higher fall = lower elevation), so water flows head->outfall consistent with `down_deg` - and the brook discharges from there off the **downhill** map edge (for Hoshigaoka's NW-high/SE-low slant, off the SE/E). Because a capped field also stops spreading cross-slope, **size the FRAME to hold village + field + the low-side margin** (Hoshigaoka went 1980x1320 -> 2420x1560 so the whole 83-acre paddy, its outfall, and the brook all sit in-frame). This resolved the "drainage doesn't go anywhere / runs the wrong way" report: with the field in-frame the drain visibly outfalls at the downhill corner and the brook runs off the SE edge.
   - **A drainage collector runs CROSS-slope, but water never runs UPHILL (`drain_flows_downhill` + `drainage_discharges_downhill`, keyed off `meta.down_deg`).** A subtlety worth stating because it looks wrong at a glance: the drain (akusui) legitimately runs ~PERPENDICULAR to the fall - it is a COLLECTOR crossing the field's low margin to gather runoff from every cascade column; a drain running *downhill* would follow one column and collect nothing. So "the drain runs cross-slope / looks perpendicular to the flow" is CORRECT, not a bug - do NOT add a rule forcing drains to run within X degrees of the slant (it would ban the very collectors that are historically right). On a **diagonal** slope (down_deg=45) this reads oddly: the outfall sits toward the far EAST (downhill on an NW-high map) which is also "up the page" (north), so the collector *looks* like it flows uphill though it does not. Three checks pin the real constraints (all from `meta.down_deg`, so record it on every sloped map): **`drain_runs_cross_slope`** REQUIRES the collector to run roughly perpendicular to the fall (along-fall fraction of its head->outfall vector < 0.65, i.e. >~50 deg off the fall) - a drain running straight downhill collects nothing and fails; **`drain_flows_downhill`** - the outfall (the end discharging to the brook / off-map) must be the LOWER-ground end (never uphill of the head); **`drainage_discharges_downhill`** - the brook must head downhill. Together they encode "a collector crosses the slope AND the water only ever moves downhill." The elevation-monotonic flow (cascade SE -> collector along the contour to the field's low corner -> brook SE off-map) is how real TERRACED / cascade valley fields drain - a contour drain meeting a valley stream at ~90 deg is normal side-drainage, not a bug. If the *perception* still misleads on a diagonal slope, fix READABILITY - NOT the hydrology (slope cues via topo lines look awkward and the channels are too thin for internal flow arrows, so those are out). The readability fix we DID adopt: the **brook CURVES out of the collector's exit heading** toward downhill over its first few steps, so the junction reads as the collector turning down the valley INTO the stream, not a hard ~90 deg corner. **`drainage_junction_smooth`** gates it: for each drain-fed brook, the turn between the drain's ARRIVAL heading (into the shared outfall) and the brook's DEPARTURE heading - each averaged over ~40px so short jittery segments do not fool it, matched to the nearest drain within 40px - must be < 65 deg. Robust by construction: it only fires on an actual drain-fed brook, skips if no drain is within reach, and the 65 deg threshold sits well clear of a real gentle bend (<30 deg) yet catches a right-angle corner (~90 deg).
   - **The connectivity check is CASCADE / ROLE-aware** (`field_ditches_reach_source_and_sink`, made role-aware for the warp-thread model). The old check required each ditch's ONE connected component to hold both a pond source and a runoff sink - correct for the Meiji end-on-drain model, but WRONG for tagoshi: here the delivery ditches END mid-field and the water cascades plot-to-plot to the drain (which sits offset ~32-48px BELOW the ditch ends), so supply and drain are SEPARATE networks bridged by the cascade, not by a ditch. The check now grounds by ROLE: a **supply** ditch (main/branch/lateral) must trace to the pond **source**; the **drain** must trace to a runoff **sink** (off-map / stream / brook). Both water models pass. To satisfy it the gen records the pond->head-race feed (`frm:pond`) and the drain->off-map (or ->brook) discharge as anchored `channels`. Teeth: `test_checks.py::test_field_ditches_reach_source_and_sink_fires_when_ungrounded` (a supply ditch with no source + a drain with no sink both fire).
   - **Plots**: pre-modern 0.02-0.25 acre, irregular; v2 carves ~0.1-0.15 acre, ~9 scattered plots per household (fragmented holdings were normal). STRAIGHT rectangular channels/plots are post-1900 consolidation (the Tedori fan's ditches were only straightened in the early 1900s) - the organic waver is period-correct, do not "clean it up".
   - **Crop + COLOUR - the paddy body is UNIFORM; colour differences MEAN something or they are not drawn (GM decision).** A rice field is ONE crop at ONE transplant/growth stage, so the irrigated body is a **single uniform green** (`RICE_GREENS` collapsed to one value - the old plot-to-plot shade jitter denoted nothing, so it was removed; the **bund network + footpath dots** carry the structure, not colour). The colours that REMAIN are the ones that carry a category: **FLOODED blue-green** = the low plots that sit ON the drain collector and pool water before the outfall (~2%, the closing rank only); **RIPE gold** = early-ripening, when a map opts into it (Hoshigaoka uses none); and the **dry-field (hatake) strip** on the upslope margin above the supply canal, where each plot is a DIFFERENT dry crop (barley / buckwheat / millet / soy) drawn in its own colour + furrow direction (a fragmented mosaic of families' strips). So a reader can trust colour: uniform green = the one rice crop; a different colour = a real difference (draining-low / ripening / a dry crop). *(Kept `RICE_GREENS` as a 3-element list of the SAME value so `R.choice` consumes the RNG stream identically - the field geometry + FLOODED count stay byte-for-byte unchanged; only the body colour went uniform.)* NOTE this is a v2 (`waterfields.py`) change; the v1 `water_field` in settlement.py (the not-yet-rebuilt dispersed maps) still carries its near-identical 3-green mottle until those maps are rebuilt.
   - **NOT terraces - bunded paddies on a gentle tilt.** Hoshigaoka is a GENTLE valley slope, and terraces (*tanada* - stacked level benches with risers) are a STEEP-slope feature. So the plot lines are **bunds** (*aze*: low water-retaining earth ridges that double as footpaths - the dotted lines are the walkable bund tops), NOT terrace risers; each plot is a separately-flooded level basin, distinct to the eye but not stepped. Dry plots have NO water-retaining bund, so their boundaries are subtler - marked by a narrow unplowed earthen **balk** (the thin stroke), boundary stones, and the crop + furrow-direction contrast (not string/posts, which are too ephemeral for a field line).
   - **Dry (hatake) plots are RECTANGLES laid out AGAINST THE CANAL they border - NOT the paddy's fall grid (GM, 2026-07).** Dry fields bordering a supply canal are surveyed *to the canal*: one edge runs ALONG the canal, the other PERPENDICULAR to it, extending upslope - clean rectangles. The original code built them in the paddy's contour/fall frame (columns down the fall, rows on the contour), and since the head-race runs ~42deg DIAGONAL to the fall, every plot came out sheared ~43deg to a parallelogram. (A first fix merely softened that lean while STILL orienting to the paddy - the GM correctly rejected it: the plots should be oriented to the CANAL, not the paddy.) FIX in `_dry_fields`: tile the plots by ARC-LENGTH along the canal polyline (`a_pts`), and build each as a rectangle offset PERPENDICULAR to the local canal tangent, upslope. Because the base rides the canal, adjacent plots abut continuously along it - **no shear (skew ~0), no steps** - and only the outer (upslope) edge is ragged. The plots therefore ORIENT WITH THE CANAL (near-horizontal along Hoshigaoka's head-race, near-vertical along Ueda's), which naturally differs from the 45deg paddy grid - that's correct. Keep them on the DRY side: both edges sit UPSLOPE of the canal (a thin `berm`), never dipping across onto the wet paddy. Only `waterfields.py` maps (Hoshigaoka, Ueda); `dry_plot_furrows_vary` + acres unchanged.
   - **Slope is a KNOB (`down_deg`)**: Kikuta is NW-high (down=SE). Southern Lion lands slope S->N (Spine of the World to the south); Dragon the reverse; Unicorn W->E (Twilight Mountains west). NOTHING may assume SE - the whole frame (threads, carve, canals, drain) rotates with `down_deg`. **Ueda is the first NE-high variant (`down_deg=135`, SW flow), staged in `pool/wip/`** - a NE->SW field grows down-and-left, so it uses a TALLER frame; its population is drawn from `village_population()` (200-500, mode 350) off the seed - SEED=3 -> 300 inhabitants / 60 households, sized down from Hoshigaoka.
   - **Pond sizing (the rule)**: sole-storage tameike run ~2,000-2,500 m3 of storage per irrigated ha (typical depth 2-4 m); a STREAM-FED pond refilling 1-2x a season is comfortable at ~1,200-1,500 m3/ha. Hoshigaoka: 31.8 ha of paddy -> ~1.5 ha pond surface (rx=145, ry=92 px at 1px=2ft) ~ 47,000 m3 at ~3 m ~ 1,470 m3/ha + feeder stream. The first draft's 0.84 ha pond (~790 m3/ha) was honestly undersized - keep pond area proportional to command area.
   - **ONE field vs MANY (design note, GM-directed)**: the number of physical field BLOCKS is TERRAIN-driven - a broad gentle valley holds ONE contiguous expanse (the base case, Hoshigaoka); broken ground gives SEVERAL blocks with non-arable land between (2-3 blocks is historically normal, NOT an anomaly); hamlets are literally farmhouse clusters around scattered arable patches; towns and cities are ringed by many separate field patches. The engine already supports this: each field = one `build_comb` call with its own source/sluice/seed/params (siting the patches apart is the caller's job). Village roster: **Hoshigaoka** = the single-field base case (this WIP); **Kikuta** = rebuilt later WITH its backstory second field - a smaller BLIGHTED block, fallow since the summer ~6 months before map-time, its sluice SHUT so no water is wasted on it (fallow render: dry plots, dry ditches, closed offtake); **Hikari no Sato** = rebuilt later as a genuinely SPLIT multi-block village.
   - **Acreage from population (the sizing rule)**: a person eats ~1 koku/yr; pre-modern yield ~1.3 koku/tan; coarse grain fills part of the diet while ~45% of rice goes to tax -> ~0.8-1.0 tan gross paddy per person -> 350 people ~ 280-350 tan = **69-86 acres** (+ dry margins later). WIP Kikuta lands ~79 acres / ~600 plots.
   - **Dry fields (hatake) + bund beans (azemame) - the non-rice crops (researched + source-verified 2026-07; this is the record).** WHERE dry crops go: wet-rice villages sort by a topographic CATENA - irrigated paddy holds the flat valley bottom / plain; DRY fields (hatake) take the HIGHER, well-drained ground the water cannot command (river terraces, natural levees / micro-highs threading the plain, alluvial-fan edges, lower slopes, AND the slightly-raised ground the homesteads sit on); coppice woodland (satoyama) crowns the hills above. Sources: satoyama land-use literature ("wet-rice in the plains and valley bottoms... satoyama woodlands/grasslands for dry-field crops"; "large middle river terraces... large areas of crop fields and small areas of paddy"); Kanto-plain historical-GIS land-use studies. So dry fields are NOT one neat strip - historically they sit in SEVERAL positions, above all AROUND the houses ("each family has some paddy and some hatake", the household's dry plots near its home). *What v2 currently models, and the deliberate simplification:* only the UPSLOPE FRINGE above the supply canal (the dominant dry position on a water-rich valley floor). The levee and near-homestead hatake are DEFERRED to when farmhouses land - at that layer, scatter some dry plots by the homesteads too, don't leave them all in the top fringe.
     - **How much (the extent is a per-map KNOB, `dry_band` depth, NOT a constant).** Nationally, Edo / early-Meiji cultivated land ran ~55-60% paddy / ~40-45% dry field BY AREA - but that average is dragged UP by hill country with little irrigable land. A PRIME irrigated valley-bottom village (Hoshigaoka: pond-fed, nearly all flat ground commandable) is the opposite extreme: dry land is scarce and squeezed to the margins, so its dry fraction is LOW (Hoshigaoka ~11% of cultivated, a thin fringe). A drier / hill-flanked village runs a DEEP dry zone (toward the national average or past it). Hence `dry_band=(min,max)` upslope depth: default is the THIN fringe (water-rich valley, the base case); WIDEN it for dry/hill villages. Do NOT hardcode a single dry percentage - it swings with water availability, which is exactly the terrain knob.
     - **Crop structure - a fragmented PATCHWORK, not clean ribbons.** Holdings were scattered (each household ~several small non-contiguous plots) and multiple-/inter-cropped, so adjacent small plots carry DIFFERENT crops that cluster rather than forming full-height single-crop stripes. v2 assigns crop PER-PLOT with spatial coherence (usually inherit the neighbour, sometimes switch) so the margin reads as a mosaic. Crops (`DRY_CROPS`): barley/wheat (mugi), millet (awa/hie/kibi), buckwheat (soba), field soybean (daizu) - grain + pulses, the winter/dry-ground staples. These are FIELD-scale hatake, distinct from the dooryard KITCHEN garden (a separate, smaller near-house feature).
     - **Dry-field FURROWS vary PER PLOT (`dry_plot_furrows_vary`) - corrected 2026-07.** Dry crops are ridge-and-furrow row-cultivated (unlike the flooded paddy sheet). The FIRST version ran every plot's furrows ALONG the contour (`theta0`, tiny jitter) on the "ridge-along-contour dams rain / checks runoff" rationale - but that is a **STEEP-slope erosion measure**, and it made every plot's rows identical, so adjacent family strips were indistinguishable (the GM caught this). On a **GENTLE** valley margin contour ridging is not forced; the accurate look is the **patchwork quilt** - a fragmented mosaic where each family strip is plowed to its OWN orientation, so neighbouring plots run their rows different ways. So v2 now gives each dry plot a furrow angle spread up to **~1.1 rad (~63 deg) either side of the contour** (never straight down-slope), assigned by dropping each plot's angle into the **widest gap** between the angles of its already-placed neighbours - which GUARANTEES a real separation from every neighbour (and uses exactly ONE `R` draw per plot, as the old contour code did, so the plot GEOMETRY is byte-identical - only the angles changed). `dry_plot_furrows_vary` gates it: no two EDGE-adjacent dry plots (centroids within ~50 px) may run within ~6 deg of the same direction (compared mod pi - a furrow is an undirected line). So a reader can tell adjacent plots apart by row direction as well as by crop colour. **PARAMETERIZED both ways** via `build_comb(..., furrow_spread=RAD)` (the half-fan either side of the contour): the default **~1.1 rad** gives the gentle-valley patchwork (Hoshigaoka); a genuinely STEEP / terraced hill village passes a **small** value (e.g. `furrow_spread=0.06`) so the rows converge back onto the contour (ridge-along-contour erosion control). `build_comb` returns `furrows_vary` (True when `furrow_spread >= ~0.3`); the gen records it as `meta(dry_furrows_vary=...)`, and `dry_plot_furrows_vary` only REQUIRES variation when that flag is True - so a declared contour village's aligned rows are correct and do not fire the check. (One `R` draw per plot either way, so `furrow_spread` never perturbs the plot geometry - only the angles.)
     - **Azemame (bund soybeans) - drawn SYMBOLICALLY.** Soybeans grown on the paddy BUND crests (aze-mame, "ridge beans") were universal - a food crop wedged onto otherwise-idle bund tops (NOT the paddy's nitrogen supply; a flooded paddy fixes its own N via cyanobacteria/azolla - see the v1 nitrogen note). At 1px=2ft a single bund-top bean row is genuinely SUB-PIXEL, so it is drawn as a green BEAD line along a fraction (`bean_frac`, ~0.28) of the paddy bunds - an honest legibility SYMBOL, not to literal scale (unlike the dry plots, paddies, and water, whose footprints ARE to scale). GM chose the beaded-bund representation over alternatives.

- **Irrigation topology: ONE pond outlet that BRANCHES, not independent taps** (informs the `channel()` layout). A *tameike* reservoir has a SINGLE outlet sluice (*hi*) - occasionally two - so all its irrigation leaves by one main channel (*yosui*) that then FORKS downstream to the fields, dividing at junctions; it is NOT several independent pipes drilled into the pond. So route every pond channel from a **single shared outlet point** on the pond's field-facing foot (Kikuta: `OUTLET`), forking out to the commons. Full topology: feeder stream -> pond (storage) -> one outlet -> main channel branching to fields -> in-paddy distributary/drainage ditches (`irrigation_ditch`) -> drainage off the low side.
- **Water-width ladder: ditches, creeks, streams, moats, ponds** (drives `irrigation_channels_hairline`, `watercourses_wider_than_ditches`, `moat_is_heaviest_watercourse`, `moat_dwarfs_ditches`, and the `channel()` / `stream()` / `moat()` / pond-feeder drawn widths). Wet-rice water systems are a strongly **tiered, self-similar hierarchy**: each tier steps up ~2-4x in width because channel width scales with the square-root of the command-area flow it carries. The honest real-world ladder, off the smallest field ditch:

  | Tier | Real width | x field-ditch (true) |
  |---|---|---|
  | Field ditch (waters one paddy) | ~0.3 m | 1x |
  | Distribution lateral | ~1 m | ~3x |
  | Village creek / brook (natural) | ~2 m | ~6x |
  | Main irrigation canal (*yosui*) | ~5 m | ~15x |
  | Town river | ~20 m | ~65x |
  | Provincial-city moat (Himeji-tier) | ~20-35 m | ~70-115x |
  | Grand-city moat (Osaka-tier) | ~75 m | ~250x |
  | *Tameike* pond (areal - draw as polygon) | ~150-200 m across | ~1.5-2x a paddy |

  Anchors: headwater streams cluster near ~0.32 m; Himeji's moats average ~20 m (max 34.5 m); Osaka's outer moat is ~70-90 m; *tameike* reservoirs run ~3-26 ha (~150-600 m across). A field ditch is **~1/300 of the 1-cho (~100 m) paddy it feeds** - at true scale a hairline, essentially invisible.
  - *The departure we make, and why.* That true linear span is ~250:1 (1700:1 with reservoirs) - undrawable on one schematic without the ditch vanishing. Per the "roughly to scale" principle we **log-compress** the ladder: keep the *ordering* and the coarse steps, pull the multipliers together. The smallest line (the field ditch) is the one feature we knowingly draw **oversized**, parking it at the legibility floor (~2.5 px) rather than its true ~1/6-of-a-creek; honesty is anchored on the **larger** features instead (the moat reads ~2-3x a creek, as in reality). Rendered widths: **channel/ditch ~2.5 px** (hairline), **pond-feeder ~5 px** (a lateral), **village-creek stream ~9 px**, **town-river / moat-feeder stream = the moat width** (conservation of flow - a stream supplying a moat carries its full flow), **city moat ~26 px** (heaviest watercourse; a provincial city, not the Osaka grand-moat tier). A pond is **areal** - drawn as a polygon at paddy scale, never on the line-width ladder; its *outlet* is a main canal. The checks pin ordering and the key gaps (ditch hairline; creek >=2.5x the ditch; moat the heaviest watercourse; moat >=4x the ditch) rather than exact pixels, so the tiers can never collapse back into one undifferentiated blue line.
- **Farmers, fields, and the economy of the wall** (drives `town_farmers_plurality`, `all_houses_field_adjacent`, `outside_fields_farmhouse_density` / `city_outside_fields_have_farmhouses`, `city_no_inwall_farms`, `wall_hugs_the_town` / `city_no_large_empty_space`). Farmers are the overwhelming majority caste (the plurality), and they build **close to the fields they work** (farmsteads ring the paddies at roughly village density). A city does NOT farm inside its walls - land within is too valuable and the wall too costly to throw around farmland - **unless** an unusual in-wall water source makes a small agricultural district worthwhile (the documented exception). Walls were expensive, so a rampart **hugs** its built-up area: no large empty swath inside, sized to hold the population densely.
- **Fire towers: the fire-defense of a dense, ENCLOSED wooden core** (drives `walled_town_has_fire_tower` / `city_has_fire_towers`, `fire_tower_in_commoner_quarter`). Premodern Japanese cities burned constantly - Edo was the "city of fires," and the Meireki fire of 1657 killed ~100,000. The institution we depict is the **fire-watch tower** (*hinomi-yagura*, 火の見櫓) - a tall braced-timber tower with an alarm bell (*hanshō*, 半鐘) the watchman struck in a cadence that told the city how *near* a fire was. **Why walled towns and cities but NOT open towns:** the tower belongs to the dense, contiguous, *enclosed* wooden core. Two reinforcing reasons. (1) **Density** - fire is catastrophic only where roofs touch (the packed intramural *machiya*/tenement warren); an open road-town strung along the highway, fields running off every edge, has natural breaks (the road, the field-gaps, the surrounding open ground), so a standing tower does not earn its cost. (2) **The wall traps the fire** - the rampart that keeps raiders out also keeps a blaze *in*, so the watch over the rooftops is most needed exactly where there is a wall. **L7R/Sengoku adaptation:** the famous chartered townsman brigades (the *machi-bikeshi* "iroha" companies) are a peace-era Tokugawa institution; in a Sengoku-flavored Rokugan the fire-watch is the **magistrate's** (yoriki/doshin/ashigaru manning the tower), not self-governing townsman companies. The tower is drawn as an OPEN braced frame with a red bell (distinct from the solid guard towers ON the rampart - a fire tower is a *civilian interior* structure watching the rooftops, not the wall); it is an overlap-checked struct, so it must stand clear of the wall, roads, buildings, and open water (the pond - Tango's west tower once landed on the pond rim, which is what closed that gap with `no_structure_on_pond`), and `fire_tower_in_commoner_quarter` keeps it in the commoner warren, never the samurai ward. It also keeps **>= 5px of daylight** around its frame (`fire_tower_standoff`): the blanket overlap check only catches true intersection, so a tower butted flush against a house passed it while reading as a collision - the glyph's roof cap overhangs the recorded frame by ~2px a side, and an open braced tower's footing and ladder sit clear of the neighboring eaves (it stands on a seam, not in a party-wall row). The one neighbor neither pass can see is a **wellhead** (`fire_tower_clear_of_wells`): wells are overlap-EXEMPT (a wellhead's nominal footprint may kiss a dense-city building, because houses ring a tenement court closely), so a tower dropped onto a well trips no generic check - but the tower's footing would stand in the well court blocking the quarter's shared draw-point (the idobata), so the tower keeps the same >= 5px of daylight from every well's clearance disc (this caught Tango's west tower standing squarely on a well; the bad manifest is pinned in `pool/regressions/`). **When a settlement rates MORE than one tower, they DISPERSE ACROSS THE QUARTERS** (`fire_towers_dispersed` + `fire_tower_amid_its_district`): historically the fire-watch was parcelled out per neighborhood - each Edo machi block-group kept its own tower, and the shogunate's official watch stations were likewise distributed one to a district - because a tower's useful watch is its visual neighborhood of rooftops. Two rules enforce it. (1) Pairwise floor: two towers inside one watch radius of each other duplicate coverage, so every pair stands at least one watch radius (the same ~230px the commoner-quarter check uses for "near its rooftops") apart. (2) District centring: a comfortable pairwise gap is NOT enough - Tango's original pair stood 341px apart yet both NW of centre, leaving the NE laborer warren (the city's biggest rooftop mass) unwatched. So every commoner dwelling (inside the walls, when walled - the extramural gate-market rows are not what the towers exist for) is assigned to its NEAREST tower; that partition is the de facto watch districting the drawn towers imply, and each tower must stand near its district's centre of mass (offset <= max(0.9 x district RMS radius, one watch radius)) - AMID the blocks it watches, not at their far edge with a whole unwatched quadrant in its ledger. In practice: put one tower amid each major commoner quarter, sized to the tower count (Tango: NE laborers, west servant/burakumin, south-central merchants -> 3 towers). Place it LAST, after the packs and `farmsteads()`, on a cleared seam the finished map already has. *(Deliberate divergence from literal scale, per the legibility license: a single tower stands in for a quarter's whole fire-watch; real cities had many.)*
- **Why we DON'T depict firebreaks (a deliberate omission, so it is not re-added).** The other historical urban fire-defense was the **firebreak** (*hiyokechi*, 火除地 / *hirokoji*, 広小路) - a cleared strip of open ground a blaze could not jump, which, too valuable to leave idle, filled with removable stalls and theater stages and so doubled as the market/amusement ground (Edo's *hirokoji* became its entertainment districts). We tried two depictions and dropped both. A **drawn** plaza (a shaded clearing placed before the dwelling packs) forces the packer to flow houses around an *invented* clearing, which reads as a designed plaza rather than the *absence* of building a firebreak actually is. A **label-only** marking (a run-aligned annotation dropped onto a gap the finished map happens to leave) is just naming existing dead space - it adds a word, not a feature, and getting the gaps and label orientations to read well takes real effort for no real payoff. So a firebreak is **not a feature of these maps**: the towers carry the fire-defense story on their own. If a future map genuinely needs a firebreak (a wide ceremonial fire-plaza that is itself the subject), build it as a real, deliberately-placed feature with its own glyph - do not resurrect the marked-gap approach.
